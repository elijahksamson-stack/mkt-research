## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Use this file to define the object being measured before comparing companies, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Upstream exploration and production, mineral and royalty interests, and the upstream portions of integrated companies. Midstream, LNG, refining, trading, and oilfield services appear only where they price, constrain, or monetize upstream output.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Conventional onshore | Reservoir development from discrete or continuous accumulations | boe/d, working-interest wells | Decline, lifting method, fiscal take, and water handling differ from tight oil |
| Tight oil and shale gas | Factory-style horizontal drilling and multistage completion | lateral feet, stages, IP30, EUR | High initial decline makes inventory quality and maintenance capital decisive |
| Offshore and deepwater | Long-cycle subsea, platform, or floating production | kbbl/d, project barrels, uptime | Large pre-first-oil capital, host capacity, weather, and abandonment dominate |
| Gas and NGL developments | Dry gas, condensate, and extracted liquids | MMBtu/d, Mcf/d, bbl/d NGL | Heating value, impurities, processing recovery, basis, and takeaway must be separated |
| Mineral, royalty, and production interests | Contractual share of production without identical operating exposure | net royalty acres, revenue interest | Working interest, net revenue interest, overriding royalty, and production sharing are not interchangeable |

### Specifications that change value

- Crude must state API gravity, sulfur, acidity/metals where relevant, location, and delivery point.
- Gas must state heating value, CO2, H2S, nitrogen, water, pressure, and residue versus wet-gas basis.
- Volumes must identify gross versus net, working-interest versus entitlement, oil-equivalent conversion, and operated versus non-operated.
- Inventory must distinguish acreage, locations, proved reserves, resource, and risked recoverable volumes.
- Economics must state benchmark, differential, transport/processing, royalty/fiscal terms, hedge, and abandonment basis.

### Role map

Mineral owner grants subsurface access; operator makes technical and spending decisions; non-operating partners fund their shares; service firms execute; midstream accepts and conditions production; refiners, LNG plants, utilities, or marketers buy; regulators and host communities control permission.

### Terms that must be explicit

- boe conversion is an energy convention, not an economic equivalence
- PDP, PDNP, PUD, probable, and contingent resource
- working interest versus net revenue interest
- type curve, EUR, recovery factor, and decline
- lifting cost versus full-cycle breakeven


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: upstream exploration and production plus integrated-company upstream operations; refining and oilfield services are addressed only where they transmit into upstream economics.

### 1. Subsurface rights and geological inventory

The first input is access to acreage, mineral rights, leases, seismic data, and drillable locations. Resource quality is defined by reservoir pressure, thickness, permeability, fluid mix, depth, and proximity to infrastructure. **[Inference]** “Inventory years” matter only after normalizing for commodity price, spacing, royalties, and full-cycle return; gross locations are not reserves.

### 2. Capital, rigs, and completion equipment

Drilling rigs, pressure-pumping fleets, tubulars, proppant, drilling fluids, bits, wellheads, subsea systems, and completion crews turn acreage into production. EIA reported 553 U.S. rotary rigs in May 2026, 417 oil-directed and 127 gas-directed (**[Fact]** — https://www.eia.gov/dnav/ng/ng_enr_drill_s1_m.htm). Rig count remains useful but productivity, lateral length, completion intensity, and drilled-but-uncompleted wells change the production response.

### 3. Water, sand, steel, chemicals, and power

Shale wells consume large volumes of water and proppant; casing/tubing link costs to steel; pumps and compression require fuel/electricity. Produced-water handling and disposal can be the binding local constraint. Offshore projects require specialized vessels, subsea equipment, and long-lead fabrication.

### 4. Labor, technology, and data

Geoscientists, drilling/completion engineers, field crews, control systems, reservoir models, and real-time data improve recovery and reduce days per well. **[Inference]** Technology creates value mainly by improving recovery per dollar and repeatability, not by maximizing initial production at the expense of decline or parent-child interference.

### 5. Midstream access and price realization

Gathering, processing, pipelines, storage, export terminals, and takeaway capacity are required inputs. Basis differentials can make local realized prices diverge sharply from Henry Hub, WTI, or Brent. In 2025 U.S. crude output reached a record 13.6 million b/d and dry gas production 39 Tcf (**[Fact]** — EIA — https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67684).

### 6. Fiscal and regulatory inputs

Royalties, severance tax, production-sharing terms, permits, methane/flaring rules, environmental liability, sanctions, and OPEC+/state policy shape returns. Government resource ownership makes above-ground risk as important as geology internationally.

### 7. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** benchmark price, basis, royalty/tax, service cost, decline rate, and lifting cost.
- **Cap capacity:** permits/acreage, takeaway, water disposal, crews, rigs/frack spreads, subsea equipment, and balance-sheet capacity.
- **Shock path:** price rise → cash flow rises immediately on unhedged barrels → activity follows with a lag → service inflation and new supply reduce returns and eventually rebalance price.

### 8. Full dependency ledger and cross-market transmission

- **Resource and access:** acreage or production-sharing rights, subsurface data, seismic crews, leases, surface access, water rights, community consent, and a legal route to market precede drilling. A technically economic reservoir without title, permits, evacuation capacity, or a fiscal regime is not commercial inventory.
- **Physical bill of inputs:** rigs, drill pipe, bits, casing, tubing, wellheads, pumps, compressors, valves, cement, proppant, drilling mud, completion chemicals, corrosion inhibitors, diesel or electricity, process heat, fresh or recycled water, and disposal capacity. Steel embeds iron ore or scrap, metallurgical coal, ferroalloys, electricity, and freight; proppant embeds sand mining, washing, drying, rail, and trucking.
- **People, technology, and services:** geoscientists, reservoir and drilling engineers, directional drillers, pressure-pumping crews, mechanics, HSE staff, land/legal teams, software, seismic interpretation, measurement, cybersecurity, and methane/leak monitoring. Offshore supply vessels, helicopters, subsea equipment, and weather windows add a separate critical chain.
- **Infrastructure and capital:** gathering, treatment, storage, pipelines, export terminals, roads, power, communications, spare parts, insurance, letters of credit, hedges, decommissioning security, and working capital. Reserve-based lending, bonds, equity, joint ventures, farm-outs, NOC budgets, and internal cash flow fund different risk stages.
- **What moves margin versus volume:** crude/gas benchmarks, location and quality differentials, royalties, tax, service intensity, steel, power, water, and realized hedge prices set cash margin. Rig/frac spread availability, sand and water logistics, takeaway, flaring limits, permits, skilled crews, and offshore equipment physically gate volumes.

The complete shock path is commodity-specific. Higher oil prices lift producer cash flow but raise diesel, steel, service, and working-capital costs with a lag; higher gas can help gas producers while hurting power-intensive LNG, refining, fertilizer, and chemicals customers. Pipeline scarcity widens local discounts rather than changing the global benchmark. Recycling water, electrifying field equipment, refracturing, enhanced recovery, and substituting regional sand can reduce an input, but usually trade lower operating cost for capital, geology, or execution risk. End-of-life plugging, site restoration, produced-water treatment, methane and CO2 liabilities are inputs to the original investment decision, not optional downstream clean-up.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Value chain

National oil companies and mineral owners control resources; E&Ps explore/develop; oilfield services execute wells; midstream gathers/processes/transports; refiners convert crude; marketers distribute products. Integrated majors span several stages. **[Inference]** Upstream captures scarcity rent when prices are high but gives it back through commodity exposure; midstream captures contracted throughput; services capture specialized bottlenecks; refining captures product cracks and operational complexity.

### 2. Industry structure

Oil is globally traded and priced against Brent/WTI; gas is more regional but LNG is connecting basins. OPEC+ manages material low-cost oil capacity, while U.S. shale is fragmented, short-cycle, and increasingly consolidated. Offshore and LNG are long-cycle and capital-heavy.

### 3. Geography

- **Middle East:** lowest-cost giant conventional resources and state control.
- **United States:** Permian is the dominant growth basin; Appalachia/Haynesville lead gas; Gulf Coast links production to refining/LNG.
- **Russia/Caspian:** large resources with sanctions and route risk.
- **Canada:** oil sands—long-life, high upfront capital and transport intensity.
- **Brazil/Guyana/West Africa:** deepwater growth with specialized offshore requirements.

### 4. Trade and security

Pipelines, chokepoints, tankers, sanctions, strategic reserves, and LNG terminals make energy a national-security asset. The U.S. remained the largest crude and gas producer in 2025 (**[Fact]** — EIA — https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67684). **[Inference]** Security premiums accrue to diversified, low-decline supply and export infrastructure when geopolitical risk rises.

### 5. Where value is migrating

Toward advantaged inventory, low breakevens, scale in the Permian, LNG-linked gas, and integrated infrastructure. Investors increasingly reward free cash flow, capital returns, and inventory depth over production growth. Consolidation transfers value from subscale operators with depleting tier-one acreage to firms that can optimize contiguous positions.

### 6. What is losing relevance

High-cost, short-reserve-life assets; growth funded by chronic external capital; stranded gas without takeaway; and reserves dependent on permanently high prices. Thermal-demand uncertainty and emissions policy raise terminal-value risk, but underinvestment can tighten near-term supply.

### 7. Disruption and counterarguments

Electrification, efficiency, and renewables erode long-duration oil demand growth; petrochemicals, aviation, heavy transport, and emerging-market demand slow the transition. Methane rules and carbon pricing differentiate barrels by emissions intensity. The strongest counterargument to “peak demand destroys value” is that decline rates require continuous reinvestment even in flat demand, supporting low-cost producers.

### 9. Full output, customer, funding, and policy map

- **Outputs and residuals:** saleable streams include crude by grade, condensate, NGLs, dry gas, helium or sulfur in selected fields, and occasionally captured CO2; associated gas, produced water, drilling waste, methane, CO2, and retirement obligations are economically material residuals. Each stream has a different specification, meter, buyer, transport path, and price.
- **Customer chain:** producers sell to refiners, gas processors, marketers, pipelines, LNG plants, petrochemical and fertilizer plants, utilities, industrial users, or governments. The contractual buyer is often not the final fuel user; refiners, utilities, airlines, shippers, and chemical producers transmit demand back upstream. Midstream operators, mineral owners, host governments, and service companies take revenue before equity holders.
- **Geography and trade:** geology fixes the resource, while pipelines, canals, ports, LNG trains, storage, sanctions, shipping insurance, and refinery configuration determine fungibility. Landlocked barrels can trade at deep discounts; gas is regional until liquefaction or pipeline capacity links basins. OPEC+ policy, NOC investment, US shale response, Asian import demand, and European security policy jointly set the global call on supply.
- **Industry structure:** supermajors optimize integrated portfolios; NOCs combine commercial and sovereign objectives; independents specialize by basin; private operators can harvest mature assets; mineral/royalty owners avoid operating cost; midstream and service firms own complementary bottlenecks. Consolidation can lower unit costs but also concentrates basin, antitrust, and abandonment exposure.
- **Funding:** public equity and debt favor inventory depth, governance, and distributions; private equity commonly funds acreage aggregation and exit; banks lend against reserves; traders prepay for offtake; project finance supports discrete LNG or infrastructure; governments fund strategic reserves, infrastructure, research, tax preferences, and NOC capital. Capital availability is pro-cyclical unless hedging or long-term contracts break the link.
- **Legislation and permission to operate:** mineral ownership, production-sharing contracts, royalties, severance and corporate tax, methane and flaring rules, water and waste permits, offshore leasing, pipeline approvals, local setbacks, sanctions, export controls, carbon pricing, and decommissioning security all differ by jurisdiction. Policy analysis must separate enacted rules, agency implementation, litigation, and political proposals.

Adjacent markets are two-way: oil sets transport and petrochemical feedstock costs; gas sets marginal power, LNG, ammonia/fertilizer, and industrial heat economics; carbon prices and clean-energy policy change substitution; steel, sand, shipping, and oilfield-service cycles change development cost. Refining capacity, not crude supply alone, determines product scarcity. The landscape therefore must be analyzed by molecule, basin, route, customer configuration, and fiscal regime rather than by a single oil-price view.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Upstream workflow

Lease/resource access → seismic and subsurface interpretation → appraisal → development plan/permitting → drilling → completion → tie-in → production → workovers/enhanced recovery → plugging and reclamation. Offshore adds platform/FPSO/subsea design and multi-year sanction-to-first-oil timelines.

### 2. Conventional vs. unconventional

Conventional wells target permeable reservoirs and may produce for decades with lower decline. Shale uses long horizontal laterals and hydraulic fracturing; output arrives quickly but declines steeply, requiring continuous drilling. **Rule:** shale is a manufacturing/portfolio business; deepwater is a project-execution business.

### 3. Production and reserve economics

Revenue = volume × realized price, split among oil, gas, and NGLs. Cash cost includes royalties/taxes, lifting, transport, and field overhead; full-cycle economics add drilling/completion, land, facilities, exploration, and abandonment. PDP reserves carry lower risk than undeveloped inventory.

### 4. KPIs

- Production and mix; realized price versus benchmark.
- Lease operating expense and transport per boe.
- Finding/development cost, recycle ratio, and capital efficiency.
- Decline rate, base maintenance capex, and reserve replacement.
- Inventory depth at stated breakevens.
- Free cash flow after maintenance capital and net debt.
- Flaring/methane intensity and abandonment liability.

### 5. Cycle signals

Benchmark curves, inventories, OPEC+ spare capacity, rigs, frack spreads, drilled-but-uncompleted wells, service pricing, and producer capex guide supply. EIA notes rig count alone is incomplete because wells drilled per rig and new-well productivity also determine output (**[Fact]** — https://www.eia.gov/petroleum/drilling/faqs.php).

### 6. Failure modes

Dry holes/reservoir underperformance, cost overruns, weak well spacing, parent-child interference, takeaway constraints, spills, political/fiscal change, hedging losses, and overpaying for inventory. High initial production is not sufficient evidence of attractive recovery or return.

### 7. Valuation

Use NAV by asset with commodity-price and cost scenarios; EV/EBITDA or FCF yield on mid-cycle prices; reserve metrics such as EV/PDP or EV/boe only with quality adjustments. Integrated majors also require downstream/chemical segment values. **[Inference]** Reported P/E is procyclical and most misleading at commodity peaks and troughs.

### 8. Complete operating and cash-conversion model

The operating chain is license/lease → seismic and appraisal → development concept and final investment decision → drilling and completion → connection and commissioning → primary production → artificial lift/compression and workovers → secondary or enhanced recovery where economic → plugging, removal, remediation, and monitoring. Commercial production is measured by oil, gas, and NGL volumes and quality, not gross fluid lifted; water cut, gas-oil ratio, pressure, decline, recovery factor, uptime, and gathering constraints determine the saleable yield.

Revenue equals measured entitlement volumes times benchmark price, adjusted for grade/location differentials, transport, processing, marketing, royalties, production sharing, and hedges. Take-or-pay transport can preserve capacity but create fixed cost when wells underperform. Joint interests create cash calls and timing differences; operators may control activity while partners fund their share. Receivables, inventory in transit, prepaid drilling, tubular and sand stocks, abandonment security, derivative collateral, and tax/royalty payment lags explain why cash flow diverges from EBITDA.

Analyze capital in layers: lease and exploration spend; finding and development cost; maintenance capital required to offset decline; growth capital; midstream commitments; acquisitions; and asset-retirement funding. A producer can report growth while destroying value if full-cycle reinvestment, dilution, or future abandonment exceeds discounted net revenue. For integrated firms, upstream results must be separated from refining, chemicals, trading, and marketing effects.

Scenario transmission should be explicit:

1. **Price decline:** revenue falls immediately; royalties partly flex; services and drilling fall later; hedges cushion temporarily; maintenance cuts accelerate future decline.
2. **Service or steel inflation:** well cost rises before output; breakevens worsen; standardized designs and slower activity may restore bargaining power.
3. **Takeaway outage:** production is curtailed or receives a wider differential even if the global benchmark is unchanged.
4. **Rate or funding shock:** high-leverage/private operators cut rigs first; reserve redeterminations and hedge collateral can amplify the cycle.
5. **Rule change:** methane, water, flaring, tax, sanctions, or decommissioning rules alter both current opex and proved-reserve economics.

The minimum KPI set is entitlement production by stream, realized price and differential, lifting cost, transportation/processing, royalty and tax take, capex per well, drilling days, initial production and decline, reserve replacement, F&D cost, recycle ratio, free cash flow after maintenance capital, leverage, hedge book, inventory life, methane/flaring/water intensity, and undiscounted retirement obligations.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points. Refresh them for the relevant asset, product, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Field cash contribution = entitlement saleable volumes × (realized benchmark ± quality/location differential) − royalties/fiscal take − transport and processing − lifting cost. Full-cycle value then subtracts exploration, development, maintenance capital, financing, and decommissioning.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| First-year shale decline | roughly 50–80% for many oil/gas type curves | Sets maintenance drilling and inventory burn |
| Conventional decline | often low-single digits to low-teens annually | Determines sustaining capital and mature-asset cash harvest |
| Lifting cost | commonly about $5–30/boe before full-cycle capital | Separates operating resilience from development economics |
| Royalty/fiscal burden | often 10–30% of revenue, but PSC/tax systems vary widely | Defines entitlement and government take |
| Onshore well cycle | days to months from spud through first production | Controls working capital and short-cycle response |
| Offshore lead time | often 5–10+ years from discovery to first oil | Creates price and execution mismatch |
| Reserve life | years of current production; interpret with decline and capital needs | Tests duration rather than simple abundance |
| F&D cost | $/boe of reserve or resource added | Connects capital to future inventory |
| Recycle ratio | cash margin per boe divided by F&D cost per boe | Tests reinvestment quality through the cycle |
| Net debt/EBITDA | through-cycle leverage rather than spot-price leverage | Measures funding resilience and covenant risk |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile production to entitlement sales, realized price to benchmark, EBITDAX to cash taxes and working capital, and reported capex to maintenance, growth, acquisition, and capitalized interest. Reserve additions are not revenue; impairments are noncash but can reveal a changed price, cost, or development assumption.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- A $10/bbl oil or $1/MMBtu gas move after hedges and fiscal take.
- Type-curve productivity, decline, spacing, recovery, and service-cost inflation.
- Basis/takeaway outages, water disposal, flaring limits, and unplanned downtime.
- Rates, reserve-based-lending redeterminations, derivative collateral, and acquisition currency.

### Valuation discipline

Use asset-level risked NAV and a corporate cash-flow bridge together. Avoid applying a single multiple across short-cycle shale, mature conventional, deepwater, gas, and royalty models.


## 6. Regulation and Public Funding


Regulatory snapshot reviewed through **2026-07-14**. Verify enacted text, implementation dates, litigation, and current guidance before use.

### Permission chain

Mineral owner grants subsurface access; operator makes technical and spending decisions; non-operating partners fund their shares; service firms execute; midstream accepts and conditions production; refiners, LNG plants, utilities, or marketers buy; regulators and host communities control permission.

### Jurisdiction and regime map

| Jurisdiction or layer | Core regimes and authorities | Economic transmission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | Federal/state/private mineral ownership; BLM/BOEM leasing; EPA/state air-water rules; PHMSA for related pipelines | Royalty, severance tax, methane/flaring, permitting, and federal acreage access alter netback and timing |
| UK, Norway, and EU-linked markets | National licensing and offshore safety; country fiscal regimes; EU methane and carbon-related rules where applicable | Tax stability, decommissioning security, and gas-market linkage dominate investment |
| Production-sharing and NOC jurisdictions | Host-government contracts, local content, state participation, OPEC policy in relevant exporters | Entitlement barrels and cost recovery differ from concession ownership |
| Cross-border and sanctioned trade | Export controls, sanctions, maritime insurance, customs, and investment screening | Can strand a grade, widen differentials, or prevent technology and capital access |

### Public and private funding

Private funding includes retained cash, equity, bonds, reserve-based loans, private equity, joint ventures, farm-outs, streaming or prepayment, and midstream commitments. Public capital enters through NOCs, sovereign infrastructure, tax treatment, strategic reserves, research, and in some jurisdictions direct state participation.

### Enforcement and liability

Primary exposure includes lease cancellation, civil or criminal environmental penalties, well-control liability, royalty/tax audits, sanctions, permit injunctions, production curtailment, and inadequately funded abandonment. Parent guarantees and joint-and-several rules can move liabilities across entities.

### Update checklist

- Confirm the legal entity, asset location, product, customer, and regulator.
- Separate enacted law, final rule, guidance, permit condition, litigation, and proposal.
- Date grants, tax provisions, tariffs, sanctions, quotas, and administered prices.
- Trace compliance cost, schedule, capacity, price, liability, and funding separately.


## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons rather than exhaustiveness.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1973–1974 oil embargo | Supply restraint met price-controlled and oil-dependent demand | Prices and policy changed faster than physical consumption or domestic supply | Geopolitics, inventories, and demand elasticity must be modeled together |
| 1986 oil-price collapse | Saudi output policy changed after years of quota pressure | High-cost supply and producer fiscal balances reset | Cartel discipline and marginal supply response are regime variables |
| 2014–2016 shale/OPEC downturn | Rapid US growth met OPEC's decision not to defend price | Service deflation improved wells but did not rescue weak balance sheets | Productivity, cost deflation, and corporate returns are different measures |
| April 2020 negative WTI | Demand collapsed while Cushing delivery/storage and futures mechanics bound | A global commodity became locally non-storable at the contract point | Storage, basis, expiry, and logistics can dominate headline supply-demand |
| 2022 European gas shock | Pipeline supply loss met limited LNG and infrastructure flexibility | Regional gas and power prices disconnected from historical norms | Molecules, routes, storage, weather, and policy determine regional clearing |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Rig/frac activity, permits, service prices, differentials, storage, hedge curves, and project sanctions.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Reserve additions without capital productivity, volume growth funded by leverage, or benchmark strength not reflected in local realization.
- **Durable lesson:** Inventory is valuable only when technically recoverable, legally accessible, financeable, connected, and economic after full fiscal and abandonment cost.

### Normalization rules

- Keep oil, gas, and NGL volumes and prices separate before any boe aggregation.

- Compare capital on a consistent acquisition and maintenance/growth basis.

### Evidence traps

- Treating boe as equal economic value across oil and gas.
- Calling every undrilled location inventory without risking title, spacing, takeaway, and capital.
- Using spot EBITDA for leverage or valuation at a commodity extreme.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

Geography changes ownership, contracting, cost, funding, regulation and risk; compare like with like.

| Geography / archetype | Operating model | Economic and risk implications |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States and Canada shale | Private/mineral leasing, short-cycle pad development, competitive midstream and service markets | Fast capital response; decline, basis, water disposal, acreage quality and hedge books drive dispersion |
| Middle East and North Africa | State ownership, NOC-operated assets or concessions/production-sharing contracts, large conventional reservoirs | Low lifting cost can coexist with fiscal, quota, security and state-budget exposure |
| Brazil, Guyana, North Sea and other offshore basins | Long-cycle licensed or production-sharing developments tied to FPSOs, platforms and subsea systems | Host capacity, local content, execution, weather, decommissioning and first-oil timing dominate |
| Russia, Central Asia and other pipeline-dependent systems | State-influenced production with constrained pipeline, shipping, equipment or buyer access | Sanctions, route concentration, discounts, currency and technology access can overwhelm reservoir economics |

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Resource and equipment inputs | Mineral rights, seismic, rigs, steel tubulars, sand, chemicals, water, power and skilled services | Steel, diesel, power or service inflation raises well cost; water, permits or equipment can cap completions |
| Evacuation and processing | Gathering, gas processing, pipelines, storage, LNG terminals and export docks | Takeaway scarcity widens basis and can force flaring limits, shut-ins or delayed drilling |
| Downstream demand | Refining, petrochemicals, utilities, LNG, industry, transport and heating | Product cracks, gas-to-coal switching, vehicle efficiency and industrial cycles change feedstock pull |
| Finance and risk transfer | Reserve-based loans, bonds, equity, JVs, farm-outs, hedges, insurance and state budgets | Price decks, collateral values and hedge counterparties determine capital availability before physical depletion |
| Externalities and end-of-life | Methane, CO2, produced water, land restoration and well plugging | Carbon rules, remediation security and orphan-well liability alter full-cycle cost and license to operate |

The practical test is to trace a shock through availability, delivered cost, working capital, output, customer economics, financing and eventual substitution—not merely name the adjacent market.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One short-cycle oil-weighted shale well, on a net-revenue-interest basis.

**Core equation:** `Full-cycle value = net saleable volume × realized netback − operating/transport/tax cost − drilling, completion, facilities, land and abandonment capital`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Net saleable volume | 900,000 boe gross EUR × 75% net revenue interest | 675,000 boe; validate decline, spacing and parent-child effects |
| Revenue | 675,000 boe × $48/boe realized netback | $32.4m before operating costs |
| Variable and operating burden | 675,000 boe × $17/boe lifting, transport and production tax | $11.5m |
| Upfront and terminal capital | $10.0m D&C + $2.0m land/facilities + $0.5m present-value plugging | $12.5m invested/committed |
| Illustrative surplus | $32.4m − $11.5m − $12.5m | $8.4m pre-tax undiscounted; NPV can be far lower because decline front-loads capital |
| Decision sensitivity | Stress realized price ±$10/boe and EUR ±20% | Price, recoverable volume and timing should be separated from service-cost productivity |

**Decision test:** Approve inventory only on a common price, differential, royalty, decline, inflation and abandonment basis; do not rank wells by IP rate or half-cycle cost.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | The highest full-cycle-cost barrel or gas molecule needed to meet demand, often short-cycle shale, offshore sanctioning or flexible OPEC capacity | Compare incentive price, spare capacity, decline replacement and financing access |
| Marginal customer | Refiner, LNG buyer, power generator or industrial user able to switch feedstock, reduce runs or destroy demand | Cracks, netbacks, fuel switching and storage economics reveal willingness to pay |
| Clearing mechanism | Global waterborne benchmarks plus quality/location differentials; regional gas hubs where transport is constrained | Inventories, freight, basis, time spreads and spare capacity show tightness |
| Cash shutdown point | Realized price below avoidable lifting, transport and tax cost, subject to reservoir and contract damage | Existing wells may operate far below the price required to drill replacements |
| New-capacity incentive | Expected risk-adjusted NPV above cost of capital after fiscal take and full infrastructure/closure cost | Forward price, service inflation and policy durability matter more than spot price alone |
| Adjustment lag | Months for shale completions; years for pipelines, LNG and offshore megaprojects | A single supply curve is invalid without time horizon |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| boe | Economic equivalence of oil and gas | Show physical commodity volumes and realized revenue separately |
| Breakeven | Corporate cash neutrality, wellhead shut-in, half-cycle drilling or full-cycle return | State price deck, tax, differential, capital scope, discount rate and time |
| Inventory | Acreage, locations, reserves or economically sanctionable wells | Risk locations for spacing, permits, infrastructure, capital and return |
| Production growth | Value creation | Bridge price, royalty, decline replacement, capital and free cash flow per share |
| Lifting cost | Full cost of supply | Add transport, tax, maintenance, development, financing and abandonment |
