## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Use this file to define the object being measured before comparing companies, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Coal, petroleum refining and products, biofuels and sustainable aviation fuel, uranium conversion/enrichment/fabrication, and other merchant fuels consumed in transport, heat, power, or industrial processes.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Thermal and metallurgical coal | Mined, washed, and transported solid fuel or reductant | saleable tons, heat value, coke quality | Thermal heat/ash/sulfur and met-coal coking quality create different markets |
| Petroleum refining | Crude conversion into gasoline, diesel, jet, LPG, asphalt, coke, sulfur, and feedstocks | barrels/day, product yield, crack spread | Complexity, crude slate, product specification, and location drive value |
| Ethanol and conventional biodiesel | Crop or oil conversion with feed and glycerin co-products | gallons, yield per bushel or pound | Commodity spread plus policy attribute; co-product credits matter |
| Renewable diesel and SAF | Hydroprocessed or other low-carbon drop-in fuels | gallons, carbon intensity, feedstock share | Hydrogen, approved feedstock, pathway score, and offtake determine economics |
| Nuclear fuel cycle | Uranium mining, conversion, enrichment, fabrication, and related services | lb U3O8, kgU, SWU, assemblies | Material, conversion, enrichment, fabrication, and utility qualification are separate markets |

### Specifications that change value

- Coal must state heat value, moisture, ash, sulfur, volatile matter, grindability, and met-coal properties.
- Crude and products require grade, sulfur, octane/cetane, vapor pressure, flash point, cold-flow, and delivery basis.
- Biofuels require feedstock, process, lifecycle carbon-intensity methodology, blend specification, and credit pathway.
- Nuclear contracts must distinguish uranium, conversion, SWU, tails assay, fabrication, origin, and delivery schedule.
- Every margin must identify physical product spread separately from tax, renewable, or low-carbon credits.

### Role map

Resource/feedstock producer supplies; converter mines, refines, ferments, hydroprocesses, enriches, or fabricates; trader and terminal blend and finance inventory; distributor/retailer delivers; utility, airline, fleet, steel mill, or household consumes; regulator defines product and environmental attributes.

### Terms that must be explicit

- crack spread versus realized capture
- refinery complexity and utilization
- carbon intensity versus renewable volume
- heat content and saleable ton
- uranium, conversion, SWU, tails, and fabrication


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: coal, refined fuels, biofuels/renewable diesel/SAF, uranium fuel services, and other fuels consumed for heat, transport, or power. Upstream crude and gas are covered separately.

### 1. Feedstocks

Refiners require crude grades matched to configuration; biofuels require corn, sugar, vegetable oils, fats, and waste oils; coal requires reserves and mine access; nuclear fuel requires uranium mining, conversion, enrichment, and fabrication. **[Inference]** Feedstock-to-product spread—not product price alone—sets conversion economics.

### 2. Plants and process equipment

Refineries, ethanol plants, hydroprocessing units, coal mines/prep plants, terminals, enrichment facilities, and fuel-fabrication plants are capital-intensive and highly regulated. Turnarounds and catalyst changeouts reduce capacity periodically; unplanned outages can move regional product margins.

### 3. Hydrogen, catalysts, chemicals, power, and water

Refining and renewable diesel consume hydrogen and catalysts; mines use diesel/explosives/equipment; enrichment consumes significant electricity; all require water and environmental controls. Specialty catalysts and licensed process technology are small but essential inputs.

### 4. Logistics

Pipelines, railcars, barges, tankers, terminals, blending infrastructure, and storage connect feedstock to plant and product to market. Coal export terminals and rail capacity matter; biofuel economics depend on feedstock radius and credit-market logistics.

### 5. Policy as a factor of production

Renewable fuel standards, tax credits, low-carbon fuel standards, emissions rules, sanctions, mine permits, and nuclear safeguards can create or destroy margins. Coal remains power-dominated: 91.7% of U.S. coal consumption in Q1 2026 was electric-power use (**[Fact]** — EIA — https://www.eia.gov/coal/production/quarterly/index.php).

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** feedstock/product spreads, energy, credits, freight, utilization, and environmental compliance.
- **Cap capacity:** permitted plant/mine capacity, catalysts/hydrogen, rail/pipeline/terminal access, enrichment capacity, and scheduled turnaround windows.
- **Shock path:** refinery outage → local product inventory falls → crack spread rises → surviving plants earn windfall margins until imports/restarts rebalance.

### 7. Full dependency ledger by fuel family

This category must not be modeled with one generic input basket. **Coal** needs reserves and mineral rights, overburden removal, explosives, diesel, tires, steel, water, ventilation, roof support, reclamation bonding, rail/barge/port capacity, and mine labor. **Refining** needs crude grades, hydrogen, natural gas, electricity, catalysts, water, blending components, bio-feedstocks, tankage, pipelines, marine/rail/truck logistics, and turnaround crews. **Biofuels, renewable diesel, and SAF** need corn, sugar, used cooking oil, tallow, soybean/canola oil or cellulosic feedstock; enzymes or catalysts; hydrogen/methanol; water and heat; collection/pretreatment; and auditable carbon-intensity and land-use data. **Nuclear fuel** needs uranium ore, conversion, enrichment, zirconium alloy cladding, fabrication, licensed transport, utility qualification, safeguards, storage, and eventual waste disposition.

Trace each physical input back one tier. Refinery hydrogen commonly embeds natural gas and power; mining explosives embed ammonia and therefore natural gas; steel embeds ore/scrap, coking coal, ferroalloys, and energy; crop feedstock embeds land, seed, fertilizer, crop chemicals, diesel, water, weather, and storage; fuel ethanol creates distillers grains and corn oil co-products whose prices feed back into the net feedstock cost. Used oils and tallow connect fuel margins to food, rendering, waste-collection, and oleochemical markets.

Capital and permission are equally binding: mine and air permits, renewable-fuel pathway approval, refinery product specifications, nuclear licenses, environmental liability, inventory and margin finance, insurance, tax-credit monetization, offtake, and rail or pipeline access. Commodity prices and credits usually set margin; mines, cokers/hydrocrackers, hydrogen, approved feedstock, enrichment, skilled operators, turnarounds, blending terminals, and transport capacity gate output. Substitution is constrained by engine/turbine/reactor qualification and product standards, so an apparently available molecule may not be commercially usable.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Distinct sub-industries

- **Refining:** regional oligopolies connected by trade; margins depend on configuration, crude access, and product demand.
- **Coal:** thermal coal tied to power generation; metallurgical coal tied to steel and international trade.
- **Biofuels:** agricultural/feedstock conversion plus policy credits.
- **Nuclear fuel:** strategically concentrated mining, conversion, enrichment, and fabrication chain.

### 2. Profit pools

Value accrues to complex refiners with advantaged crude/logistics, low-cost met-coal reserves, scarce enrichment/conversion capacity, and biofuel operators with advantaged feedstock plus policy access. Commodity conversion without logistical or regulatory advantage earns cyclical returns.

### 3. Geography and trade

Gulf Coast refining links deep conversion, pipelines, ports, and export markets. Powder River/Appalachian coal economics differ by geology and rail distance. Biofuels cluster near feedstock or low-carbon markets. Nuclear-fuel security is geopolitical because conversion/enrichment are concentrated internationally.

### 4. Structural change

U.S. coal generation rebounded in 2025 but EIA expected retirements to drive average annual declines through 2027 (**[Fact]** — https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67005). Transport electrification pressures gasoline over time; aviation and heavy transport support middle distillates and SAF. Nuclear restarts/new demand increase the strategic value of fuel services.

### 5. Gaining relevance

Renewable diesel/SAF with secured low-carbon feedstock; high-complexity export refiners; met coal where blast-furnace steel persists; uranium conversion/enrichment; and blending/logistics assets that monetize multiple pathways.

### 6. Losing relevance

High-cost thermal coal tied to retiring plants, simple refineries without feedstock advantage, and biofuel projects reliant on one temporary credit. **[Inference]** Policy-created EBITDA deserves a discount proportional to rule-change and feedstock-crowding risk.

### 7. Value migration

Value moves from undifferentiated fuel volume toward logistics, complexity, carbon attributes, and security of supply. Transition economics are uneven: declining domestic demand can improve incumbent margins if capacity exits faster, before terminal value eventually erodes.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, and funding map

- **Outputs:** thermal and metallurgical coal; gasoline, diesel, jet, marine fuel, LPG, petroleum coke, asphalt, sulfur and petrochemical feedstocks; ethanol, biodiesel, renewable diesel, SAF and co-products; fabricated nuclear fuel and enrichment services. Emissions, ash, slag, spent catalyst, wastewater, tailings, depleted uranium and spent fuel are part of the output system because disposal cost and liability affect economics.
- **Customers and payers:** utilities, steel mills, industrial boilers, motorists, trucking fleets, airlines, shipping companies, militaries, distributors, traders, refiners, blenders, reactor operators, and governments maintaining strategic stocks. The user may buy through a retailer, airport hydrant, bunker supplier, utility fuel contract, or long-term enrichment agreement; channel margins and taxes separate producer realization from the end-user price.
- **Geography:** mines and biomass follow resources; refineries follow crude access, demand centers and ports; biofuel plants balance feedstock radius against product logistics; enrichment and nuclear fabrication are geopolitically concentrated. Rail, river levels, pipelines, product tankers, blending terminals, airport infrastructure, and sanctioned trade routes create regional dislocations even when global feedstock is adequate.
- **Competitive structure:** refining and mining are scale/cost-curve businesses with high exit liabilities; biofuels combine commodity conversion with policy-created attributes; nuclear fuel services have qualification, licensing, security and sovereign barriers. Merchant traders, integrated producers, state-owned firms, cooperatives, private operators, and public utilities can have different return thresholds.
- **Funding:** private funding through internal cash, bank revolvers, inventory finance, project finance, strategic equity, farmers/cooperatives and private sponsors supports mature and conversion fuels; utilities pre-contract nuclear supply. Public funding through export-credit agencies, tax credits, low-carbon fuel programs, grants, loan guarantees, defense procurement and regulated cost recovery supports strategic capacity, remediation and security and can be more valuable than the physical commodity spread.
- **Policy:** mine safety and reclamation, air and water permits, fuel sulfur/octane and blending standards, renewable-fuel mandates, lifecycle-carbon rules, tax and excise law, food-versus-fuel restrictions, sanctions, maritime rules, nuclear safeguards, nonproliferation, waste policy and price controls must be evaluated by jurisdiction and effective date.

Cross-market effects are central: crude slate and natural-gas prices shape refinery cracks; crop and vegetable-oil prices link fuel to food and feed inflation; coal competes with gas and renewables in power but complements iron ore in blast-furnace steel; uranium services interact with utility decarbonization and sovereign security; carbon markets and credit values can reverse the merit order among fuels. Demand is a stock-and-flow system—vehicle, aircraft, boiler, furnace and reactor fleets change slowly even when fuel policy moves quickly.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Conversion workflows

- **Refining:** distillation separates crude; cracking, coking, reforming, hydrotreating, and blending convert fractions into gasoline, diesel, jet, and other products.
- **Ethanol:** ferment sugar/starch, distill, dry, and sell coproducts.
- **Renewable diesel/SAF:** pretreat fats/oils, hydrotreat/isomerize, separate, and certify product.
- **Coal:** mine, crush/wash, transport, and burn or coke.
- **Nuclear fuel:** mine/mill → convert → enrich → fabricate assemblies.

### 2. Unit economics

Conversion margin = product value + coproducts + policy credits − feedstock − variable processing − freight. Fixed depreciation and maintenance make utilization important. Refining “cracks,” biofuel crush spreads, coal cash cost per ton, and enrichment SWU economics are the appropriate units.

### 3. Capacity

Measured in barrels/day, gallons/year, tons/year, or SWU. Effective capacity falls during turnaround, mine geotechnical disruption, feedstock shortage, or logistics outage. Inventories buffer short disruptions and are essential cycle signals.

### 4. KPIs

- Throughput and utilization; yield by product.
- Feedstock differential and product crack/crush spread.
- Renewable credits per gallon and feedstock carbon intensity.
- Cash cost and realized price per coal ton; export mix.
- Turnaround days, maintenance capex, and incident rate.
- Inventory levels and regional import/export balance.

### 5. Failure modes

Fire/outage, poor crude-feedstock match, policy-credit collapse, feedstock crowding, rail/pipeline bottleneck, reserve/geology miss, environmental liability, and using peak spreads to justify expansion.

### 6. Valuation

Use mid-cycle EBITDA/FCF and replacement value for refineries; reserve NAV and normalized price for mines; project DCF with explicit credit/feedstock scenarios for biofuels; and capacity/backlog economics for nuclear-fuel services. Separate sustaining from growth capex and include closure/remediation liabilities.

### 8. Complete conversion, pricing, and cash model

Model every fuel as a yield-and-quality conversion. Mines convert in-place reserves into clean, sized product after recovery and washing losses. Refineries convert a crude slate through distillation and upgrading into a product barrel whose value depends on yield, specification and local cracks. Biofuel plants convert feedstock into fuel plus saleable co-products, with mass balance and verified carbon intensity determining credit eligibility. Nuclear-fuel companies convert, enrich and fabricate uranium under long-dated, specification-heavy contracts.

Gross margin must include product and co-product revenue plus environmental attributes, less delivered feedstock, energy, chemicals/catalysts, purchased hydrogen, freight, compliance and variable conversion cost. Throughput alone can mislead: refinery utilization can be high with a poor crude/product slate; a biofuel plant can run while losing money on feedstock-to-fuel spreads; a mine can ship more low-quality tons while degrading realization. Separate spot, formula, tolling, take-or-pay, cost-plus, regulated-recovery and mandate-backed revenue.

Working capital is material because crude, products, crops, coal and uranium can sit in long logistics chains. Inventory gains are not repeatable operating margin; margin calls, letters of credit, blending-credit positions and tax-credit receivables can consume cash. Maintenance turnarounds, mine development, catalyst replacement, reclamation, refinery environmental projects, and nuclear security/waste obligations belong in sustaining capital; capacity additions and feedstock-conversion projects belong in growth capital.

Use family-specific KPIs: mine cash cost and realized price per saleable ton, strip ratio, recovery and reserve life; refinery throughput, utilization, Nelson complexity, capture versus benchmark crack, yield and turnaround days; biofuel feedstock spread, yield, carbon intensity, credit realization and contracted share; nuclear order book, separative-work-unit volume and price, tails assay, conversion/fabrication capacity and contract duration. Across all fuels, test logistics outage, feedstock spike, demand recession, carbon-policy change, credit collapse, sanctions, unplanned downtime and customer-asset retirement.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points. Refresh them for the relevant asset, product, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Conversion margin = value of specification-compliant products + co-products + realized environmental attributes − delivered feedstock − variable energy, hydrogen, chemicals, freight, and compliance cost. Cash return then subtracts fixed operating cost, turnarounds, sustaining capital, financing, and closure.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Refinery utilization | roughly 80–95% in normal operation; outage cycles matter | Fixed-cost absorption and product supply |
| Refinery turnaround interval | major work commonly every 3–6 years by unit/site | Creates planned outage and cash cycles |
| Ethanol yield | about 2.7–3.0 gallons per bushel of corn | Links feedstock to fuel and co-products |
| Coal heat value | roughly 8,000–14,000 Btu/lb across major ranks/products | Normalizes tons to useful energy |
| Coal strip or mining ratio | site-specific waste per saleable ton | Major mine-cost and reserve-quality driver |
| Product inventory | days of cover by region and product | Measures logistics tightness, not only crude supply |
| Refinery yield | percent of barrel by gasoline, distillate, jet, resid, and other | Determines exposure to each crack |
| Biofuel carbon intensity | gCO2e/MJ under the applicable program | Determines low-carbon attribute value |
| Enrichment capacity | million SWU/year and contracted utilization | Identifies nuclear-fuel bottlenecks |
| Closure and reclamation | funded liability per asset or saleable unit | Tests whether apparent cash margin is full-cycle |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Separate commodity inventory gains/losses from current conversion margin; separate physical margin from environmental credits; include co-product revenue consistently; normalize turnarounds; and distinguish sustaining, compliance, conversion, and growth capital. Nuclear order books require price, escalation, origin, and delivery timing.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Crude/product cracks, natural gas, hydrogen, power, coal freight, crop/oil feedstocks, and uranium-cycle prices.
- Policy-credit value, carbon methodology, blending limits, and tax-credit monetization.
- Unplanned unit outage, rail/river/port interruption, drought, and approved-feedstock availability.
- Demand destruction, vehicle/furnace/reactor fleet turnover, sanctions, and strategic stock policy.

### Valuation discipline

Value distinct assets on through-cycle spreads, configuration, logistics, liabilities, and policy durability. Do not apply refinery multiples to mandate-dependent biofuel credits or utility-like nuclear contracts.

Private funding includes inventory revolvers, commodity trade finance, project debt/equity, cooperative capital, utility procurement, and strategic offtake. Public funding includes tax credits, mandates, grants, loan guarantees, stockpiles, reclamation support, and sovereign nuclear or energy-security investment.

### Enforcement and liability

Exposure includes fuel-spec violations, credit invalidation, mine-safety shutdown, air/water penalties, excise and customs audits, nuclear license action, sanctions, product recall, and unfunded remediation or waste obligations.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons rather than exhaustiveness.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1979–1980 refinery and fuel disruption | Crude shock met regulated and inflexible product supply | Queues and product scarcity exceeded simple crude arithmetic | Product configuration and policy allocation matter |
| 2005 US refining disruptions | Hurricanes removed Gulf refining and logistics capacity | Product cracks spiked even where global crude remained available | Refining and distribution are independent bottlenecks |
| 2011 Fukushima aftermath | Reactor shutdowns changed uranium demand and utility procurement | Long-cycle fuel supply faced a sudden demand reset | Installed fleet and policy can overwhelm mine-cost narratives |
| 2020 transport-fuel collapse | Mobility stopped faster than refineries and inventories could adjust | Jet and gasoline demand diverged; storage and run cuts bound | Model each product and shutdown constraint separately |
| 2022 energy-security shock | Gas, coal, refined products, power, and nuclear availability interacted | Fuel switching raised prices across nominal substitutes | Substitution is limited by equipment, logistics, and emissions rules |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Cracks, product inventories, refinery outages, feedstock spreads, credit prices, coal freight, reactor restarts, utility contracting, and SWU/conversion prices.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** High utilization with poor capture margin, biofuel EBITDA unsupported without a specific credit bridge, or nuclear/coal capacity claims lacking permits and logistics.
- **Durable lesson:** The commodity is not the product until it meets specification, location, policy, and end-equipment requirements.

### Evidence traps

- Using a generic oil price as a refinery earnings proxy.
- Treating every low-carbon gallon as economically identical.
- Combining uranium commodity and enrichment capacity into one price.

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Extraction inputs | Land/mineral rights, explosives, diesel, tires, equipment, water, labor and ventilation | Fuel, strip ratio, geology, safety and equipment availability set mine cost and output |
| Logistics and processing | Wash plants, rail, barges, ports, stockpiles, conversion and enrichment | A mine can be economic at gate but uncompetitive delivered; nuclear fuel can bottleneck after mining |
| Demand markets | Power generation, steelmaking, cement, industrial heat, nuclear generation and district energy | Power dispatch, steel cycles, gas prices and reactor schedules determine demand by fuel quality |

The practical test is to trace a shock through availability, delivered cost, working capital, output, customer economics, financing and eventual substitution—not merely name the adjacent market.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One saleable tonne of export thermal coal from a surface mine.

**Core equation:** `FOB cash contribution = benchmark-adjusted sale price − ROM mining/yield cost − washing − inland freight/port − royalty − sustaining and closure accrual`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Saleable output | 1.33 ROM tonnes × 75% wash yield | 1.00 saleable tonne; ash, sulfur, moisture and calorific value still require adjustment |
| Realized FOB revenue | $100/t benchmark after quality adjustment | $100/t |
| Mine and preparation | 1.33 × $40/ROM t + $6/t washing | $59/t saleable |
| Rail, port and royalty | $18/t logistics + $7/t royalty | $25/t |
| Sustaining/closure | $7/t sustaining + $2/t rehabilitation accrual | $9/t |
| Illustrative cash contribution | $100 − $59 − $25 − $9 | $7/t; freight or yield moves can erase it quickly |

**Decision test:** Model delivered energy or reductant value by specification; mine-gate cost alone cannot compare thermal coal, metallurgical coal, uranium or biomass.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Highest delivered-cost mine/fuel-cycle source required after inventory draw | Use quality-adjusted delivered cost, not reported mine cash cost |
| Marginal customer | Power plant or steel mill able to reduce utilization, switch grade/fuel or import | Spark/dark spreads, steel margins and stockpile days measure demand response |
| Clearing mechanism | Regional contracts and seaborne indices with quality, freight and timing adjustments; uranium uses long-term and spot contracting | Tender outcomes, inventories, freight and contract coverage reveal balance |
| Cash shutdown point | Price below avoidable mining, processing and committed logistics cost | Take-or-pay rail, labor and rehabilitation can delay closure |
| New-capacity incentive | Long-run delivered price supports development, infrastructure, closure and risk-adjusted return | Permitting, customer contracting and finance availability raise the hurdle |
| Adjustment lag | Weeks for stockpiles/dispatch, months for mine plans, years for mines, rail and nuclear fuel-cycle capacity | Shortage duration determines which supply response is feasible |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
