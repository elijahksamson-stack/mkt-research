## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Use this file to define the object being measured before comparing companies, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Oilfield and adjacent energy equipment, drilling, completion, subsea, production, intervention, chemicals, rental, digital, maintenance, and abandonment services.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Land drilling and directional services | Rig time, footage, steering, and downhole tools | rigs, rig days, footage | AC rigs, walking capability, basin fit, and crew quality matter |
| Pressure pumping and completion | Hydraulic horsepower, stages, wireline, sand/water logistics | stages, fleets, HHP | Available integrated fleet and maintenance condition beat nameplate HHP |
| Offshore and subsea | Rigs, vessels, trees, manifolds, umbilicals, installation | rig/vessel days, subsea trees | Water depth, pressure, specification, and certification segment capacity |
| Production and intervention | Artificial lift, chemicals, compression, workover, inspection | wells served, treatment volume, service jobs | Installed base and uptime create recurring demand |
| Equipment, digital, and lifecycle | Capital equipment, parts, software, remote operations, decommissioning | orders, backlog, subscriptions, jobs | Manufacturing backlog and field-service revenue have different cash profiles |

### Specifications that change value

- State pressure, temperature, sour-service, diameter, material class, and offshore depth for equipment.
- State marketable and available fleet, not only nameplate rig, HHP, or vessel count.
- Separate newbuild, rental, service, consumable, parts, and software revenue.
- Backlog must include cancellation, escalation, customer funding, and delivery assumptions.
- Performance must specify footage/stages/time, nonproductive time, failure, and customer acceptance.

### Role map

Operator budgets and designs the well/project; integrated or specialist service firms execute; OEMs and lower tiers supply; certifiers and regulators approve; leasing and capital markets fund fleets; crews and customer representatives accept work.

### Terms that must be explicit

- nameplate versus marketable versus active capacity
- day rate versus revenue per job/stage
- nonproductive time and lost-time allocation
- book-to-bill and executable backlog
- maintenance capex versus fleet growth


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


### 1. Customer capital budgets

The decisive input is E&P, midstream, LNG, refining, and offshore capital spending. Demand is derived from expected commodity prices, project inventory, customer balance sheets, and service intensity. **[Inference]** Oil price affects services through customer budgets with a lag; rig and completion activity are closer operating indicators.

### 2. Steel, engines, electronics, and specialized components

Rigs, pressure pumps, tubulars, drill bits, valves, wellheads, subsea trees, compressors, and turbines depend on steel/alloys, engines, bearings, controls, power electronics, and semiconductors. Premium tubulars and subsea equipment can be long-lead constraints; commodity steel inflation is primarily a margin issue when contracts lag.

### 3. Skilled labor and field logistics

Crews, directional drillers, mud engineers, pressure-pumping operators, offshore mariners, welders, and technicians are capacity. Housing, trucking, sand/water logistics, and maintenance networks matter in remote basins. Labor leaves rapidly in downturns and returns slowly, amplifying upcycles.

### 4. Technology, data, and IP

Rotary-steerable systems, measurement/logging while drilling, reservoir imaging, completion design, artificial lift, and digital optimization improve customer recovery and days per well. Proprietary tools and installed bases support higher-margin rental/service models.

### 5. Capital and fleet condition

Equipment is expensive and maintenance-intensive. Economic capacity is lower than nameplate when idle fleets require reactivation or refurbishment. The May 2026 U.S. rig count was 553 (**[Fact]** — EIA/Baker Hughes data — https://www.eia.gov/dnav/ng/ng_enr_drill_s1_m.htm), but rig quality and utilization matter more than the count alone.

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** customer pricing, labor, steel, fuel, mobilization, maintenance, and utilization.
- **Cap capacity:** qualified crews, premium rigs/frack fleets, subsea/pressure-control equipment, tubulars, and working capital.
- **Shock path:** sustained commodity strength → customer budget increase → utilization tightens → service price rises → fleet reactivation and newbuilds eventually restore capacity.

### 8. Full bill of resources and hidden dependencies

Demand is the first input: customer commodity prices, hedges, depletion, reserve inventory, balance-sheet capacity and final investment decisions determine service volume. The physical bill then includes alloy and carbon steel, iron castings and forgings, cement, proppant, drilling and completion chemicals, elastomers, bearings, hydraulics, pumps, engines, transmissions, generators, electronics, sensors, semiconductors, fiber, batteries, explosives, welding consumables, coatings and spare parts. Steel traces to ore or scrap, metallurgical coal, ferroalloys, power and freight; engines and pumps trace to precision machining and long-lead foundry capacity.

Operational inputs include rigs, pressure-pumping horsepower, vessels, helicopters, cranes, yards, warehouses, rail, trucking, water, diesel or field power, satellite/telecom links, software, geological and well data, certified maintenance, and 24/7 crews. Scarce roles include directional drillers, toolpushers, subsea engineers, pressure-control specialists, welders, mechanics, mariners and pilots. Safety culture and customer qualification are productive assets because one failure can suspend a fleet or vendor.

Capital sources differ by asset: public debt/equity and cash flow for scaled fleets; equipment loans, leases and private equity for smaller operators; customer advances and long-term contracts for bespoke subsea/LNG equipment; export credit for international packages. Insurance, bonding, warranties, performance guarantees and spare-parts inventory absorb capital. Class certification, offshore/aviation rules, well-control standards, local-content requirements, sanctions/export controls, chemical rules and emissions/noise permits govern where assets can work.

Commodity steel and labor primarily move cost; customer pricing and utilization determine recovery. Engines, electronics, high-pressure pumps, subsea trees, offshore vessels, qualified crews, permits and customer acceptance can cap capacity. Used-equipment markets, remanufacturing, cannibalized parts, multi-basin redeployment and equipment electrification are substitutes, but certification, mobilization and configuration frequently prevent quick interchange.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Value chain

The sector includes seismic/data; drilling contractors; pressure pumping and completion; drilling tools and evaluation; production equipment; subsea; compression; and maintenance. Customers are E&Ps, national oil companies, midstream/LNG operators, and refiners.

### 2. Profit pools

**[Inference]** Commodity-like horsepower and basic land rigs compete on utilization and price. Returns are better in proprietary downhole tools, mission-critical well construction, subsea systems, compression, aftermarket, and performance-based digital services where switching cost and failure cost are high.

### 3. Market structure

Large diversified service firms compete globally; regional specialists dominate basin-specific work; drilling contractors own fleets; equipment makers monetize installed bases. Consolidation removes capacity after downturns, but high pricing can invite reactivation and new entrants.

### 4. Geography

North American shale is short-cycle and price-responsive. Middle East land activity is steadier and state-led. Offshore Brazil, Guyana, Gulf of Mexico, West Africa, and Norway requires deepwater technology and long backlogs. LNG and gas infrastructure concentrate demand on the U.S. Gulf Coast and key export regions.

### 5. Gaining relevance

- Offshore/subsea equipment with multi-year backlogs.
- Gas compression, LNG infrastructure, and production optimization.
- Electrified/automated fleets and lower-emissions operations.
- Digital tools tied to measurable drilling or recovery outcomes.
- Aftermarket and rental models on proprietary installed equipment.

### 6. Losing relevance

Old, inefficient rigs and pressure-pumping fleets; undifferentiated services without density; technology sold as dashboards without customer productivity proof. **[Inference]** Revenue growth from reactivating marginal assets is lower quality than pricing and utilization on high-spec capacity.

### 7. Value migration

Value migrates from newbuild equipment toward lifecycle service, from pure oil exposure toward gas/LNG and production optimization, and from labor intensity toward automation. The clean-energy transition also creates selective adjacencies—carbon capture, geothermal, offshore wind—but these should be valued on awarded backlog and returns, not addressable-market claims.

### 9. Complete market-system coverage

Outputs span seismic and reservoir data; drilling and completion footage/stages; cementing, logging and testing; installed wellheads, subsea systems, artificial lift, pumps and compressors; production chemicals and monitoring; maintenance, intervention and abandonment; equipment rental; software; and parts/remanufacturing. Customers include NOCs, majors, independents, refiners, LNG and pipeline operators, utilities, geothermal and carbon-storage developers, and engineering contractors. Procurement may be controlled by an operator while cost and risk are shared with partners; the user at the wellsite is often an internal crew or another contractor.

Geography divides into short-cycle onshore basins, remote international land markets, offshore shelves and deepwater, and manufacturing/service hubs. Local content, cabotage, visa rules, sanctions, climate, port depth, road/bridge limits, and mobilization time can make nominally global equipment local. North American pressure pumping is fragmented and cyclical; high-end offshore/subsea equipment and selected digital tools are more concentrated, qualified and backlog-driven.

The capital ecosystem includes private funding from public-company markets, sponsor-owned fleets, regional contractors, customer-funded engineering and leasing, plus export finance and sovereign procurement. Public funding and legislation also arrive indirectly through government R&D, offshore leasing, carbon capture, geothermal, hydrogen, methane reduction, defense/energy-security programs and infrastructure tax incentives. A service provider must identify whether the customer has an actual budget and permit, not merely a policy target.

Value migrates between operators and suppliers with utilization. In scarcity, day rates, service intensity, replacement cost and performance differentiation support supplier returns; in downturns, customers rebid, idle assets and force consolidation. Cross-market adjacencies include mining and construction equipment, industrial automation, aerospace-grade materials, maritime services, data analytics, carbon storage, geothermal and offshore wind. These create redeployment options, but only where technical specifications and commercial returns match.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Workflow by well stage

Seismic/resource evaluation → well planning → rig mobilization → drilling and directional steering → logging/evaluation → casing/cementing → hydraulic fracturing/completion → flowback → artificial lift/production → intervention and abandonment. Different vendors earn at each stage.

### 2. Revenue models

- **Day-rate:** drilling rig/vessel earns per operating day; utilization × day rate drives revenue.
- **Per-stage/per-job:** pressure pumping and completion; efficiency and consumables determine margin.
- **Tool rental/service:** proprietary downhole equipment plus specialists; attractive when tools are scarce.
- **Equipment sale + aftermarket:** volatile original equipment, steadier service/spares.
- **Long-term project:** subsea/LNG equipment; backlog visibility but execution risk.

### 3. Cost and capacity

Fixed depreciation and maintenance make utilization the primary margin lever. Economic capacity depends on crewed, certified, maintained assets—not stacked nameplate units. New capacity has long payback and is vulnerable to commodity downturns.

### 4. KPIs

- Rig/frack/vessel utilization and pricing.
- Revenue per rig/day or fleet; stages per day.
- Backlog, book-to-bill, and cancellation terms.
- Incremental margin and maintenance capex.
- Reactivation/newbuild spending and fleet age.
- Customer concentration and international mix.
- Free-cash-flow conversion and net leverage.

### 5. Cycle mechanics

Activity follows customer cash flow and budgets; pricing follows utilization; capital spending follows pricing. This creates a delayed cobweb cycle. Productivity can let producers grow with fewer rigs, so investors must track feet drilled, completion intensity, and service content—not rigs alone.

### 6. Failure modes

Overbuilding at peak day rates, under-maintaining fleets, fixed-price project overruns, customer bankruptcy, safety incidents, obsolete equipment, and mistaking backlog for guaranteed margin.

### 7. Valuation

Use normalized EV/EBITDA and FCF through a cycle, replacement value for standardized fleets, backlog quality for project businesses, and sum-of-parts for diversified firms. At the trough, tangible asset value and liquidity matter; at the peak, low P/E is often a warning rather than a bargain.

### 8. Complete operating, contract, and capital mechanics

The service workflow runs tender/qualification → engineering and job design → procurement/manufacture or fleet preparation → mobilization → execution → measurement and customer acceptance → demobilization → repair/recertification → invoicing and warranty tail. Revenue can be day-rate, per foot, per stage, per job, rental, milestone, cost-plus, outcome/performance-based, software subscription, parts or long-term service. Backlog is useful only after testing cancellation rights, customer funding, input escalation clauses and delivery capacity.

Economics are driven by available—not nameplate—capacity. Track active and marketable rigs, pumps, vessels or tool fleets; revenue days; utilization; price per day/stage; repair and consumable intensity; crew efficiency; nonproductive time; mobilization reimbursement; and mix. A fixed-price equipment order with rising alloy or labor cost behaves differently from an indexed service contract; a capital-light software or chemical sale behaves differently from a vessel or pressure-pumping fleet.

Cash conversion depends on deposits, progress payments, inventory, unbilled milestones, retainage, receivable days, customer disputes, warranty reserves, rental losses and maintenance capex. Growth capex adds fleet or plant; maintenance capex preserves certification, reliability and earning power. Fleet book value can overstate recoverable value in a downturn, while depreciation can understate replacement cost in an upcycle.

Stress tests should connect the customer's cycle to the supplier with the right lag: commodity-price decline → customer budget cuts → fewer tenders and lower utilization → price compression → cannibalization and delayed replacement → eventual capacity attrition. Conversely, a rapid activity rebound first consumes idle equipment, then qualified crews and parts, and only later supports newbuilds. Also test steel/component inflation, port or sanction disruption, a major safety incident, customer concentration, technology displacement, and whether carbon-storage/geothermal work truly offsets declining hydrocarbon activity.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points. Refresh them for the relevant asset, product, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Service contribution = activity units × realized price per unit − crew, consumable, fuel, mobilization, and repair cost. Fleet return then subtracts idle fixed cost, maintenance/recertification capital, working capital, financing, and replacement cost.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Land-rig utilization | below ~70% weak; above ~85% often supports pricing | Bargaining power and reactivation |
| Pressure-pumping utilization | available fleet hours/stages, not total HHP | Maintenance and pricing discipline |
| Offshore contract duration | months to multiple years | Revenue visibility and repricing lag |
| Newbuild lead time | months for standard tools; years for complex subsea/vessels | Supply response |
| Maintenance capex | often meaningful percent of fleet EBITDA; asset-specific | Tests true cash generation |
| Nonproductive time | percent of job or rig time | Direct measure of execution quality |
| Book-to-bill | orders divided by revenue; interpret cancellations | Manufacturing cycle direction |
| Backlog conversion | months/years and gross-margin content | Distinguishes volume from value |
| Working-capital intensity | inventory, unbilled milestones, and receivable days | Cash conversion risk |
| Customer concentration | top customers as percent of revenue/backlog | Budget and credit exposure |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile equipment orders to backlog and revenue, service revenue to activity and price, EBITDA to fleet maintenance and working capital, and asset book value to marketable replacement capacity. Customer advances and progress payments can improve cash before delivery; idle assets can remain on the balance sheet above economic value.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Customer budget response to oil/gas price, hedges, decline, and FID timing.
- Steel, engines, electronics, labor, fuel, chemicals, and mobilization escalation.
- Utilization crossing the pricing threshold and availability after maintenance.
- Customer concentration, cancellation, safety failure, and reactivation cost.

### Valuation discipline

Use replacement cost and through-cycle earnings for fleets; backlog quality and margin for equipment; installed-base/service mix for OEMs; and retention/recurring revenue for digital. Avoid one multiple for all.


## 6. Regulation and Public Funding

### Permission chain

Operator budgets and designs the well/project; integrated or specialist service firms execute; OEMs and lower tiers supply; certifiers and regulators approve; leasing and capital markets fund fleets; crews and customer representatives accept work.

### Jurisdiction and regime map

| Jurisdiction or layer | Core regimes and authorities | Economic transmission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | OSHA, EPA/state environmental rules, BSEE offshore rules, customer qualification, sanctions/export controls | Safety record, local permits, offshore certification, and trade restrictions gate activity |
| North Sea and European markets | National offshore safety cases, marine/class rules, chemical registration, emissions policy | Higher certification and decommissioning standards create barriers and liabilities |
| Middle East and other NOC markets | Tendering, local content, visas, state customer terms, export finance | Localization and receivable/payment terms shape return |
| Cross-energy adjacencies | Rules for geothermal, carbon storage, offshore wind, hydrogen, and methane monitoring | Can open new demand but requires different certification and counterparties |

### Public and private funding

Private funding includes public markets, sponsor equity, equipment loans/leases, asset-backed facilities, customer advances, and long-term contracts. Public funding affects demand through leasing, carbon storage, geothermal, hydrogen, methane, defense, export credit, and infrastructure programs.

### Enforcement and liability

Serious incidents can trigger stop-work orders, fleet suspension, civil/criminal liability, customer disqualification, warranty claims, contract termination, and insurance exclusions. Export and sanctions breaches can remove markets and service access.

### Update checklist

- Confirm the legal entity, asset location, product, customer, and regulator.
- Separate enacted law, final rule, guidance, permit condition, litigation, and proposal.
- Date grants, tax provisions, tariffs, sanctions, quotas, and administered prices.
- Trace compliance cost, schedule, capacity, price, liability, and funding separately.


## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons rather than exhaustiveness.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1986 service collapse | Oil-price fall caused abrupt budget and rig cuts | Utilization and day rates fell faster than asset capacity exited | Fleet supply attrition determines recovery |
| 2014–2016 North American downturn | Shale retrenchment stranded rigs and pressure-pumping assets | Equipment impairments followed utilization collapse | Book value is not earning capacity |
| 2016–2021 offshore trough | Project cancellations met long vessel/rig lives | Stacking preserved optionality but consumed cash | Long-cycle assets need balance-sheet duration |
| 2021–2022 component shortage | Chips, engines, labor, and logistics constrained reactivation | Nominal idle capacity could not return immediately | Marketable capacity must be audited |
| 2023–2025 offshore/subsea recovery | Long underinvestment and project sanctions tightened qualified supply | Backlogs and rates improved before large newbuild response | Certification and replacement cost can restore pricing |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Operator capex, permits, FIDs, rig/frac utilization, tender counts, day rates, equipment orders, supplier lead times, and reactivation.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Backlog without deposits or customer finance, idle assets counted as capacity, or pricing gains offset by repair and labor intensity.
- **Durable lesson:** The scarce unit is a qualified, crewed, maintained, correctly configured asset at the customer's site—not an equipment count.

### Normalization rules

- Use available/marketable capacity and revenue days.
- Separate price, volume, mix, mobilization, pass-through, and FX.
- Reconcile backlog to funded customer projects and contract rights.
- Normalize maintenance across stacked and active assets.

### Evidence traps

- Counting stacked equipment as immediately usable.
- Treating all service activity as proportional to rig count.
- Valuing cyclical fleets on peak EBITDA without replacement capital.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

| Geography / archetype | Operating model | Economic and risk implications |
| --- | --- | --- |

| Global offshore | Concentrated rigs, subsea, seismic and vessel capacity under long contracts | Dayrates, backlog quality, reactivation cost, shipyard timing and customer credit dominate |

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One active hydraulic-fracturing fleet day.

**Core equation:** `Fleet cash contribution = completed stages × net price per stage − fuel/material/logistics − crew and repairs − allocated fixed cost − maintenance capital`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Productive output | 20 stages/day × $75,000 net revenue/stage | $1.50m daily revenue |
| Direct variable cost | Fuel/power, chemicals, consumables, trucking and field support | $0.85m/day |
| Crew and fixed fleet cost | Labor, insurance, base, IT and idle-capacity allocation | $0.25m/day |
| EBITDA contribution | $1.50m − $0.85m − $0.25m | $0.40m/day at stated utilization |
| Maintenance capital | Pump-end, engine and iron replacement accrual | $0.20m/day |
| Illustrative pre-tax cash | $0.40m − $0.20m | $0.20m/day; falls sharply when stages/day or active days decline |

**Decision test:** Bid on contribution after realistic utilization, mobilization, maintenance and receivable days; nominal horsepower and backlog do not establish cash returns.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Highest-cost fleet, rig, vessel or crew reactivated to meet customer schedules | Reactivation capital, labor and certification determine real—not stacked—capacity |
| Marginal customer | Producer whose next well/project barely clears its capital hurdle | Customer price deck and acreage quality set service willingness to pay |
| Clearing mechanism | Spot bids in land markets; tenders and term contracts offshore/internationally | Leading-edge rate, utilization, contract length and escalation clauses must be read together |
| Cash shutdown point | Rate below avoidable crew, fuel, repair and mobilization cost | Keeping a fleet warm may be rational even below fully allocated cost |
| New-capacity incentive | Term pricing covers equipment build, maintenance, downtime and cost of capital | Peak spot rates alone rarely justify a new offshore rig or premium fleet |
| Adjustment lag | Weeks/months for land fleets; years for rigs, vessels, subsea systems and manufacturing | Segment-specific supply elasticity prevents one industry-wide utilization metric |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Horsepower or rig count | Revenue-producing capacity | Adjust for specification, crew, maintenance, location, contract and active status |
| Backlog | Guaranteed revenue or margin | Check cancellation, escalation, mobilization, customer credit and execution cost |
| Utilization | Comparable productivity | Define denominator and pair with stages, footage, uptime or revenue per active day |
| EBITDA | Cash earnings | Subtract maintenance capital, working capital, reactivation and interest |
| Dayrate | Unit profitability | Normalize pass-throughs, mobilization, downtime, crew scope and contract duration |
