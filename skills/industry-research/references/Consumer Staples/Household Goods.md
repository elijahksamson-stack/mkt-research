## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Cleaning, tissue/paper, personal and household care, absorbent hygiene and related consumables, including branded/private-label and professional formats.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Home and fabric cleaning | Detergents, surface, dish and air care | units, doses, households | Formula, concentration, claims, appliance and water |
| Tissue and paper | Bath, towel, facial and related paper | cases, tons, sheets | Fiber, ply, basis weight, softness and freight |
| Personal and household care | Grooming, oral, skin and household consumables | units, users, regimen | Ingredient, claim, channel and repeat |
| Absorbent hygiene | Diapers, feminine and incontinence products | units, users, absorbency | Fluff/SAP/nonwoven, fit, skin and demographics |
| Professional/private label/refill | Institutional, retailer brand and alternative systems | volume, contracts, refills | Customer, concentration, format and dispensing |

### Specifications that change value

- State active/formula, concentration/dose, fragrance/preservative, claim and applicable product category.
- Paper needs fiber source, recycled content, basis weight, ply and sheet count.
- Absorbent needs fluff/SAP/nonwoven content, fit and performance.
- Price needs unit, dose/use, pack count, promotion and retailer funding.
- Sustainability needs lifecycle, refill/reuse, recycled content and actual recovery.

### Role map

Ingredient/pulp/resin supplier provides; formulator/brand designs; contract plant converts; retailer distributes; consumer/institution uses; advertiser influences; regulator governs claims/safety; waste/water systems receive.

### Terms that must be explicit

- price per pack versus per dose/use
- formula concentration and active content
- virgin, recovered and certified fiber
- branded versus private-label price gap
- recyclable/refillable versus adopted


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: household cleaning, laundry, paper/home consumables, oral/personal care where sold as staple consumer goods, and related durable brand systems.

### 1. Chemicals and formulations

Surfactants, solvents, fragrances, enzymes, bleaches, polymers, pulp, absorbents, resins, oils, and specialty actives form the product. Many inputs are petrochemical- or natural-oil-derived, linking costs to oil/gas, palm and chemical cycles. Formulations and safety dossiers are low-volume, high-leverage IP.

### 2. Packaging and manufacturing

Plastic bottles, films, closures, cartons, aerosol cans, and corrugate are continuous inputs. P&G buys almost all raw and packaging materials from third parties, including some single-source suppliers, and identifies fuel, natural gas, and derivatives as important manufacturing and transport commodities (**[Fact]** — FY2025 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/80424/000008042425000076/pg-20250630.htm).

### 3. Brands, advertising, and retailer access

Brand trust and efficacy claims require sustained R&D, testing, advertising, and shelf availability. Retail concentration is material: P&G's top ten customers were 43% of FY2025 sales and Walmart alone was about 16% (**[Fact]** — same filing). **[Inference]** Retailer access is as necessary an input as chemicals; losing distribution destroys scale economics.

### 4. Labor, energy, logistics, and capital

Plants are automated but require skilled operators, quality staff, mixing/filling equipment, warehouses, and working capital for many SKUs. Energy is especially relevant in paper/tissue and drying processes; freight is relevant because many products are bulky relative to value.

### 5. Regulation

Chemical rules, product claims, disinfectant/pesticide registration, ingredient disclosure, packaging rules, and waste policy can force reformulation. EPA's updated Safer Choice standard tightened ingredient and packaging criteria, including recyclability/reuse and restrictions on intentionally added PFAS for certified products (**[Fact]** — https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-strengthens-safer-choice-standard-commercial-and-household-cleaning-products).

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** resin, pulp, chemicals, freight, FX, advertising, promotions, and retailer terms.
- **Cap capacity:** approved formulas, specialty actives/enzymes, bottles/closures, qualified contract manufacturers, and regulatory registration.
- **Shock path:** resin/chemical inflation → COGS pressure → pack/price action → consumer trade-down/private-label share risk → productivity and mix determine recovery.

### 7. Full formulation, substrate, packaging, and demand ledger

Cleaning and personal/household products use surfactants, solvents, alcohols, bleach/chlorine chemistry, acids/bases, enzymes, fragrances, preservatives, chelants, polymers and water; paper/tissue uses virgin/recovered fiber, pulp chemicals and large water/steam loads; absorbent products add fluff pulp, superabsorbent polymer, nonwovens and adhesives; durable household items may add steel/aluminum, glass, ceramics, plastics, rubber, wood and electronics. Feedstocks trace to oil/gas, salt, palm/coconut oils, forestry, minerals, metals and power.

Packaging consumes HDPE/PET/PP and film, aerosol cans, aluminum/steel, glass, paperboard/corrugated, pumps/sprayers, labels, inks and pallets. Manufacturing needs mixing/reactors, paper machines, molding, converting/filling, compressed air, heat, sanitation, labs and wastewater. Product safety, formulation stability, microbiology and retailer qualification gate launch.

Brand, consumer insight, advertising, shelf/search access, distributors and e-commerce fulfillment are demand inputs. Suppliers include contract manufacturers and fragrance/ingredient houses with proprietary value. Capital includes corporate/private markets, supplier credit, working capital and customer-backed capacity; public recycling/waste systems support end-of-life.

Feedstock, pulp/resin, packaging, energy, freight, advertising and trade spend set margin; paper-machine/filling/molding capacity, pumps/closures, qualified ingredients, regulatory approval and retailer placement gate volume. Concentrates/refills, reusable formats, private label and material substitution reduce some inputs but can trade convenience, hygiene, performance and adoption.

**Substitutes, complements, and adjacent markets:** Substitutes include private-label equivalents, concentrates, refills, reusable formats, bulk dispensing, household-made formulations, and—in some categories—product elimination. Complements include appliances and dispensers, municipal water and waste systems, recycling, packaging, retail media, logistics, and public-health or hygiene practices. Petroleum, oleochemical, pulp, resin, power, freight, and recycling economics therefore transmit directly from commodity, forestry, energy, transportation, and waste markets into household-goods margins and demand.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Structure and profit pools

Global branded leaders compete with regional brands and retailer private label across laundry, home care, tissue, oral care, grooming, and personal care. Value accrues to trusted brands with demonstrable efficacy, proprietary formulations, scaled advertising, and preferred shelf placement. It is competed away in commoditized paper, basic cleaners, and undifferentiated contract manufacturing.

### 2. Route to market

Mass merchants, clubs, grocery, drug, dollar stores, e-commerce, and professional channels each require different packs and price points. Large retailers have bargaining power, but category leaders also drive traffic. Direct-to-consumer is useful for testing and data, yet shipping bulky low-ticket staples usually favors retailer fulfillment.

### 3. Geography

Brands can be global, but product form, fragrance, water hardness, income, and retail structure are local. Manufacturing is regional to reduce freight and meet regulation. Emerging-market growth often starts with small affordable packs; developed markets rely more on premiumization and productivity.

### 4. Gaining relevance

- Concentrated/refill formats that reduce freight and packaging.
- Premium efficacy, sensitive-skin, clinical, and professional claims.
- Retail media and data-driven pack/promotion architecture.
- Automation and SKU simplification.
- Recyclable/reusable packaging and safer-chemistry credentials where consumers or regulators reward them.

### 5. Losing relevance

Me-too brands without efficacy differentiation, excessive SKU complexity, and sustainability claims unsupported by lifecycle economics. **[Inference]** Private label gains most when branded price gaps widen without a visible performance gap.

### 6. Consolidation and disruption

Scale advantages in advertising, procurement, and retailer negotiations favor incumbents, but niche brands can enter through e-commerce and social discovery. The hard step is not launch; it is repeat purchase plus profitable mass distribution. Value migrates toward science-backed, high-repeat brands and away from low-differentiation volume.

### 7. Investor framing

The sector's central debate is whether price/mix reflects durable brand power or temporary inflation recovery. Evidence is unit share, repeat, distribution, gross-margin reinvestment, and innovation that expands the category rather than merely changes packaging.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs deliver cleaning, hygiene, grooming, protection, storage and household convenience; brands, dosing and packaging are part of the value proposition. Wastewater chemicals, aerosols/VOCs, microplastics, wipes, disposable absorbents, packaging and forestry impacts are residuals; recycled fiber/plastic and refill systems are secondary loops.

Consumer uses the product, but retailers and distributors control access, caregivers buy for households, and institutions buy professional formats. Branded, private-label, direct-to-consumer, professional and emerging-market sachet/bulk channels have different economics.

Bulk liquids and paper products favor regional production because freight is high; concentrated formulas and premium personal care travel farther. Feedstocks and packaging trade globally. Multinationals, category leaders, private label, local brands, ingredient firms, contract manufacturers and retailers divide value.

Private funding is mainly corporate; public funding and policy influence the market through waste/recycling infrastructure, sanitation procurement and forestry/industrial programs. Rules cover ingredient and chemical safety, cosmetics/biocide claims, child-resistant packaging, labeling, advertising/green claims, forestry sourcing, water discharge, plastics/EPR/recycled content and product liability.

The sector connects chemicals, forestry/pulp, packaging, retail, water/waste and energy. Oil/palm/pulp/resin shocks reach formulas and packs; retailer consolidation shifts bargaining; e-commerce favors durable/nonleaking packs; water scarcity encourages concentrates but also affects paper and chemical production; regulation can move cost from virgin package to refill/recovery systems.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Production workflow

Consumer insight and formulation → stability/efficacy/safety testing → regulatory/claims approval → chemical blending or paper conversion → filling/packing → quality release → regional distribution → retail promotion and replenishment.

### 2. Operating models

- **Branded integrated:** owns formulation, plants, marketing, and customer relationships; maximum control and fixed cost.
- **Asset-light/contracted:** faster entry and lower capex; less control and weaker procurement leverage.
- **Private label:** retailer owns shelf/customer; supplier competes on cost, service, and formulation.
- **Paper/tissue:** more capital- and energy-intensive than liquid home/personal care; mill utilization and pulp/fiber costs dominate.

### 3. Unit economics

Net sales reflect units × realized price/mix after trade spending. COGS includes chemicals/pulp, packaging, conversion, and freight. Gross margin funds advertising and R&D, which sustain the moat. Colgate said higher raw and packaging costs reduced North American gross profit by 330 basis points in 2025, partly offset by 200 basis points of productivity savings (**[Fact]** — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/21665/000002166526000006/cl-20251231.htm).

### 4. KPIs

- Organic sales: volume, price, and mix.
- Gross margin and productivity savings.
- Category/brand share and household penetration.
- Repeat rate, distribution, and sales velocity.
- Advertising and R&D intensity.
- SKU count, service level, inventory days, and plant utilization.
- Free-cash-flow conversion and ROIC.

### 5. Innovation test

Real innovation earns repeat purchase at a sustainable premium, expands distribution, and survives competitor response. Promotional innovation produces trial but no repeat. Claims must be supported, scalable, and manufacturable with qualified ingredients.

### 6. Failure modes

Recall or contamination, ineffective reformulation, retailer delisting, price gaps that trigger trade-down, brand underinvestment, FX mismatch, single-source packaging/actives, and cost programs that damage service or innovation.

### 7. Valuation

Mature leaders trade on P/E, EV/EBITDA, FCF yield, organic-growth quality, margin durability, and ROIC. Paper-heavy businesses require mid-cycle EBITDA, replacement cost, and leverage analysis. Early brands require repeat cohorts, contribution margin after trade spend, and credible CAC/payback—not gross sales growth alone.

### 8. Complete formula-to-household and cash mechanics

The chain is formulate and qualify ingredients → source/blend/react or pulp/form → convert/fill → test/release → pack/distribute → retailer/consumer use → wastewater or waste/recovery. Yield, line speed, format change, giveaway/dose, scrap, energy and package compatibility determine saleable units.

Net revenue is price/mix and volume less promotion, coupons, retailer funding and returns. Shipment versus consumer takeaway and pantry loading must be separated. Premium innovation earns margin only if repeat purchase survives after trial and marketing support.

Working capital includes chemicals/pulp/resin, packaging, finished goods, retailer receivables and trade-spend accruals. Commodity hedges delay input effects; supplier and contract-manufacturer terms matter. Maintenance capex preserves paper/converting/filling assets; growth includes new formats, automation and brand/R&D investment.

Track organic sales by price/volume/mix, household penetration/share and repeat, gross margin bridge, input and pass-through lag, trade/advertising efficiency, private-label gap, yield/utilization, service/in-stock, inventory and cash conversion, innovation contribution and ROIC. Stress commodity/packaging spike, retailer destocking, private-label trade-down, formula or claim regulation, recall, water/energy constraint and failed innovation.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Product contribution = net revenue per unit/dose − formula/fiber/SAP − packaging − conversion/energy − freight − trade/promotion − returns. Portfolio cash subtracts advertising, R&D, working capital and line capital.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gross margin | ~35–65% branded; lower private label/commodity | Brand/formula economics |
| Advertising/trade | often ~15–30%+ of sales combined | Demand investment |
| Private-label gap | price and share by category | Trade-down risk |
| Household penetration | percent households and buy rate | Demand base |
| Repeat rate | cohort repurchase | Product quality |
| Pulp/resin/formula share | percent COGS by category | Input sensitivity |
| Line utilization | roughly 70–90% | Fixed-cost absorption |
| Inventory days | raw/pack/finished by category | Cash |
| Dose lightweighting | grams/ml/sheets per use | Material productivity |
| Innovation retention | sales/distribution after 12–24 months | Launch durability |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile gross/list to net sales, trade/coupon, retailer inventory versus consumer takeaway, commodity hedge, pack/count changes, advertising, restructuring, brand amortization and working capital.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Pulp, resin, surfactants/oils, SAP, packaging, energy, water, freight and FX.
- Price/pack, private label, promotion, household formation and demographics.
- Formula claim, retailer shelf/search, advertising and innovation repeat.
- Chemical/cosmetic/biocide, green claims, forestry, plastics/EPR and water discharge.

### Valuation discipline

Use category cohorts, penetration/repeat, net price and gross margin after brand investment. Separate paper/commodity from formula/brand and private label.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1980s detergent concentration | Formula improvements reduced dose and packaging | Value shifted from volume to performance | Physical volume can fall while value rises |
| 1990s–2000s retailer consolidation | Large retailers increased shelf and price power | Trade spend and private label grew | Route to market is a moat and tax |
| 2008 recession trade-down | Consumers sought value in selected categories | Private label gained unevenly | Staples still have elasticity |
| 2020 hygiene surge | Cleaning/tissue demand spiked and inventories emptied | Plants/logistics ran at limits then normalized | Pantry loading distorts demand |
| 2022 pulp/resin/energy inflation | Multiple inputs rose together | Pricing and pack architecture protected margins with lags | Measure per-use price and volume |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Pulp/resin/oil, retailer takeaway/inventory, price/pack, promotion, private label, household cohorts, distribution and claims regulation.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Shipment above takeaway, innovation without repeat, or sustainability based only on package weight.
- **Durable lesson:** Expertise is performance per use, repeat behavior and net shelf economics after retailer and materials.

### Normalization rules

- Use per dose/use.
- Net trade/promotion.
- Prefer takeaway.
- Separate paper/formula.

### Evidence traps

- Comparing packs with different counts.
- Calling price/mix pure pricing.
- Ignoring retailer inventory.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One wholesale case of a branded household product.

**Core equation:** `Case contribution = gross invoice − trade/promotion − formulation − packaging − conversion − freight − allocated brand/R&D/overhead` 

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gross invoice | Customer list/invoice value | $50/case |
| Trade and promotion | Rebates, displays, coupons and customer accruals | $10; net revenue $40 |
| Formulation | Active/base chemicals, fragrance, pulp or absorbent inputs | $8 |
| Packaging | Bottle/can/paper, closure, label and corrugated | $6 |
| Conversion and freight | $4 manufacturing + $3 freight | $7 |
| Illustrative contribution | $40 − $8 − $6 − $7 − $8 brand/R&D − $4 overhead | $7/case before tax and major capital |

**Decision test:** Model SKU/customer gross-to-net, formulation and packaging separately; promotional lift and private-label response determine whether headline pricing sticks.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Last qualified formula, converter or filler able to meet specification and retailer timing | Pumps, actives and packaging can bind before bulk plant |
| Marginal customer | Household/institution switching brand, private label, refill, concentrate or usage frequency | Performance and hygiene constrain elasticity |
| Clearing mechanism | Retail price/promotion mediated by retailer margin and manufacturer trade terms | List price differs from net case realization |
| Cash shutdown point | SKU runs while net revenue covers avoidable formula, package, conversion and service | Shelf architecture can retain low-margin SKUs |
| New-capacity incentive | Expected category/brand demand covers filling/converting, tooling, working capital and return | Contract manufacturing changes capital but not qualification risk |
| Adjustment lag | Weeks for promotion/material substitution, months for qualification, years for paper/chemical capacity | Retail demand responds faster than upstream feedstocks |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Volume/cases | Comparable use | Normalize dose, concentration, sheet/unit count, pack size and channel inventory |
| Price/mix | Net pricing | Separate pack changes, promotion, trade, premiumization, channel and FX |
| Market share | Brand loyalty | Define value/units, category, channel, geography and private label |
| Product claim | Demonstrated consumer outcome | Check test method, dose, conditions, regulation and comparative basis |
| Recycled/sustainable packaging | Lower lifecycle impact | Measure source, content, weight, collection, yield, reuse and product waste |
