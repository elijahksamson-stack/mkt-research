## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Agricultural ingredient conversion, branded/private-label packaged food, alcoholic and nonalcoholic beverage, bottling, and related co-products. Grocery retail is separate.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Staple and snack foods | Grain, bakery, cereal, snacks and meals | units, pounds, servings | Commodity/formula, pack, category and shelf life |
| Protein, dairy and refrigerated | Meat, seafood, dairy and chilled products | pounds, head, liters, units | Feed, yield, cold chain, disease and perishability |
| Nonalcoholic beverages | Water, carbonated, coffee/tea, energy and juice | cases, liters, servings | Concentrate/bottler, package, channel and sugar/caffeine |
| Alcoholic beverages | Beer, wine and spirits | cases, barrels, proof gallons | Aging, excise, distribution and legal channel |
| Ingredients and private label | Oils, milling, flavors and contract/retailer brands | tons, volume, contracts | Commodity/co-product, formulation, customer and margin |

### Specifications that change value

- State recipe/ingredient, nutrient/allergen, package/serving, shelf life, storage and channel.
- Agricultural inputs need grade, origin, crop year, moisture/protein/fat and contract basis.
- Yield needs saleable product and co-products from input mass.
- Revenue needs shipment, retail takeaway, price, pack/mix, promotion and returns.
- Capacity is qualified line, package, sanitation and cold/dry logistics.

### Role map

Farmer/fisher supplies; trader/processor converts; brand/private-label owner formulates; co-manufacturer/bottler makes; distributor moves; retailer/food service buys; consumer eats/drinks; regulator ensures safety/label.

### Terms that must be explicit

- shipment versus consumer takeaway
- price, pack and mix
- gross commodity input versus net co-product cost
- sell-by/best-by and actual shelf life
- brand owner, bottler and distributor economics


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: branded packaged food, snacks, nonalcoholic beverages, beer/spirits, and their bottling/manufacturing systems. Restaurants and grocery retail are excluded.

### 1. Agricultural commodities

The physical inputs are sugar and corn sweeteners, grains, potatoes, edible oils, dairy, meat, fruit/juice, coffee, tea, cocoa, and water. Crop weather, disease, geopolitics, and trade policy create price and availability shocks. **[Inference]** Farm commodities matter, but processing, packaging, labor, logistics, and retailing explain much of the shelf price; USDA explicitly notes that farm prices are only one component of food-at-home prices (https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/ag-and-food-statistics-charting-the-essentials/food-prices-and-spending).

### 2. Packaging is often the larger near-term swing factor

Aluminum cans, PET resin/preforms, glass, paperboard, films, closures, and labels are continuous-use inputs. Coca-Cola Consolidated states that aluminum, plastic bottles, CO2, sweetener, labels, and other packaging—excluding concentrate—are about 20% of annual cost of sales; 2025 input-cost inflation included tariff-affected aluminum (**[Fact]** — 2025 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/317540/000162828026009057/coke-20251231.htm). **[Inference]** A can or PET shortage caps output faster than an agricultural-price increase, which is more often a margin problem.

### 3. Concentrate, flavor systems, and brand IP

In franchised beverages, the brand owner sells concentrate/syrup while bottlers add water and sweetener, package, and distribute. Coca-Cola describes two lines of business—concentrate and finished product—and a global bottling network (**[Fact]** — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/21344/000162828026010047/ko-20251231.htm). Proprietary formulas are low-dollar but non-substitutable inputs; advertising and trade promotion maintain the consumer pull that makes them valuable.

### 4. Manufacturing, labor, energy, and water

Plants require food-safe processing, cold storage for selected categories, reliable water, steam, electricity, natural gas, sanitation chemicals, and quality-control labor. Water is both ingredient and process utility in beverages. **[Inference]** Energy is usually a smaller cost than ingredients/packaging but becomes material in glass, refrigeration, baking, and long-distance distribution.

### 5. Distribution and retailer access

Direct-store delivery, warehouse delivery, distributors, refrigerated transport, and shelf/cooler placement are inputs to sell-through. Customer concentration matters: the same Coca-Cola bottler reported Walmart and Kroger at 29% of 2025 net sales combined (**[Fact]** — 2025 10-K above). Retailer bargaining power appears through slotting, promotions, payment terms, and private-label competition.

### 6. Regulation

FDA/USDA food safety, labeling, alcohol excise/distribution rules, sugar taxes, container-deposit laws, and extended-producer-responsibility rules affect formulation and packaging. Qualification and recall systems are capacity gates: a contaminated line can stop production regardless of input price.

### 7. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** agricultural baskets, aluminum/PET, freight, labor, energy, trade promotion, and FX.
- **Cap capacity:** approved ingredients, clean water, food-safe line availability, cans/bottles, CO2, cold-chain space, and recall/quality clearance.
- **Shock path:** crop failure → commodity price increase → hedges delay COGS impact → gross margin compresses unless price/mix offsets it → elastic categories lose volume. Packaging shortage → immediate missed cases and shelf space.

### 8. Full farm-to-formulation and packaging ledger

The agriculture basket includes corn, wheat, rice and other grains; sugar and sweeteners; soy/palm/canola and other oils; dairy, eggs, meat and fish; fruit/vegetables; cocoa, coffee and tea; nuts, spices and natural flavors. These inputs embed land/soil, seed/genetics, fertilizer (natural gas/ammonia, phosphate, potash), crop chemicals, feed, water, energy, farm labor, weather, veterinary health and storage. Animal products also embed feed conversion and processing/cold chain.

Formulation adds salt, acids, starches, cultures/enzymes, preservatives, colors/flavors, vitamins, emulsifiers, carbonation gas and water with category-specific quality. Packaging draws on aluminum, steel, glass, PET/HDPE/film, paperboard/corrugated, closures, labels and pallets. Plants consume steam, refrigeration, electricity, water/cleaning chemicals, cooking/baking/filling lines, sanitation, labs and wastewater capacity.

Cold/dry warehouses, tankers, rail, trucks, ports, distributors and retailer/food-service slots are enabling inputs. Brand, recipes, sensory science, food safety/traceability, quality teams, advertising and consumer data matter as much as plant capacity. Alcohol and certain categories add licenses, age controls and excise.

Funding includes corporate/cooperative/family capital, working-capital and commodity lines, farm credit, private equity, supplier terms, public agricultural support, crop insurance, nutrition procurement, export credit and manufacturing grants. Commodity, packaging, energy, labor, freight and trade spend set margin; harvest, disease, water, certified ingredients, packaging, cold chain, sanitation, permits and retailer authorization gate supply. Reformulation, alternative proteins/sweeteners, recycled packaging and different origin can substitute, with taste, nutrition, allergen, label and customer approval constraints.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Value chain and profit pools

The chain runs agriculture → ingredients → branded formulation/manufacturing → bottling/co-packing → distribution → retail/foodservice. **[Inference]** Economic rent concentrates in brands and route-to-market, not commodity conversion. Concentrate owners, global snack brands, premium spirits, and category leaders can earn attractive returns because consumer habit, shelf space, and marketing scale reinforce one another. Commodity processors and undifferentiated co-packers compete on utilization and cost.

### 2. Industry structures differ by category

- **Carbonated beverages:** brand-owner oligopoly plus territorially organized bottlers; concentrate is asset-light, bottling is asset- and route-heavy.
- **Snacks and packaged foods:** scale brands compete with private label and local tastes; distribution density is a moat.
- **Beer and spirits:** brand portfolios, distributor regulation, and premiumization matter; alcohol demand is mature in many developed markets.
- **Fresh/perishable food:** shorter shelf life and weaker brand moats shift value toward logistics and retailers.

Coca-Cola says products bearing its trademarks represent 2.2 billion of roughly 65 billion beverages consumed globally each day (**[Fact]** — 2025 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/21344/000162828026010047/ko-20251231.htm). That scale supports advertising, procurement, and bottling economics.

### 3. Customers and bargaining power

Customers range from concentrated mass retailers and clubs to fragmented convenience, restaurants, and independent stores. Large retailers have price and shelf leverage; convenience and foodservice can carry higher per-unit realization. Private label is the structural counterweight to branded pricing power, strongest where product differentiation is low.

### 4. Geography and trade

Production is usually regional because beverages are heavy and expensive to ship, while concentrates, flavors, coffee, cocoa, fruit, and alcohol cross borders. Emerging markets offer volume growth but add FX, affordability, route-to-market, and regulatory risk. **[Inference]** “Global brand, local manufacturing” is the dominant resilient model.

### 5. What is gaining relevance

- Zero-sugar, functional, hydration, energy, protein, and portion-controlled products.
- Revenue-growth management: pack architecture, channel-specific SKUs, and price/mix analytics.
- Direct consumer data and retail media, though retailers own much of the transaction data.
- Recycled/lightweighted packaging and refill systems as regulation raises virgin-material costs.

### 6. What is losing relevance

Undifferentiated center-store products, overextended SKU portfolios, and brands dependent on repeated price increases without volume support are vulnerable. **[Inference]** Premiumization is real only when repeat purchase and mix persist after promotion; a new SKU count is not evidence of durable innovation.

### 7. Disruption and value migration

Value is migrating toward brands that combine health/functional claims with proven repeat behavior, and toward bottlers/distributors with dense cold availability. It migrates away from commoditized manufacturing and weak mid-tier brands toward either premium leaders or private label. GLP-1 adoption is a category-mix risk, not a uniform sector collapse: smaller portions and protein/function may win while high-calorie impulse categories face pressure.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs include ingredients, branded/private-label foods, alcoholic/nonalcoholic beverages, nutrition and convenience. Milling, brewing, oilseed crushing, dairy and meat processing create feed, oils, hides, whey, spent grain and other co-products; food loss, wastewater, organic waste, packaging and emissions are residuals. Shelf life and safety are part of the product.

Consumer is the user; retailer, restaurant, distributor, school/hospital or government program may be buyer; brands and retailers jointly shape price/promotion; caregivers often choose for households. Retail, food service, e-commerce, vending and institutional channels have different pack, margin and demand.

Agriculture follows climate/soil/water; processing balances farm origin, ports and population; brands distribute globally but tastes, religion, language and rules localize portfolios. Farmers/cooperatives, traders, ingredient processors, branded producers, private label, bottlers, distributors, retailers and food service split value.

Capital is private/cooperative plus public farm support, crop insurance, research, nutrition programs, food reserves and export/development finance. Policy covers farm and water, food/feed safety, ingredients/additives, labeling/nutrition/allergens, alcohol/excise, marketing to children, packaging/waste, trade/sanitary rules, competition and labor.

Food links energy/fertilizer, climate/water, packaging, freight, grocery/restaurant and healthcare. Crop shocks can move feed, meat, dairy and biofuel simultaneously; fuel mandates compete for oils/grain; stronger retailer private label compresses brands; weight-loss medicines and nutrition policy can alter category demand; packaging policy can shift cost between substrate and waste systems.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Operating workflow

Demand forecast → ingredient/packaging procurement and hedging → formulation and batch/continuous processing → filling/packing → quality release → warehouse/distribution → retailer replenishment → promotion and sell-through. Food safety, traceability, and shelf life determine usable yield.

### 2. Competing operating models

- **Concentrate/franchise:** brand owner keeps formula and marketing; bottler bears plant, packaging, and route assets. Wins when local distribution intensity matters.
- **Owned finished-product:** more revenue and control, but more working capital and lower asset turns.
- **Co-manufacturing:** lowers capital needs and adds flexibility, but sacrifices control and capacity certainty.
- **Direct-store delivery vs. warehouse:** DSD wins for high-velocity snacks/beverages because merchandising protects availability; warehouse wins for lower-velocity, easier-to-handle products.

### 3. Unit economics

Net revenue per case/unit = list price − retailer discounts − promotions − returns. Gross profit subtracts ingredients, packaging, conversion, and inbound freight; operating profit then subtracts selling, delivery, advertising, and corporate cost. **[Inference]** Price/mix can conceal weak units temporarily, so investors should reconcile organic revenue growth into volume and effective price/mix.

### 4. Capacity and working capital

Capacity is measured in cases, liters, tons, line hours, or throughput per minute. Utilization matters, but excessive utilization reduces maintenance flexibility and raises service risk. Inventory must balance shelf life with service levels. Hedges delay—but do not eliminate—commodity exposure, creating lags between spot inputs and reported margins.

### 5. KPIs

- Organic revenue growth: volume versus price/mix.
- Gross margin and input-cost inflation/deflation.
- Market share, household penetration, repeat purchase, and velocity per point of distribution.
- Numeric/weighted distribution and cooler/shelf availability.
- Advertising and promotion as a percentage of sales.
- Inventory days, service level, waste, and plant utilization.
- Free-cash-flow conversion and ROIC.

### 6. Failure modes

Food-safety recall, failed price elasticity, retailer delisting, brand underinvestment, excessive SKU complexity, bottler misalignment, and channel inventory loading. A shipment beat that is not matched by retail consumption is low quality.

### 7. Valuation across the cycle

Mature branded leaders are commonly assessed on organic growth, margin durability, ROIC, free-cash-flow yield, and EV/EBITDA or P/E relative to history. Bottlers deserve more asset-heavy comparisons and attention to capex/working capital. Early brands need cohort repeat, distribution productivity, contribution margin, and a credible path through trade spending—not revenue multiples alone.

### 8. Investor watchpoints

The most useful quarterly bridge is: units + price/mix → net revenue → commodity/packaging productivity → gross margin → advertising reinvestment → operating margin. Watch retail consumption versus shipments and test whether innovation is incremental or merely cannibalizing the core.

### 9. Complete farm-to-shelf and cash mechanics

The chain is contract/buy crop or ingredient → inspect/store → clean/mill/extract/ferment/cook/blend → fill/package → test/release → warehouse/distribute → retailer/food service → consume → recover/dispose packaging and waste. Yield, recipe, giveaway, downtime, changeovers, spoilage and shelf life convert input tons into saleable units.

Net revenue equals volume × list price less discounts, promotion, trade spend, returns and slotting/customer terms. Shipment can precede consumer takeaway; promotion may borrow volume from adjacent periods. Co-manufacturing and bottling shift asset intensity but add supplier and territory economics.

Working capital includes seasonal crops, ingredients, packaging, finished goods, receivables, trade accruals and supplier payables. Commodity hedges smooth timing, not structural inflation. Maintenance capital preserves food safety, refrigeration and line reliability; growth includes new capacity, formats and brand investment. Agricultural co-products and waste credits belong in unit economics.

Track price, volume and mix; household penetration, buy rate and share; retail takeaway versus shipments; gross margin bridge; commodity/packaging cover; trade-spend ROI; yield/waste and line utilization; service/in-stock; inventory age; innovation repeat; cash conversion and ROIC. Stress harvest/disease, fertilizer/energy, packaging shortage, retailer destock, private-label trade-down, recall, regulation/reformulation and consumer-health preference change.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Product contribution = net price × saleable units − agricultural/ingredient − packaging − conversion/energy/labor − freight − trade/promotion − spoilage/returns. Portfolio cash subtracts brand, innovation, working capital and line capital.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gross margin | ~25–60% by branded/commodity category | Brand and mix |
| Agricultural yield | saleable pounds/liters plus co-products per input | Process economics |
| Packaging share | often material and sometimes larger than primary ingredient | Input sensitivity |
| Trade/promotion | often ~10–30%+ of gross sales by channel/category | Net revenue |
| Shelf life | days to years by category | Inventory and waste |
| Line utilization | roughly 70–90% balanced | Fixed cost |
| Waste/yield loss | percent input/finished units | Efficiency |
| Household penetration | percent buyers and repeat | Demand quality |
| Inventory | days by crop/finished/cold chain | Working capital |
| Innovation repeat | trial-to-repeat and distribution retention | Launch quality |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile gross/list to net sales, shipment to takeaway, trade accruals, commodity hedges, co-products, crop/biological inventory, bottling/franchise, returns, spoilage and brand/acquisition amortization.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Crops, feed, fertilizer, water, weather/disease, oils/sugar/cocoa/coffee and FX.
- Packaging, energy, cold chain, labor, freight and sanitation.
- Retailer bargaining/private label, promotion, price elasticity and health preferences.
- Food safety, labeling, alcohol/excise, marketing and packaging/waste.

### Valuation discipline

Separate branded recurring categories, commodity processors, bottlers and alcohol aging. Use share/penetration, net pricing, yield, reinvestment and working capital.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1970s commodity inflation | Agricultural and energy inputs rose | Price/pack and margins adjusted with lags | Brand pass-through is tested in shocks |
| 1980s–2000s category consolidation | Scale brands and retailers gained bargaining | Trade spend and shelf power grew | Customer concentration changes economics |
| 2008–2011 food inflation | Crop/energy and consumer stress interacted | Private label and pack architecture mattered | Elasticity is category-specific |
| 2020 food-service/retail split | At-home demand surged while restaurants fell | Plants/packages/channels were mismatched | Same food in another channel is not fungible |
| 2022–2025 cocoa/coffee and input shocks | Weather and supply concentrated price risk | Confectionery/beverage pricing and pack changed | Agricultural origin belongs in brand economics |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Crop/weather, commodity curves, retailer takeaway, promotion, distribution, household cohorts, plant yield, recalls and private-label gaps.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Shipment growth above takeaway, price gains with collapsing units/households, or innovation without repeat.
- **Durable lesson:** Food expertise connects farm quality and process yield to net shelf economics and repeat consumption.

### Indicator stack

- **Leading:** weather/crop; commodity; retailer orders; promotion; distribution; package supply.
- **Coincident:** takeaway; price/volume/mix; yield; inventory; margin; service.
- **Lagging:** household repeat; share; crop replenishment; brand impairment; capacity.

### Minimum dashboard

1. **Gross margin** — ~25–60% by branded/commodity category; Brand and mix.
2. **Agricultural yield** — saleable pounds/liters plus co-products per input; Process economics.
3. **Packaging share** — often material and sometimes larger than primary ingredient; Input sensitivity.
4. **Trade/promotion** — often ~10–30%+ of gross sales by channel/category; Net revenue.
5. **Shelf life** — days to years by category; Inventory and waste.
6. **Line utilization** — roughly 70–90% balanced; Fixed cost.
7. **Waste/yield loss** — percent input/finished units; Efficiency.
8. **Household penetration** — percent buyers and repeat; Demand quality.
9. **Inventory** — days by crop/finished/cold chain; Working capital.
10. **Innovation repeat** — trial-to-repeat and distribution retention; Launch quality.

### Normalization rules

- Use takeaway before shipment.
- Net sales after trade.
- Pack/serving/unit.
- Include co-products and waste.

### Evidence traps

- Using commodity spot for contracted cost.
- Calling price/mix pure pricing.
- Ignoring shelf life.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One 12-unit case of a branded shelf-stable food or beverage.

**Core equation:** `Case contribution = gross invoice − trade/promotion − ingredients − packaging − conversion − freight − allocated brand/R&D/overhead` 

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gross invoice | Customer list/invoice value | $30/case |
| Trade and promotion | Off-invoice, display, rebates and accruals | $6; net revenue $24 |
| Ingredients | Formula/commodity content including process loss | $4 |
| Packaging | Primary/secondary container, closure, label and case | $5 |
| Conversion and freight | $3 plant conversion + $2 outbound freight | $5 |
| Illustrative contribution | $24 − $4 − $5 − $5 − $5 brand/R&D/overhead | $5/case before tax and major capital |

**Decision test:** Bridge gross-to-net and commodity/packaging lag by SKU, customer and route; case volume without mix, trade and input basis can mislead.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Last qualified farm/processor/plant/route needed for a SKU and market | Quality, harvest timing and packaging make supply product-specific |
| Marginal customer | Consumer or foodservice buyer able to switch brand, pack, protein/ingredient or occasion | Affordability and taste constrain substitution |
| Clearing mechanism | Retail/menu price mediated by retailer margin and trade promotion; commodities clear upstream | Consumer price and manufacturer realization are distinct |
| Cash shutdown point | SKU/line runs while net revenue covers avoidable ingredients, packaging, conversion and service | Portfolio/shelf strategy can preserve low-margin items |
| New-capacity incentive | Sustained demand supports plant, farming, brand and route capital at target return | Harvest and plant lags differ |
| Adjustment lag | Days for price/promotion, seasons for crops, years for orchards/herds/plants | Input shocks may outlast hedge coverage |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Volume/cases | Comparable consumption | Normalize pack size, concentration, servings, channel inventory and product mix |
| Price/mix | List-price realization | Separate trade, promotion, pack architecture, premiumization and currency |
| Gross margin | Brand economics | Include trade accrual, waste, freight, co-manufacturing, recalls and commodity hedges |
| Market share | Consumer preference | Define value/volume, channel, geography, category and private label |
| Organic growth | Underlying demand | Adjust acquisitions, FX, pricing, inventory, discontinued SKUs and calendar |
