## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Use this file to define the object being measured before comparing companies, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Drinking-water sourcing, treatment, distribution, wastewater collection/treatment, reuse, and regulated or public water-service economics. Equipment vendors appear where they create technology or capacity constraints.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Drinking-water utilities | Source, treat, store, pressurize, meter, and deliver potable water | customers, MGD, gallons/customer | Source quality, safe yield, pressure zones, and compliance |
| Wastewater utilities | Collect, treat, recover, and discharge sewage | MGD, load, permit limits | Hydraulic flow and pollutant load are separate capacities |
| Investor-owned regulated water | Utility service under commission-set revenue requirement | rate base, customers, gallons | Acquisition and rate recovery differ from municipal systems |
| Municipal and regional authorities | Publicly governed water/wastewater systems | connections, debt service, coverage | Tax-exempt finance and public affordability objectives |
| Industrial/reuse/desalination | Specialized treatment, reclaimed water, desalination, and private operations | MGD, recovery, $/1,000 gal | Energy, brine/residuals, feed quality, and contract allocation |

### Specifications that change value

- State source type, legal right, safe yield, drought reliability, and raw-water quality.
- Capacity must distinguish hydraulic MGD, peak day, pollutant loading, storage, pressure, and permitted discharge.
- Quality must identify contaminant, detection method, maximum level or permit limit, and sampling point.
- Non-revenue water must distinguish physical loss, apparent loss, authorized unbilled use, and denominator.
- Bills must separate fixed, volumetric, sewer, stormwater, connection, and assistance components.

### Role map

Watershed/rights authority allocates source; utility abstracts, treats, distributes, collects, and bills; laboratories certify; industrial and household customers use; wastewater plant treats; regulator/public board sets standards/rates; environment and downstream users receive discharge.

### Terms that must be explicit

- safe yield versus treatment nameplate
- average day versus maximum day demand
- hydraulic flow versus biological/nutrient load
- non-revenue water versus leak rate
- revenue requirement versus cash capex


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


### 1. Water rights and source supply

Surface water, groundwater, purchased water, reservoirs, watersheds, rights/permits, and drought resilience determine raw supply. Source quality affects treatment complexity and energy/chemical use.

### 2. Treatment and distribution infrastructure

Intakes/wells, treatment plants, pumps, storage, transmission mains, distribution pipe, meters, hydrants, wastewater collection, and treatment assets form the network. EPA estimates $625B of U.S. drinking-water infrastructure need over 20 years, including $422.9B for distribution/transmission (**[Fact]** — https://www.epa.gov/dwsrf/epas-7th-drinking-water-infrastructure-needs-survey-and-assessment).

### 3. Chemicals, power, equipment, and labor

Coagulants, disinfectants, membranes, activated carbon/resins, pumps, pipe, valves, meters, lab equipment, electricity, and licensed operators are continuous inputs. Energy intensity rises with pumping distance, desalination, and advanced treatment.

### 4. Capital and regulation

State commissions set investor-owned utility rates; municipal systems rely on rates, bonds, grants, and revolving funds. Drinking-water and wastewater standards, lead-service rules, PFAS and discharge requirements drive capital. Affordability determines recoverability.

### 5. Data and resilience

Leak detection, pressure management, meter data, asset mapping, cybersecurity, backup power, and emergency interconnections improve reliability. Climate risk affects source availability, flood/fire damage, and water quality.

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set earnings/cost:** allowed return, purchased water, chemicals, power, labor, leakage, and regulatory lag.
- **Cap capacity:** source rights, treatment/pipe/pump capacity, permits, operators, and financing.
- **Shock path:** contamination standard tightens → treatment capex rises → rate-base opportunity if approved → bills/affordability pressure → adoption timing and grants determine shareholder return.

### 7. Full source-to-discharge input ledger

Inputs start with legally and physically reliable surface water, groundwater, purchased water, desalination or reuse supply; watershed protection, intake and well capacity; and rights that survive drought and competing agricultural, industrial and ecological claims. Raw materials and equipment then include electricity, pumps, motors, pipe, concrete, steel, ductile iron, PVC/HDPE, valves, meters, membranes, activated carbon, resins, sand/media, chlorine or hypochlorite, alum/ferric salts, lime, polymers, ozone/UV equipment, laboratory reagents and replacement parts. Chemicals trace to salt, chlorine-alkali, sulfur, natural gas and mineral chains; pipe embeds metals or petrochemicals.

Distribution and wastewater require reservoirs, mains, sewers, lift stations, storm systems, treatment plants, outfalls, biosolids capacity, land/easements, excavation, paving, telemetry, mapping, cyber-secure controls and emergency power. Scarce inputs include licensed operators, engineers, lab staff, pipefitters, electricians, contractors and regulator-approved technical capacity. Sludge disposal, nutrient limits, brine, contaminated media and PFAS treatment residuals make environmental-assimilative capacity a real input.

Capital comes from customer rates, municipal revenue or general-obligation bonds, utility debt/equity, state revolving funds, grants, development banks, connection fees and developer contributions. Affordability programs and public health mandates determine collectible revenue and timing. Water rights, drinking-water standards, discharge permits, dam safety, procurement, public-utility rate approval and local land-use permission gate projects.

Power, chemicals, labor and purchased water move operating cost; source yield, drought, treatment and discharge permits, plant hydraulics, storage, main/sewer capacity and skilled crews cap service. Leak reduction, pressure management, conservation, reuse, stormwater capture, aquifer recharge and decentralized treatment can substitute for new supply, but can also reduce volumetric revenue while leaving fixed network cost.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Structure

Most U.S. water/wastewater systems are municipal or public; investor-owned utilities operate regulated territories and often consolidate small systems. The chain covers source/watershed, treatment, transmission, distribution, collection, wastewater treatment, and discharge/reuse.

### 2. Profit pools

Investor-owned utilities earn regulated returns on prudent rate base; engineering/equipment suppliers earn from treatment and replacement cycles. **[Inference]** Scale creates procurement, expertise, financing, and compliance benefits, especially for small systems facing complex standards.

### 3. Infrastructure need

EPA estimates $625B of drinking-water and at least $630B of wastewater/stormwater need over 20 years (**[Fact]** — https://www.epa.gov/dwsrf/epas-7th-drinking-water-infrastructure-needs-survey-and-assessment; https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/new-epa-survey-highlights-wastewater-infrastructure-needs-protect-waterbodies). This supports long-duration investment but also creates affordability and execution constraints.

### 4. Geography

Water is hyperlocal. Arid regions face source scarcity and growth limits; older Northeast/Midwest systems face pipe/lead replacement; coastal systems face saltwater/flood risk; agricultural regions face nutrient/groundwater issues.

### 5. Gaining relevance

Leak detection/advanced metering, PFAS/advanced treatment, reuse, desalination where economics permit, stormwater resilience, and consolidation of undercapitalized systems. Industrial and data-center demand increases focus on water availability and recycling.

### 6. Losing relevance

Flat volumetric pricing that discourages conservation or underfunds fixed networks; deferred maintenance; speculative technology without lifecycle cost proof. Conservation can reduce sales while fixed cost remains, requiring regulatory design.

### 7. Value migration

Toward regulated consolidators, treatment technology, network analytics, and reuse/resilience; away from small systems lacking capital/technical staff. The core tension is necessary capital versus customer affordability.

### 8. Complete outputs, customers, geography, capital, and policy map

Outputs are potable and non-potable water, fire-flow readiness, sewer collection, wastewater treatment, reclaimed water, stormwater management, discharge quality and public-health protection. Biosolids, biogas, recovered nutrients and heat can be co-products; sludge, brine, contaminants, leaks, sewer overflows and construction disturbance are residuals. Reliability and compliance—not gallons alone—are the product.

Customers include households, landlords, commercial buildings, hospitals, industry, farms, power plants, developers, fire services and wholesale municipal systems. The user, property owner, bill payer, regulator and public-health beneficiary may differ. Large industrial customers can create concentration and pretreatment risk; low-income customers make affordability part of system sustainability.

Every market is watershed- and network-local, but equipment, chemicals, finance and regulation are broader. Investor-owned utilities, municipal departments, regional authorities, cooperatives, private operators, engineering firms, equipment vendors, bottled-water suppliers and decentralized systems occupy different niches. Arid growth markets face source scarcity; older cities face replacement and combined-sewer needs; flood-prone regions face stormwater capacity; coastal systems may face saltwater intrusion.

Private funding includes utility capital and developer contributions; public funding combines local rates and bonds with national/state grants, revolving funds and development finance. Public support often targets health, disadvantaged communities, resilience and compliance where local rate bases cannot finance the need. Policy covers water allocation, drinking-water contaminants, wastewater and storm discharge, lead service lines, dam safety, procurement, environmental justice, conservation, rate design and private-utility returns.

Water interacts with agriculture, mining, semiconductors, power, chemicals, food, real estate and insurance. Drought reallocates value among users; power prices affect pumping/desalination; treatment standards pull demand for chemicals, membranes and waste capacity; real-estate growth can fund extensions but strain sources; floods and wildfire degrade raw-water quality. Bottled water and point-of-use treatment are substitutes for drinking, not for sanitation, firefighting or industrial network service.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Physical workflow

Source/intake or well → screen/coagulate/sediment/filter/disinfect (process varies) → pump/store → distribute/meter → collect wastewater → primary/secondary/advanced treatment → discharge or reuse → manage residuals.

### 2. Regulated economics

Revenue requirement covers O&M, depreciation, tax, and return on rate base. Bills typically combine fixed and volumetric charges; purchased-water and other adjustments may pass through. Infrastructure replacement grows rate base but must be matched with affordability and timely cases.

### 3. Capacity and loss

Track million gallons per day of source, treatment, peak-day demand, storage, and permitted discharge. Non-revenue water includes physical leaks and metering/billing losses. Reducing it can defer supply capex and lower chemical/energy cost.

### 4. KPIs

Customer growth, usage/customer, rate-base growth, allowed/earned ROE, capex, O&M/customer, non-revenue water, main breaks, compliance events, treatment capacity, drought/source mix, FFO/debt, equity needs, and bill affordability.

### 5. Failure modes

Contamination, treatment failure, drought/source loss, main break, permit violation, cyberattack, capex disallowance, cost overrun, and political resistance to needed rates. Acquisitions fail when promised scale savings do not offset purchase premium and required remediation.

### 6. Valuation

Investor-owned utilities: P/E, dividend yield, rate-base/EPS growth, earned ROE, and credit/equity funding, typically at premiums for long visibility. Equipment/treatment firms require organic growth, installed-base/service, margin, and ROIC. Test terminal demand, climate capex, and affordability rather than extrapolating rate base mechanically.

### 7. Complete throughput, billing, and lifecycle mechanics

The chain is protect and withdraw source → convey → treat to intended standard → store and pressurize → distribute and meter → collect wastewater/stormwater → treat and recover resources → discharge or reuse → manage residuals. Bottleneck capacity must be measured through source yield, treatment process, clearwell/storage, pressure zone, pipe, sewer, plant and permitted outfall under peak and wet-weather conditions.

Revenue may combine fixed, volumetric, inclining-block, wholesale, industrial-strength, connection and stormwater charges. Regulated or municipal processes set revenue requirements; purchased water, power or chemicals may be adjusted separately. Conservation lowers variable cost and defers capital but can create a fixed-cost revenue shortfall. Developer contributions and connection fees may finance growth while creating future maintenance obligations.

Working capital includes seasonal receivables, delinquency, chemical and parts inventory, grants receivable, construction work, regulatory assets and emergency repair. Separate routine maintenance, mandated replacement and treatment upgrades from capacity growth. Depreciation schedules can understate physical replacement cost; a low accounting age is not evidence of good asset condition.

Track source mix and safe yield, treatment and permitted capacity, peak/average demand, customer and connection growth, use per customer, non-revenue water, pressure, main breaks, sewer overflows, compliance results, energy/chemical intensity, O&M per connection, capex by driver, bill burden, collections, allowed/earned return, leverage and emergency reserves. Stress prolonged drought, flood/turbidity, contaminant-rule tightening, power outage, chemical shortage, large industrial demand, cyberattack, construction inflation and political resistance to rate increases.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points. Refresh them for the relevant asset, product, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Revenue requirement = O&M + purchased water/power/chemicals + depreciation + tax + allowed return or debt coverage. Unit service cost = lifecycle source, treatment, distribution, collection, residuals, and capital cost divided by billed volume and connections.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Non-revenue water | often 10–30% globally; system definition matters | Leak, metering, and revenue opportunity |
| Peak/average demand | commonly around 1.5–3x depending climate/use | Sizing and resilience |
| Energy intensity | kWh per 1,000 gallons; source/treatment dependent | Pumping and desalination exposure |
| Main-break rate | breaks per 100 miles/year | Asset condition |
| Collection/treatment capacity | MGD and pollutant load utilization | True bottleneck |
| Bill burden | water/wastewater bill as percent of household income | Affordability headroom |
| Debt-service coverage | commonly covenanted above 1.0x, often 1.2x+ targets | Public-system finance |
| Allowed ROE | often high-single to low-double digits for US IOUs | Regulated equity return |
| Capex per connection | annual and replacement-cycle normalized | Infrastructure intensity |
| Compliance events | number, duration, population/load affected | Service quality and liability |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Municipal fund accounting, grants, developer contributions, regulated assets, and capitalized construction need reconciliation to economic replacement cost and cash. Conservation can reduce volume revenue while avoiding variable cost and deferring capex; do not treat all lost gallons as equal margin.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Drought, flood/turbidity, source contamination, wildfire, salt intrusion, and peak demand.
- Power, chemicals, membranes/media, labor, contractors, and residual disposal.
- Rate timing, grants, debt costs, collections, and affordability programs.
- New contaminant standards, discharge limits, lead/service replacement, and industrial load changes.

### Valuation discipline

Assess source security, asset condition, compliance need, rate affordability, and finance before applying utility multiples. Acquisition value must include remediation and customer bill impact.


## 6. Regulation and Public Funding


Regulatory snapshot reviewed through **2026-07-14**. Verify enacted text, implementation dates, litigation, and current guidance before use.

### Permission chain

Watershed/rights authority allocates source; utility abstracts, treats, distributes, collects, and bills; laboratories certify; industrial and household customers use; wastewater plant treats; regulator/public board sets standards/rates; environment and downstream users receive discharge.

### Jurisdiction and regime map

| Jurisdiction or layer | Core regimes and authorities | Economic transmission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | EPA Safe Drinking Water Act and Clean Water Act programs; state primacy; state/local rates; SRF funding | Contaminant and discharge rules, lead replacement, grants/loans, and rate approval |
| European Union and United Kingdom | EU drinking water and water-framework rules; national economic/environmental regulators | Quality, leakage, investment plans, and performance penalties |
| Water-scarce and emerging markets | Concessions, public tariffs, abstraction rights, development finance, desalination/reuse rules | Collection, currency, subsidy, drought, and sovereign risk |
| Watershed and local land-use bodies | Allocation, drought restrictions, growth connections, stormwater and source protection | Can cap growth despite plant capacity |

### Public and private funding

Private funding includes investor-owned utility capital, concessions, industrial contracts, and developer contributions. Public funding includes rates, municipal bonds, taxes, State Revolving Funds, grants, development banks, disaster/resilience funds, and affordability support.

### Enforcement and liability

Boil-water notices, permit orders, fines, mandated capital, criminal liability, rate disallowance, consent decrees, customer claims, and loss of operating authority can follow contamination, discharge, reporting, or asset failures.

### Update checklist

- Confirm the legal entity, asset location, product, customer, and regulator.
- Separate enacted law, final rule, guidance, permit condition, litigation, and proposal.
- Date grants, tax provisions, tariffs, sanctions, quotas, and administered prices.
- Trace compliance cost, schedule, capacity, price, liability, and funding separately.


## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons rather than exhaustiveness.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1993 Milwaukee cryptosporidium outbreak | Treatment and monitoring failed against source contamination | Large-scale illness followed despite conventional infrastructure | Barrier performance and source risk must be specific |
| 2014–2019 Flint water crisis | Source change, corrosion control, governance, and sampling failures interacted | Lead exposure and trust collapse persisted beyond source reversal | Governance and chemistry are operating assets |
| 2012–2016 California drought | Hydrology and restrictions reduced use while fixed cost remained | Revenue and affordability pressure rose alongside conservation | Volume, source security, and fixed-cost recovery conflict |
| Combined-sewer overflow consent programs | Wet-weather flows exceeded legacy collection/treatment capacity | Multi-decade capital programs followed enforcement | Peak hydraulic load can dominate annual averages |
| PFAS treatment buildout | New detection and standards changed treatment and residual needs | Activated carbon/resin/membrane and disposal demand increased | A standard can create capacity and waste markets simultaneously |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Reservoir/groundwater, drought indices, breaks, water quality, permit notices, chemical/power prices, rate filings, grants, industrial connections, and collections.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Low current bills paired with deferred replacement, low average utilization hiding peak or pollutant constraints, or acquisitions that exclude remediation.
- **Durable lesson:** A gallon is valuable only after secure legal source, treatment, pressure, collection, discharge, and affordable funding.

3. **Energy intensity** — kWh per 1,000 gallons; source/treatment dependent; Pumping and desalination exposure.
4. **Main-break rate** — breaks per 100 miles/year; Asset condition.
5. **Collection/treatment capacity** — MGD and pollutant load utilization; True bottleneck.
6. **Bill burden** — water/wastewater bill as percent of household income; Affordability headroom.
7. **Debt-service coverage** — commonly covenanted above 1.0x, often 1.2x+ targets; Public-system finance.
8. **Allowed ROE** — often high-single to low-double digits for US IOUs; Regulated equity return.
9. **Capex per connection** — annual and replacement-cycle normalized; Infrastructure intensity.
10. **Compliance events** — number, duration, population/load affected; Service quality and liability.

### Normalization rules

- Use both connections and billed volume denominators.
- Separate drinking water, wastewater, stormwater, and reuse.
- Compare flow and pollutant-load utilization.
- State whether NRW uses system input or authorized consumption denominator.

### Evidence traps

- Using average MGD as the only capacity measure.
- Assuming conservation always improves utility cash flow.
- Ignoring residual disposal created by advanced treatment.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** A combined water utility serving 50,000 accounts and 5bn gallons annually.

**Core equation:** `Allowed operating residual = fixed + volumetric revenue − treatment/distribution O&M − depreciation − financing/tax ± regulatory true-ups`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Volumetric revenue | 5.0bn gallons ÷ 1,000 × $8.00/kgal | $40.0m |
| Fixed charges | 50,000 accounts × $20/month × 12 | $12.0m |
| Total service revenue | $40.0m + $12.0m | $52.0m |
| O&M | Source water, power, chemicals, labor, testing, repairs and billing | $23.0m |
| Depreciation/financing/tax | Illustrative annual burden | $20.0m |
| Illustrative residual | $52.0m − $23.0m − $20.0m | $9.0m before drought decoupling, bad debt and growth capital |

**Decision test:** Test revenue stability under conservation and drought, then compare the marginal source cost—reuse, transfer, groundwater or desalination—rather than average embedded cost.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Most expensive reliable source needed at peak or during drought | Desalination, reuse, transfers and deep groundwater often set incremental cost |
| Marginal customer | Use that can conserve, recycle or relocate at the lowest social cost | Tiered tariffs reveal but do not fully price scarcity or essential-use value |
| Clearing mechanism | Administered tariff and allocation rules rather than free market price | Political affordability and water rights mediate scarcity |
| Cash shutdown point | Essential systems cannot simply shut; source plants dispatch on avoidable treatment/pumping cost and reliability | Public-health obligations dominate narrow economics |
| New-capacity incentive | Approved tariffs, contracts and grants cover long-lived source/network capital | Demand uncertainty and conservation can strand oversized projects |
| Adjustment lag | Hours for pumping, seasons for conservation, years/decades for reservoirs, reuse and pipe replacement | Drought planning must use multiple horizons |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Water produced | Water billed | Reconcile source, treatment loss, leakage, theft, meter error and authorized unbilled use |
| Nonrevenue water | Physical leakage | Separate real loss, apparent loss and authorized unbilled consumption |
| Average tariff | Marginal resource cost | Separate fixed/volumetric blocks, subsidies, wastewater and scarcity cost |
| Connections | Served population or demand | Adjust for household size, intermittency, industrial mix and meter coverage |
| Capex | Capacity addition | Split compliance, replacement, resilience, growth and source development |
