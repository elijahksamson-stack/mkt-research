## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Use this file to define the object being measured before comparing companies, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Regulated local gas distribution, municipal gas systems, city-gate supply and storage/peak-shaving, and related customer service. Upstream production and interstate pipelines are inputs rather than the core business.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Residential and small commercial distribution | Heating, cooking, and water-heating delivery | customers, therms, design-day load | Weather, fixed-cost recovery, and affordability dominate |
| Industrial and large commercial transport | Firm or interruptible high-volume delivery | Dth/day, peak demand | Customer bypass, fuel switching, and contract demand matter |
| Municipal/public gas systems | Locally governed commodity and delivery service | customers, throughput, debt service | Public finance and tariff objectives differ |
| Storage and peak shaving | Seasonal inventory, LNG/propane-air or other peak support | Bcf, withdrawal rate, design-day contribution | Deliverability matters more than annual volume |
| Renewable-gas and hydrogen interfaces | Interconnection, quality control, attributes, blending pilots | MMBtu, blend percent, CI | Gas quality, safety, economics, and policy limit fungibility |

### Specifications that change value

- State heating value, pressure, moisture, sulfur/odorant, interchangeability, and pipeline tariff quality.
- Capacity must be design-day and segment/city-gate specific.
- Separate commodity, upstream transport/storage, and local delivery charges.
- Leak measures must state production, throughput, or main-mile denominator and survey method.
- Mains must state material, vintage, pressure class, miles, and service count.

### Role map

Producer/marketer supplies gas; interstate pipeline and storage deliver; LDC receives, odorizes, regulates, distributes, meters, and bills; customer/appliance burns; commission or public board approves rates; PHMSA/state safety offices oversee integrity.

### Terms that must be explicit

- therm, Dth, Mcf, and MMBtu
- firm versus interruptible service
- design day versus annual throughput
- purchased-gas adjustment versus base rate
- lost-and-unaccounted-for gas versus verified emissions


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: regulated local distribution companies and related gas utility infrastructure; upstream production and interstate pipelines are contextual inputs.

### 1. Gas supply, transport, and storage

Utilities contract for commodity gas, pipeline capacity, and storage to meet seasonal peaks. The U.S. network contains about 3 million miles of mainline and other gas pipelines (**[Fact]** — EIA — https://www.eia.gov/energyexplained/natural-gas/natural-gas-pipelines.php). Supply diversity and firm transport matter more than annual average price during cold snaps.

### 2. Distribution infrastructure

City gates, pressure regulation, mains, services, meters, compressors, odorization, and control systems deliver gas. Replacement of leak-prone pipe and integrity management drive rate-base investment. Steel/plastic pipe, valves, meters, excavation crews, and right-of-way are inputs.

### 3. Capital and regulation

State commissions set allowed recovery, ROE, capital structure, and purchased-gas clauses. Utilities finance long-lived networks externally and need timely recovery of safety programs. Customer affordability constrains capex and commodity pass-through.

### 4. Labor, safety, and data

Qualified gas technicians, leak detection, emergency response, mapping, one-call systems, and cybersecurity protect the network. Methane measurement and replacement prioritization increasingly influence compliance and capital allocation.

### 5. Demand and policy

Weather, customer count, industrial load, building codes, electrification policy, and gas-fired power shape throughput. LDCs delivered 94% of residential/commercial end-use gas in 2023 (**[Fact]** — EIA — https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=64624).

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set earnings:** rate base, allowed ROE, O&M, weather normalization, customer growth, and regulatory lag.
- **Cap capacity:** pipeline/storage entitlement, mains/services, crews, permits, and financing.
- **Shock path:** cold snap → demand spike → storage/firm pipeline protects supply → commodity cost passes through later; inadequate capacity creates curtailment and prudence/reliability risk.

### 7. Full physical, financial, and regulatory input ledger

Core inputs are pipeline-quality gas, interstate and intrastate transport, storage and peak-day supply; city gates, regulators, odorant, pressure-control equipment, compressors, meters, valves, steel and polyethylene pipe, fittings, cathodic protection, leak-detection systems, excavation, paving and emergency-response equipment. Pipe embeds hydrocarbon resin or steel and its ore/scrap, alloy and energy chain. Electricity and telecom are required for compression, metering and control even though the sold product is gas.

Land, franchise rights, easements, street-opening permits, mapping, weather/load forecasts, qualified welders and fusion crews, engineers, dispatchers, call centers, contractors and mutual assistance are necessary inputs. Replacement work competes with water, electric and telecom utilities for road space and skilled construction capacity. Insurance, damage prevention, cybersecurity and verified asset records are part of safe operating capacity.

Funding comes from regulated cash flow, utility debt/equity, municipal bonds, public infrastructure or low-income programs, and occasionally project finance for storage or renewable-gas interconnection. Purchased-gas adjustment mechanisms finance commodity pass-through only after timing and prudence risk; high winter receivables and collateral still require liquidity.

The gas commodity usually passes through and therefore sets bills more than utility margin. Labor, pipe, contractors, interest rates and regulatory lag move earnings. Interstate delivery, storage withdrawal, peak-shaving, city-gate pressure, qualified crews, permits and allowed capital plans cap service. Efficiency, electrification, district heat, propane, fuel oil, renewable natural gas and hydrogen blending are potential substitutes or complements, but safety, appliance compatibility, emissions, customer economics and infrastructure determine feasible substitution.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Structure

Upstream producers feed gathering/processing, interstate/intrastate transmission and storage, then LDC city gates and local networks. LDCs are regulated territorial monopolies; interstate pipes are generally FERC-regulated; municipal systems also serve many customers.

### 2. Profit pools

LDC earnings derive primarily from allowed return on distribution rate base, not commodity gas. Interstate pipeline value rests on contracted capacity and route scarcity. **[Inference]** Safety replacement offers visible growth, but terminal value depends on policy, customer density, and avoiding stranded investment.

### 3. Geography

Cold climates and older cities have dense heating networks; producing regions and Gulf Coast LNG/petrochemicals need transmission/gathering; regions with electrification mandates face higher long-term demand uncertainty. In 2025 completed U.S. pipeline projects added about 6.3 Bcf/d, 85% directed to the South Central region (**[Fact]** — EIA — https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67225).

### 4. Gaining relevance

Leak-prone pipe replacement, LNG/export and power-generation links, storage/peaking, renewable natural gas in selective applications, and network safety/digital monitoring. Data-center power demand can support gas infrastructure where power generation is constrained.

### 5. Losing relevance

Unprotected new residential expansion in jurisdictions pushing building electrification, speculative hydrogen blending without proven economics, and pipelines lacking contracted demand. Renewable gas supply is limited and usually higher cost.

### 6. Policy and security

Methane rules, pipeline safety, building codes, emissions goals, permitting, and cost allocation define growth. Gas reliability can be valuable during power stress, while dependence on gas generation can create electric-gas coordination risk.

### 7. Value migration

Toward contracted transmission/storage, safety modernization, and growing industrial/LNG/power corridors; away from pure volumetric residential growth. The strongest LDC models align capex with visible safety need and constructive affordability outcomes.

### 8. Complete customer, geography, funding, and policy map

The principal output is safe, pressurized delivery and metered sale or transport of gas; connection availability, peak-day readiness, balancing and emergency response are separate services. Methane leakage, combustion emissions, excavated material and retired pipe are material externalities or waste streams. Customers include households, commercial buildings, industry, power plants, district systems and transport or renewable-gas producers seeking interconnection. The occupant uses the gas, but an owner, utility commission, marketer, assistance program or employer may influence the equipment and payment decision.

Gas distribution is inherently local, while commodity and pipeline supply can be continental. Basin production, interstate constraints, storage geology, LNG imports/exports, winter weather and city-gate design create regional basis risk. Investor-owned utilities, municipal systems, cooperatives, pipeline affiliates, competitive marketers, propane distributors, heating-oil firms and electric utilities compete or complement one another.

Private funding relies on regulated rate recovery and credit markets; public funding supports low-income bills, weatherization, safety replacement, resilience and sometimes methane reduction or alternative fuels. Industry structure is shaped by franchise law, rate regulation, pipeline safety, building codes, appliance standards, excavation/damage-prevention rules, emissions and methane policy, renewable-gas standards, consumer protection, cyber rules and local restrictions on new connections. Analysts must distinguish bans or mandates on new buildings from the much slower economics of the installed base.

Cross-market transmission runs both directions: gas prices set heating bills and often marginal power prices; power outages can impair gas compressors while gas shortages constrain generators; electrification raises electric load and reduces gas throughput; LNG exports connect local production to global prices; biomethane links utilities to agriculture, landfills and waste; hydrogen proposals connect to power, water and industrial policy. Fixed network cost spread over declining throughput creates a long-run affordability and stranded-asset problem even if near-term rate-base growth remains positive.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Physical workflow

Production → processing → transmission/storage → city gate pressure reduction/odorization → distribution mains → service line/meter → customer. Line pack and storage help balance hourly/seasonal demand.

### 2. Regulated economics

Revenue requirement covers operating expense, depreciation, tax, and return on rate base. Purchased gas is usually recovered through adjustment clauses and is largely pass-through; utility profit comes from infrastructure investment and service, subject to prudence.

### 3. Customer billing and weather

Bills combine fixed/customer charges, volumetric distribution charges, and commodity gas. Heating-degree days drive volume; decoupling/weather normalization can stabilize revenue. Peak-day demand—not average annual demand—sizes infrastructure.

### 4. KPIs

Rate-base growth, allowed/earned ROE, customer growth, throughput by class, heating-degree days, O&M/customer, leak-prone miles replaced, lost-and-unaccounted-for gas, safety incidents, FFO/debt, equity needs, and bill affordability.

### 5. Pipeline/storage mechanics

Firm capacity contracts reserve transport regardless of use; interruptible service monetizes spare capacity. Storage injection/withdrawal and peak deliverability are distinct. Pipeline expansions require contracts, permits, construction, and eventual in-service rate treatment.

### 6. Failure modes

Explosion/leak, inaccurate mapping, pipeline constraint, commodity procurement failure, capex disallowance, affordability backlash, demand erosion, cyberattack, and long-lived stranded assets.

### 7. Valuation

LDCs: P/E, dividend yield, rate-base/EPS growth, earned ROE, and credit/equity funding. Pipelines: contracted EBITDA/DCF, coverage, duration, recontracting and counterparty risk. Apply terminal-value sensitivity for electrification and methane policy rather than assuming perpetual customer growth.

### 7. Complete flow, billing, and transition mechanics

The workflow is nominate and contract supply/transport → receive at city gate → regulate pressure and odorize → store or peak-shave where available → distribute through mains and services → meter and bill → survey leaks and maintain/replace pipe → respond to damage/emergency → abandon or retire safely. Capacity must be tested at design-day demand and at the most constrained segment, not by annual throughput.

Bills combine commodity, upstream transport/storage and local delivery charges. Purchased-gas clauses generally true up commodity variance, while base rates recover labor, depreciation, tax and return on invested capital. Fixed versus volumetric design determines weather sensitivity and conservation incentives; weather normalization and decoupling change but do not eliminate cash timing. Large customers may buy commodity separately and pay only transport.

Working capital peaks when winter gas purchases precede collections. Receivables, arrears, bad debt, hedges, storage inventory, pipeline imbalance charges and collateral can matter more than reported commodity pass-through. Separate leak-prone-pipe replacement and integrity work from growth extensions; both may enter rate base, but only replacement avoids safety and future maintenance liabilities.

Track customer count, use per customer, degree days, peak-day sendout, contracted transport/storage, lost-and-unaccounted-for gas, leak grades and repair time, main miles by material/age, capex and rate-base growth, allowed/earned ROE, regulatory lag, arrears, bill burden, FFO/debt and emissions. Stress a cold peak with upstream interruption, warm winters, commodity spikes, high rates, construction inflation, delayed cases, accelerated electrification, stricter methane standards and falling throughput per connection.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points. Refresh them for the relevant asset, product, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Delivery revenue requirement = O&M + depreciation + tax + allowed return on rate base ± riders. Customer bill = commodity + upstream transport/storage + local delivery + tax/fees, with weather and true-up mechanisms determining timing.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Design-day reserve | percent buffer above modeled peak requirement | Peak reliability |
| Load factor | annual average divided by design-day capacity | Network utilization |
| Lost/unaccounted gas | often low-single-digit percent, method-dependent | Integrity, metering, and emissions signal |
| Main replacement | miles/year and percent of leak-prone inventory | Safety and rate-base pace |
| Allowed ROE | typically high-single to low-double digits in US cases | Equity return |
| Customer usage | therms/customer weather-normalized | Electrification and efficiency trend |
| Commodity share of bill | varies strongly with price and rate design | Affordability versus utility margin |
| Storage deliverability | withdrawal rate, not only Bcf volume | Cold-peak coverage |
| FFO/debt | rating and jurisdiction specific | Finance capacity |
| Arrears/bad debt | percent of revenue or customers delinquent | Affordability and collection |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Commodity is commonly passed through but creates receivable, collateral, and prudence timing. Reconcile capex to rate base, riders, depreciation, weather normalization, regulatory assets, customer deposits, and debt/equity financing.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Heating degree days and peak cold, customer growth, efficiency, and electrification.
- Commodity basis, upstream pipeline/storage constraints, and hedging.
- Pipe, contractor, labor, rates, allowed return, and regulatory lag.
- Methane rules, building policy, appliance standards, and declining throughput.

### Valuation discipline

Value the regulated network on earned return, rate-base durability, safety capex, affordability, and funding. Test terminal throughput and stranded-cost allocation rather than extrapolating replacement capex indefinitely.


## 6. Regulation and Public Funding


Regulatory snapshot reviewed through **2026-07-14**. Verify enacted text, implementation dates, litigation, and current guidance before use.

### Permission chain

Producer/marketer supplies gas; interstate pipeline and storage deliver; LDC receives, odorizes, regulates, distributes, meters, and bills; customer/appliance burns; commission or public board approves rates; PHMSA/state safety offices oversee integrity.

### Jurisdiction and regime map

| Jurisdiction or layer | Core regimes and authorities | Economic transmission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | State PUC/municipal rates; PHMSA/state pipeline safety; FERC interstate inputs; EPA methane/environmental rules | Cost recovery, integrity, commodity prudence, franchise, and building policy |
| European Union and United Kingdom | National network revenue controls, EU gas market and methane/decarbonization rules | Throughput decline, hydrogen/biomethane standards, and affordability |
| Public/emerging-market systems | Administered tariffs, subsidies, import/LNG contracts, sovereign utilities | Currency, subsidy arrears, and security of supply |
| Local building jurisdictions | Connection restrictions, building codes, appliance/emissions rules | New-customer growth and long-run asset utilization |

### Public and private funding

Private funding is utility debt/equity and retained cash. Public funding includes municipal bonds, low-income assistance, weatherization, safety and methane programs, public infrastructure, and in some jurisdictions transition or renewable-gas support.

### Enforcement and liability

Pipeline incidents can bring civil/criminal penalties, mandated replacement, disallowance, franchise consequences, and tort liability. Commodity procurement, billing, disconnection, and consumer-protection violations can require refunds.

### Update checklist

- Confirm the legal entity, asset location, product, customer, and regulator.
- Separate enacted law, final rule, guidance, permit condition, litigation, and proposal.
- Date grants, tax provisions, tariffs, sanctions, quotas, and administered prices.
- Trace compliance cost, schedule, capacity, price, liability, and funding separately.


## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons rather than exhaustiveness.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1970s US gas curtailments | Price controls and interstate supply rules constrained delivery | Industrial users were interrupted despite underlying resource | Regulation can create physical scarcity |
| 1990s–2000s restructuring | Commodity procurement and transport were separated in many markets | LDC delivery became distinct from gas merchant risk | Map legal title and cost recovery |
| 2018 Merrimack Valley incident | Overpressure during construction caused widespread damage | Procedure, sensing, and control failure overwhelmed physical redundancy | Integrity management includes change execution |
| 2021 Texas winter storm | Production, processing, pipelines, power, and distribution froze | Gas and electric systems failed together | Model cross-infrastructure dependency |
| Building-electrification transition | New-building policy and equipment economics changed growth outlook | Fixed network cost faces a changing customer base | Volume decline can coexist with near-term rate-base growth |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Weather, storage/basis, pipeline notices, customer additions, heat-pump economics, main replacement approvals, arrears, and building-code changes.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Rate-base growth accompanied by falling usage and rising bill burden without a credible cost-allocation path.
- **Durable lesson:** The network is sized for a few peak hours but funded across an annual and potentially shrinking throughput base.


## 8. Data Sources and Monitoring Dashboard


Use observation-period data with preserved units, definitions, geography, and revision vintage.

### Primary source map

| Source | Cadence | Best use | Caveat |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| [EIA natural gas data](https://www.eia.gov/naturalgas/) | weekly to annual | Supply, storage, prices, deliveries | National/state aggregation hides city-gate constraints |
| [PHMSA gas distribution data](https://www.phmsa.dot.gov/data-and-statistics/pipeline/gas-distribution-gas-gathering-gas-transmission-hazardous-liquids) | annual and incident-driven | Miles, materials, leaks, incidents | Reporting definitions change |
| [FERC gas data and orders](https://www.ferc.gov/industries-data/natural-gas) | event-driven | Interstate tariffs, certificates, constraints | Not local retail rate data |
| [State utility commissions](https://www.naruc.org/about-naruc/regulatory-commissions/) | case-driven | Rates, riders, integrity plans | Jurisdiction specific |
| [NOAA climate data](https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/) | daily to monthly | Heating degree days and extremes | Map weather to service territory |

### Indicator stack

- **Leading:** weather forecasts; storage; basis; pipeline notices; rate and integrity filings; heat-pump adoption.
- **Coincident:** sendout; peak; customer usage; leaks; commodity cost; collections.
- **Lagging:** rate base; main replacement; earned ROE; arrears; throughput/customer.

### Minimum dashboard

1. **Design-day reserve** — percent buffer above modeled peak requirement; Peak reliability.
2. **Load factor** — annual average divided by design-day capacity; Network utilization.
3. **Lost/unaccounted gas** — often low-single-digit percent, method-dependent; Integrity, metering, and emissions signal.
4. **Main replacement** — miles/year and percent of leak-prone inventory; Safety and rate-base pace.
5. **Allowed ROE** — typically high-single to low-double digits in US cases; Equity return.
6. **Customer usage** — therms/customer weather-normalized; Electrification and efficiency trend.
7. **Commodity share of bill** — varies strongly with price and rate design; Affordability versus utility margin.
8. **Storage deliverability** — withdrawal rate, not only Bcf volume; Cold-peak coverage.
9. **FFO/debt** — rating and jurisdiction specific; Finance capacity.
10. **Arrears/bad debt** — percent of revenue or customers delinquent; Affordability and collection.

### Normalization rules

- Weather-normalize use and identify design-day assumptions.
- Separate commodity margin/pass-through from delivery earnings.
- Use service-territory rather than national gas basis.
- Track main material and vintage, not only total miles.

### Evidence traps

- Assuming commodity pass-through eliminates liquidity or political risk.
- Using annual throughput to assess peak capacity.
- Ignoring terminal cost allocation under electrification.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

Geography changes ownership, contracting, cost, funding, regulation and risk; compare like with like.

| Geography / archetype | Operating model | Economic and risk implications |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States and Canada | Local distribution companies under state/provincial cost-of-service regulation | Purchased gas is commonly passed through; rate base, weather normalization and pipeline capacity drive economics |
| United Kingdom and European Union | Unbundled regulated networks with competitive suppliers and decarbonization mandates | Allowed returns, volume decline, biomethane/hydrogen policy and asset-life risk matter |
| Japan, Korea and import-dependent systems | LNG-linked wholesale supply feeding city-gas networks | Contract indexation, currency, storage and import infrastructure shape customer bills |
| Emerging urban systems | Concessions or state utilities expanding networks and LPG/CNG substitution | Customer density, affordability, meter loss and foreign-currency supply contracts determine viability |
| Cold-climate constrained regions | High seasonal heating load tied to pipeline/storage deliverability | Peak-day capacity, interruptible demand and weather create infrastructure value beyond annual throughput |

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gas supply | Production basins, gathering, processing, interstate pipelines, LNG and storage | Commodity price may pass through, but capacity reservation and basis affect affordability and reliability |
| Network equipment | Steel/plastic pipe, compressors, regulators, meters, odorant and leak-detection systems | Replacement programs convert safety obligations into rate-base growth and execution risk |
| Customers and substitutes | Heating, cooking, industry, CHP, power generation; electricity, heat pumps, LPG and district heat | Building codes, relative energy prices and retrofit cost determine attrition |
| Capital and policy | Utility bonds/equity, customer rates, public grants and low-income assistance | Affordability limits recovery even when investment is technically prudent |

The practical test is to trace a shock through availability, delivered cost, working capital, output, customer economics, financing and eventual substitution—not merely name the adjacent market.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** A local distribution utility with 100,000 customers and 60m annual therms.

**Core equation:** `Distribution cash contribution = fixed customer revenue + volumetric distribution margin − O&M − depreciation/tax − financing; purchased gas is modeled separately as pass-through`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fixed charge | 100,000 customers × $18/month × 12 | $21.6m |
| Volumetric distribution margin | 60m therms × $0.60/therm | $36.0m |
| Net distribution revenue | $21.6m + $36.0m | $57.6m, excluding commodity gas |
| O&M | Leak surveys, field labor, billing, maintenance and emergency response | $28.0m |
| Depreciation/tax/financing | Illustrative annual requirement | $21.0m |
| Illustrative residual | $57.6m − $28.0m − $21.0m | $8.6m before regulatory true-ups and growth capital |

**Decision test:** Separate commodity pass-through from distribution margin and stress peak-day obligations, customer attrition, bad debt and unrecovered replacement capital.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Highest-cost pipeline path, storage withdrawal, LNG cargo or interruptible supply serving the constrained city gate | Peak-day deliverability—not annual gas abundance—sets local scarcity |
| Marginal customer | Industrial or power load able to interrupt or switch fuel | Interruptible tariffs reveal value of firm residential service |
| Clearing mechanism | Hub price + basis + capacity reservation + regulated delivery tariff | Customer bill and utility margin must be decomposed |
| Cash shutdown point | Network rarely shuts for commodity economics; individual supply paths dispatch on avoidable delivered cost | Safety and service obligations override simple price signals |
| New-capacity incentive | Approved recovery or contracted demand supports pipeline/network capital over asset life | Electrification and policy risk raise stranded-asset hurdle |
| Adjustment lag | Daily storage/linepack, seasons for contracting, years for pipe replacement or new capacity | Peak design and long-lived assets require scenario analysis |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Throughput | Utility margin | Separate commodity pass-through, transportation-only volumes and tariff class |
| Customer count | Demand growth | Adjust for use per customer, weather, efficiency and electrification |
| Leak rate | Comparable methane impact | Define measurement boundary, lost-and-unaccounted gas and detection method |
| Rate-base growth | Value creation | Test customer affordability, depreciation, allowed return and asset-life risk |
| Pipeline capacity | Available peak deliverability | Account for contract rights, pressure, maintenance, storage and downstream constraints |
