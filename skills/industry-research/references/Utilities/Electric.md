## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Use this file to define the object being measured before comparing companies, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Regulated and public electric generation, transmission, distribution, retail service, competitive generation, and system-support services. Renewable developers are covered separately when project economics dominate.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Vertically integrated regulated utilities | Generation, wires, retail, and planning under cost-of-service | MWh, MW peak, rate base | Fuel recovery and integrated-resource obligations distinguish model |
| Transmission owners and operators | High-voltage transport and interconnection | MW transfer, circuit miles, rate base | Regional planning, formula rates, and cost allocation are central |
| Distribution utilities | Local wires, metering, connection, reliability | customers, peak MW, line miles | Storm, wildfire, DER, and local load drive capex |
| Competitive generation | Merchant or contracted energy, capacity, and ancillary services | MWh, accredited MW | Nodal price, fuel, dispatch, and capture determine revenue |
| Public, municipal, and cooperative systems | Service under public/member governance | customers, MWh, debt service | Tax status, public finance, and non-equity objectives differ |

### Specifications that change value

- Distinguish nameplate, available, accredited, firm, and energy-limited capacity.
- State nodal/zone price, time interval, losses, congestion, and capture price.
- Reliability must specify SAIDI/SAIFI method, exclusions, reserve criterion, and weather normalization.
- Rate base must identify allowed items, construction work, depreciation, capital structure, and test year.
- Load must separate annual energy, peak, load factor, customer class, and interruptibility.

### Role map

Generators convert fuel/resource; RTO/ISO or system operator balances; transmission moves bulk power; distributor connects and meters; retailer bills; customer consumes or self-generates; regulator or public board authorizes cost and service.

### Terms that must be explicit

- MW versus MWh
- nameplate versus accredited capacity
- energy price versus capacity and ancillary revenue
- rate base versus capex
- allowed versus earned ROE


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


### 1. Generation fuels and purchased power

Natural gas, coal, nuclear fuel, hydro conditions, wind/solar resource, and market purchases supply energy. In 2025 U.S. utility-scale generation was about 41% natural gas, 24% renewables, 18% nuclear, and 17% coal (**[Fact]** — EIA — https://www.eia.gov/energyexplained/electricity/electricity-in-the-us.php). Fuel clauses often pass costs through, but lag and prudence reviews matter.

### 2. Grid equipment and land

Transformers, switchgear, breakers, cable, steel poles/towers, meters, substations, generation equipment, land/right-of-way, and interconnection are essential. Long-lead transformers and turbines can cap growth. FERC allows selected transmission incentives including construction-work-in-progress recovery and incentive ROEs (**[Fact]** — https://www.ferc.gov/electric-transmission).

### 3. Capital and regulation

Utilities continuously finance long-lived assets with debt and equity. Credit rating, allowed capital structure, ROE, rate base treatment, and regulatory lag determine economics. Public power/co-ops have different funding and tax structures from investor-owned utilities.

### 4. Labor, reliability, and technology

Lineworkers, plant operators, engineers, vegetation management, mutual assistance, control systems, cybersecurity, weather forecasting, and spare equipment maintain service. Reliability standards and wildfire/storm hardening are capacity requirements, not optional overhead.

### 5. Load and customer interconnection

Demand, especially large data centers and industry, must be forecast and connected. EIA says electricity demand grew 2.1% annually over the last five years after a long plateau and projects 0.9%–1.6% through 2050, with data centers a major factor (**[Fact]** — https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/aeo/).

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set earnings:** rate base, allowed ROE, capital structure, O&M, regulatory lag, and load.
- **Cap capacity:** generation/resource adequacy, transmission, transformers, permits, interconnection, skilled crews, and financing.
- **Shock path:** rapid load request → grid studies/upgrades → capex and rate-base growth opportunity → near-term reliability/funding pressure → customer contribution and regulatory treatment decide shareholder value.

### 7. Full resource, equipment, and institutional input ledger

Electric service requires more than fuel and generators. Inputs include coal, natural gas, uranium, water, wind/solar/hydro resource, purchased power and renewable attributes; turbines, boilers, reactors, panels, inverters, blades, batteries, transformers, switchgear, breakers, relays, conductors, poles, towers, meters and control systems; copper, aluminum, electrical steel, specialty steel, concrete, glass, silicon, silver, rare earths, lithium, graphite, nickel and fire-suppression materials. Each processed material embeds mining, refining, chemicals, power, water and freight.

The system also consumes land, easements, rights-of-way, interconnection positions, transmission capacity, frequency response, reactive power, fuel storage, rail/pipeline capacity, cooling water, weather and load data, communications, cloud/OT cybersecurity, spare transformers, mutual-assistance arrangements and trained operators, lineworkers, engineers, nuclear specialists, vegetation crews and storm contractors. Environmental capacity—air permits, water withdrawal/discharge, ash or spent-fuel storage, habitat mitigation and noise—is a finite input.

Capital comes through regulated customer bills, utility debt and equity, municipal revenue bonds, cooperative borrowing, tax equity and transferred credits, project finance, PPAs, government grants/loans and public appropriations. Credit quality matters because utilities finance years of construction before recovery. Regulators, market operators, siting bodies, reliability standards, interconnection rules and cost allocation are productive inputs: an unpermitted or unallocated project has no usable capacity.

Fuel and purchased-power prices typically move short-run energy cost; labor, maintenance and financing affect revenue requirement; generation, transmission, interconnection, transformer, gas-delivery, cooling-water and permitting constraints cap dependable capacity. Demand response, storage, efficiency, distributed generation, reconductoring, recycling and life extension can substitute for new supply, but must be valued for location, timing, duration and reliability rather than nameplate megawatts.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Industry structure

Vertically integrated utilities own generation, transmission, and distribution in regulated territories; restructured markets separate competitive generation from wires; municipal utilities and co-ops serve many regions; RTOs/ISOs dispatch regional markets. Transmission is federally regulated in many contexts; retail distribution/generation recovery is primarily state-regulated.

### 2. Profit pools

Regulated wires and prudently approved generation earn a return on rate base. Competitive generators earn market energy, capacity, ancillary, and hedging margins. **[Inference]** The most durable utility value is not the electricity commodity but the monopoly network and regulatory compact—provided affordability and execution preserve support.

### 3. Geography

Market design, fuel mix, weather, wildfire/hurricane risk, population growth, and regulation vary radically by state/region. ERCOT is energy-market-driven; PJM and other regions use capacity constructs; the Southeast remains more vertically integrated; the West has hydro and wildfire/transmission complexity.

### 4. Gaining relevance

Transmission, distribution hardening, storage, gas generation for reliability in selected regions, nuclear uprates/restarts, demand response, and power-rich territories serving data centers/industry. Solar/wind reduce energy cost but increase the value of flexible capacity and networks.

### 5. Losing relevance

Uncompetitive plants without recovery, unsupported coal, and utilities that grow capex faster than bills/regulators can absorb. EIA projects installed generating capacity must rise 50%–90% by 2050 across cases to meet demand and replacement needs (**[Fact]** — https://www.eia.gov/pressroom/releases/press587.php).

### 6. Policy and security

Reliability standards, environmental policy, tax credits, state clean-energy mandates, wildfire liability, and national-security/cyber concerns shape investment. FERC interconnection and transmission planning rules determine whether new resources reach load.

### 7. Value migration

Toward wires, grid flexibility, firm capacity, and sites with interconnection/fuel; away from standalone energy-only assets without contracts or locational advantage. Data-center load creates opportunity only where contracts, contributions, and tariffs protect existing customers.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

The industry's outputs are energy (MWh), capacity (MW), grid connections, voltage and power-quality support, balancing and ancillary services, reliability, renewable or emissions attributes, and customer data/services. Heat, ash, spent fuel, emissions, land use, retired equipment and restoration obligations are co-products or externalities. The product is time- and location-specific: one MWh at a constrained node during peak demand is not equivalent to off-peak energy elsewhere.

Customers include households, commercial buildings, industry, data centers, transport charging, municipal systems and other utilities. The customer may buy from a retailer while wires service comes from a distributor and generation is settled through a wholesale market. Large users can self-generate or sign direct PPAs; low-income and medically vulnerable users create affordability and reliability obligations distinct from average load.

Geography follows balancing areas, utility franchises, fuel/water access and transmission topology rather than political borders alone. Interties move power, but congestion and losses keep markets regional. Vertically integrated investor-owned utilities, municipal utilities, cooperatives, state/federal power entities, competitive generators, retailers, distributed-resource aggregators and community-energy providers have different capital costs and objectives.

Funding mixes customer-backed rate base, public and cooperative finance, project debt/equity, tax incentives, development banks, grants and sovereign investment. Public support can fund transmission, resilience, nuclear, storage, rural systems and low-income access; private capital supplies generation and contracted infrastructure where market and policy risk are financeable. Legislation and regulation cover franchise rights, rates, resource adequacy, market design, reliability, emissions, clean-energy mandates, tax credits, siting, eminent domain, interconnection, wildfire liability, nuclear safety, consumer protection and cyber/physical security.

Cross-market interactions are decisive: natural gas often sets marginal power price; electrification moves fuel demand from oil/gas into power; data centers and factories change local load and infrastructure bargaining; drought affects hydro and thermal cooling; carbon and renewable-credit markets change dispatch; batteries connect the grid to minerals and manufacturing; power prices feed back into aluminum, chemicals, semiconductors, hydrogen and every other electricity-intensive industry.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Physical workflow

Generate/import → step up voltage → transmit → substation step-down → distribute → meter/bill. Operators balance supply and demand continuously while maintaining frequency, voltage, reserves, and contingency capability.

### 2. Regulated earnings

Revenue requirement ≈ operating expense + depreciation + tax + allowed return × rate base. A rate case sets test-year costs, rate base, capital structure, and allowed ROE. Fuel/purchased power may pass through separately. Earnings grow when prudently invested rate base outpaces dilution and disallowance.

### 3. Competitive generation

Revenue comes from energy price × MWh, capacity payments, ancillary services, contracts/hedges, and renewable credits. Margin reflects heat rate, fuel/basis, availability, emissions, and location. Spark spread approximates power price less gas cost adjusted for heat rate.

### 4. Capacity and reliability

Nameplate MW differs from dependable capacity and MWh generation. Track capacity factor, forced-outage rate, peak demand, reserve margin, SAIDI/SAIFI, and transmission congestion. Storage is energy-limited: MW power and MWh duration both matter.

### 5. KPIs

Rate-base growth, capex, allowed/earned ROE, FFO/debt, equity needs, O&M, load growth, customer bills, regulatory lag, outage metrics, generation availability, and wildfire/storm exposure.

### 6. Failure modes

Cost disallowance, capex overrun, affordability backlash, underfunded balance sheet, catastrophic wildfire/storm, plant outage, fuel constraint, cyberattack, and signing speculative large loads without protections.

### 7. Valuation

Regulated utilities: P/E, EV/EBITDA, dividend yield, rate-base/EPS growth, and premium/discount to allowed versus earned ROE, with credit/equity-funding analysis. Competitive generators: sum-of-parts/DCF by fleet using power, capacity, fuel, and hedge curves. Never treat all MW as equal.

### 8. Complete dispatch, settlement, and capital mechanics

The physical chain is forecast and procure fuel/resource → commit and dispatch generation or demand response → step up and transmit → balance frequency/voltage in real time → step down and distribute → meter, bill and settle → maintain, restore and retire. Planning must reconcile nameplate capacity with accredited capacity, availability, weather correlation, fuel deliverability, transmission and duration. Reliability failures usually emerge from interactions among these elements rather than a simple shortage of installed MW.

Revenue models include regulated cost-of-service, performance-based rates, formula rates, wholesale energy/capacity/ancillary settlements, bilateral PPAs, tolling, merchant spreads, retail supply margins, connection fees and environmental attributes. Fuel and purchased-power adjustment clauses may pass costs with a lag; prudence reviews can disallow them. Decoupling changes the link between volume and revenue. Wholesale price can be set by the marginal unit while a generator's realized capture depends on its generation profile and node.

Working capital includes fuel inventory, collateral and margin, storm receivables, under/over-recovered clauses, construction work in progress and customer deposits. Separate maintenance capital from new generation, transmission, distribution hardening and customer connections. Rate-base growth creates value only when assets are used and useful, allowed returns cover the cost of capital, bills remain affordable, and equity issuance does not absorb the growth.

Minimum KPIs include load and peak growth, customer count and usage, generation by source, heat rate, capacity factor and availability, forced outages, accredited capacity, reserve margin, congestion/capture price, fuel inventory, SAIDI/SAIFI, losses, interconnection queue, capex by category, allowed versus earned ROE, rate base, regulatory lag, FFO/debt, bill burden, emissions/water intensity and storm/wildfire exposure. Stress the system for extreme heat/cold, drought, fuel interruption, transformer shortage, cyberattack, large-load addition, high rates, commodity spike and delayed rate relief.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points. Refresh them for the relevant asset, product, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Regulated revenue requirement = operating cost + depreciation + tax + allowed return on rate base ± riders/true-ups. Competitive generator contribution = dispatched MWh × captured energy price + capacity/ancillary/attribute revenue − fuel, variable O&M, emissions, and start cost.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Load factor | average load divided by peak; class and system specific | Determines asset utilization |
| Reserve margin | often low-teens to around 20% depending accreditation/rules | Resource adequacy buffer |
| Thermal heat rate | roughly 6–12 MMBtu/MWh by technology/age | Fuel efficiency and dispatch |
| Capacity factor | wide: peakers <20%; baseload/renewables technology-specific | Utilization and fixed-cost absorption |
| Line losses | commonly mid-single-digit percent across T&D | Network efficiency |
| SAIDI/SAIFI | minutes and interruptions/customer/year | Reliability and storm exposure |
| Allowed ROE | often high-single to low-double digits in US cases | Equity return benchmark |
| FFO/debt | credit metric with thresholds by rating and risk | Funding capacity |
| Capex/rate base | annual growth and construction duration | Future earnings and bill pressure |
| Customer bill burden | bill as percent of income or usage-normalized cost | Affordability and political headroom |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile capex to in-service rate base, allowance for construction funds, depreciation, regulatory assets/liabilities, riders, storm deferrals, fuel true-ups, and cash financing. Merchant mark-to-market and hedges should be separated from physical generation margin.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Peak and annual load, weather, large-load connection, and customer mix.
- Fuel/purchased power, congestion, carbon and renewable attributes.
- Interest rates, allowed returns, regulatory lag, and equity issuance.
- Storm, wildfire, drought, forced outage, transformer and interconnection delay.

### Valuation discipline

Regulated value depends on sustainable rate-base growth, earned return, affordability, and financing. Merchant value depends on asset-specific dispatch, capture, contracts, capacity, fuel, and terminal cost—not generic EBITDA.


## 6. Regulation and Public Funding


Regulatory snapshot reviewed through **2026-07-14**. Verify enacted text, implementation dates, litigation, and current guidance before use.

### Permission chain

Generators convert fuel/resource; RTO/ISO or system operator balances; transmission moves bulk power; distributor connects and meters; retailer bills; customer consumes or self-generates; regulator or public board authorizes cost and service.

### Jurisdiction and regime map

| Jurisdiction or layer | Core regimes and authorities | Economic transmission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | State PUC retail/rate jurisdiction; FERC wholesale/transmission; NERC reliability; NRC nuclear; EPA environmental | Cost allocation, prudence, interconnection, market design, reliability, and environmental rules |
| European Union and United Kingdom | National regulators plus EU market, network, carbon, and state-aid frameworks | Revenue controls, market coupling, capacity mechanisms, and carbon affect returns |
| Public and emerging-market systems | Ministry/public-utility governance, tariff/subsidy policy, sovereign fuel and finance | Collection, currency, subsidy arrears, and political tariffs can dominate |
| Cross-border grids | Interconnector rights, reliability coordination, trade and carbon rules | Congestion rent and security policy alter regional price convergence |

### Public and private funding

Private funding includes utility debt/equity, project finance, tax equity or transfers, and PPAs. Public funding includes municipal/cooperative bonds, grants, loans, tax credits, public power, resilience, low-income programs, nuclear support, and transmission or rural infrastructure.

### Enforcement and liability

Potential outcomes include cost disallowance, penalties, license/market suspension, reliability sanctions, wildfire or environmental liability, customer refunds, mandated investment, and criminal exposure after safety failures.

### Update checklist

- Confirm the legal entity, asset location, product, customer, and regulator.
- Separate enacted law, final rule, guidance, permit condition, litigation, and proposal.
- Date grants, tax provisions, tariffs, sanctions, quotas, and administered prices.
- Trace compliance cost, schedule, capacity, price, liability, and funding separately.


## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons rather than exhaustiveness.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2000–2001 California power crisis | Market-design, supply, transmission, and retail-price constraints interacted | Wholesale costs rose while utilities could not fully recover | Market layers and hedging must be modeled together |
| 2003 Northeast blackout | Protection and situational failures cascaded across networks | Local faults became regional outage | Reliability is a system property |
| 2011 Fukushima and nuclear policy reset | Extreme event changed reactor operation and national policy | Replacement fuel and power prices rose in affected markets | Tail risk can change an entire generation stack |
| 2021 Texas winter storm | Cold disabled generation, gas supply, and infrastructure simultaneously | Scarcity pricing and customer/retailer failure followed | Correlated fuel-weather-design stress defeats simple reserve counts |
| 2022–2024 load and interconnection pressure | Clean generation and large loads met queue and transmission constraints | Connection rights and deliverability gained value | Generation nameplate without network capacity is not supply |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Load forecasts, interconnection requests/withdrawals, forward curves, reserve auctions, fuel basis, outages, rate filings, capex approvals, and customer arrears.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Rate-base growth without earned returns or affordability, installed MW without accreditation/deliverability, or merchant EBITDA driven by temporary hedges.
- **Durable lesson:** Electricity value is time-, location-, duration-, and reliability-specific.

7. **Allowed ROE** — often high-single to low-double digits in US cases; Equity return benchmark.
8. **FFO/debt** — credit metric with thresholds by rating and risk; Funding capacity.
9. **Capex/rate base** — annual growth and construction duration; Future earnings and bill pressure.

### Normalization rules

- Use weather-normalized load and distinguish energy from peak.
- Compare price at the asset node and generation-weighted capture.
- Use accredited/firm capacity and duration, not nameplate.
- Reconcile regulatory accounting to cash and financing.

### Evidence traps

- Treating every MWh or MW as interchangeable.
- Assuming rate-base growth automatically creates per-share value.
- Ignoring gas deliverability, transmission, or distribution bottlenecks.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

Geography changes ownership, contracting, cost, funding, regulation and risk; compare like with like.

| Geography / archetype | Operating model | Economic and risk implications |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States regulated utilities | State-regulated vertically integrated or wires-only utilities with federal wholesale oversight | Rate base, allowed return, fuel riders, storm recovery and state policy drive earnings |
| European liberalized systems | Unbundled generation, networks and retail with national regulation and cross-border markets | Network returns coexist with merchant power, hedging and policy intervention |
| China and other state-directed systems | State-owned grids/generators with administered tariffs and planning-led investment | Reliability and industrial policy can outrank near-term market returns |
| Emerging and island systems | Vertically integrated utilities, concessions, IPPs and public/off-grid providers | Fuel import dependence, losses, affordability, currency and sovereign credit dominate |
| High-renewable nodal markets | Competitive dispatch with transmission constraints, storage and ancillary services | Curtailment, congestion, negative pricing and capacity adequacy separate energy from reliability value |

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** A regulated distribution/generation utility with $1.0bn of net rate base.

**Core equation:** `Annual revenue requirement = operating cost + depreciation + tax + allowed debt cost + allowed equity return ± riders/true-ups`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Capital structure | $500m equity and $500m debt supporting $1.0bn rate base | Illustrative 50/50 regulatory capitalization |
| Allowed capital return | 9.5% × $500m equity + 5.5% × $500m debt | $75.0m before income-tax gross-up |
| Depreciation | Approved annual recovery of invested plant | $35.0m |
| Operating cost | Labor, maintenance, billing, vegetation and non-pass-through operations | $180.0m |
| Tax and other allowed items | Illustrative net requirement | $18.0m |
| Base revenue requirement | $75m + $35m + $180m + $18m | $308m before pass-through fuel/purchased power and true-ups |

**Decision test:** Value capital spending only after testing prudence, construction lag, regulatory lag, customer affordability and whether the asset enters rate base on schedule.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Last dispatched generator, storage resource, import or demand response needed in a location and interval | Heat rate, fuel, emissions, start cost and grid constraints set short-run offer |
| Marginal customer | Load able to curtail, shift time or self-generate | Scarcity pricing and demand response reveal reliability value |
| Clearing mechanism | Nodal/zonal energy and ancillary markets, bilateral contracts or administered tariffs | Energy price does not equal capacity, network or resilience value |
| Cash shutdown point | Merchant unit runs when expected energy/ancillary revenue exceeds avoidable fuel, emissions and cycling cost | Reliability must-run and regulated recovery can override |
| New-capacity incentive | Expected energy + capacity + ancillary + contract/policy revenue covers fixed cost and return | Interconnection and transmission timing are part of the hurdle |
| Adjustment lag | Seconds for dispatch, years for generation, transmission and regulatory approval | Short-run scarcity can coexist with long-run overbuild |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Capacity (MW) | Energy production or dependable capacity | Report MWh, capacity factor, accredited capacity, outage and coincidence with peak |
| Rate base | Replacement value or free cash | Reconcile gross plant, accumulated depreciation, construction work, disallowances and recovery lag |
| Allowed ROE | Earned ROE | Adjust for regulatory lag, outages, cost overruns, capital structure and nonregulated items |
| LCOE | Delivered system cost | Add integration, transmission, firming, curtailment and capacity value |
| Load growth | Revenue or earnings growth | Separate customer mix, tariffs, efficiency, weather, peak and required capital |
