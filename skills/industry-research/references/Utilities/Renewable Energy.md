## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Use this file to define the object being measured before comparing companies, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Utility-scale and distributed solar, onshore/offshore wind, battery storage, hydro where relevant, and project development/ownership. General electric-utility networks are treated as customers and constraints.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Utility-scale solar | PV generation and hybrid projects | MWdc/MWac, MWh, capacity factor | DC/AC ratio, tracker, module, inverter, node, and curtailment matter |
| Distributed solar | Residential, commercial, community, and behind-meter systems | kW, customers, attachment rate | Retail tariff, tax appetite, credit, roof, and dealer quality drive economics |
| Onshore wind | Land-based turbine arrays | MW, MWh, capacity factor | Turbine class, hub height, wake, transmission, and land agreements |
| Offshore wind | Seabed leases, large turbines, export cables, ports and vessels | MW, lease area, availability | Long lead, marine construction, offtake and supply-chain risk |
| Battery and hybrid storage | Energy shifting, capacity, and ancillary services | MW, MWh, duration, cycles | Charging source, degradation, augmentation, safety, and market stacking |
| Hydro and other renewables | Reservoir/run-of-river, geothermal or other resources where included | MW, MWh, resource flow | Water/steam resource, permits, and long asset life create different risks |

### Specifications that change value

- Always state MWdc versus MWac, inverter loading, expected P50/P90 energy, degradation, and curtailment.
- Wind needs turbine class, rotor/hub, gross-to-net losses, wake, availability, and interconnection node.
- Storage needs power, energy, usable duration, efficiency, cycle/degradation warranty, augmentation, and state of charge.
- Contracts must specify shape, node/basis, curtailment, negative-price, imbalance, attribute, and change-in-law allocation.
- Pipeline MW must be tagged by site control, permit, queue, offtake, financing, equipment, and notice-to-proceed.

### Role map

Developer controls site/queue/permits; OEM and EPC build; tax/credit investor, lender, and sponsor fund; utility/corporate/market buys; operator dispatches; landowner/host community grants access; regulator and grid operator connect and govern.

### Terms that must be explicit

- MWdc versus MWac
- P50, P90, gross and net generation
- capture price and basis
- round-trip efficiency and degradation
- development pipeline versus executable backlog


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: utility-scale and distributed solar, wind, batteries, and renewable project owners/developers; hydro is included where relevant.

### 1. Resource, land, and site control

Solar irradiance, wind regime, hydrology, geotechnical conditions, acreage, leases, community support, and environmental constraints determine production and permitting. Resource quality is locational and cannot be transported.

### 2. Equipment and minerals

Modules/cells/wafers/polysilicon, inverters, trackers, turbines/blades/towers, batteries/cells, transformers, cable, steel, aluminum, copper, and power electronics make the plant. Trade rules and concentrated supply chains affect landed cost. DOE reports more than 95 GW of U.S. solar supply-chain manufacturing capacity additions announced/added since the IRA, including nearly 42 GW of module capacity (**[Fact]** — https://www.energy.gov/cmei/systems/quarterly-solar-industry-update).

### 3. Interconnection and transmission

Grid studies, network upgrades, transformers, substations, and transmission rights are often the binding inputs. FERC Order 2023 shifted toward cluster studies, readiness requirements, and penalties to improve queue processing (**[Fact]** — https://www.ferc.gov/explainer-interconnection-final-rule).

### 4. Capital, contracts, and tax attributes

Construction debt, tax equity/credit transfer, sponsor equity, PPAs, hedges, renewable credits, and capacity payments fund projects. Rates and equipment cost can make contracted projects uneconomic between bid and financing.

### 5. Labor and execution

Engineering, procurement, civil/electrical construction, interconnection specialists, commissioning, and operations labor must align. Weather windows and turbine/vessel availability constrain wind, particularly offshore.

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set returns:** capex, capacity factor, capture price, financing, tax value, degradation, and O&M.
- **Cap capacity:** interconnection/transmission, permits, equipment/transformers, site control, and contracted offtake.
- **Shock path:** module price falls → project capex improves only if tariffs, interconnection cost, and rates do not offset it → more projects queue → congestion/cannibalization can lower capture price.

### 7. Full resource, materials, and finance ledger

Solar requires irradiance, land/roofs, polysilicon, wafers, cells, modules, glass, aluminum frames, silver/copper paste, polymers, inverters, transformers, racking and cleaning. Wind requires wind resource, leases, steel plate and rebar, concrete, fiberglass or carbon fiber, resins, copper, permanent magnets or geared drivetrains, towers, bearings, cranes and vessels offshore. Batteries require cells, cathode/anode materials, lithium, graphite and chemistry-specific nickel/cobalt/manganese/iron/phosphate, copper/aluminum foil, electrolyte, separators, thermal management, power electronics and fire safety. Hydro adds hydrology, civil works, turbines and watershed rights.

All technologies also need surveys, geotechnical work, roads/ports, interconnection studies, queue position, transmission, communications, forecasting, insurance, spares, cybersecurity, construction labor, high-voltage engineers and long-term O&M. Critical-mineral and component chains include mining, refining, chemicals, energy, water, environmental permitting and cross-border freight; country of cell assembly alone does not describe origin.

Capital-stack inputs include development equity, deposits and security, tax equity or transferred credits, construction debt, project debt, sponsor equity, PPAs, merchant hedges, yield/infrastructure capital, grants and public loans. Bankability depends on resource studies, title, permits, interconnection, offtake, equipment warranties, curtailment allocation, creditworthy counterparties and a financeable change-in-law regime.

Equipment price, rates, tax attributes, power/certificate prices and curtailment set returns; interconnection, transmission, permitting, transformers, qualified labor, vessels/cranes and firm offtake gate build. Repowering, life extension, recycling, alternative chemistries, demand response, efficiency and hybridization can substitute for greenfield capacity. Their value must be tested on hourly profile, location, degradation and duration, not simple $/MW comparisons.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Value chain

Raw materials → component manufacturing → development/interconnection → EPC → ownership/operation → power markets/offtakers → recycling/repowering. Equipment manufacturing and project ownership have different cycles and moats.

### 2. Industry structure and profit pools

Solar modules are globally commoditized; inverters, trackers, software, and specialized equipment have more differentiation but remain competitive. Developers create value through site, permit, interconnection, contract, and financing; owners monetize operating cash flow. **[Inference]** Scarce interconnection and power-delivery rights increasingly matter more than module procurement.

### 3. Geography

Solar leads in high-irradiance regions but is deployable broadly; onshore wind clusters in strong-resource corridors; offshore wind needs ports/vessels/transmission; hydro is site-limited. Texas and the Southwest combine resource and demand but can face congestion/negative pricing.

### 4. Scale and growth

EIA said the U.S. added 53 GW in 2025 and developers planned a record 86 GW for 2026, 51% solar, 28% batteries, and 14% wind (**[Fact]** — https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67205). Planned does not equal completed: queues, permits, contracts, and financing screen projects.

### 5. Gaining relevance

Solar-plus-storage, standalone batteries, repowering, grid-forming/control capabilities, domestic supply where policy supports it, and hybrid sites with existing interconnection. Corporate/data-center demand may support contracts, but around-the-clock claims require portfolio/firming.

### 6. Losing relevance

Merchant projects in saturated nodes, speculative queues, equipment makers without cost/technology edge, and fixed-price contracts signed before cost/rate inflation. Offshore wind faces acute capex, vessel, and contract-reset risk in some markets.

### 7. Value migration

From modules toward interconnection, development rights, storage, power marketing, and operating optimization; from standalone intermittent output toward shaped/firmed clean power. Policy remains important, so returns must be tested without heroic subsidy assumptions.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and legislation map

Outputs include time- and location-specific electricity, accredited capacity where rules allow, ancillary services, renewable/clean-energy certificates, tax attributes, carbon reductions and sometimes lease/community payments. Curtailment, imbalance, habitat effects, land use, blade/module/battery waste and decommissioning are negative or residual outputs. Storage is not primary generation: it buys or receives energy, loses some through round-trip efficiency, and sells timing, capacity and grid services.

Customers are utilities, community-choice or municipal buyers, corporations, traders, wholesale markets, households and host communities. The offtaker, grid operator, tax-credit buyer, lender and end-user each underwrite a different risk. Distributed installers also depend on homeowners, landlords, commercial property owners, dealers, lenders and utilities approving interconnection.

Geography reflects resource quality, land, permitting, grid congestion and load—not resource alone. Manufacturing is globally traded and policy-sensitive; projects are immovable after construction. Offshore wind depends on ports, vessels and seabed leases; hydro on watersheds; distributed solar on retail tariffs and roofs. Developers, IPPs, utilities, infrastructure funds, manufacturers, installers, aggregators and community/nonprofit owners capture different parts of the value chain.

Funding combines private project finance and corporate capital with tax credits, auctions, feed-in tariffs, contracts for difference, renewable obligations, grants, public banks and regulated procurement. Current US credit eligibility also depends on technology, timing, labor, domestic-content, community and foreign-entity rules; EU and other jurisdictions use different auction, carbon and state-aid systems. Siting, wildlife, water, transmission, market, building, fire, recycling, labor and local-content rules can be as important as energy legislation.

Renewables interact with gas generation, nuclear, coal, storage, transmission, demand response, electric vehicles, data centers, hydrogen and mineral markets. High penetration can depress capture prices and increase the value of flexibility; electrification can absorb output if networks and load shapes align. Policy can accelerate demand while simultaneous permitting and grid constraints prevent supply, shifting value from generation equipment to scarce interconnection, transmission and firming.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Project workflow

Site/resource screen → lease/control → interconnection request/studies → environmental/local permit → offtake/market plan → equipment/EPC contract → financing/tax structuring → construction → commissioning → operate/repower/decommission.

### 2. Generation mechanics

Annual MWh = MW × 8,760 × capacity factor, adjusted for availability, curtailment, losses, and degradation. Solar output is daytime; wind follows weather; hydro follows water; batteries shift energy and provide capacity/ancillary services but consume energy and degrade.

### 3. Revenue models

Fixed/escalating PPA, contract-for-difference, utility rate base, merchant energy, capacity, ancillary services, renewable credits, and tax benefits. Capture price is the generation-weighted power price; it can fall below average price when similar resources produce simultaneously.

### 4. Unit economics

LCOE annualizes capex/financing plus O&M over expected generation, but project equity value also needs taxes, credits, curtailment, basis, merchant tail, and contract terms. Battery value depends on price spread, cycles, duration, degradation, and capacity/ancillary payments.

### 5. KPIs

MW/MWh backlog and operating, interconnection maturity, contracted percentage/price/tenor, capex per kW, capacity factor, availability, curtailment, capture price, degradation, cash available for distribution, and project/sponsor leverage.

### 6. Failure modes

Queue delay, network-upgrade surprise, permit/community loss, fixed-price EPC failure, equipment defect, resource miss, curtailment, negative prices, PPA counterparty risk, tax-rule change, and refinancing mismatch.

### 7. Valuation

Operating assets: project DCF using contracted cash flow and conservative merchant tail. Developers: risked MW pipeline by milestone—site control is worth far less than notice-to-proceed. Manufacturers: normalized margin, utilization, technology/cost position, and balance sheet. Avoid uniform EV/MW across technologies and stages.

### 8. Complete development, dispatch, and cash-conversion model

The end-to-end chain is site/resource screening → land and community agreements → environmental and interconnection studies → permits and offtake → equipment procurement and financing → construction → testing/commissioning → dispatch and settlement → O&M/augmentation or repowering → decommissioning and recycling. Development attrition is normal; a pipeline should be probability-weighted by control, permit, queue, offtake and finance rather than summed at nameplate MW.

Operating output equals resource × equipment availability × conversion efficiency, reduced by wake/soiling/temperature losses, degradation, electrical losses, outages and curtailment. Revenue can include contracted energy, merchant nodal price, capacity, ancillary services, certificates and tax attributes. PPA shape, basis, imbalance, negative-price and curtailment provisions determine who bears mismatch risk. Storage revenue requires separate treatment of charging cost, round-trip loss, degradation and augmentation.

Cash precedes operations through land options, studies, interconnection deposits, equipment advances and construction draws. After operation, receivables, reserve accounts, debt service, tax-credit timing and warranty claims matter. Separate development gains from recurring generation cash flow; maintenance capex, inverter replacement, blade/gearbox work and battery augmentation from growth capex. Project IRR, sponsor IRR and corporate ROIC differ when leverage, fees or asset sales are involved.

Track pipeline conversion, time to notice-to-proceed, installed cost, resource and production versus plan, capacity factor, availability, curtailment, capture price, contracted share and tenor, operating cost, degradation, interconnection cost, DSCR, project leverage, tax-credit realization and decommissioning funding. Stress lower resource, equipment delay, rate increase, basis congestion, negative prices, offtaker default, warranty failure, policy change, mineral tariff/sourcing restriction and transmission delay.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points. Refresh them for the relevant asset, product, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Project equity cash = delivered MWh × captured energy price + capacity/ancillary/attribute revenue + monetized tax benefits − operating, land, insurance, balancing, and augmentation cost − debt service − maintenance and decommissioning capital.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Solar capacity factor | roughly 15–30% by resource/design | Energy yield from nameplate |
| Onshore wind capacity factor | roughly 25–50% by resource/turbine | Resource and turbine fit |
| Battery round-trip efficiency | roughly 75–92% by technology/system | Charging loss and arbitrage hurdle |
| Battery duration | commonly 1–4 hours; longer-duration markets differ | Capacity and use case |
| Module degradation | often around 0.3–0.8% annually | Long-run production |
| Wind/solar construction lead | roughly 1–3 years after mature development | Equipment and build execution |
| Development lead | often 3–7+ years; offshore longer | Queue, permit, offtake, finance |
| Project debt service coverage | often underwritten around 1.2–1.5x depending contract/risk | Debt capacity |
| Curtailment/capture discount | percent of potential output or hub price | Grid saturation risk |
| Installed cost | $/W, $/kW, or $/kWh on like-for-like scope | Capital competitiveness |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Separate development gains and asset sales from recurring generation; tax-credit value from operating revenue; gross from net generation; and construction spend from in-service assets. Normalize battery augmentation and inverter/major-component replacement. Consolidation and tax-equity accounting can obscure project leverage.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Resource, degradation, availability, curtailment, basis, and negative prices.
- Equipment, interconnection, construction, rates, tax-credit transfer, and offtaker credit.
- Queue, permit, land, community, wildlife, vessel/port, transformer, and transmission delay.
- Merchant tail price, repowering, recycling, and decommissioning.

### Valuation discipline

Use probability-weighted development value plus project DCFs with node, shape, contract, tax, degradation, and capital structure. Avoid valuing early pipeline MW like operating MW.


## 6. Regulation and Public Funding


Regulatory snapshot reviewed through **2026-07-14**. Verify enacted text, implementation dates, litigation, and current guidance before use.

### Permission chain

Developer controls site/queue/permits; OEM and EPC build; tax/credit investor, lender, and sponsor fund; utility/corporate/market buys; operator dispatches; landowner/host community grants access; regulator and grid operator connect and govern.

### Jurisdiction and regime map

| Jurisdiction or layer | Core regimes and authorities | Economic transmission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | FERC/state interconnection and market rules; federal/state/local siting; IRS credits; environmental/wildlife; fire/building | Queue, credit eligibility, domestic/foreign sourcing, permitting, and offtake |
| European Union and United Kingdom | Auctions/CfDs or national support, EU electricity/carbon/state-aid, planning and maritime rules | Auction terms, negative prices, grid, local content, and permitting |
| China and major Asian markets | National/provincial targets, administered auctions/tariffs, industrial and grid policy | Manufacturing scale, curtailment, local rules, and state finance |
| Emerging markets | Sovereign utility offtake, development-bank finance, land and currency regulation | Counterparty, convertibility, transmission, and political risk |

### Public and private funding

Private funding includes sponsor/development equity, construction debt, project debt, PPAs/hedges, tax equity or transfers, infrastructure funds, and customer finance. Public funding includes tax credits, grants, public loans/guarantees, auctions, CfDs/feed-in tariffs, regulated procurement, and public grid/port investment.

### Enforcement and liability

Risks include permit/lease cancellation, interconnection security forfeiture, tax-credit recapture, market penalties, wildlife/environmental injunction, fire/product liability, offtake damages, and decommissioning claims.

### Update checklist

- Confirm the legal entity, asset location, product, customer, and regulator.
- Separate enacted law, final rule, guidance, permit condition, litigation, and proposal.
- Date grants, tax provisions, tariffs, sanctions, quotas, and administered prices.
- Trace compliance cost, schedule, capacity, price, liability, and funding separately.


## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons rather than exhaustiveness.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2000s–2010s solar cost decline | Manufacturing scale, learning, policy, and competition reduced module cost | Value migrated from modules toward development, distribution, and scarce sites/grid | Technology cost and profit pool can move in opposite directions |
| 2011–2013 solar trade and bankruptcies | Rapid capacity and price decline overwhelmed leveraged manufacturers | Demand growth did not protect undifferentiated producers | Cost curve and balance sheet beat TAM |
| 2021 Texas storage/renewable stress | Extreme weather tested correlated generation, fuel, and grid availability | Nameplate portfolios did not equal firm winter supply | Accreditation and weather correlation matter |
| 2022–2024 offshore-wind resets | Inflation, rates, supply chain, and fixed offtake terms impaired projects | Developers sought cancellations or repricing | Long development plus fixed price creates macro mismatch |
| High-renewable negative-price episodes | Output correlation met transmission and inflexible demand | Capture prices fell below hub averages and curtailment rose | Flexibility and location gain value as penetration rises |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Queue position, interconnection cost, permit milestones, equipment and freight, rates, auction/PPA prices, capture/curtailment, tax guidance, and transmission build.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Pipeline growth without land/queue/offtake, falling LCOE paired with worsening capture, or battery revenue stacks double-counting the same availability.
- **Durable lesson:** Resource quality is necessary; interconnection, contract, capital, and hourly system value determine bankability.

- **Lagging:** degradation; augmentation; repowering; recycling; project IRR and defaults.

### Minimum dashboard

1. **Solar capacity factor** — roughly 15–30% by resource/design; Energy yield from nameplate.
2. **Onshore wind capacity factor** — roughly 25–50% by resource/turbine; Resource and turbine fit.
3. **Battery round-trip efficiency** — roughly 75–92% by technology/system; Charging loss and arbitrage hurdle.
4. **Battery duration** — commonly 1–4 hours; longer-duration markets differ; Capacity and use case.
5. **Module degradation** — often around 0.3–0.8% annually; Long-run production.
6. **Wind/solar construction lead** — roughly 1–3 years after mature development; Equipment and build execution.
7. **Development lead** — often 3–7+ years; offshore longer; Queue, permit, offtake, finance.
8. **Project debt service coverage** — often underwritten around 1.2–1.5x depending contract/risk; Debt capacity.
9. **Curtailment/capture discount** — percent of potential output or hub price; Grid saturation risk.
10. **Installed cost** — $/W, $/kW, or $/kWh on like-for-like scope; Capital competitiveness.

### Normalization rules

- Use AC/DC and gross/net definitions consistently.
- Compare hourly node-weighted capture, not annual hub price.
- Separate operating, construction-ready, advanced, and early pipeline.
- Include tax, interconnection, augmentation, and decommissioning in installed cost.

### Evidence traps

- Adding pipeline MW without probability weighting.
- Using LCOE as a complete revenue metric.
- Treating storage discharge as net generation without charging cost and loss.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

Geography changes ownership, contracting, cost, funding, regulation and risk; compare like with like.

| Geography / archetype | Operating model | Economic and risk implications |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | Tax-credit-supported merchant, utility-PPA and corporate-PPA projects in nodal markets | Interconnection queues, tax capacity, basis, transferability and curtailment shape realized value |
| European Union and United Kingdom | Auctions/CfDs, merchant exposure, guarantees of origin and mature balancing markets | Negative prices, capture rates, permitting and grid build determine returns |
| China | Scale manufacturing and state-directed buildout with provincial grid/dispatch dynamics | Equipment cost advantage can be offset by curtailment, tariffs and payment timing |

| Remote, island and off-grid systems | Hybrid renewables, storage and microgrids displacing imported fuel | Avoided diesel cost is high, but small scale, maintenance and financing raise required returns |

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Materials and manufacturing | Polysilicon, wafers, modules, glass, aluminum, copper, rare earths, steel, batteries and power electronics | Trade policy, qualification and manufacturing concentration affect delivered equipment cost |
| Sites and networks | Land/seabed, resource data, permits, roads, ports, interconnection, transmission and system operators | A completed plant without network rights has little economic output |

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** A 100 MWac utility-scale solar project.

**Core equation:** `Unlevered project cash yield = (delivered MWh × realized price + attributes − operating/land/interconnection cost) ÷ total installed capital`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gross generation | 100 MW × 8,760 hours × 25% capacity factor | 219,000 MWh/year |
| Delivered generation | Gross less 4% curtailment/availability losses | 210,240 MWh/year |
| Energy revenue | 210,240 MWh × $45/MWh | $9.46m/year |
| Operating burden | $18/kW-year O&M + $0.60m land, insurance and network cost | $2.40m/year |
| Unlevered operating cash | $9.46m − $2.40m | $7.06m/year before tax, degradation and major replacements |
| Installed capital and yield | $100m capex; $7.06m ÷ $100m | 7.1% year-one unlevered cash yield |

**Decision test:** Underwrite hourly capture price, degradation, curtailment, network basis, tax eligibility and replacement capital—not nameplate MW or headline PPA price.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Incremental project whose all-in capture revenue just covers capital and operating cost | Resource quality, interconnection, financing and policy create different curves by node |
| Marginal customer | Utility, corporate buyer or market load choosing between contract, self-generation and grid purchases | Credit, hourly load shape and hedge need determine willingness to pay |
| Clearing mechanism | Auctions/PPAs for long-run capacity; nodal energy and ancillary prices for dispatch | Contract price and merchant capture price answer different questions |
| Cash shutdown point | Near-zero short-run marginal cost, but negative prices can exceed subsidy/contract value or cause curtailment | Dispatch depends on contractual and tax-credit treatment |
| New-capacity incentive | Risk-adjusted contracted/merchant revenue covers capex, network, financing, degradation and return | Queue position and deliverability are part of supply cost |
| Adjustment lag | Months for equipment, years for permits/interconnection/transmission | Manufacturing abundance cannot resolve grid scarcity immediately |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nameplate MW | Delivered energy or firm capacity | Show capacity factor, losses, curtailment, accredited capacity and node |
| PPA price | Project realized revenue | Include escalators, basis, shape, curtailment, certificates, floors and settlement point |
| LCOE | Equity return or system value | Add financing, tax, capture rate, grid and firming; model cash timing |
| Capacity factor | Resource quality alone | Separate weather, degradation, outages, clipping and curtailment |
| Backlog/pipeline | Buildable projects | Risk land, permits, interconnection, offtake, equipment, capital and notice to proceed |
