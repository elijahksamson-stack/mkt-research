## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Hospitals, physician/ambulatory, managed care/insurance, post-acute/home/behavioral services, pharmacy/lab and enabling service organizations where care delivery or reimbursement risk dominates.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hospitals and health systems | Inpatient, emergency, outpatient and ancillary care | admissions, visits, cases, staffed beds | Payer, acuity, service line, employed/independent and site |
| Physician and ambulatory | Primary/specialty, surgery, urgent and retail care | visits, procedures, providers | Fee/capitation, referral, site and ownership |
| Managed care and risk-bearing providers | Premium/capitation, network and medical management | member months, premium, claims | Population, product, risk adjustment and delegation |
| Post-acute, home and behavioral | Skilled nursing, rehab, home health, hospice and behavioral | census, episodes, visits, beds | Referral, staffing, authorization and reimbursement |
| Pharmacy, labs and service enablers | Dispensing, diagnostics, revenue cycle and other services | scripts, tests, claims, transactions | Gross/net, spread/fee, payer and regulation |

### Specifications that change value

- State site, service line, patient acuity, payer, coding group, risk adjustment and geographic market.
- Capacity is staffed beds/rooms/providers/equipment and discharge path, not licensed count.
- Revenue must use net reimbursement after denials, contractual allowance, risk settlement and bad debt.
- Quality needs risk-adjusted outcome, denominator, follow-up and reporting definition.
- Risk contracts need population, benchmark, corridor, attribution, quality and stop-loss.

### Role map

Patient uses; clinician orders; provider delivers; payer/employer/government funds; PBM/pharmacy/lab supports; family/caregiver adds capacity; regulator/accreditor licenses; vendor supplies; lender/donor funds facilities.

### Terms that must be explicit

- gross charge versus net patient revenue
- admission, discharge, patient day and encounter
- licensed versus staffed capacity
- fee-for-service versus capitation/value risk
- medical-cost ratio versus provider margin


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope note: "Healthcare Services" here means the care-delivery and care-financing layer — hospitals/health systems, physician groups, managed-care insurers (payers), pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), drug/medical-product distributors, clinical laboratories, dialysis and other specialty services. It excludes drug manufacturing (Pharma) and device manufacturing (Med Devices), which are UPSTREAM inputs to this industry. The defining economic feature is that the industry's "output" is a claim paid by a third party (government or insurer), so **reimbursement policy is itself a raw-material-grade input**.

---

### 1. Labor — the dominant input

Labor is by far the largest cost and the primary capacity constraint. **[Fact]** Labor accounted for roughly 56% of U.S. hospitals' operating expenses in 2024, and hospital labor costs reached about $839 billion, near 60% of the average hospital's expenses (AHA, 2025 — https://www.aha.org/costsofcaring). **[Fact]** Contract/agency staffing spend was about $51.1 billion in 2023 (AHA, 2024 — https://www.aha.org/guidesreports/2025-04-28-2024-costs-caring). **[Fact]** Registered-nurse salaries grew about 26.6% faster than inflation over roughly four years to 2025 (AHA, 2025 — https://www.aha.org/system/files/media/file/2025/04/The-Cost-of-Caring-April-2025.pdf).

Why labor is a bottleneck, not just a cost: physicians require ~7–11 years of post-college training; the pipeline is capped by Medicare-funded residency slots (largely frozen since the Balanced Budget Act of 1997, with only incremental additions). Nursing supply is capped by nursing-school faculty and clinical placements. **[Inference]** This makes clinical labor a genuine single point of failure: a health system cannot expand admissions faster than it can credential and staff nurses and physicians, and it cannot cut wages without losing staff to competitors, so labor holds strong pricing power over the industry. During 2021–2023 that power was acute — agency nurses commanded multiples of staff wages, pushing many hospitals to operating losses. **[Inference]** Labor cost inflation flows almost dollar-for-dollar into hospital cost because reimbursement rates (especially Medicare) are set administratively and reset slowly, so hospitals cannot pass wage shocks through in real time — the classic margin squeeze of the sector.

Skill scarcity is uneven: primary-care physicians, psychiatrists, and bedside RNs are chronically short; administrative/billing labor is abundant. Specialty physicians (anesthesiology, radiology, emergency medicine) have enough scarcity that staffing firms and private equity have rolled them up to capture their bargaining power against hospitals and payers.

---

### 2. Pharmaceuticals and medical-surgical products (pass-through inputs)

For distributors, PBMs, and pharmacies, **the drug itself is the raw material** — and it is the largest dollar flow in the whole system. **[Fact]** The Big Three distributors — McKesson (~$309B FY2024 revenue, ~37% share), Cencora/AmerisourceBergen (~$262B FY2023), Cardinal Health (~$227B FY2024) — together move over 90% of U.S. wholesale drug volume by revenue (IntuitionLabs, 2024 — https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/drug-wholesaler-market-concentration). **[Fact]** Distributor gross margins are razor-thin (McKesson ~3.56%) precisely because the molecule is a pass-through; distributors earn a spread on logistics, not on the drug (Morningstar, 2025 — https://www.morningstar.com/stocks/drug-distribution-industry-trends-2025).

Cost sensitivity here is peculiar: because distributors buy and hold inventory, **generic price deflation and brand list-price inflation both move distributor economics**. Buy-side inflation on brand drugs generates inventory-appreciation profit; generic deflation erodes it. The mix shift toward high-cost specialty and cell/gene therapies raises revenue but not proportional gross profit, so the "margin percent" keeps falling even as dollars grow.

For hospitals, med-surg supplies and drugs are ~10–20% of costs and are procured through **Group Purchasing Organizations (GPOs)** — Vizient (~468,000 staffed beds, ~29% of U.S. beds), Premier (~333,000 beds), HealthTrust (Definitive Healthcare, 2024 — https://www.definitivehc.com/blog/top-10-gpos-by-staffed-beds). **[Inference]** GPOs aggregate hospital demand to claw pricing power back from concentrated manufacturers; they are the industry's structural answer to strong upstream drug/device suppliers.

---

### 3. Reimbursement and regulation as a "raw material"

**[Inference]** The single most important input to Healthcare Services is the administratively-set price. Unlike commodities, this "input" is legislated, not mined. Key price-setters:

- **CMS (Medicare/Medicaid):** sets DRGs (inpatient), OPPS (outpatient), the Physician Fee Schedule, the ESRD bundle for dialysis, and the Clinical Laboratory Fee Schedule. **[Fact]** Government programs are ~56% (Medicare/MA) and 8% (Medicaid) of DaVita's U.S. dialysis revenue, commercial only ~33% but paying far higher rates (DaVita 10-K FY2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/927066/000092706625000012/dva-20241231.htm).
- **PAMA** cut Medicare lab-fee rates, directly compressing LabCorp/Quest margins (AInvest, 2025 — https://www.ainvest.com/news/quest-diagnostics-capture-growth-273b-diagnostic-market-2602/).
- **Medicare Advantage risk-adjustment (V28):** CMS's phase-in from V24 to V28 (2024: 67% V24 / 33% V28) directly changes payer revenue per member (CMS, 2024 — https://www.cms.gov/files/document/report-congress-risk-adjustment-medicare-advantage-december-2024.pdf).

**[Inference]** A shock to a reimbursement rule propagates faster and harder than any commodity shock: a 2% Medicare rate cut hits ~40–60% of a provider's revenue base immediately, with no substitute buyer. This is why the sector treats CMS rulemaking the way an airline treats jet-fuel prices.

---

### 4. Financial capital

Healthcare Services is capital-intensive at the provider end and float-driven at the payer end.

- **Providers:** hospitals are asset-heavy (real estate, imaging, ORs). They fund capex through tax-exempt municipal bonds (non-profits, roughly half of U.S. hospitals) and corporate debt / REIT sale-leasebacks (for-profits). **[Inference]** Rising interest rates raise this input's cost directly and depress hospital construction; rating downgrades (Moody's/S&P) tighten access to it.
- **Payers:** insurers run on **float** — premiums collected before claims are paid — plus statutory reserves required by state regulators. Their effective cost of capital is partly the Medical Loss Ratio (MLR) floor. **[Fact]** MA plans must return ≥85% of premium as medical spend (CMS — https://www.cms.gov/medicare/health-drug-plans/medical-loss-ratio); the 2024 MA market ran ~89–90% (Milliman, 2024 — https://www.milliman.com/en/insight/medicare-advantage-organizations-financial-results-for-2024).

Private-equity capital is itself a structural input to physician practices, ambulatory surgery, and dialysis roll-ups.

---

### 5. Software, data, and IP

The industry runs on three information inputs: **electronic health records (EHRs)**, **claims/billing systems**, and **actuarial/risk data**.

- EHR is a near-duopoly input: Epic and Oracle Health (Cerner) dominate large-hospital installs. **[Inference]** This is a supplier with real pricing power — switching costs are enormous (multi-year, nine-figure implementations), so Epic in particular extracts strong economics from health systems.
- For payers and PBMs, the IP is the **formulary, network, and risk-adjustment models** — proprietary and the actual source of margin.
- **[Inference]** Data itself increasingly determines who wins: whoever holds longitudinal patient/claims data (UnitedHealth's Optum, CVS Health) can underwrite risk and steer care better than rivals.

---

### 6. Physical infrastructure, energy, logistics

Hospitals are energy-intensive (24/7 HVAC, sterilization, imaging); energy is a small but non-trivial cost and a resilience risk. Cold-chain logistics matter increasingly for specialty biologics and vaccines — a dependency on refrigerated distribution and, for cell/gene therapy, cryogenic handling. **[Inference]** These are rarely margin-determining except in tail events (a distributor cold-chain failure can destroy a six-figure shipment; a cyberattack on a clearinghouse — e.g., the 2024 Change Healthcare outage — can freeze cash flow system-wide).

---

### 7. Dependencies on outside industries

- **Pharma & Med-Device manufacturers** — upstream suppliers with the strongest pricing power over the system (a sole-source drug or patented device has no substitute).
- **IT/cloud** (AWS/Azure/Google) for payers, PBMs, telehealth.
- **Real estate / Health REITs** (Welltower, Ventas) as landlords to providers.
- **Staffing agencies** as the marginal supplier of scarce labor.
- **Reinsurance** for stop-loss on self-insured employer plans.

---

### 7b. Supplier pricing power — who squeezes whom

**[Inference]** The industry is best understood as a chain of bargaining games, and pricing power alternates stage by stage:

- **Sole-source branded pharma and patented devices** hold the strongest hand — no substitute, so they set price and the rest of the chain absorbs it. This is why the whole system builds counterweights (GPOs, PBM formularies, CMS/IRA price negotiation) specifically to blunt manufacturer power.
- **Clinical labor** holds strong power over providers (Section 1) — a genuine hard constraint that also caps capacity, since you cannot open beds you cannot staff.
- **EHR vendors (Epic)** hold power over hospitals via switching costs.
- **Providers vs. payers** is a local tug-of-war: a must-have regional hospital system out-negotiates payers, while a fragmented specialty practice is a price-taker. This is exactly why physician roll-ups exist — to manufacture bargaining power against payers.
- **PBMs and GPOs** hold power over pharmacies and manufacturers respectively through the ability to grant or withhold formulary/contract access to millions of covered lives.

**[Inference]** The through-line: in this sector, control over **access to covered lives** (formulary placement, network inclusion, capitated membership) is the ultimate source of pricing power — more than any physical input. Whoever aggregates the demand of insured patients extracts rents from every supplier that needs to reach them.

---

### Where the money and the constraints actually sit

**[Inference]** Two inputs determine sector margins and capacity above all others:

1. **Clinical labor** — caps how much care can be delivered and sets ~60% of provider cost. It is the true production bottleneck and holds pricing power over providers.
2. **Reimbursement rate-setting (CMS + commercial contracts)** — the "price" input that providers cannot control and payers only partly control. Payer mix (commercial vs. government) is the dominant swing factor; commercial rates can be 2–4x Medicare for the same service.

Drug cost is enormous in dollars but largely a pass-through, so it drives revenue more than margin for distributors. **[Inference]** The clearest shock-propagation chain: a wage spike or a Medicare rate cut lands directly on provider margin because the buyer is fixed and the cost base is sticky — there is no inventory to run down and no alternate customer. That is precisely why this sector's margins are thin and policy-sensitive rather than commodity-cyclical.

### 8. Complete patient, labor, facility, and payment input ledger

Patients and clinical need are the demand input, but service capacity requires licensed physicians, nurses, pharmacists, therapists, technicians, aides, behavioral-health staff, coders, schedulers and support teams at the right location and shift. Training slots, credentialing, immigration, burnout and housing/transport affect usable labor. Agency labor and telehealth substitute only for selected tasks.

Physical inputs include hospitals/clinics/beds, operating and imaging rooms, labs, pharmacies, ambulances, utilities and backup power, oxygen/medical gases, water, food/laundry, drugs, devices, implants, PPE, diagnostics, blood, sterile supplies, IT/records, telecom and waste handling. These embed pharma, chemicals, polymers, metals, semiconductors, transport and cold-chain markets.

Payment capacity is an input: public programs, commercial insurance, employers, patients, risk-adjustment and charity funding determine what service can be delivered and collected. Contracts, coding, prior authorization, networks, quality reporting and medical-necessity rules turn clinical work into billable output. Licenses, certificates/approvals, accreditation, scope-of-practice, privacy and malpractice coverage gate capacity.

Funding includes operating cash, nonprofit/public appropriations and philanthropy, municipal and taxable bonds, bank/private credit, private equity, grants, academic research and value-based/capitated payments. Wage, drug/supply and reimbursement trends set margin; licensed staff, beds/rooms, equipment, referral networks, authorizations, payer contracts and discharge/post-acute capacity gate throughput. Prevention, outpatient/home care, telehealth and retail clinics substitute for higher-acuity sites only where clinical risk and payment allow.

## 3. Market Landscape


The U.S. is the anchor market: **[Fact]** national health expenditure reached ~$4.9 trillion in 2023, 17.6% of GDP, up 7.5% year-over-year; physician & clinical services alone were $978.0B (20% of the total) and hospital spending grew 10.4% (CMS / Health Affairs, 2024 — https://www.cms.gov/data-research/statistics-trends-and-reports/national-health-expenditure-data/nhe-fact-sheet). This is the largest single-industry spend in the world's largest economy, and its structure is unlike any other country's because payment is fragmented across government and private insurers rather than a single national payer.

---

### 1. The value chain, stage by stage

**Financing / risk-bearing (payers):** government programs (Medicare, Medicaid, VA/TRICARE) and private insurers. **[Fact]** Medicare Advantage — private plans administering Medicare — reached 32.8M enrollees, ~54% of eligible beneficiaries in 2024 (KFF/AJMC, 2024 — https://www.kff.org/medicare/medicare-advantage-in-2024-enrollment-update-and-key-trends/). Leaders: UnitedHealthcare, Humana, Elevance, CVS/Aetna, Cigna, Centene, plus non-profit Blues.

**Pharmacy benefit management (PBMs):** **[Fact]** the Big 3 — CVS Caremark (~27% of claims), Express Scripts/Cigna (~30%), OptumRx/UnitedHealth (~23%) — controlled ~80% of U.S. prescription claims in 2024 (Drug Channels, 2025 — https://www.drugchannels.net/2025/03/the-top-pharmacy-benefit-managers-of.html). All three are owned by or paired with a top insurer — the defining vertical integration of the sector.

**Distribution:** **[Fact]** McKesson, Cencora, Cardinal Health move >90% of drugs by revenue (IntuitionLabs, 2024 — https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/drug-wholesaler-market-concentration).

**Care delivery (providers):** hospitals/health systems (for-profit HCA, Tenet, Community Health Systems; large non-profits Kaiser, Ascension, CommonSpirit, Advocate Health), physician groups (increasingly owned by Optum, private equity, or hospitals), ambulatory surgery centers, dialysis (DaVita, Fresenius), labs (LabCorp, Quest), post-acute and home health.

**Purchasing / enablement:** GPOs (Vizient ~29% of beds, Premier, HealthTrust), revenue-cycle firms, EHR vendors (Epic, Oracle Health), clearinghouses (Change Healthcare/Optum).

**Regulators:** CMS (rate-setter and largest customer), FDA (upstream), state insurance commissioners, state Certificate-of-Need boards, FTC and DOJ Antitrust (consolidation and PBM conduct), HHS-OIG (fraud).

**Customers, precisely defined.** **[Inference]** The industry has a split customer: the **patient** consumes the service but the **payer** pays and therefore drives most behavior. For providers, the real customers are the payers whose contracts set the rates; for PBMs and insurers, the customers are **employers and government (CMS/states)** who sponsor the coverage; for distributors, the customers are pharmacies and providers. This split is why "consumer" branding matters far less here than in most industries — satisfying the payer/plan-sponsor, not delighting the patient, is what determines revenue. It also explains the sector's weak price signals: the end user rarely sees or bears the true price, so demand is inelastic and utilization, not price competition, drives volume.

---

### 2. Where profits accrue vs. where they are competed away

**[Inference]** Profit in U.S. healthcare accrues to whoever sits closest to the **information and the risk-adjustment lever**, and to whoever holds a **local network monopoly** — not to whoever touches the patient.

- **Highest, most durable profit:** integrated payer-PBM-care platforms (UnitedHealth/Optum) that capture the insurance margin, the PBM spread/rebate, and the care-delivery margin on the same dollar. **[Fact]** Optum's ~$253B revenue and ~7.4% Optum Health margin sit inside a $400.3B group and grow faster than the insurance arm (UnitedHealth 2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/731766/000073176625000022/a2024q4exhibit991.htm).
- **High, locally-monopolistic profit:** dominant regional hospital systems that can force commercial payers to accept high rates because employers need them in-network. Commercial cross-subsidy is the whole game (dialysis: ~33% commercial patients fund nearly all profit — DaVita 10-K FY2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/927066/000092706625000012/dva-20241231.htm).
- **Competed away:** distribution (~1% operating margin), commodity lab testing (PAMA-squeezed), and any FFS provider that is a price-taker to both CMS and dominant payers. **[Inference]** These are volume/scale businesses where margin is thin by structure and pricing power sits upstream (manufacturers) or with the payer.

---

### 3. Regional clusters and why they exist

**[Inference]** Care delivery is inherently local — you cannot offshore an emergency room — so "clusters" are about corporate HQ, insurance concentration, and innovation hubs rather than production geography:

- **Payer/PBM HQ concentration:** Minnesota (UnitedHealth), Connecticut (Cigna/Aetna legacy), Louisville KY (Humana), St. Louis (Express Scripts/Centene), Indianapolis (Elevance).
- **For-profit hospital cluster:** Nashville, TN — HCA, CHS, and a dense ecosystem of health-services companies and investors ("the Nashville health-care cluster").
- **Medicare Advantage geography:** **[Fact]** penetration exceeds 60% in states like AL, CT, MI, FL, ME, RI, and hits ~95% in Puerto Rico (KFF, 2024) — so MA-focused providers concentrate in high-penetration Sun Belt markets.
- **Digital-health / venture:** San Francisco, Boston, New York for capital and talent.

---

### 4. Trade, national security, and industrial policy

**[Inference]** Healthcare Services is overwhelmingly domestic and non-tradable — the service is consumed where produced — so classic import/export exposure is limited. The real cross-border dependencies sit upstream: **active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) and generics** heavily sourced from China and India, **medical devices and PPE** from Asia, and **cross-border clinical labor** (foreign-trained nurses and physicians filling shortages via visa pathways). **[Inference]** National-security concerns are therefore about supply-chain resilience (drug/API shortages, PPE during COVID), health-data security (payer/EHR breaches, foreign access to patient data), and cyber resilience of clearinghouses. Industrial policy operates chiefly through CMS reimbursement (the largest lever any government has over this industry), the Inflation Reduction Act's Medicare drug-price negotiation (upstream, but it reshapes PBM rebate economics), and antitrust posture toward consolidation and PBMs.

---

### 5. Regulatory and political pressure reshaping the map

**[Fact]** The FTC filed a 2024 administrative complaint against the three largest PBMs over insulin rebate practices, and reached a proposed consent settlement with Express Scripts in early 2026 (FTC, 2024 — https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/07/ftc-releases-interim-staff-report-prescription-drug-middlemen; Goodwin, 2026 — https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/02/alerts-lifesciences-antc-express-scripts-settles-pbm-ftc-action). **[Fact]** CMS's V28 risk-adjustment overhaul (phasing 2024–2026) directly cuts MA plan revenue by curbing aggressive diagnosis coding (CMS, 2024 — https://www.cms.gov/files/document/report-congress-risk-adjustment-medicare-advantage-december-2024.pdf). **[Inference]** Both attack the two richest profit pools in the sector — PBM opacity and MA coding intensity — and mark the biggest regulatory risk to the vertically-integrated model.

---

### 6. What is gaining vs. losing relevance

**Gaining:**
- **Vertical integration / "payviders."** Payers buying providers and PBMs (UnitedHealth/Optum the template; CVS buying Oak Street and Signify; Cigna/Evernorth). **[Inference]** This is where value is migrating — controlling the medical-cost dollar end-to-end.
- **Site-of-care shift to ambulatory/home.** ASCs, hospital-at-home, home dialysis, and retail/virtual primary care erode hospital inpatient volume.
- **Value-based / capitated primary care** for seniors (the target of the MA money).
- **AI in the revenue cycle, coding, prior authorization, and clinical documentation** — genuine cost-out where labor is ~60% of provider cost.

**Losing / under pressure:**
- **Standalone community hospitals** — squeezed between labor inflation, government payer mix, and dominant systems; many merging or closing.
- **Commodity clinical labs** — PAMA rate cuts and hospital-lab competition.
- **Opaque PBM spread/rebate economics** — regulatory target; transparent pass-through models (and disruptors like Mark Cuban's Cost Plus Drugs) chip at the model.
- **Retail-clinic experiments that lacked a risk model** — several large retailers retreated from primary care after failing to make the unit economics work.

---

### 7. Real progress vs. promotional claims

**[Inference]** Distinguishing signal from noise:
- **Real:** the migration of surgery to ASCs, home-dialysis penetration, and AI-driven administrative cost reduction have measurable unit-economic backing. Vertical integration's ability to capture margin is proven by Optum's disclosed financials.
- **Overstated:** many "digital health" and "AI diagnosis" claims are promotional — they add a thin software layer without bending total cost of care, and most have not shown they can beat capitation math at scale. **[Inference]** The tell is whether a model takes **risk** (capitation, at-risk contracts) or merely bills FFS with a tech veneer; only the former demonstrates it actually lowers cost.

---

### 8. Who is set to gain or lose

**[Inference]**
- **Gain:** integrated payer-provider platforms with proprietary data (UnitedHealth/Optum, and to a lesser degree CVS/Aetna, Humana/CenterWell, Cigna/Evernorth); dominant regional systems with commercial pricing power; ASC and home-based operators; RCM/AI enablers.
- **Lose or de-rate:** pure PBMs exposed to transparency mandates; MA plans over-reliant on coding intensity as V28 bites and MLRs rise (2024 MA MLR ~89–90% — Milliman, 2024 — https://www.milliman.com/en/insight/medicare-advantage-organizations-financial-results-for-2024); commodity labs; and undifferentiated community hospitals.

**[Inference]** Net: economic value is migrating from the point of care and from opaque intermediaries toward **whoever controls the risk-bearing contract and the patient data** — and, at the margin, toward lower-cost care sites. The durable question over the next decade is whether regulators (FTC on PBMs, CMS on MA coding) can force enough of that captured value back to plan sponsors, taxpayers, and patients to erode the integrated-platform advantage.

### 9. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs are diagnosis, treatment, prevention, rehabilitation, long-term support, health information and financial risk management; the desired output is health and functional status, not visits. Infections, errors, avoidable readmissions, clinician burnout, inequitable access and medical waste are negative outputs.

Patient uses care, clinician chooses much of it, payer/employer/government finances, provider delivers, pharmacy/lab/post-acute vendors support, and family caregivers supply unpaid capacity. Fee-for-service, capitation/value-based, public budget, self-pay and charity channels create different incentives. Payers can be customers of provider networks while also suppliers of reimbursement.

Care is local because emergencies, licenses and patient travel are local; specialty centers and telehealth extend radius. Urban academic systems, community hospitals, ambulatory centers, physician groups, post-acute/home care, behavioral health, pharmacies, labs, insurers, public systems and nonprofits form interdependent referral networks. Consolidation can improve bargaining or integration while raising access/competition concerns.

Funding is mixed public/private at every layer: taxes and social insurance, employer premiums, individual premiums/out-of-pocket, bonds, philanthropy, research grants, private equity and corporate capital. Policy covers benefit and payment rules, provider licensing/scope, facility approval, quality and price transparency, privacy/interoperability, antitrust, fraud/abuse, labor, emergency care, drug/device procurement, public-health powers and tax-exempt obligations.

Healthcare services interacts with pharma/devices, insurance, real estate, labor, food, transport and technology. Aging and chronic disease raise demand; staffing and housing cap supply; payer rate changes reallocate margin; drug/device innovation may shift site of care; hospital discharge depends on nursing/home capacity; recession changes insurance mix and elective use rather than eliminating clinical need.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


The industry's economics are governed by one structural fact: **the person receiving the service is almost never the person paying for it.** Care is produced by a provider, paid by a third party (government or insurer), and the flows between them — claims, rebates, risk adjustment, cross-subsidies — are where nearly all the profit and loss live. Below: the workflows, unit economics, KPIs, and valuation methods across the main sub-sectors.

---

### 1. The core service workflows

**Provider (hospital / physician) revenue cycle** — the "production line":
1. Patient encounter → clinical documentation (in the EHR).
2. **Coding**: diagnoses (ICD-10) and procedures (CPT/HCPCS) are translated into billable codes. For inpatients, codes map to a **DRG** (Diagnosis-Related Group) that determines a fixed Medicare payment regardless of actual cost.
3. **Claim submission** to the payer via a clearinghouse.
4. **Adjudication**: the payer applies the contracted rate, checks medical necessity, and may deny or downcode.
5. **Payment + patient responsibility** (deductible/coinsurance) collection.

**[Inference]** The revenue cycle is itself a technology and a battleground: denials management, prior authorization, and coding optimization determine whether a hospital collects 95% or 88% of what it bills. This is why revenue-cycle software and outsourcing (Optum, R1 RCM) is a distinct profit pool.

**Payer (insurer) workflow**: price the risk (actuarial/underwriting) → collect premium → build provider networks and negotiate rates → adjudicate and pay claims → manage medical cost (utilization management, care management). Profit = premium − medical cost − admin, subject to the MLR floor.

**PBM workflow**: negotiate formulary placement with manufacturers (extracting rebates) → build pharmacy networks → adjudicate pharmacy claims in real time → reconcile rebates. **[Fact]** PBMs earn on **spread pricing** (billing the plan more than they reimburse the pharmacy) and on retained rebates; the FTC found the Big 3 generated ~$1.4B from spread on certain drugs (FTC, 2024 — https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/07/ftc-releases-interim-staff-report-prescription-drug-middlemen).

---

### 2. Competing methods — and why players choose differently

**Fee-for-service (FFS) vs. capitation / value-based care (VBC).** Under FFS a provider is paid per unit of activity — more volume, more revenue. Under capitation a provider receives a fixed per-member-per-month (PMPM) sum and keeps the difference if it spends less. **[Inference]** The trade-off: FFS rewards throughput and is simple to operate but drives overutilization and is what payers are trying to escape; capitation rewards keeping people healthy but transfers actuarial risk to the provider, who can be bankrupted by a few catastrophic cases. **A player chooses capitation when it has the data, scale, and primary-care footprint to manage total cost of care** — exactly UnitedHealth Optum Health's and Humana CenterWell's thesis. It chooses FFS when it lacks that infrastructure or operates high-acuity specialties where risk is hard to price.

**Vertical integration vs. focus.** UnitedHealth pairs an insurer (UnitedHealthcare) with a care-delivery/services arm (Optum), so medical spend paid by one segment becomes revenue for another. **[Fact]** Optum generated ~$253B of 2024 revenue inside a $400.3B company (UnitedHealth, 2024 results — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/731766/000073176625000022/a2024q4exhibit991.htm). **[Inference]** The integrated model wins when it can redirect the 85–90% MLR dollars into owned assets and capture that margin twice; the focused model (a pure hospital operator) wins on operational depth but is a price-taker against integrated payers.

**Inpatient vs. ambulatory site-of-care.** Care is migrating from hospitals to ambulatory surgery centers (ASCs), retail clinics, and the home. ASCs perform the same procedure at lower cost and are chosen by payers to cut spend and by physician-owners to capture facility fees. **[Inference]** This is the single largest ongoing shift in provider mechanics — value migrates to whoever owns the low-cost site.

---

### 3. Asset types and their economics

- **Hospitals**: high fixed cost, high operating leverage. A bed is only profitable above a utilization threshold; the marginal admission carries very high contribution margin once fixed costs are covered, which is why census swings earnings violently.
- **Dialysis centers**: standardized, replicable units. **[Fact]** DaVita and Fresenius control ~80% of U.S. facilities and ~90% of revenue (Mordor Intelligence, 2024 — https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/us-dialysis-services-market). The model runs on a **commercial cross-subsidy**: Medicare pays a fixed bundle roughly at or below cost, while commercial payers (~33% of DaVita revenue) pay multiples of Medicare and generate essentially all the profit (DaVita 10-K FY2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/927066/000092706625000012/dva-20241231.htm).
- **Labs**: high-throughput, automation-heavy, fixed-cost-dominated → scale is decisive. **[Fact]** LabCorp (~$13.9B) and Quest (~$9.9B) 2024 revenue, each only ~10–15% of a fragmented market where hospital labs hold most volume (AInvest, 2025 — https://www.ainvest.com/news/quest-diagnostics-capture-growth-273b-diagnostic-market-2602/).
- **PBMs / distributors**: asset-light, working-capital-intensive, thin-margin, scale-driven.
- **Insurers**: balance-sheet businesses running on float and statutory reserves.

---

### 4. Unit economics and the cost stack

**Hospital (marginal-unit logic):** Revenue per case = DRG/contracted rate. Costs: ~56–60% labor, ~15% supplies/drugs, remainder facilities/overhead (AHA, 2025 — https://www.aha.org/system/files/media/file/2025/04/The-Cost-of-Caring-April-2025.pdf). **[Inference]** Because ~60% of cost is labor and much of it is fixed staffing, the marginal patient (given open capacity) is highly profitable, but a hospital below break-even census loses money fast — the definition of operating leverage.

**Insurer:** For every $1 of premium, ~$0.85–0.90 goes to medical claims (the MLR), ~$0.08–0.12 to admin, leaving low-single-digit net margin. **[Fact]** UnitedHealth's consolidated medical care ratio was 85.5% in 2024 vs 83.2% in 2023 — the ~230bp rise (Medicare funding cuts + higher utilization) is enormous at insurer scale (UnitedHealth 2024 — same SEC exhibit). **[Inference]** Insurers are a spread business on a huge notional: tiny MLR moves swamp everything else.

**Distributor:** ~3–4% gross margin, ~1% operating margin — a logistics spread on ~$300B of throughput (McKesson; Morningstar, 2025 — https://www.morningstar.com/stocks/drug-distribution-industry-trends-2025). **[Inference]** Pennies per pill × billions of pills; near-invulnerable to competition (scale + supplier ties) but structurally low-margin.

---

### 5. KPIs practitioners actually track

- **Hospitals**: occupancy/census, adjusted admissions, case-mix index (CMI), length of stay (LOS), revenue per adjusted admission, **payer mix** (commercial %), denial rate, EBITDA margin, cost per adjusted discharge.
- **Insurers**: MLR / medical care ratio, membership growth, premium PMPM, **Star Ratings** (drive MA bonus payments), SG&A ratio, days-claims-payable.
- **VBC / capitated groups**: PMPM cost, MER (medical expense ratio), risk-adjustment factor (RAF) accuracy, admissions per 1,000 members.
- **PBMs**: scripts adjudicated, generic dispensing rate, rebate retention, spread.
- **Dialysis / labs**: revenue per treatment / per requisition, treatments per day, commercial mix.

---

### 6. Timelines and characteristic failure points

Development lead times: a new hospital tower is 3–5+ years and needs a state Certificate of Need in many states; an ASC or clinic, 12–24 months; a value-based contract ramps over 2–3 years before it de-risks. **[Inference]** Characteristic failure modes: (1) **utilization shocks** — a bad flu season, or conversely deferred care, blows up insurer MLR; (2) **rate cuts** — a CMS change strands a provider's cost base; (3) **payer-mix deterioration** — Medicaid redetermination or an MA shift replaces high-paying commercial patients with low-paying government ones; (4) **denials/regulatory** — coding audits (RADV for MA; DOJ False Claims Act) claw back revenue retroactively; (5) **contract loss** — a PBM losing a large client (e.g., a ~20M-member book moving between Caremark and Express Scripts) reshapes share overnight; (6) **cyber/clearinghouse outage** — the 2024 Change Healthcare attack froze provider cash flow nationally.

---

### 7. Valuation across life-stages

#### (a) Mature, cash-generative businesses (managed care, distributors, large labs)
Valued on **P/E and EV/EBITDA on stable forward earnings**, plus free-cash-flow yield. **[Fact]** Median healthcare-services EV/EBITDA moderated to ~11.5x in 2025–26 from ~14.5x in 2024 (FOCUS, 2026 — https://focusbankers.com/healthcare-ebitda-multiples/). Insurers trade on P/E and on **medical-cost-trend visibility** — the market pays up when MLR is predictable and punishes trend surprises. Distributors trade on EPS growth and capital return, not multiple expansion (too low-margin to re-rate).

#### (b) Cyclical / asset-heavy providers (hospitals) across the cycle
Valued on **EV/EBITDA**, but the multiple is **payer-mix- and cycle-dependent**. **[Fact]** Stable hospital multiples compressed ~8.5x → ~7.9x recently (FOCUS, 2026). **[Fact]** Payer mix is the dominant driver: a ~75% commercial platform can trade 14–16x EBITDA while a ~55% government-mix platform trades 8–10x (FOCUS / IB guide, 2025 — https://focusbankers.com/valuation-multiples-by-industry/). **[Inference]** Across the cycle, value hospitals on **normalized (mid-cycle) EBITDA and per-bed / per-adjusted-admission benchmarks**, not peak or trough — census, labor cost, and mix all mean-revert. At trough census the same asset can show negative EBITDA, so operating leverage must be modeled explicitly.

#### (c) Pre-revenue / early businesses (digital health, VBC start-ups, care roll-ups)
No earnings to capitalize, so value rests on **lives under management, addressable lives, contracted PMPM, and a probability-adjusted path to positive medical margin**. Methods: **revenue multiples** (EV/Sales), **per-member / per-life value**, and **DCF with explicit scenario-weighting** on whether the group can drive MLR below its capitation rate. **[Inference]** For a capitated care company the entire value is the option that it will manage total cost of care below its fixed payment at scale — analytically like a milestone-driven asset: mature-state economics (target PMPM margin × lives) × probability of reaching it, discounted back. For roll-ups, value is the **arbitrage** between the entry multiple on small clinics (~5–7x) and the exit multiple on the platform (~10–14x), plus synergy capture — so the KPI is de-novo/acquisition unit economics and integration pace, not current EBITDA.

**[Inference]** A cross-cutting valuation rule for the whole sector: **discount government-heavy, regulation-exposed cash flows and pay up for durable commercial revenue and structural cost control.** The same dollar of EBITDA is worth far more from a commercially-insured, hard-to-disintermediate service than from a Medicare-rate-taking one.

### 8. Complete episode-to-payment and capacity mechanics

The workflow is access/triage → eligibility and authorization → diagnose → plan and deliver care → document/code → discharge or transition → submit/adjust claim or risk payment → collect → follow outcomes and readmissions. Clinical, operational and billing workflows must align; undocumented or denied care can consume full capacity without revenue.

Revenue models include fee schedule/DRG or case rate, per diem, capitation, shared savings/risk, premium, pharmacy spread/rebate, subscription and self-pay. Gross charges are not price. Payer mix, acuity/risk adjustment, coding, contract rates, denials and bad debt turn volume into net revenue. Under capitation, unused capacity and prevention can be valuable; under fee-for-service, utilization drives revenue.

Capacity is staffed and equipped capacity: licensed beds without nurses, ORs without anesthesia, scanners without technicians or discharge without post-acute slots are unavailable. Working capital includes claims receivable, denials/appeals, risk settlements, drug/supply inventory, payroll and provider accruals. Capital includes facilities/equipment/IT, maintenance and strategic expansion; lease and joint-venture structures can obscure obligations.

Track visits/admissions/procedures/member months, occupancy and staffed capacity, length of stay, acuity/case mix, payer mix and rate, labor hours and agency use, supply/drug cost, denials and days in A/R, medical loss/cost ratio for risk holders, quality/outcomes, readmissions, infections, referrals/leakage and capex. Stress epidemic/surge, staff shortage, payer cut or mix shift, drug/device spike, cyber outage, authorization change, recession and site-of-care migration.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Provider contribution = net reimbursement per case/visit/member − clinical labor − drugs/supplies − variable facility/claims cost. Enterprise cash adjusts denials, receivables, risk settlements, leases, maintenance and growth capex.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Occupancy | roughly 60–85% for many hospitals; local/service-specific | Capacity and throughput |
| Length of stay | days risk/acuity adjusted | Bed productivity |
| Labor share | often ~50%+ of provider operating expense | Primary cost |
| Payer mix | percent Medicare/Medicaid/commercial/self-pay | Net rate |
| Denial rate | percent of claims/value denied | Revenue-cycle leakage |
| Days in A/R | often ~35–60+ days | Cash conversion |
| Medical-cost ratio | often ~80–90%+ by insurance product/rule | Risk margin |
| Agency labor | percent of clinical hours/cost | Staffing stress |
| Readmission/quality | risk-adjusted percent | Outcome and penalty |
| Capex/bed or service unit | maintenance and growth basis | Asset intensity |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Gross charges are not price. Reconcile encounters to coding, allowed amount, contractual adjustments, denials, claims, risk adjustment, bad debt and cash. Separate provider and insurer revenue, intercompany eliminations, medical claims reserves, leases and restricted philanthropy.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Volume, acuity, payer mix/rate, coding, authorization and quality.
- Wage/agency staffing, drugs/devices/supplies, occupancy and length of stay.
- Member risk, medical trend, network price, utilization and risk adjustment.
- Cyber, malpractice, regulation, site-of-care, referral and public funding.

### Valuation discipline

Use service-line/provider unit economics, payer contracts, quality, staffing, local share and capital. Risk-bearing models need claims reserves/capital; nonprofits/public systems require debt and community obligations.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1983 Medicare DRG adoption | Inpatient payment shifted toward prospective case rates | Length of stay and hospital operations changed | Payment unit changes behavior |
| 1990s managed-care expansion | Payers used networks and utilization controls | Provider/payer bargaining intensified | Who bears utilization risk matters |
| 2000s–2010s outpatient migration | Technology/payment moved procedures from hospitals | Sites and capital shifted | Care setting is an economic variable |
| 2020–2022 pandemic | Surges, elective deferral and public support hit simultaneously | Staffing and case mix changed cash and capacity | Licensed beds are not staffed beds |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Staffed capacity, labor/agency, admissions/visits, authorization, payer rates, claims/denials, medical trend, referrals and cyber uptime.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Volume growth without staffing/collections, premium growth below medical trend, or bed count without staffed and discharge capacity.
- **Durable lesson:** Care economics sit at the intersection of clinical pathway, staffed capacity, payment rule and cash collection.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
