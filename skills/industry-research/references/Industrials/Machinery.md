## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Construction, mining, agricultural and adjacent industrial machinery, power systems, attachments, parts, rental, remanufacturing, software and captive finance.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Construction and compact equipment | Excavators, loaders, dozers, skid steers and road equipment | units, operating hours | Weight class, hydraulics, dealer and application |
| Mining and large equipment | Haul trucks, shovels, drills and processing support | units, payload, availability | Long life, tires, site integration and service |
| Agricultural machinery | Tractors, combines, implements and precision systems | units, horsepower, acres | Crop income, season, dealer and software |
| Engines, power and components | Diesel/electric powertrains, hydraulics and driveline | engines, kW/hp, components | Emissions, certification, integration and merchant/owned |
| Aftermarket, rental and finance | Parts, service, reman, rental, telematics, loans and leases | installed base, machine hours, receivables | Utilization, residuals, dealer and funding |

### Specifications that change value

- State weight/horsepower/payload class, duty cycle, attachments and emissions market.
- Capacity means completed, tested machines with engines/hydraulics/chips/tires and dealer setup.
- Retail sales, dealer orders, OEM shipments and registrations are separate.
- Installed-base economics need age, utilization, warranty, captive versus independent service and parts content.
- Captive finance requires loan/lease mix, funding, credit loss and residual assumptions.

### Role map

Supplier provides metal/components; OEM designs/assembles; dealer inventories/sells/services; rental fleet buys and rents; captive/third-party lender finances; operator uses; project/farm/mine owner funds utilization; regulator certifies.

### Terms that must be explicit

- retail sales versus shipment
- dealer inventory and floorplan
- price realization versus mix
- installed base and parts absorption
- finance penetration and residual value


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: the "yellow-iron" and adjacent equipment makers — Caterpillar, Komatsu, Deere (construction & ag), CNH, Volvo CE, Liebherr, Hitachi CM, and the Chinese majors XCMG/SANY/Zoomlion — plus their upstream supply base. This file maps what these OEMs consume and where the leverage over their cost and capacity actually sits.

The industry is fundamentally an **assembly-and-integration** business bolted onto a heavy metalworking core. An excavator or haul truck is a structural steel body + a diesel/electric powertrain + a hydraulic system + electronics/controls, joined by castings, forgings, bearings, and fasteners. The OEM's own value-add is engineering, machining of critical parts, final assembly, the dealer channel, and software. That structure dictates every input dependency below.

### 1. Raw materials — steel is the master variable

**[Fact]** Steel and iron dominate the bill of materials. Caterpillar describes its inputs as "unformed materials [that] include a variety of steel products… cut or formed to shape and machined" plus "steel and iron castings and forgings" (Caterpillar 10-K FY2018, 2019 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/18230/000001823019000034/cat_10-kx12312018.htm). A large machine is 60-75% steel by mass **[Estimate]**, so a move in flat/plate/bar steel and in scrap (which drives cast/forged parts) flows almost linearly into COGS.

**[Fact]** Steel-price and tariff shocks are material, not cosmetic. Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum drove Caterpillar to guide a **net $1.5-1.8 billion** tariff hit for 2025, pushing full-year adjusted operating margin to the bottom of its target range (Detroit News / MINING.com, 2025 — https://www.mining.com/web/caterpillar-sees-tariff-impact-of-up-to-1-8-billion-this-year/). Against ~$65 B of revenue that is ~250-280 bps of margin from one input category. Earlier, the 2018 tariff round alone added ~$110 M of material cost (Caterpillar 10-K FY2018).

**[Inference]** Because steel is a globally traded, exchange-priced commodity, **the OEM has essentially no pricing power over the input** — pricing power sits with integrated mills (in the US: Nucor, Cleveland-Cliffs, U.S. Steel) and, upstream of them, iron-ore and metallurgical-coal producers and the scrap market. OEMs defend margin only through hedging, multi-year supply contracts, dual-sourcing, and — the real lever — **passing cost through in list price** ("price realization"). Caterpillar offset 2024 volume declines with **+$1.238 B of favorable price realization** (Caterpillar 10-K FY2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/18230/000001823025000008/cat-20241231.htm), which is exactly this pass-through mechanism working.

Other structural materials: aluminum (cabs, radiators, some structures), copper (wiring, motors — rising with electrification), rubber (tires, hoses, seals — see below), and, increasingly, lithium/nickel/graphite for battery-electric machines. Copper and battery materials are the inputs whose importance is *growing* as the powertrain electrifies.

### 2. Purchased major components — where single points of failure live

The OEM buys, rather than makes, several high-value subsystems. This is where supply bottlenecks bite hardest because substitution is slow and qualification is costly.

**Engines / powertrain.** Caterpillar, Komatsu, Deere and Liebherr make many of their own diesel engines (a deliberate vertical-integration moat). But a large slice of the industry — compact equipment makers, many Chinese and European OEMs, and specific model lines — buys engines from **Cummins** (2024 revenue $34.1 B; its Engine segment builds 2.8-15 L, 48-715 hp units for exactly the off-highway market — Cummins 2024 results / 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/26172/000002617225000007/cmi-20241231.htm), plus Deutz, Yanmar, Kubota (dominant in sub-25 hp/compact), Perkins (a Caterpillar brand sold externally), and FPT (CNH). **[Inference]** For a non-integrated OEM the engine is the single most valuable purchased part and the one most exposed to emissions-regulation timing; Cummins/Deutz hold real pricing power here because switching engine suppliers means re-certifying the whole machine to Tier 4 Final / Stage V.

**Hydraulics — the second critical subsystem.** Pumps, motors, valves and cylinders convert engine power to digging/lifting force; on an excavator hydraulics can be 20-30% of machine cost **[Estimate]**. The merchant market is concentrated: **Bosch Rexroth (Germany), Danfoss (Denmark, which absorbed Eaton's hydraulics), Kawasaki Heavy Industries and KYB (Japan), and Parker Hannifin (US)** are the named leaders; the top players hold a combined global share "over 60%" per MarketsandMarkets (2025 — https://www.marketsandmarkets.com/ResearchInsight/hydraulics-market.asp). Kawasaki/KYB in particular are near-sole-source for the high-pressure pumps on large Japanese-design excavators. **[Inference]** This concentration is a genuine single point of failure: a hydraulics-supplier disruption caps excavator output industry-wide, and these suppliers hold pricing power over all but the vertically integrated giants.

**Bearings, gears, drivetrain:** SKF, Schaeffler, Timken, NSK (bearings); Dana, ZF, Carraro (axles/transmissions). **Electronics/controls:** displays, ECUs, sensors, telematics modules, and — the 2021-22 chokepoint — **semiconductors**. The chip shortage physically capped machine output for two years; easing of it in 2023-24 is precisely what let dealer backlogs clear into surplus (BOK Financial, 2024 — https://thestatement.bokf.com/articles/2024/02/heavy-equipment-industry-confronts-inventory-bulge). **Tires:** large off-the-road (OTR) radials from Michelin, Bridgestone, Titan are periodically supply-constrained and are a recurring bottleneck for mining haul trucks specifically.

### 3. Castings, forgings & machining capacity

Structural and drivetrain parts start as castings/forgings, then are machined to spec. This is capital-intensive, energy-intensive foundry/forge work that OEMs increasingly outsource to specialist suppliers (many in China, India, and Eastern Europe). **[Inference]** Foundry capacity and skilled machining are a quiet capacity constraint: they are dirty, energy-hungry, and hard to permit/expand quickly, so a demand upcycle runs into casting/forging lead times before it runs into OEM assembly capacity.

### 4. Labor — skilled, aging, scarce

**[Fact]** The binding labor input is mid-skill trades: machinists, welders, assemblers, maintenance techs. BLS counts ~299,500 machinists in the US with ~34,200 openings/year through 2034 — openings driven by **retirement, not growth** (Trade Colleges citing BLS OOH, 2025-26 — https://tradecolleges.org/blog/trade-programs/cnc-machining-career-opportunities). NAM/Deloitte project up to **2.1 million unfilled US manufacturing jobs by 2030** (AMTEC summary of NAM/Deloitte — https://www.amtec.us.com/blog/manufacturing-workforce-report). **[Inference]** Labor scarcity does two things: it raises unit cost (wage premiums for machinists/welders) and it is a *structural argument for automation* — both factory automation and the autonomous/electric product roadmap. Engineering labor (controls, software, AI/autonomy) is a second, faster-growing scarcity as the product becomes software-defined.

### 5. Software, IP & data

Modern machines are "software-defined." Telematics (Cat VisionLink, Komatsu Komtrax, Deere Operations Center), autonomy stacks, emissions-control software, and precision-ag algorithms are now core IP. **[Fact]** Deere runs software gross margins ~85% and an ecosystem of >1 M connected machines / 500 M acres (precision-ag disclosures, 2024 — https://www.deere.com/en/news/all-news/see-spray-herbicide-savings/). Upstream software dependencies: chip/compute (NVIDIA-class GPUs for autonomy dev), GNSS/RTK positioning, and cloud infrastructure. **[Inference]** This is the input where value is *migrating in-house* — OEMs are insourcing software and buying startups because it is becoming the differentiator and the recurring-revenue engine.

### 6. Energy & logistics

Manufacturing is energy-intensive (foundries, heat-treat, paint, machining), so electricity and natural-gas prices flow into cost — though for OEMs energy is a smaller line than steel. **[Inference]** More important is **inbound/outbound logistics**: machines are heavy, low-density, and expensive to ship. Ocean freight, rail, and heavy-haul trucking costs, plus port throughput, materially affect landed cost and were a visible 2021-22 cost driver (Caterpillar cited "higher freight" repeatedly in that period's 10-Ks). This is why OEMs regionalize production ("build-where-you-sell") — it hedges both freight and tariffs.

### 7. Financial capital — the captive-finance dependency

**[Fact]** Equipment is bought on credit, so a captive lender is a structural input, not a sideline. Cat Financial's Financial Products segment posted ~$4.05 B revenue in 2024 (Caterpillar 10-K FY2024). **John Deere Financial is the largest captive equipment-finance company in the US** and second-largest overall behind Bank of America Global Leasing (FIG IB Guide — https://ibinterviewquestions.com/guides/fig-investment-banking/captive-finance-companies). **[Inference]** Interest rates are therefore a demand-side input: high rates raise the customer's monthly payment *and* the dealer's floor-plan carrying cost, which is exactly why 2023-24's high rates turned cleared backlogs into a costly dealer inventory glut. The captive arm's own input is wholesale funding (its credit rating / cost of debt).

### 8. Regulation as an input

Emissions standards (US EPA Tier 4 Final, EU Stage V, China IV) dictate engine/aftertreatment content and R&D timing; they are a cost input and a moat (they favor scaled players who can amortize certification). Safety (ROPS/FOPS roll-over and falling-object protection, ISO standards) and increasingly carbon/electrification mandates shape product. Trade regulation — Section 232 steel tariffs, and tariffs on Chinese equipment/components — is now a first-order cost input (see §1).

### 9. How a shock propagates (worked example)

A steel-price spike (say +25%): raw steel and, with a lag, purchased castings/forgings/fabrications rise → machine COGS rises several points → OEM tries to recover via list-price increases ("price realization"), which succeeds *if* demand is firm but compresses margin if demand is soft → simultaneously, if the spike is tariff-driven, non-tariff Chinese OEMs gain relative cost advantage in third markets. A **hydraulics or chip disruption** propagates differently: it doesn't raise unit cost much but **hard-caps unit output**, converting a revenue problem into lost sales and lengthening backlogs — the 2021-22 pattern. **[Inference]** The general rule: *commodity* input shocks hit margin; *component/capacity* input shocks hit volume.

### Which inputs set margins vs. cap capacity

- **Set margins:** steel/tariffs, freight, labor wage rates, and — offsetting — pricing power / aftermarket-parts mix.
- **Cap capacity:** hydraulics (Kawasaki/KYB/Rexroth), engines for non-integrated OEMs (Cummins/Deutz), semiconductors, castings/forgings, OTR tires, and skilled labor.

That split is the single most useful thing to carry forward: this industry's margin story is a *steel-and-price-realization* story, while its capacity story is a *components-and-skilled-labor* story.

### 10. Completeness addendum: secondary inputs and substitutions

Beyond the primary steel, hydraulics and powertrain chain, full coverage includes foundry sand and binders, coke and ferroalloys, cutting tools, lubricants/coolants, paints and solvents, welding gases, fasteners, filters, batteries, glass, cab plastics, seats, HVAC refrigerants, emissions aftertreatment catalysts/urea, wiring copper, electronic-grade materials and packaging. Tire and hose supply traces to natural/synthetic rubber, carbon black, steel cord and petrochemicals. Electric machinery adds cells, motors, inverters, charging and higher copper demand; autonomy adds cameras, radar/lidar, GNSS and compute.

Factories need land, grid and gas service, presses, machine tools, robots, heat treatment, paint booths, testing, waste treatment, cybersecurity and certified quality data. Dealers add inventory lots, service bays, diagnostic tools, technicians, parts warehouses and floorplan finance. Customer operation depends on diesel/electricity, operators, site infrastructure and attachments, so those inputs affect equipment demand and residual values even when the OEM does not buy them.

Funding includes OEM cash/debt/equity, supplier working-capital lines, equipment leases, captive finance and securitization, dealer floorplan, export credit, customer project/agricultural/mining finance, and government incentives for manufacturing or low-emission equipment. Safety, engine emissions, road homologation, right-to-repair, radio/cyber, trade, local-content and product-liability rules affect bill of material and market access.

Capacity constraints should be tested at each tier: raw material can be available while castings, pumps, chips, tires, batteries, transformers, skilled labor, freight or dealer setup is not. Remanufactured parts, rental fleets, used equipment, life extension, multi-brand components and alternative powertrains provide substitutes, but warranty, uptime, emissions certification, software locks and attachment compatibility limit interchangeability.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Value chain

Steel/castings, engines, hydraulics, electronics, and software feed component suppliers and OEM assembly; dealers distribute, finance, service, and resell machines; customers operate equipment in construction, mining, agriculture, logistics, and factories. **[Inference]** The highest lifetime value often sits in proprietary parts, dealer service, software, and captive finance—not the original machine sale.

### 2. Market structure

Heavy machinery categories are oligopolistic because reliability, dealer coverage, residual value, and installed fleets reinforce incumbents. Industrial components are more fragmented but consolidate around specialized motion, automation, and controls. Chinese OEMs use scale and cost to gain share, especially outside North America/Europe.

### 3. Geography and clusters

Production clusters around skilled machining, foundries, suppliers, and end markets: U.S. Midwest/South, Germany/Italy, Japan, Korea, and China. Large machines are expensive to ship, encouraging regional assembly. Mining equipment follows resource regions; agricultural demand follows farm income.

### 4. Profit pools

Proprietary aftermarket parts, dealer labor, telematics subscriptions, precision agriculture, automation, and captive finance carry higher-quality economics. New equipment is cyclical and absorbs working capital; used equipment and rentals influence residual values and new pricing.

### 5. Gaining relevance

Autonomy, electrification where duty cycle permits, machine vision, precision application, predictive maintenance, warehouse/factory automation, and remanufacturing. Software is real when it raises uptime, yield, or labor productivity and customers pay or remain locked into the ecosystem.

### 6. Losing relevance

Undifferentiated mechanical hardware, diesel-only architectures in suitable electrification niches, and dealers without scale/data. **[Inference]** Full autonomy claims should be discounted unless validated in constrained commercial environments with safety and utilization data.

### 7. Value migration

From iron toward software-enabled productivity and lifecycle revenue; from independent components toward integrated electro-hydraulic/control systems; and from one-time sales toward service, rental, and outcome-based offerings. Strong dealer networks remain a moat because physical uptime cannot be delivered digitally.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs include machines, engines/powertrains, attachments, parts, remanufactured components, software/telematics, financing, rental and service. The functional output is productive uptime and cost per ton/acre/hour, not units alone. Scrap, fluids, batteries, tires and end-of-life equipment create recovery and liability streams; used-equipment and auction markets set residual values and new-equipment affordability.

Customers include contractors, miners, farmers, foresters, ports, warehouses, industrial plants, municipalities, militaries, rental fleets and dealers. An operator uses the machine, a fleet manager specifies it, a dealer sells/services it, and a lender or project owner may fund it. Contractors' backlog, crop income, mineral prices and public budgets therefore transmit into OEM orders through different channels.

Production is regionalized around supplier clusters and demand, while components and finished units trade globally. Freight, tariffs, local-content rules, emissions standards and dealer density shape landed competitiveness. Global OEMs, Chinese scaled challengers, specialized manufacturers, dealers, rental companies, independent service shops and software/autonomy firms compete and complement one another.

Private funding includes corporate markets, captive-finance debt and securitization, dealer floorplan, leases, rental-fleet capital and customer borrowing. Public funding and policy enter through export credit, infrastructure, agriculture, mining and defense budgets and manufacturing, emissions or automation incentives. Policy also covers safety, spectrum/data, autonomy, repair access, trade, sanctions, procurement and road/weight rules.

Machinery interacts with steel and metals, semiconductors, energy, construction, mining, agriculture, transport and rates. Rental and used equipment cushion shortages but amplify downturns when fleets dispose assets. Electrification shifts spend from engines/fuel systems to batteries, motors, charging and grid connections; autonomy shifts value to software/data while increasing cybersecurity and liability exposure.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Production workflow

Platform design → supplier/tooling qualification → cast/forge/machine components → engine/powertrain/hydraulic assembly → final assembly → testing → dealer shipment → retail delivery/finance → service, parts, rebuild, and resale.

### 2. Operating models

- **Integrated OEM + dealer:** high control and aftermarket capture; dealer health is critical.
- **Component supplier:** wins through specification, IP, and platform content; risks customer concentration.
- **Rental:** earns utilization × rental rate, with residual value and maintenance central.
- **Automation/controls:** combines hardware, software, and integration; recurring service raises quality.

### 3. Unit economics and cycle

Machine gross margin reflects realized price, material/freight/labor, mix, factory utilization, and warranty. Dealer inventory separates OEM shipments from end demand. When backlogs clear, shipments can remain high while retail demand weakens—creating later production cuts.

### 4. KPIs

- Retail sales versus dealer shipments; dealer inventory months.
- Orders, backlog, book-to-bill, and cancellations.
- Price realization versus material/freight cost.
- Factory utilization and decremental/incremental margin.
- Parts/service growth and installed-base utilization.
- Rental utilization/rates and used-equipment prices.
- Captive-finance delinquencies, credit losses, and spreads.

### 5. Failure modes

Overproduction into dealer inventory, supplier bottlenecks, warranty/design defects, residual-value collapse, captive-finance credit losses, and buying cycle-peak earnings at a low P/E. Software investment fails when it lacks interoperability or customer ROI.

### 6. Valuation

Value mature OEMs on normalized/mid-cycle EPS, EV/EBITDA, FCF, and ROIC; separate finance arms and pension obligations. Rental firms require fleet-age, utilization, and replacement-cost analysis. Automation firms may earn higher multiples when recurring software/service and structural growth are demonstrated.

### 7. Cycle rule

Orders turn first, then backlog, production, revenue, margin, and finally reported dealer/credit stress. Used-equipment prices and dealer inventory often reveal the turn earlier than headline revenue.

### 9. Complete order-to-uptime and cash mechanics

The lifecycle is platform design and certification → supplier nomination/tooling → forecast and dealer order → component fabrication and final assembly → test → dealer inventory/setup → retail sale, lease or rental → utilization/service → overhaul/remanufacture → resale/scrap. Retail demand, dealer orders and OEM production are different variables; channel inventory can make shipments diverge from end-market use.

Revenue includes equipment, attachments, engines, parts, service, subscriptions, warranties, rental and finance spread. Price realization must be separated from mix, currency and surcharge; backlog must be tested for deposits and cancellation. Installed-base parts are usually higher margin but depend on machine age, utilization, dealer execution and independent alternatives.

Working capital sits in raw materials, work in process, finished machines, dealer inventory, receivables and finance loans/leases. Floorplan support can shift rather than remove inventory risk. Captive-finance earnings require credit loss, funding cost and residual values; subsidized rates may be an equipment discount in another form. Maintenance capex preserves plants/tooling; growth and new-platform R&D should be assessed against through-cycle ROIC.

Track retail sales versus shipments, dealer inventory months, backlog, production, price/cost, segment margin, parts/service mix, installed base, machine hours, warranty, supplier shortages, captive-finance receivables, net interest margin, delinquencies, losses and residuals. Stress rates, commodity/customer budget decline, steel tariff, chip/hydraulic outage, channel destocking, used-price collapse, emissions transition and construction/mining/agriculture cycles separately.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Machine contribution = realized wholesale price − material/components − labor/conversion − freight/warranty/channel support. Enterprise cash subtracts R&D/tooling, plant and working capital; captive finance adds spread less funding, credit and residual loss.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Dealer inventory | months of retail demand by product/region | Channel health |
| Backlog | months and cancellation/deposit quality | Production visibility |
| Price/cost | price realization less material/labor/logistics | Cycle margin |
| Parts/service mix | percent of revenue and installed-base capture | Recurring quality |
| Machine utilization | hours/day or month | End demand and parts |
| Warranty | percent of sales or cost/unit | Launch and quality |
| Plant utilization | roughly 70–90% across normal cycles | Fixed-cost absorption |
| Finance penetration | percent of retail financed by captive | Demand support and risk |
| Delinquency/loss | percent of finance receivables | Customer health |
| Used/residual value | auction or lease residual versus original | New demand and lease risk |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile wholesale to retail and channel inventory; price, mix, FX and surcharge; parts versus OE; production inventory; supplier payables; dealer support; warranty; and captive-finance assets, securitization, allowances and residuals.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Steel, castings, hydraulics, engines, chips, tires, batteries, freight and labor.
- Construction/mining/crop budgets, rates, commodity prices and public infrastructure.
- Dealer inventory/floorplan, used equipment, rental disposal and captive credit.
- Emissions/electrification, autonomy/software, right-to-repair and tariffs.

### Valuation discipline

Separate OE cycle, aftermarket, rental and finance. Use mid-cycle retail demand and channel inventory, replacement cost, installed-base value, and captive credit quality.

Recall, product liability, emissions noncompliance, dealer/franchise conflict, lending/consumer action, export/sanctions breach and government-procurement exclusion are material.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2008–2009 equipment collapse | Construction and credit fell while channel inventory remained | OEM production cuts exceeded retail decline | Channel inventory amplifies cycle |
| 2011–2013 mining capex boom/bust | High commodity prices drove large-equipment orders | Later project cuts reduced demand but installed base supported parts | OE and aftermarket cycles differ |
| 2014–2016 agriculture downturn | Crop economics and dealer stocks weakened | Discounts and production cuts followed | Farmer income, used values and dealer finance interact |
| 2021–2022 component shortages | Semiconductors, hydraulics and logistics constrained completions | Backlogs rose while unfinished inventory consumed cash | One missing part caps the machine |
| Precision/autonomy adoption | Connected hardware and software improved productivity | Value shifted toward data, subscriptions and integrated controls | Measure paid use and customer outcome, not demos |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Dealer retail/inventory, used auctions, rental utilization, customer capex, crop/mineral/construction data, supplier lead times, orders and captive delinquencies.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Shipment growth above retail, parts claims without installed-base capture, or backlog rising with cancellations and unfinished inventory.
- **Durable lesson:** OEM economics are channel, installed-base and finance systems—not factory shipments alone.

### Indicator stack

- **Leading:** customer budgets; commodity/crop; orders; backlog; dealer stock; used values; supplier leads.
- **Coincident:** retail; shipments; production; hours; price/cost; parts.
- **Lagging:** finance losses; warranty; installed-base replacement; plant capacity.

### Minimum dashboard

1. **Dealer inventory** — months of retail demand by product/region; Channel health.
2. **Backlog** — months and cancellation/deposit quality; Production visibility.
3. **Price/cost** — price realization less material/labor/logistics; Cycle margin.
4. **Parts/service mix** — percent of revenue and installed-base capture; Recurring quality.
5. **Machine utilization** — hours/day or month; End demand and parts.
6. **Warranty** — percent of sales or cost/unit; Launch and quality.
7. **Plant utilization** — roughly 70–90% across normal cycles; Fixed-cost absorption.
8. **Finance penetration** — percent of retail financed by captive; Demand support and risk.
9. **Delinquency/loss** — percent of finance receivables; Customer health.
10. **Used/residual value** — auction or lease residual versus original; New demand and lease risk.

### Normalization rules

- Prioritize retail over wholesale.
- Separate OE, parts, rental and finance.
- Use product class and geography.
- Adjust used values for age/hours.

### Evidence traps

- Reading shipments as end demand.
- Ignoring captive-finance subsidy.
- Treating software announcements as recurring revenue.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

Geography changes ownership, contracting, cost, funding, regulation and risk; compare like with like.

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Components/materials | Steel, castings, forgings, engines, hydraulics, bearings, controls, chips and batteries | Shortage in a small qualified component can stop a high-value machine |
| Customer capital cycles | Farms, mines, factories, contractors, warehouses and utilities | Commodity income, utilization and cost of capital drive replacement timing |
| Distribution/finance | Dealers, rental companies, captive finance, banks, auctions and used-equipment channels | Credit and residual values support new pricing but create balance-sheet exposure |
| Aftermarket/digital | Parts, service technicians, telematics, software and predictive maintenance | Installed-base uptime creates recurring high-margin demand and switching cost |
| Policy/trade | Emissions/safety rules, tariffs, localization, infrastructure and industrial incentives | Regulation can force replacement or fragment platforms |

The practical test is to trace a shock through availability, delivered cost, working capital, output, customer economics, financing and eventual substitution—not merely name the adjacent market.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One mid-sized industrial machine sold through a dealer.

**Core equation:** `Lifecycle value = net equipment revenue − bill of material − conversion − freight/warranty − dealer support − allocated fixed cost + discounted parts/service/finance contribution`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Net equipment revenue | Invoice after discount and incentives | $500k |
| Bill of material | Steel, engine/drive, hydraulics, controls and purchased assemblies | $220k |
| Conversion | Direct labor, machining, paint, test and factory burden | $70k |
| Freight, warranty and dealer support | Delivery, expected claims, training and channel economics | $80k |
| Allocated fixed cash cost | Engineering, plant and commercial support | $60k |
| Illustrative equipment contribution | $500k − $220k − $70k − $80k − $60k | $70k before lifecycle parts, software and finance value |

**Decision test:** Price the machine and installed-base annuity together, while stress-testing dealer inventory, used values, warranty, financing losses and true maintenance capital.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Highest-cost qualified factory, import or overtime shift serving a product class | Component availability and dealer inventory constrain supply before nameplate assembly |
| Marginal customer | Owner deciding to replace, repair, rent, buy used or defer | Utilization, downtime cost and financing set willingness to pay |
| Clearing mechanism | List price less dealer/customer incentives, configured options and finance support | Price realization must include mix and subsidized credit |
| Cash shutdown point | Factory output falls when contribution fails to cover avoidable material/labor and channel support | OEMs may maintain rates to protect suppliers or installed base |
| New-capacity incentive | Cycle-average equipment plus aftermarket return covers tooling, engineering and dealer investment | Brownfield flexibility often dominates greenfield assembly |
| Adjustment lag | Weeks for schedules, months for components/channel destocking, years for platforms and plants | Orders and shipments sit at different points in the cycle |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Orders | Final demand | Adjust for cancellations, duplicates, dealer stocking, lead times and price |
| Backlog | Revenue at current margin | Reprice for configuration, inflation, customer credit and delivery cost |
| Dealer inventory | OEM inventory or demand | Measure months by product, age, floorplan terms and retail sell-through |
| Installed base | Service opportunity | Adjust for age, utilization, owner geography, warranty and third-party parts |
| Price realization | Pure price | Separate mix, options, currency, surcharges, financing and incentives |
