## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Airlines, railroads, trucking, ocean shipping, parcel/logistics, forwarding and related terminals. Vehicle manufacturing is outside scope; public transit appears where network economics are comparable.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Air transport | Passenger, cargo and charter flights | RPK/ASM, ton-km, departures | Network, cabin, stage length, slots and fleet |
| Rail | Freight carloads/intermodal and passenger service | carloads, RTM, train miles | Commodity mix, network, service and regulation |
| Trucking | Truckload, LTL, dedicated and last mile | loads, miles, shipments | Contract/spot, density, empty miles and driver/equipment |
| Ocean shipping and ports | Container, dry bulk, tanker and terminal services | TEU, ton-miles, vessel days | Vessel class, route, charter, canal/port and fuel |
| Parcel, forwarding and logistics | Time-definite parcel, brokerage, warehousing and forwarding | packages, shipments, volume, spread | Density, owned capacity, customs and service level |

### Specifications that change value

- State mode, lane, distance, weight/cube, time, service level, ownership and contract/spot basis.
- Capacity is departure/slot/lane/time-specific; fleet totals are insufficient.
- Rate must include fuel surcharge, accessorials, toll/port/terminal and brokerage treatment.
- Utilization needs load factor, empty/repositioning, dwell/turn and scheduled availability.
- Owned, leased, chartered and contracted capacity have different fixed and residual risk.

### Role map

Shipper/traveler creates demand; broker/forwarder books; carrier operates; infrastructure owner grants access; terminal transfers; customs/security clears; lessor/lender finances; regulator sets safety/access.

### Terms that must be explicit

- yield versus rate and length of haul
- capacity unit and load factor
- spot, contract, charter and concession
- dwell, turn and empty repositioning
- owned versus leased fleet economics


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: freight rail, trucking/logistics, parcel, airlines/air cargo, maritime shipping, and related transport infrastructure operators.

### 1. Physical networks and vehicles

Roads, rails, ports, airports, terminals, warehouses, tracks, slots, gates, containers, locomotives, trucks, aircraft, ships, and handling equipment define capacity. Ownership varies: railroads own much infrastructure; trucks and airlines depend heavily on public or landlord infrastructure; shipping depends on ports and canals.

### 2. Fuel and power

Diesel, jet fuel, marine fuel, and electricity are major variable inputs. Surcharges pass through some fuel moves with lags and imperfect formulas. **[Inference]** Fuel volatility affects cash and demand even when accounting pass-through protects margin percentage.

### 3. Labor

Drivers, pilots, mechanics, dockworkers, mariners, dispatchers, train crews, and warehouse labor are capacity gates. Work rules, seniority, safety limits, training pipelines, and unions make labor less fungible than headcount suggests.

### 4. Equipment and maintenance

Vehicles require engines, tires, parts, avionics, railcars/containers, maintenance shops, and certified spares. Aircraft and locomotives are long-lived; maintenance condition determines effective capacity. Insurance and safety performance are economic inputs.

### 5. Data, route rights, and regulation

Routing/dispatch systems, yield management, customs data, and tracking optimize assets. Operating authority, airport slots, cabotage, emissions, crew rules, and safety regulation restrict entry. Cross-border trucking carried over $1T of U.S.-Canada/Mexico freight value in 2025 (**[Fact]** — BTS — https://www.bts.gov/newsroom/transborder-freight-data-annual-report-2025-0).

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** price/yield, fuel net of surcharge, labor, maintenance, insurance, and asset utilization.
- **Cap capacity:** qualified crews, tractors/aircraft/ships, terminals/slots, maintenance availability, and network congestion.
- **Shock path:** port closure → vessels and containers queue → inland flows miss schedules → spot rates rise → shippers reroute/inventory buffers rise → normalization can trigger a rate and equipment glut.

### 8. Full modal input and infrastructure ledger

All modes consume vehicles or vessels, fuel or electricity, labor, terminals and rights-of-way, but their detailed chains differ. Airlines need aircraft/engines, jet fuel, airports/gates/slots, maintenance parts, pilots and traffic control; rail needs locomotives, wagons, track, ballast, signaling, yards and crews; trucking needs tractors/trailers, diesel, tires, roads, drivers, maintenance and parking; ocean shipping needs ships, bunker fuel, ports, containers, crews, canals and classification; parcel/logistics adds hubs, warehouses, aircraft/vans, scanners and IT.

Raw materials embedded in vehicles include steel, aluminum, composites, rubber, copper, batteries, semiconductors and lubricants. Infrastructure embeds land, concrete, steel, asphalt, cranes, power and communications. Fuel chains include crude/refining/logistics, gas/LNG or grid generation; sustainable fuels add biomass and environmental attributes. Data, routing, customs, weather, cybersecurity, navigation, insurance and safety certification are essential intangible inputs.

Funding includes carrier debt/equity and leases, aircraft/ship/railcar asset finance, equipment loans, securitization, export credit, infrastructure concessions, municipal/sovereign bonds, public grants and operating subsidies. Customer trade credit and fuel hedging add liquidity needs. Labor law, cabotage, safety/hours, emissions, customs, port/airport/rail regulation and security gate deployment.

Fuel, wage, maintenance, toll/port and lease rates set variable margin; aircraft/ship/driver/crew availability, slots, track/road/port capacity, containers, canals, weather and customs cap throughput. Modal substitution depends on distance, value density, time sensitivity, infrastructure and emissions; better utilization, intermodal transfer, pooling, backhauls and digital matching substitute for physical capacity but cannot eliminate bottleneck nodes.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Modal structure

Truck dominates flexible short/medium-haul freight; rail excels at heavy long-haul and intermodal; maritime carries low-cost global volume; air carries time-sensitive high-value goods; parcel integrates hubs and last mile. Demand is derived from trade, industrial output, inventories, and consumer spending.

### 2. Profit pools and market power

**[Inference]** Network density, scarce rights-of-way/slots, terminal access, and route scale create rents. Rail is a local oligopoly; parcel has dense hub-and-spoke barriers; trucking and brokerage are fragmented and cyclical; container shipping consolidates but repeatedly over-orders capacity; airlines have scarce slots yet high fixed cost and volatile returns.

### 3. Geography and trade

Ports, border crossings, manufacturing corridors, population centers, and resource basins shape flows. BTS counted $1.586T of U.S. freight with Canada and Mexico in 2025, with truck at 55.7% of Canada trade and 73.6% of Mexico trade by value (**[Fact]** — https://www.bts.gov/newsroom/transborder-freight-data-annual-report-2025-0).

### 4. Gaining relevance

Intermodal where service is reliable, nearshoring/cross-border logistics, dedicated/contract capacity, automated warehouses, dynamic routing, and asset-light platforms that add real density. Airlines benefit from premium/loyalty economics; freight networks benefit from end-to-end visibility.

### 5. Losing relevance

Undifferentiated spot brokerage, structurally subscale carriers, excess ocean capacity, and automation claims unsupported by safety/utilization. **[Inference]** Autonomous trucking will likely enter constrained routes and yards before general long-haul replacement; labor savings must exceed supervision, insurance, and equipment cost.

### 6. Policy and security

Trade policy, port labor, cabotage, emissions rules, infrastructure funding, and national-security screening reshape routes and fleets. Transportation resilience increasingly commands value after pandemic and geopolitical disruptions exposed single-node dependence.

### 7. Value migration

From pure line-haul toward integrated contract logistics, data, and high-service networks; from transactional capacity toward dedicated relationships; and from owned assets toward orchestration only where the platform controls demand or density. Physical nodes remain decisive—the software cannot route around missing terminal capacity indefinitely.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs are passenger journeys, ton-miles, TEU moves, parcel stops, warehousing/forwarding, network access and time-definite delivery. Reliability, frequency, safety and cargo condition are part of the service. Emissions, noise, congestion, accidents, packaging loss and empty repositioning are external or residual outputs.

Customers include travelers, employers, governments, shippers, beneficial cargo owners, retailers, manufacturers, freight forwarders and other carriers. The ticket buyer may differ from passenger; a forwarder books capacity for the cargo owner; a marketplace or retailer can set service while a third party transports. Contract, spot and regulated/public-service demand should be separated.

Geography follows trade lanes and network nodes: airports, ports, canals, rail corridors, highways, border crossings and fulfillment hubs. Network density creates scale and service advantage, but local slots, gates, landing rights, trackage, terminal concessions and cabotage create barriers. Global carriers coexist with regional operators, owner-operators, public transit and state-owned infrastructure.

Capital structures range from asset-heavy ownership to leasing, brokerage and franchised/contracted capacity. Public funding builds roads, ports, airports, rail and transit and may subsidize essential service; private capital funds fleets, terminals, logistics technology and concessions. Policy includes safety, labor and hours, emissions/fuels, competition, passenger rights, customs, cabotage, security, infrastructure access, pricing/tolls and public procurement.

Transportation interacts with trade, fuel, retail, manufacturing, agriculture, tourism and inventory. Higher freight cost can raise delivered commodity prices and induce nearshoring; unreliable transit raises safety stock; e-commerce shifts freight toward parcels/air and last mile; remote work changes commuting; carbon policy changes fleet and modal choice. One constrained canal, port, airspace or border can reroute global flows and redistribute rates.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Network economics

Revenue = volume × yield, but profit depends on asset/crew utilization, balance, and network fluidity. Empty repositioning, dwell, missed connections, and congestion consume capacity without revenue. A dense network lowers pickup/delivery and improves asset turns.

### 2. Modal operating units

- **Trucking:** revenue/load or mile; loaded miles, empty miles, driver utilization, and insurance.
- **Rail:** carloads/intermodal units, revenue ton-miles, train speed, dwell, operating ratio.
- **Parcel:** packages/day, revenue/package, stops/route, hub sort utilization.
- **Airline/cargo:** available seat/ton miles, load factor, yield, unit revenue and unit cost.
- **Ocean:** TEU, freight rate/TEU, vessel utilization, charter/bunker cost.

### 3. Contract versus spot

Contract rates provide visibility but reset with market conditions; spot prices clear marginal capacity and turn first. Fuel surcharges transfer benchmark moves, not all operating inefficiency. Broker gross margin is shipper price less carrier purchase.

### 4. Cycle

Demand rises → utilization tightens → spot rates rise → contracts reset → capacity orders/hiring increase → demand slows → spot falls first → bankruptcies/scrapping restore balance. Asset delivery lags create overshoot, especially in ships and aircraft.

### 5. KPIs

Volume/yield, utilization/load factor, empty miles, on-time service, dwell, fuel efficiency, labor productivity, maintenance, capex, free cash flow, and leverage. BTS maintains current port, freight movement, labor/capacity, spot-rate, rail-speed, and dwell indicators (**[Fact]** — https://www.bts.gov/freight-indicators).

### 6. Failure modes

Over-ordering capacity, labor disruption, safety incident, poor network recovery, fuel/pass-through mismatch, customer concentration, cyber outage, and acquiring volume without density. Service failure can trigger a negative loop: customers leave → network density worsens → unit cost rises.

### 7. Valuation

Use mid-cycle EV/EBITDA/P-E and FCF for asset-heavy carriers; replacement value and orderbook for fleets; revenue/gross-profit multiples only for asset-light brokers with durable growth and cash conversion. Airlines require lease-adjusted debt and loyalty economics; shipping requires NAV and cycle-aware charter assumptions.

### 8. Complete network, pricing, and cash-conversion mechanics

The service chain is forecast/book → position vehicle and crew → load/board → line-haul → transfer/connect → last mile/unload → proof, settle and reposition → inspect/maintain. Capacity is per departure, lane and time window, not fleet total. Load factor, weight/cube, dwell, turn time, empty miles, schedule integrity and connection quality determine usable throughput.

Pricing includes tickets/fares, spot and contract freight, fuel surcharges, accessorials, terminal/toll fees, ancillary passenger revenue, brokerage spread and public-service payments. Yield must be read with mix and distance; a fuel surcharge may recover dollars with a lag but not demand destruction. Network revenue management trades price against utilization and downstream connection value.

Cash structures differ: passenger tickets can be collected before travel; freight receivables follow delivery; charter and long-term contracts may use deposits; lessors receive fixed rent while taking residual and credit risk. Working capital includes fuel, spares, containers, customer advances, claims and hedging collateral. Maintenance expense, overhaul reserves and replacement capex must be normalized across owned and leased fleets.

Track capacity and demand units by mode, load factor/utilization, yield/rate, revenue per capacity unit, fuel per output, labor productivity, on-time performance, cancellations, dwell/turns, empty repositioning, maintenance events, fleet age, lease obligations, capex, leverage and free cash flow. Stress recession/trade decline, fuel spike, labor disruption, port/canal closure, severe weather, cyber outage, fleet grounding, rate shock and infrastructure congestion.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Trip/lane contribution = loaded capacity × realized yield − fuel/energy − crew − access/toll/terminal − variable maintenance and handling. Network cash subtracts fixed fleet/lease, infrastructure, overhaul, working capital and replacement capital.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Airline load factor | often ~75–90% by market/season | Capacity pricing |
| Trucking empty miles | commonly low-teens to 20%+ by operation | Network productivity |
| Rail operating ratio | expense/revenue; mix and accounting matter | Efficiency |
| Container ship utilization | TEU loaded versus effective slots | Freight pricing |
| Parcel stops per route hour | density-specific | Last-mile unit cost |
| Fuel share | often one of the largest variable costs | Surcharge and hedge |
| Fleet age | years and maintenance cycle | Capex/reliability |
| On-time performance | percent within service definition | Customer value |
| Dwell/turn time | hours/days by node/asset | Capacity release |
| Lease/debt fixed charge | coverage and maturity | Downcycle resilience |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Normalize fuel surcharges, pass-through purchased transportation, loyalty/deferred tickets, maintenance capitalization/overhaul reserves, leases, aircraft/ship residuals, claims, and customer advances. GMV or gross freight spend is not brokerage revenue.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Fuel, labor, tolls/ports, maintenance, insurance and leases.
- Trade, industrial production, retail inventory, travel, rates and currency.
- Port/canal/airspace/rail/road capacity, weather, strikes and customs.
- Fleet supply, orderbook, scrapping, charter terms and regulation.

### Valuation discipline

Use network and mode-specific mid-cycle yield/utilization, fleet/lease obligations and maintenance capital. Asset-light brokers and infrastructure require separate models.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1978 US airline deregulation | Entry/pricing rules changed | Networks, fares and bankruptcies reshaped industry | Regulation defines competitive architecture |
| 2008–2009 freight collapse | Trade and industrial demand fell abruptly | Rates, utilization and fleets reset | Capacity exits with lags |
| 2016 container-shipping crisis | Overcapacity and weak rates culminated in Hanjin failure | Cargo and counterparties were stranded | Alliance, balance sheet and orderbook matter |
| 2020 pandemic split | Passenger travel collapsed while parcel and some freight surged | Modes diverged sharply | Aggregate transport data can mislead |
| 2021 Suez and port congestion | Node outages and demand imbalance tied up global capacity | Freight and inventories spiked | Network dwell destroys effective fleet capacity |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Bookings, tender rejection, spot-contract spreads, port dwell, canal queues, capacity schedules, fuel, fleet orders, inventories and travel searches.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Fleet growth without lane demand, rate gains driven solely by surcharge, or asset-light claims with large guarantees/leases.
- **Durable lesson:** Transportation clears by lane, node, time and service reliability—not total fleet.

- **Leading:** bookings; spot rates; tender rejection; travel search; inventories; fleet schedules/orders.
- **Coincident:** loads; passengers; ton-miles; yield; utilization; dwell; on-time.
- **Lagging:** fleet deliveries/scrap; claims; credit loss; infrastructure completion.

### Minimum dashboard

1. **Airline load factor** — often ~75–90% by market/season; Capacity pricing.
2. **Trucking empty miles** — commonly low-teens to 20%+ by operation; Network productivity.
3. **Rail operating ratio** — expense/revenue; mix and accounting matter; Efficiency.
4. **Container ship utilization** — TEU loaded versus effective slots; Freight pricing.
5. **Parcel stops per route hour** — density-specific; Last-mile unit cost.
6. **Fuel share** — often one of the largest variable costs; Surcharge and hedge.
7. **Fleet age** — years and maintenance cycle; Capex/reliability.
8. **On-time performance** — percent within service definition; Customer value.
9. **Dwell/turn time** — hours/days by node/asset; Capacity release.
10. **Lease/debt fixed charge** — coverage and maturity; Downcycle resilience.

### Normalization rules

- Normalize by distance, weight/cube and service.
- Separate surcharge and pass-through.
- Use effective scheduled capacity.
- Include leases and overhaul.

### Evidence traps

- Comparing rates without length of haul.
- Using fleet count as capacity.
- Aggregating modes with opposite cycles.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One over-the-road truckload tractor operated for a year.

**Core equation:** `Operating cash/tractor = loaded miles × net revenue/mile + accessorials − fuel − driver − maintenance/tolls − lease/depreciation − allocated overhead`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Freight revenue | 100,000 loaded miles × $2.60/mile | $260k |
| Accessorial/other revenue | Detention, fuel surcharge net and ancillary services | $20k |
| Fuel | Net of contractual surcharge recovery | $70k |
| Driver | Wages, benefits, recruiting and travel | $90k |
| Maintenance, tolls and equipment | $35k repairs/tolls + $45k lease/depreciation | $80k |
| Illustrative operating cash | $280k − $70k − $90k − $80k − $20k overhead | $20k/tractor before interest and replacement-value inflation |

**Decision test:** Measure contribution per constrained asset-day and loaded mile, then reconcile empty miles, dwell, network imbalance, fuel recovery and replacement capital.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Last carrier, vessel, train path or aircraft rotation needed on a lane/time | Capacity is lane-specific and falls with congestion, empty repositioning and labor limits |
| Marginal customer | Shipper/passenger able to defer, reroute, change mode or absorb inventory delay | Value of time and product margin determine willingness to pay |
| Clearing mechanism | Spot/contract freight rates, tariffs, yield management and charter markets | Average revenue per unit masks lane and timing scarcity |
| Cash shutdown point | Rate below avoidable fuel, crew, toll/port and trip maintenance cost | Debt/lease and repositioning can keep assets moving below full cost |
| New-capacity incentive | Cycle-average rate supports equipment, infrastructure, labor and return at expected utilization | Orderbook and public infrastructure have different lags |
| Adjustment lag | Days for routing, months for tractors/aircraft, years for ships, rail and terminals | Spot spikes do not automatically imply durable scarcity |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Capacity | Fleet or vessel count | Adjust for size, route, speed, dwell, maintenance, labor and empty repositioning |
| Revenue per mile/unit | Rate | Separate fuel surcharge, accessorials, mix, length of haul and utilization |
| Load factor | Profitability | Combine with yield, cargo/passenger mix, stage length and trip cost |
| On-time performance | Network quality alone | Define measurement window, cancellations, handoffs and customer promise |
| Asset-light | Capital-light or low risk | Include leases, guarantees, working capital, carrier credit and technology spend |
