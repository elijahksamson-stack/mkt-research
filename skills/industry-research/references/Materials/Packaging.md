## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Paper/corrugated, flexible and rigid plastic, metal, glass, labels, closures, and protective packaging for consumer and industrial products.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Corrugated and paperboard | Cases, cartons and paper protective formats | tons, MSF, boxes | Fiber mix, basis weight, box design, local freight |
| Flexible plastics | Films, pouches, laminates and labels | pounds/tons, area | Resin, layers, barrier, print, seal and recyclability |
| Rigid plastics | Bottles, jars, tubs, closures and components | units, grams, resin | Resin, mold/tool, food-contact and lightweighting |
| Metal and glass | Cans, ends, aerosol, bottles and jars | units, tons | Sheet/cullet, furnace/line, coating, weight and returnability |
| Specialty and protective | Healthcare, hazardous, temperature-controlled and industrial protection | units, qualified systems | Validation, sterile barrier, UN/ISTA performance and customer line fit |

### Specifications that change value

- State substrate, gauge/grammage, recycled content, barrier, coating/ink/adhesive, and food/drug contact status.
- Performance needs top-load, drop, seal, migration, shelf-life, moisture/oxygen/light and filling-line speed.
- Separate material weight reduction from product damage or shelf-life effect.
- Recyclability must state collection, sorting, reprocessor and end-market, not design claim alone.
- Price must distinguish substrate pass-through, conversion, tooling, freight, and EPR/deposit fees.

### Role map

Substrate producer supplies; converter forms/prints; brand owner specifies; co-packer fills; retailer/logistics tests performance; consumer uses; municipality/producer-responsibility system collects; recycler sorts/reprocesses.

### Terms that must be explicit

- basis weight and caliper/gauge
- virgin, post-industrial and post-consumer content
- material pass-through versus conversion margin
- recyclable in design versus recycled in practice
- one-way, refill, returnable and reusable systems


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: corrugated/containerboard, paperboard, flexible and rigid plastics, metal cans, glass, closures, and converted packaging.

### 1. Substrates

Wood fiber/recovered paper, plastic resins and films, aluminum/steel, silica/glass inputs, inks, coatings, adhesives, and closures are the main physical inputs. Fiber quality, recovered-paper collection, resin grades, and metal sheet availability matter by format.

### 2. Energy, water, and manufacturing assets

Paper mills, furnaces, extruders, printing presses, can lines, corrugators, and converting plants require reliable energy and maintenance. Paper/glass are energy- and capital-intensive; flexible converting is lighter but depends on film and printing technology.

### 3. Customer artwork, qualification, and tooling

Packaging must protect product, run on customer filling lines, meet shelf/transport requirements, and carry compliant artwork. Molds, dies, plates, barrier specifications, and food-contact approvals create switching cost. **[Inference]** Packaging is low-cost relative to the product but failure can stop a customer's line, supporting value for reliable suppliers.

### 4. Logistics and recovered-material systems

Corrugated boxes and empty rigid containers are bulky, making local plant density important. Recycling economics depend on collection, sorting, contamination, and end markets—not recyclability claims alone.

### 5. Regulation

Food-contact rules, labeling, recycled-content mandates, deposit systems, extended producer responsibility, and single-use restrictions reshape formats. Packaging Corporation of America added 800,000 tons of containerboard capacity through a 2025 acquisition (**[Fact]** — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/75677/000119312526074129/pkg-20251231.htm), illustrating consolidation around scale and integration.

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** substrate/energy, price-cost lag, mix, freight, and utilization.
- **Cap capacity:** qualified lines/tooling, mill/furnace/extrusion capacity, customer approval, and local converting.
- **Shock path:** resin/fiber rise → converter margin compresses until pass-through → customers lightweight/redesign → demand and mix adjust with lag.

### 7. Full substrate, conversion, and circularity ledger

Paper packaging requires virgin wood fiber, recovered paper, pulp, starch, sizing, coatings, inks, adhesives, water, steam and electricity. Plastic packaging requires resin—PET, HDPE, LDPE, PP, PS or multilayer barrier polymers—plus colorants, additives, labels and sometimes aluminum foil; resin traces to oil/gas, NGLs and petrochemicals. Metal cans require aluminum sheet or tinplate steel, coatings and ends; glass requires silica sand, soda ash, limestone, cullet and high-temperature fuel/power. Closures, pallets, films and secondary packaging add rubber, wood, steel and plastics.

Conversion needs paper machines, corrugators, extruders, injection/blow molders, thermoformers, furnaces, can lines, printing, cutting and filling-compatible quality systems. Food, beverage, pharma and hazardous-goods packaging adds migration testing, sterilization, clean production, traceability and customer line qualification. Tooling, molds, inks, adhesives, spare parts, water treatment, compressed air, warehouses and freight are necessary.

Recycled supply is not simply a commodity substitute: collection, deposit systems, sorting, washing, de-inking, food-grade approval, color contamination and yield determine usable content. Design choices trade barrier performance, weight, shelf life and recyclability. Lightweighting saves material and freight but can raise damage or line failures.

Funding includes corporate cash/debt/equity, private-equity platforms, equipment leases, customer-backed capacity and public recycling infrastructure or grants. Substrate, energy, freight and labor set cost; paper-machine, furnace, tooling, food-grade recycled resin, aluminum sheet, customer qualification and filling-line compatibility can gate output. Packaging taxes, recycled-content rules, extended producer responsibility, deposit return, food-contact law and labeling requirements turn compliance and verified data into direct inputs.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Value chain and formats

Material producers feed mills/extruders/furnaces, converters print/form packages, and consumer/industrial customers fill them. Corrugated, paperboard, flexible plastic, rigid plastic, metal, and glass compete on protection, cost, weight, machinability, consumer experience, and regulation.

### 2. Profit pools

Commodity substrate is cyclical; converting is locally competitive; attractive niches have high qualification, proprietary dispensing/closures, healthcare/sterile requirements, complex barriers, or design integration. Integrated containerboard/corrugated firms can balance mill and box demand but remain utilization-sensitive.

### 3. Geography and consolidation

Corrugated and rigid containers are regional because shipping air is costly; flexible film and metal sheet can travel farther. Scale supports procurement, R&D, and customer coverage, driving consolidation. Customers are often large CPG, food/beverage, healthcare, and industrial companies with bargaining power.

### 4. Gaining relevance

E-commerce corrugated, lightweighting, mono-material/recyclable structures, recycled content, refill/dispensing, and high-barrier food/healthcare formats. Digitally printed short runs gain where SKU proliferation and customization justify cost.

### 5. Losing relevance

Over-specified material, hard-to-recycle multilayer structures where alternatives perform, newsprint/office-paper-adjacent capacity, and “sustainable” redesign that increases food waste or total lifecycle emissions. **[Inference]** The winning format minimizes system cost and product loss, not packaging grams alone.

### 6. Value migration

Toward design, material science, closures, healthcare, and circular systems with secured feedstock; away from commodity conversion. Regulation may transfer cost from municipalities to producers, increasing the value of scale and compliance data.

### 7. Investor framing

Separate volume from substrate pass-through, organic mix from acquisition growth, and true capacity discipline from temporary downtime. Consolidation creates value only if plants close, routes improve, or customer value rises.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs include corrugated cases, cartons, labels, flexible films and pouches, rigid containers, bottles, jars, cans, closures and protective/industrial packaging. The functional outputs are containment, barrier, dosing, tamper evidence, shelf life, transport efficiency, branding and regulatory information. Trim, off-spec material, breakage, contamination and post-consumer waste are material residuals; recovered fiber, metal, glass and resin are secondary outputs.

Customers span food/beverage, household and personal care, pharma, e-commerce, industrial and agricultural shippers, retailers and food service. Brand owners specify performance and graphics, converters manufacture, co-packers fill, retailers influence shelf and sustainability requirements, and consumers/municipalities bear much end-of-life handling. This split matters when allocating packaging fees and redesign cost.

Empty packaging is bulky, so converting is regional even when resin, pulp, aluminum and glass inputs trade globally. Plants cluster near mills, fillers and consumer markets. Corrugated is local and fragmented; beverage cans, glass and high-barrier formats need larger scale; specialty healthcare packaging has qualification moats.

Public companies, family converters, sponsor-owned consolidators, integrated mills and brand-owner captive operations coexist. Private capital funds consolidation and equipment; public capital supports collection, sorting and recycling; producer fees and deposit systems redirect funding toward end-of-life. Rules cover food/drug contact, hazardous transport, weights/measures, labeling, plastics restrictions, recycled content, deposit return, extended producer responsibility, waste exports and green claims.

Packaging interacts with chemicals, forestry, aluminum/steel, glass minerals, logistics, retail, e-commerce, food waste and municipal waste. Removing material can increase product loss, so lifecycle analysis must compare packaging footprint with preserved product and reverse logistics. High resin or fiber prices improve recycling incentives; weak virgin prices can strand recycling capacity unless mandates or fees support demand.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Workflows

- **Corrugated:** pulp/recycled fiber → containerboard → corrugator combines liner/medium → print/die-cut/fold/glue.
- **Flexible:** resin → film extrusion/orientation → print/laminate/coating → slit/pouch.
- **Rigid plastic:** resin → injection/blow/thermoform → decorate → ship.
- **Metal:** roll sheet → form cans/ends → coat/print; **glass:** batch → melt → form/anneal/inspect.

### 2. Unit economics

Revenue is price × area/units/tons with mix for complexity. COGS is substrate, energy, labor, depreciation, scrap, ink/coatings, and freight. Price escalators/pass-throughs reduce but do not eliminate timing risk. Lightweighting can lower revenue per unit but improve customer economics and margin if value is shared.

### 3. Capacity

Track mill tons, corrugator square footage, extrusion/furnace/can-line throughput, utilization, yield, downtime, and changeover. Product mix can constrain capacity before nameplate is reached because complex SKUs require more setup time.

### 4. KPIs

Organic volume, price/mix, substrate-cost lag, EBITDA/ton or per unit, utilization, scrap/yield, on-time delivery, capex, working capital, recycled content, and customer concentration.

### 5. Failure modes

Customer line failure, migration/barrier defect, artwork error, contamination, furnace/mill outage, overcapacity, resin/fiber spike, and integration failure. Volume gained at below-replacement economics is not strategic.

### 6. Valuation

Commodity paper/containerboard: normalized EV/EBITDA, FCF, replacement value, and capacity cycle. Diversified/healthcare packaging: higher multiples supported by organic growth, retention, and margin. Adjust for leases, pensions, environmental liabilities, restructuring, and sustaining capex.

### 7. Complete conversion, contract, and recovery mechanics

The workflow is substrate procurement → formulation or sheet/pulp preparation → form, print, coat and cure → inspect → palletize → deliver to customer filling line → perform through distribution/use → collect, sort, recycle, reuse, incinerate or landfill. Economics depend on material yield, gauge/grammage, line speed, setup and color changes, scrap recovery, energy, downtime and freight.

Revenue may be per thousand units, area, weight or custom program. Contracts often include resin, paper or metal pass-through with indices and lags, but conversion margin can still move through volume, mix and under-absorption. Custom tooling and qualification create stickiness; commoditized formats face bid pressure. Customer concentration and retailer/brand destocking can produce sharp converter volume swings.

Working capital includes volatile substrate inventory, custom finished goods, tooling, receivables and energy hedges. Maintenance capital keeps continuous lines reliable; growth capital adds high-speed lines, lightweighting, digital print, barriers or recycling. Deposit/EPR obligations, customer rebates and recycled-content certificates can create additional cash timing.

Track volume by substrate/end market, price/mix, material-to-sales ratio, pass-through lag, yield/scrap, line utilization and speed, downtime, freight, customer retention, new wins, working capital, capex, ROIC, recycled content and recovery rate. Stress substrate spike, energy outage, brand-owner destocking, food-contact rule change, EPR fee, recycling contamination, customer filling-line failure and a shift between plastic, paper, glass and metal.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Conversion margin = units/area/weight × price − substrate − ink/coating/adhesive − energy − scrap − labor − freight. Full return subtracts tooling, maintenance, qualification, working capital, EPR/deposit obligations, and growth equipment.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Material share of COGS | often the majority in commodity formats | Pass-through and margin lag |
| Corrugated freight radius | typically regional due low value density | Plant-network advantage |
| Line utilization | roughly 70–90% depending changeovers/mix | Fixed-cost absorption |
| Scrap/yield loss | low-single to double-digit percent by process | Conversion quality |
| Customer qualification | weeks to years; longest in healthcare/high-speed filling | Switching and ramp |
| Inventory turns | substrate and custom-finished goods | Working capital |
| Recycled content | percent by substrate and legal/customer requirement | Secondary-material demand |
| Lightweighting | grams or basis-weight reduction per unit | Material saving versus failure |
| Energy intensity | especially high for glass, metal and paper | Power/fuel exposure |
| Customer concentration | top accounts as percent of revenue | Bargaining and tooling risk |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Separate indexed substrate pass-through from conversion price/mix; gross versus net resin/paper invoicing; customer-owned versus converter tooling; scrap credits; returnable-asset depreciation; and EPR/deposit balances. Volume must be normalized for lightweighting.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Pulp/recovered fiber, resin, aluminum/steel, glass fuel, energy, freight and FX.
- Brand and retailer destocking, SKU proliferation, e-commerce, and food/beverage volume.
- Line speed, changeovers, scrap, tooling, quality, and customer filling failures.
- Recycled-content/EPR/deposit rules and substrate substitution.

### Valuation discipline

Commodity formats depend on network density, utilization and pass-through; specialty formats on qualification, IP and retention. Compare returns per line and customer, not only consolidated tons.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1970s–1990s lightweighting | Resin/metal/process innovation reduced package weight | Material per unit fell while performance improved | Volume and material demand can diverge |
| 2008 demand and inventory collapse | Industrial and consumer customers cut orders and stocks | Converters experienced rapid under-absorption | Packaging magnifies customer inventory cycles |
| 2017–2018 recovered-paper trade reset | China tightened waste-import standards | Recovered-fiber prices and domestic sorting economics shifted | End-market policy determines recycling value |
| 2020 e-commerce and food shifts | Channel mix changed corrugated, food-service and beverage demand | Formats moved in opposite directions | End-market mix beats aggregate consumption |
| 2022–2026 EPR/reuse expansion | Producer fees and reuse/recycled rules spread | Design and system cost became explicit | End-of-life moves into product economics |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Substrate prices, customer order/inventory, box shipments, beverage/food volumes, line utilization, scrap, EPR rules, and recycled-material spreads.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Sustainability claim without collection/end market, price growth that only reflects substrate pass-through, or capacity additions lacking customer qualification.
- **Durable lesson:** Packaging value is delivered product protection and line performance per lifecycle cost—not material weight alone.

### Normalization rules

- Adjust volumes for lightweighting and unit/area/weight mix.
- Separate substrate pass-through from conversion margin.
- State recycled-content denominator and source.
- Match shipments to customer sell-through and inventory.

### Evidence traps

- Equating recyclable with recycled.
- Comparing tons without mix and gauge.
- Ignoring product-loss avoided by packaging.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

Geography changes ownership, contracting, cost, funding, regulation and risk; compare like with like.

| Geography / archetype | Operating model | Economic and risk implications |
| --- | --- | --- |
| North America | Consolidated corrugated, containerboard, cans and rigid-plastic systems with integrated recycling | Box demand, mill integration, freight and customer concentration shape margins |

| Emerging consumer markets | Growing flexible, rigid and beverage packaging serving formalizing retail | Resin, imported equipment, collection systems and affordability determine format |

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Raw materials | Virgin/recycled fiber, resin, aluminum, steel, glass batch, coatings, inks and adhesives | Commodity moves transmit with contractual lags; recycled quality affects yield |
| Conversion equipment | Paper machines, corrugators, extruders, molding, can lines, furnaces and printing | Format-specific assets and tooling constrain rapid mix shifts |

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One thousand square feet (MSF) of converted corrugated packaging.

**Core equation:** `Cash contribution/MSF = net box price − containerboard/fiber − energy/conversion labor − freight − allocated mill/converting fixed cost`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Net revenue | Customer price after mix, rebates and print/tooling allocation | $900/MSF |
| Fiber/containerboard | Liner and medium consumption net of trim/yield | $380/MSF |
| Energy and conversion | Steam/power, starch, ink, direct labor and maintenance | $210/MSF |
| Freight | Local delivery of bulky finished boxes | $70/MSF |
| Fixed cash cost | Mill/converting overhead at normal utilization | $80/MSF |
| Illustrative contribution | $900 − $380 − $210 − $70 − $80 | $160/MSF before sustaining capital and working capital |

**Decision test:** Separate price from substrate pass-through and mix; measure contribution per constrained machine hour when SKU changes, print complexity or downtime bind.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Highest delivered-cost converter, mill or imported package qualified for the customer | Freight, tooling and filling-line approval make supply local and sticky |
| Marginal customer | Brand/shipper able to redesign, lightweight, switch substrate or defer orders | Product protection and line efficiency limit price elasticity |
| Clearing mechanism | Negotiated contracts with resin/fiber/metal pass-throughs, plus spot exposure | Published material indices do not equal converted-package realization |
| Cash shutdown point | Net price below avoidable substrate, energy, labor and delivery cost | Continuous furnaces/mills and customer service discourage short shutdowns |
| New-capacity incentive | Contracted demand and cycle-average margin cover line, tooling, working capital and return | Debottlenecking and mix upgrades may be superior to headline capacity |
| Adjustment lag | Weeks for inventory/substrate changes, months for tooling, years for mills/furnaces | Format substitution is constrained by customer equipment |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Volume | Comparable physical output | Use tonnes, units, MSF or equivalent cases by substrate and mix |
| Price increase | Net margin expansion | Separate raw-material pass-through, freight, mix, rebates and lag |
| Recycled content | Recyclability or closed-loop recovery | State input share, collection rate, yield, contamination and end market |
| Capacity | Qualified available supply | Adjust for format, tooling, width, color, food-contact status, downtime and location |
| Lightweighting | Lower system cost or emissions | Test damage, barrier performance, filling efficiency, transport and recovery |
