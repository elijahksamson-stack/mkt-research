## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Exploration, extraction, beneficiation, smelting/refining, primary metals, and recycling across bulk, base, precious, and critical minerals.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Bulk commodities | Iron ore, metallurgical coal and other high-volume ores | wet/dry metric tons, grade | Grade, moisture, impurities, freight and steel route |
| Base metals | Copper, zinc, lead, aluminum value chains | contained/payable metal, cathode/ingot | Ore route, recovery, treatment charges, power and by-products |
| Precious metals | Gold, silver and PGM mining/refining | ounces, grade, recovery | Co-product, reserve, jurisdiction and security |
| Battery and critical minerals | Lithium, nickel, cobalt, graphite, rare earths and others | LCE, Ni content, concentrate, oxide | Chemistry, conversion, impurity, qualification and policy |
| Processing and recycling | Smelters, refiners, scrap collection and secondary metal | throughput, recovery, utilization | Feed quality, energy, emissions and collection economics |

### Specifications that change value

- State ore type, reserve/resource code, grade, cut-off, dilution, recovery, payable terms, impurities and moisture.
- State product form: ore, concentrate, matte, hydroxide, carbonate, cathode, ingot, alloy, oxide or metal.
- Costs must identify by-product credits, royalties, freight, treatment/refining charges, sustaining capital and closure.
- Capacity must distinguish mine, mill, smelter/refinery, transport and permitted tailings.
- Recycled metal must state alloy/contamination, collection source, yield and closed-loop eligibility.

### Role map

Explorer defines resource; developer permits/finances; mine extracts; mill concentrates; smelter/refiner produces saleable metal; trader/logistics finance and move; fabricator/OEM qualifies; state/community owns rights and receives tax/royalty.

### Terms that must be explicit

- resource versus reserve
- ore grade versus payable metal
- strip ratio, dilution, recovery and yield
- cash cost versus AISC and all-in cost
- treatment/refining charges and payability


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


### 1. Orebody and legal title

Grade, tonnage, mineralogy, strip ratio, depth, metallurgy, by-products, reserve classification, royalties, and tenure determine resource quality. USGS's Mineral Commodity Summaries provide production, trade, reserves, and import reliance across more than 90 minerals (**[Fact]** — https://www.usgs.gov/centers/national-minerals-information-center/mineral-commodity-summaries).

### 2. Energy, water, reagents, and consumables

Diesel/electricity power haulage, crushing, grinding, pumping, and ventilation. Water availability and tailings capacity can bind mines. Explosives, grinding media, tires, cyanide/acids, lime, and smelter inputs affect cost and recovery.

### 3. Equipment, labor, and infrastructure

Large trucks, shovels, mills, conveyors, shafts, rail, roads, ports, and power lines require long lead times. Geologists, mining/metallurgical engineers, operators, maintenance trades, and local/community capability are essential.

### 4. Capital and permits

Discovery-to-production can take a decade or more through drilling, studies, environmental review, consultation, financing, construction, and ramp. Cost inflation and schedule risk compound during this period. Closure, reclamation, and tailings liabilities are real capital inputs.

### 5. Smelting/refining and trade

Concentrates need treatment/refining capacity; payability, impurities, treatment charges, freight, and export rules affect netback. Strategic minerals may have concentrated refining even when mines are diversified. USGS estimated U.S. net imports of processed metals/materials at $185B in 2025 (**[Fact]** — https://www.usgs.gov/news/national-news-release/value-us-mineral-production-rose-last-year-driven-precious-metals-prices).

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** realized commodity price, grade/recovery, energy, labor, freight, and royalties.
- **Cap capacity:** permitted reserves, water/power, equipment, tailings, processing, and logistics.
- **Shock path:** grade decline → more rock per metal unit → energy/reagent/haul cost rises → unit cost and emissions rise → sustaining capital increases.

### 7. Full resource, consumables, infrastructure, and capital ledger

The first inputs are orebody quality, mineral title, geological data, social license, land and water rights. Development then requires drilling, explosives, diesel, electricity, tires, steel grinding media, crushers, mills, flotation reagents, acids, lime, cyanide where applicable, smelter fluxes, refractories, oxygen, anodes, cathode blanks, rail, slurry pipelines, roads, ports and laboratories. Steel, explosives and chemicals themselves trace to iron/scrap, coal, natural gas, sulfur, salt, minerals and power.

Each commodity has distinct metallurgical inputs: iron ore needs beneficiation/pelletizing and bulk freight; copper needs grinding/flotation plus smelting/refining or acid leach/SX-EW; aluminum needs bauxite, caustic soda, alumina refining, carbon anodes and enormous power; nickel/cobalt/lithium routes vary by ore/brine and chemistry; gold may require cyanide, pressure oxidation or roasting. Water balance, tailings capacity, grade, recovery and impurities determine whether the nominal reserve becomes saleable product.

Scarce skills include geologists, mine planners, metallurgists, operators, electricians, maintenance technicians, environmental and community specialists. Automation needs communications, sensors, software and cyber resilience. Insurance, reclamation bonds, streaming/royalty finance, working capital and customer qualification add nonphysical constraints.

Funding spans internal cash, public debt/equity, private equity, joint ventures, project and export finance, offtake/prepayment, royalties/streams, state ownership and government grants/loans for strategic minerals. Commodity price, grade, recovery, energy, reagents, freight, royalties/tax and FX set margins; permits, community consent, water/power, tailings, shafts, mills, smelters, rail/port and skilled labor gate output. Scrap, urban mining, material efficiency and substitution create secondary supply, but collection, alloy contamination, product life and qualification limit speed.

**Substitutes, complements, and adjacent markets:** Substitution is application-specific: aluminum can displace steel or copper; fiber, plastics, engineered wood, or composites can displace selected metals; different battery chemistries alter lithium, nickel, cobalt, manganese, graphite, and phosphate intensity; and thrifting lowers material per unit. Complements include power generation and grids, construction, vehicles, electronics, defense systems, machinery, smelters, recycling networks, freight, and commodity finance. Qualification, safety, conductivity, strength, corrosion, installed equipment, and recycling value constrain switching even when relative prices move sharply.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Commodity structures

Bulk iron ore and seaborne coal are concentrated and logistics-heavy; copper is globally traded with long project lead times; aluminum spans bauxite/alumina/smelting with power central; gold is reserve/currency driven; battery/rare-earth chains often have concentrated processing.

### 2. Profit pools

**[Inference]** Rent accrues to tier-one orebodies: high grade, large scale, long life, low strip, good jurisdiction, and advantaged infrastructure. Trading, royalties/streaming, and scarce processing can also earn attractive returns. Marginal mines set price in tight markets but destroy capital across cycles.

### 3. Geography and security

Resources are fixed geographically, while refining may be concentrated elsewhere. China is central in many processed-mineral chains; Australia/Latin America/Africa/Canada hold major reserves; Indonesia is important in nickel; Chile/Peru in copper; Australia/Brazil in iron ore. National policy increasingly conditions permits, exports, and local processing.

### 4. Gaining relevance

Copper/grid materials, selected critical minerals, brownfield expansions, automation, ore sorting, and recycling. Royalties/streamers gain when capital scarcity pushes miners to alternative finance. High-quality existing mines benefit because new supply is slow.

### 5. Losing relevance

Low-grade remote projects without power/water, commodity narratives without downstream demand proof, and high-cost mines valued on spot peak prices. Battery chemistry can change mineral intensity, so “critical” does not guarantee pricing power.

### 6. Policy and ESG

Permitting, indigenous/community consent, water, tailings safety, sanctions, export controls, and resource nationalism can dominate geology. USGS estimated U.S. mineral production value at $112B in 2025 and mineral-reliant industries at $4.09T (**[Fact]** — https://www.usgs.gov/news/national-news-release/value-us-mineral-production-rose-last-year-driven-precious-metals-prices).

### 7. Value migration

Toward existing low-cost assets, processing security, recycling, and jurisdictions able to permit; away from speculative greenfield tons. Scarcity value rises where demand grows faster than permitting and substitution.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs include ore, concentrates, cathodes, ingots, alloys and refined precious or battery metals; co-products can include gold, silver, molybdenum, sulfuric acid, cobalt and other payable elements. Waste rock, tailings, slag, acid drainage, emissions, water changes and closure liabilities are equally important outputs. Contract economics depend on grade, moisture, impurities, treatment/refining charges, payability and freight—not headline benchmark alone.

Customers include smelters/refiners, steel and alloy mills, fabricators, traders, battery and chemical producers, construction, automotive, electronics, utilities, aerospace and jewelry/investment markets. Traders and exchanges provide liquidity and inventory finance; OEMs increasingly use direct offtake to secure strategic materials. Governments may be owner, licensor, tax collector, financier and strategic buyer simultaneously.

Geology fixes mine location, while low-cost power, water and ports locate refining. Bulk ores follow rail and maritime corridors; precious metals can fly; concentrates may be constrained by hazardous impurities. China is central to many refining chains even where mining is elsewhere, making trade and industrial policy a separate layer from resource ownership.

Large diversified miners, single-asset developers, state-owned enterprises, artisanal/informal miners, royalty/streaming companies, traders, recyclers and processors have distinct risk. Private funding through public equity markets, specialist private/sovereign capital, royalties and streams supports proven scale or staged de-risking; public funding through development banks, export agencies and government programs supports infrastructure, strategic processing and stockpiles.

Mining, environmental, water, indigenous/community, labor and safety law; royalties and tax; export bans, local-beneficiation mandates, sanctions, investment screening, strategic-mineral policy, carbon pricing and product traceability determine value capture. Metals interact with energy, construction, autos, electronics, defense and currencies. A clean-energy buildout raises copper/aluminum/battery demand but also power and water needs; high metal prices induce thrift, substitution, scrap and delayed demand.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Workflow

Explore/drill → resource model → scoping/prefeasibility/feasibility → permit/finance → construct → strip/develop → mine → crush/grind → concentrate/leach → smelt/refine → transport → close/reclaim.

### 2. Mining methods

Open pit wins for shallow large orebodies but moves waste; underground suits deeper/high-grade deposits at higher complexity; solution/leach methods reduce material movement where metallurgy permits. Processing route follows mineralogy and impurities.

### 3. Unit economics

Revenue = payable metal × realized price plus by-products. Site cash cost includes mining, processing, site G&A, and freight/treatment; AISC adds sustaining capital and selected corporate/closure costs. Copper equivalent or by-product credits can obscure primary cost—reconcile physical outputs.

### 4. KPIs

Ore mined/milled, grade, recovery, payable production, strip ratio, cash cost/AISC, sustaining/growth capex, reserve life, resource conversion, equipment availability, and safety. For smelters track treatment/refining charges and utilization.

### 5. Development risk

NPV is most sensitive to price, recovery, capex, schedule, and discount rate. Ramp-up rarely reaches nameplate instantly. Reserve/resource categories carry different confidence; inferred resource is not equivalent to proven/probable reserve.

### 6. Failure modes

Grade reconciliation miss, geotechnical failure, tailings breach, recovery shortfall, water/power interruption, cost overrun, fiscal change, community opposition, and price-cycle leverage.

### 7. Valuation

Producing miners: NAV by mine plus normalized EV/EBITDA/FCF and cost-curve position. Developers: risked NAV with explicit permit/finance/build probabilities. Explorers: comparable value per resource unit only after grade, metallurgy, infrastructure, and jurisdiction adjustments. Never capitalize resources at spot value without extraction cost and dilution.

### 7. Complete reserve-to-cash and closure mechanics

The lifecycle is explore → define resource → engineer and permit → finance/build infrastructure and plant → develop mine → extract → crush/grind/separate or leach → smelt/refine → sell/ship → progressively rehabilitate → close and monitor. Resources are geological estimates; reserves require economic, metallurgical, legal and modifying factors. Grade, strip ratio, dilution, recovery, throughput and payability convert rock moved into payable metal.

Revenue equals payable metal × realized benchmark, adjusted for quotational periods, treatment/refining charges, penalties, premiums, hedges and freight. By-product accounting can move a mine down the reported cost curve but should not hide exposure to co-product prices. Royalties, production sharing and windfall taxes alter operating leverage.

Working capital includes ore stockpiles, concentrate in transit, consumables, receivables, provisional pricing and derivative collateral. Sustaining capital covers stripping, underground development, tailings, equipment rebuilds and plant integrity; growth capital expands mine/plant or builds new assets. Closure security and water treatment can persist for decades and must be included in NAV and leverage.

Track reserve/resource grade and life, strip ratio, ore and waste moved, throughput, recovery, payable production, unit cash cost and all-in sustaining cost, realized price, treatment charges, energy/water intensity, equipment availability, capex versus plan, project schedule, safety, permit/community status and closure liability. Stress lower grade/recovery, commodity and FX moves, power/water outage, tailings restriction, freight disruption, tax/royalty change, capex inflation, labor action and delayed ramp-up.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Payable-metal margin = recovered contained metal × realized price − treatment/refining, penalties, freight, royalties and operating cost. Full-cycle value subtracts sustaining stripping/development, growth capex, financing, tax, reclamation, closure, and dilution.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ore grade | commodity and deposit specific; track trend versus reserve | Productivity and depletion |
| Recovery | commonly 70–95% for many flotation/leach systems, route-specific | Metal lost in process |
| Strip ratio | waste tons per ore ton | Open-pit cost and future stripping |
| Reserve life | often 8–25+ years for operating mines | Duration and reinvestment |
| AISC or sustaining cost | $/oz or $/lb including defined sustaining items | Through-cycle cash resilience |
| Treatment/refining charges | $/ton concentrate and cents/lb or metal terms | Mine-smelter bargaining |
| Energy intensity | kWh/GJ per ton or metal unit | Power and carbon exposure |
| Water intensity | withdrawal/consumption per ton | Site and social constraint |
| Project lead time | often 7–15+ years from discovery to operation | Supply response |
| Closure security | bond/provision versus current closure estimate | Unfunded tail liability |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile reserves and mine plan to payable output; inventory across ore stockpile, concentrate, and metal; provisional pricing; stripping capitalization; by-product credits; royalties/streams; sustaining versus expansion capital; and undiscounted closure/water obligations.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Commodity, FX, grade, recovery, strip ratio, treatment charges, freight, power and reagents.
- Permits, community/indigenous consent, water, tailings, labor, rail/port and politics.
- Project schedule/capex, ramp yield, reserve conversion and financing.
- Scrap supply, substitution, downstream qualification, export bans and strategic stockpiles.

### Valuation discipline

Use risked asset NAV with mine plans, commodity/FX decks, capital, tax/royalty, and closure; compare corporate multiple and balance sheet. Resource ounces/tons without modifying factors are not value.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2003–2011 commodity supercycle | Chinese industrialization met slow mine supply | Prices and capex surged; later projects arrived into weaker markets | Long lead creates cyclic overshoot |
| 2015 mining downturn | Prior expansion met slower demand and high leverage | Impairments, dividend cuts and asset sales followed | Balance sheet is part of cost curve |
| 2015 Samarco and 2019 Brumadinho disasters | Tailings failures caused fatalities and shutdowns | Liability, standards and social license changed globally | Waste infrastructure is production capacity |
| 2014–2020 Indonesian nickel policy | Ore-export restrictions encouraged domestic processing | Investment and supply shifted geography and technology | Industrial policy can relocate the value chain |
| 2021–2024 battery-mineral volatility | Demand expectations induced rapid investment and inventory changes | Lithium/nickel prices and project economics reversed sharply | TAM growth does not prevent commodity cycles |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Prices/curves, inventories, TC/RC, grade/recovery, permits, capex, project milestones, freight, power, export policy, and scrap.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Resource growth without recovery/capital/permit, cost improvement driven only by by-product price, or strategic-premium claims without qualification/offtake.
- **Durable lesson:** Value sits in payable, permitted, financeable metal after metallurgy, infrastructure, fiscal take, and closure.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One payable metric tonne of copper from an operating mine.

**Core equation:** `Pre-tax site cash/t = contained metal × recovery/payability × realized price + by-products − mining/processing − treatment/freight − royalty − sustaining/closure`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Payable revenue | $9,000/t copper × 97% payability | $8,730/t before hedges and by-products |
| Mine and concentrator cost | Drill/blast/haul, grind, flotation, labor, power and reagents | $4,200/t payable |
| Treatment, refining and freight | Smelter terms, penalties and logistics | $650/t |
| Royalty | Illustrative production/value-linked burden | $350/t |
| Sustaining and closure accrual | $1,000/t sustaining + $150/t closure/water liability | $1,150/t |
| Illustrative pre-tax cash | $8,730 − $4,200 − $650 − $350 − $1,150 | $2,380/t; grade and recovery changes can dominate price |

**Decision test:** Model the reserve block by grade, recovery, strip, throughput, impurity and schedule; reported cash cost with by-product credits cannot substitute for a full payable-metal bridge.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Highest incentive-cost mine, expansion, scrap source or stockpile needed to balance refined demand | Ore quality, co-products, jurisdiction and processing route make the curve metal-specific |
| Marginal customer | Fabricator/end user able to thrift, substitute, delay inventory or scrap less | Downstream margins and qualification limit demand response |
| Clearing mechanism | Exchange/contract benchmarks plus location, grade, treatment, freight and premium/discount | Mine price, concentrate terms and refined price are distinct |
| Cash shutdown point | Revenue below avoidable mine/processing cost, considering care-and-maintenance and restart | High fixed and closure costs can sustain loss-making output temporarily |
| New-capacity incentive | Long-run price supports exploration, development, infrastructure, tax, closure and risk-adjusted return | Permitting and construction duration create scarcity premiums |
| Adjustment lag | Months for scrap/stockpiles, years for expansions, often a decade-plus for greenfield mines | Forecast horizon determines the relevant supply curve |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Contained metal | Recovered or payable metal | Apply dilution, recovery, smelter payability, penalties and ownership |
| Ore grade | Economic quality | Combine with hardness, strip ratio, mineralogy, recovery, scale and infrastructure |
| Cash cost/AISC | Comparable full cost | Normalize by-products, sustaining scope, royalties, corporate cost and closure |
| Resources | Reserves or funded production | Apply economics, modifying factors, permits, schedule and capital |
| Copper-equivalent output | Fungible revenue | State price assumptions, recoveries, payabilities and commodity mix |
