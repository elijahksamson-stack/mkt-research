## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Commodity and specialty chemicals, petrochemicals, industrial gases, fertilizers, coatings/additives, and formulation businesses. Pharmaceuticals and finished consumer products are excluded except as customers.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Olefins, aromatics, and polymers | Ethylene/propylene/aromatics and resins | tons, nameplate capacity, spread | Feedstock slate, integration, grade, and derivative chain |
| Chlor-alkali and inorganics | Chlorine, caustic, soda ash, acids, pigments, industrial gases | tons, ECU, purity | Co-product balance, power, local logistics, and hazardous handling |
| Nitrogen, phosphate, and potash | Ammonia, urea, phosphates, potash and blends | nutrient tons, product tons | N/P/K nutrient basis, gas/mineral source, season and freight |
| Specialty and performance chemicals | Additives, catalysts, electronic chemicals, flavors, adhesives and process aids | kg/tons, formulation, retention | Customer qualification, application value, purity, and IP |
| Coatings and agricultural chemicals | Formulated protective, decorative, crop and seed-treatment products | gallons/tons, active ingredient | Active ingredient, formulation, regulation, channel inventory and season |

### Specifications that change value

- State molecule/formulation, concentration, grade, purity, impurity limits, and approved application.
- Identify feedstock route and co-product slate; ethane, naphtha, coal, and bio routes are not equivalent.
- Separate nameplate, effective, operating, and saleable-grade capacity.
- State contract index, freight basis, packaging, minimum order, and pass-through lag.
- For specialties, identify customer qualification, formulation ownership, switching cost, and value-in-use.

### Role map

Resource/refiner supplies molecules or minerals; basic producer converts; derivative and formulator tailor; distributor holds inventory/credit; customer qualifies and consumes; regulators govern plant emissions, transport, worker safety, and product use.

### Terms that must be explicit

- ECU and co-product economics
- cracker and derivative integration
- commodity spread versus specialty contribution margin
- active ingredient versus formulated product
- mechanical recycling, chemical recycling, and mass balance


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


### 1. Hydrocarbon and mineral feedstocks

Ethane, propane, butane, naphtha, condensate, natural gas, salt, sulfur, phosphates, potash, and industrial minerals feed chemical chains. Dow identifies hydrocarbon feedstocks as its major raw-material streams and buys natural gas and power for energy (**[Fact]** — 2025 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1751788/000175178826000018/dow-20251231.htm). Feedstock geography creates cost advantage: U.S. ethane versus European/Asian naphtha is a recurring spread.

### 2. Energy, utilities, and infrastructure

Steam crackers, chlor-alkali, ammonia, and other processes are power/heat intensive. Reliable gas, electricity, steam, cooling water, storage, rail, pipelines, ports, and waste treatment determine viable sites. **[Inference]** Energy is both feedstock and fuel, so gas shocks can damage chemical margin twice.

### 3. Catalysts, technology, and skilled operations

Process licenses, catalysts, formulations, application labs, engineers, and safe plant operators are high-leverage inputs. Specialty chemicals depend more on application knowledge and customer qualification; commodities depend more on scale and cost curve.

### 4. Capital and turnarounds

World-scale plants require large, irreversible capital and periodic turnarounds. Long construction lead times and cyclic pricing create supply overshoot. Environmental liabilities and closure obligations raise the true capital requirement.

### 5. Regulation and trade

Chemical registration, product stewardship, emissions, hazardous transport, anti-dumping duties, tariffs, and carbon policy shape costs and market access. Customer sustainability requirements drive recycled/renewable feedstocks, but economics require collection, sorting, and end-demand systems.

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** product-feedstock spread, energy, utilization, freight, and price/mix.
- **Cap capacity:** permitted plants, crackers/reactors, catalysts, utilities, turnarounds, and logistics.
- **Shock path:** gas/feedstock rise → variable cost rises → commodity spreads compress unless capacity exits → specialties reprice later through contracts and formulation value.

### 7. Full feedstock and enabling-input ledger

The feedstock tree must be mapped molecule by molecule. Petrochemicals begin with ethane, propane, butane, naphtha, condensate, aromatics and refinery streams; chlor-alkali begins with salt, water and electricity; ammonia and methanol usually begin with natural gas or coal plus air, water and power; fertilizers add phosphate rock, potash, sulfur and ammonia; inorganics draw on sulfur, limestone, fluorspar, titanium minerals, soda ash and industrial gases; specialties may add silicones, fluorochemicals, pigments, solvents, enzymes, bio-based oils and high-purity intermediates. Recycled, captured-carbon and biomass routes require collection, sorting, pretreatment and chain-of-custody rather than “free” feedstock.

Plants also require catalysts, process licensors and proprietary recipes, steam, electricity, cooling and demineralized water, industrial gases, corrosion-resistant alloys, reactors, compressors, heat exchangers, tanks, instrumentation, filters, packaging, railcars, pipelines, barges and hazardous-material logistics. Catalyst supply can depend on platinum-group metals, rare earths or specialty supports. Reliable waste treatment, flare and emissions capacity are productive inputs because they constrain permitted throughput.

Skilled operators, chemical/process engineers, maintenance crafts, application scientists, toxicologists, regulatory data, quality systems, cybersecurity and customer qualification are critical intangible inputs. Capital needs include working capital for feedstock/inventory, turnarounds, environmental reserves, insurance and long-cycle plant construction; funding may be internal cash, debt/equity, joint ventures, project finance, government grants/loans or customer offtake.

Feedstock-product spread, energy, utilization, freight, FX and carbon cost set commodity margins; patents/formulation value and customer productivity set specialty pricing. Crackers, reactors, power, hydrogen, chlorine handling, catalysts, permits, waste treatment, storage and transport gate capacity. Down-gauging, reformulation, mechanical or chemical recycling, bio-feedstocks and material substitution can reduce virgin use, but often shift cost to sorting, quality loss, energy, land or another commodity chain.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Industry layers

Upstream oil/gas/minerals → basic petrochemicals and inorganics → polymers/intermediates → formulated specialties → distribution → end markets. Commodity chemicals compete on cost curve and utilization; specialties compete on formulation, qualification, service, and switching cost.

### 2. Profit pools

**[Inference]** Durable rent is stronger in catalysts, electronic materials, coatings/additives, water treatment, and other products that are low-cost in the customer's bill but high-cost to fail or requalify. Bulk polyethylene, PVC, methanol, and similar products are cyclical and vulnerable to new low-cost capacity.

### 3. Geography

U.S. Gulf Coast combines shale feedstock, pipelines, ports, and scale. Middle East producers pair low-cost hydrocarbons with export plants. China is the largest marginal source of demand and capacity in many chains. Europe faces higher energy cost and rationalization pressure; Japan/Europe/U.S. retain specialty and process strengths.

### 4. Customers and channels

Automotive, construction, packaging, electronics, agriculture, consumer goods, and industrial production drive demand. Distributors add fragmented-customer access, inventory, blending, and compliance. Qualification can lock specialty suppliers in for product cycles.

### 5. Gaining relevance

Electronic/semiconductor chemicals, battery materials, water treatment, lightweighting, lower-emissions process technology, and verified circular feedstocks. Application expertise and customer co-development deepen moats.

### 6. Losing relevance

High-cost undifferentiated European capacity, commodity expansions justified by peak spreads, and circular projects without economic feedstock collection. Dow said lower 2025 feedstock/energy costs were offset partly by lower operating rates (**[Fact]** — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1751788/000175178826000018/dow-20251231.htm), illustrating that cheap inputs do not fix oversupply.

### 7. Value migration

From volume commodity exposure toward specialties, services, and closed-loop customer solutions; from high-cost regions to advantaged feedstock/energy basins; and from virgin-only products toward circular/renewable content where policy and customer willingness pay.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, capital, and policy map

Outputs range from olefins, aromatics, polymers, chlor-alkali, methanol, ammonia and fertilizers to coatings, adhesives, additives, electronic chemicals, catalysts, crop protection, flavors/fragrances and water-treatment chemicals. Hydrogen, steam, sulfur products and other co-products can carry value; CO2, contaminated water, hazardous waste, off-spec material and legacy remediation carry cost. A plant's economics therefore depend on the entire product slate, not one headline molecule.

Customers include converters and compounders, packaging, automotive, construction, electronics and semiconductor fabs, agriculture, mining, energy, consumer-goods companies, healthcare, water utilities and other chemical producers. Distributors serve fragmented customers and hold inventory/credit; large customers qualify formulations over long periods. The purchaser may optimize unit price while the user's process values yield, downtime, safety or regulatory compliance.

Feedstock advantage and logistics create regional clusters: hydrocarbon hubs near low-cost molecules and ports; fertilizer near gas or minerals and farm demand; specialties near technical customers and research talent. Pipelines integrate local complexes, while bulk solids and liquids move by ship/rail/barge; hazardous and high-purity products have narrower qualified routes. Chinese capacity, Middle Eastern feedstocks, US gas economics and European energy/carbon costs interact through trade.

Commodity chemicals are cyclical and scale-intensive; specialties are more fragmented but can have application and qualification moats. State-owned firms, integrated refiners, diversified public groups, sponsor-owned carve-outs and private niche formulators have different capital thresholds. Private funding includes corporate debt/equity, project finance and customer-supported capacity; public funding includes export finance, industrial-policy grants/loans and tax incentives.

Policy includes chemical registration and disclosure, worker/process safety, air and water permits, hazardous transport, product liability, pesticide/food/pharma approvals, plastics and packaging rules, carbon pricing and border adjustments, anti-dumping duties, sanctions and site remediation. Cross-markets include oil/gas and refining, power, agriculture and food, mining, construction, packaging, semiconductors and waste. A chemical price shock can propagate several tiers into manufactured goods even when chemical value is a small share of final retail price.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Production workflow

Feedstock preparation → reaction/cracking/polymerization → separation/purification → compounding/formulation → quality testing → bulk or packaged shipment. Continuous plants maximize uptime; batch plants offer flexibility and specialty mix.

### 2. Commodity vs. specialty mechanics

- **Commodity:** price set by marginal cost and regional trade; utilization and feedstock spread dominate.
- **Specialty:** value-in-use, formulation, qualification, and service drive price; mix and innovation matter more than tons.
- **Industrial gas/captive contracts:** onsite plants and long-term take-or-pay can create utility-like cash flows.

### 3. Capacity and cycle

Capacity is nameplate tons, but effective output depends on yields, turnaround, debottlenecking, and operating rate. New world-scale plants arrive in lumps, creating multi-year downcycles. Commodity inventory and spreads turn before reported earnings.

### 4. KPIs

Volume, price/mix, product-feedstock spread, operating rate, EBITDA/ton, maintenance/turnaround spending, working capital, ROIC, and free cash flow. For specialties add new-product vitality, retention, and end-market mix.

### 5. Failure modes

Plant incident, catalyst/process failure, oversupply, customer destocking, environmental liability, fixed-cost under-absorption, and confusing price with mix. “Specialty” is not a label: products must show pricing, retention, margin, and lower cyclicality.

### 6. Valuation

Commodity producers: normalized EV/EBITDA, replacement value, and cost-curve position. Specialties: P/E/EV-EBITDA, organic growth, margin, FCF, and ROIC. Use sum-of-parts for mixed portfolios and adjust EBITDA for pension, environmental, and turnaround obligations.

### 7. Complete conversion, contract, and lifecycle mechanics

The operating chain is source and qualify feedstock → store/blend → react, separate and purify → formulate or polymerize → test/certify → package or pipe → deliver and support application → recover, treat or dispose residuals → turnaround and eventually remediate. Mass balance, stoichiometric yield, selectivity, energy intensity, uptime, grade transitions, co-product credits and off-spec rates determine true output.

Pricing ranges from daily/weekly commodity benchmarks and formula contracts to value-based specialty pricing, tolling, licensing and take-or-pay. Feedstock and energy escalators can protect dollar margin while compressing percentage margin or working capital. Contract floors, ceilings and lags matter most during shocks. Distributor sell-in can overstate end demand when inventory is building.

Working capital is driven by feedstock, in-process and finished inventory, railcars/tankers, receivables, customer rebates and volatile replacement cost. Maintenance capital includes turnarounds, catalyst, integrity and environmental work; growth capital includes debottlenecks, new trains and application labs. Environmental provisions and closure costs must be added to full-cycle economics.

Track volume by product/grade, price versus feedstock spread, utilization, yield, energy and water intensity, co-product credit, fixed-cost absorption, turnaround days, order and inventory trends, contract pass-through lag, specialty retention and new-product mix, capex, ROIC and incident/emissions performance. Stress feedstock outage, energy spike, customer destocking, Chinese oversupply, tariff/carbon-border change, catalyst shortage, unplanned shutdown, drought and a tighter product-safety rule.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Product contribution = saleable product and co-product revenue − feedstock − energy/utilities − catalysts/chemicals − variable conversion − packaging/freight. Full return subtracts fixed plant cost, turnaround, sustaining/environmental capital, R&D/application support, and closure.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Commodity-plant utilization | roughly 80–95% in balanced markets | Fixed-cost absorption and cycle |
| World-scale plant lead time | often 3–6+ years | Supply response and overshoot |
| Major turnaround interval | commonly 3–6 years by unit | Normalized uptime and cash |
| Energy/feedstock share | often the majority of cash conversion cost in basics | Sensitivity to gas/oil/power |
| Specialty gross margin | frequently materially above commodity, but portfolio-specific | Value-in-use and qualification |
| Working capital | inventory and receivable days by molecule/channel | Cash impact of price inflation |
| Yield/selectivity | saleable output per feedstock and co-product profile | Process advantage |
| Freight radius | local for hazardous/bulky products; global for high-value products | Market boundary |
| R&D/application spend | percent of sales and technical headcount | Specialty moat investment |
| Carbon/water intensity | per ton of saleable product | Policy and site constraint |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Separate inventory-price effects from current spread, transfer prices within integrated chains, and co-product credits. Normalize turnarounds, environmental remediation, restructuring, and commodity under-absorption. Acquisition amortization should not erase the cash cost of replenishing specialty innovation.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Oil, NGL, natural gas, coal, electricity, minerals, carbon, and freight.
- Utilization, Chinese/Middle Eastern additions, customer destocking, and contract lags.
- Yield, catalyst, unplanned outage, water/waste permit, and hazardous logistics.
- Customer qualification, patent/formulation loss, channel inventory, and regulation of use.

### Valuation discipline

Use asset and chain-specific through-cycle spreads for basics; retention, organic price/volume, application moat, and ROIC for specialties. Mixed portfolios need sum-of-parts.


## 6. Regulation and Public Funding

### Permission chain

Resource/refiner supplies molecules or minerals; basic producer converts; derivative and formulator tailor; distributor holds inventory/credit; customer qualifies and consumes; regulators govern plant emissions, transport, worker safety, and product use.

### Jurisdiction and regime map

| Jurisdiction or layer | Core regimes and authorities | Economic transmission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | EPA air/water/TSCA/pesticide programs; OSHA process safety; DOT hazardous transport | Plant permits, chemical review, use approval, remediation, and transport |
| European Union | REACH/CLP, industrial emissions, EU ETS and CBAM exposure, product-specific rules | Registration, substitution, carbon, and import compliance |

| International trade | Anti-dumping, tariffs, sanctions, hazardous-goods codes, food/electronics customer standards | Market access and chain-of-custody |

### Public and private funding

Private funding includes corporate debt/equity, project finance, JVs, customer-backed capacity and sponsor carve-outs. Public funding includes export credit, grants/loans, tax incentives, industrial parks/utilities, recycling and decarbonization support.

### Enforcement and liability

Exposure includes plant shutdown, product cancellation, registration loss, cleanup liability, criminal process-safety action, transport claims, anti-dumping duty, and customer disqualification.

### Update checklist

- Confirm entity, asset, product, customer, location, and authority.
- Separate law, final rule, guidance, permit, litigation, and proposal.
- Date funding, tax, tariff, sanction, quota, and administered-price terms.
- Trace cost, schedule, capacity, price, liability, and funding separately.


## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1970s oil shocks | Petrochemical feedstock costs repriced rapidly | Integrated and alternative-feedstock positions diverged | Feedstock route is a strategic asset |
| 2005 Gulf hurricanes | US refining and chemical sites/logistics shut | Regional chlorine/resin and feedstock markets tightened | Cluster efficiency creates correlated outage risk |
| 2008–2009 demand collapse | Industrial customers destocked faster than end demand | Volumes and utilization fell abruptly | Inventory cycles amplify GDP |
| 2021 Texas freeze | Power/gas/water outages shut interconnected plants | Shortages propagated into autos, packaging, and construction | Utility resilience is chemical capacity |
| 2022 European gas shock and later oversupply | Gas-intensive European cost rose as Asian capacity expanded | Margins compressed and closures increased | Regional cost curve and trade matter more than global demand alone |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Feedstock/product spreads, operating rates, capacity announcements, inventories, customer orders, freight, gas/power, antidumping actions, and outages.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Specialty claims without retention or price/mix, commodity EBITDA dependent on inventory gains, or new capacity without derivative/logistics detail.
- **Durable lesson:** A chemical market is a molecule, route, grade, co-product, site, and customer qualification—not a generic ton.

### Indicator stack

- **Leading:** feedstock spreads; customer PMIs/orders; capacity starts; anti-dumping; inventories.
- **Coincident:** production; utilization; price/mix; energy use; freight; outages.
- **Lagging:** closures; remediation; customer reformulation; ROIC; emissions capital.

### Minimum dashboard

1. **Commodity-plant utilization** — roughly 80–95% in balanced markets; Fixed-cost absorption and cycle.
2. **World-scale plant lead time** — often 3–6+ years; Supply response and overshoot.
3. **Major turnaround interval** — commonly 3–6 years by unit; Normalized uptime and cash.
4. **Energy/feedstock share** — often the majority of cash conversion cost in basics; Sensitivity to gas/oil/power.
5. **Specialty gross margin** — frequently materially above commodity, but portfolio-specific; Value-in-use and qualification.
6. **Working capital** — inventory and receivable days by molecule/channel; Cash impact of price inflation.
7. **Yield/selectivity** — saleable output per feedstock and co-product profile; Process advantage.
8. **Freight radius** — local for hazardous/bulky products; global for high-value products; Market boundary.
9. **R&D/application spend** — percent of sales and technical headcount; Specialty moat investment.
10. **Carbon/water intensity** — per ton of saleable product; Policy and site constraint.

### Normalization rules

- Use nutrient, concentration, purity, and grade-adjusted tons.
- Separate price, volume, mix, feedstock, energy, FX and inventory.
- Map transfer prices across integrated chains.
- Date capacity by mechanical completion, qualification, and saleable ramp.

### Evidence traps

- Calling every specialty product equally defensible.
- Treating announced capacity as qualified supply.
- Ignoring co-product balance such as chlorine/caustic.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

Geography changes ownership, contracting, cost, funding, regulation and risk; compare like with like.

| Geography / archetype | Operating model | Economic and risk implications |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States Gulf Coast | Integrated feedstock-advantaged petrochemical complexes tied to gas liquids, pipelines and export docks | Ethane/gas advantage, hurricanes, logistics and export demand drive cycles |
| Europe | Older integrated sites facing gas/power, carbon and regulatory costs with specialty strengths | Energy spreads and restructuring can outweigh nominal demand |
| China | Large state/private integrated coal-, oil- and import-linked capacity serving domestic manufacturing | Policy-led additions and self-sufficiency can reset global balances |
| Middle East | Low-cost feedstock and large export-oriented joint ventures | Feedstock allocation, partner contracts and freight shape economics |
| India and other growth markets | Import substitution, refining integration and expanding local demand | Infrastructure, tariffs and customer qualification influence buildout |

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Feedstocks | Oil, gas, NGLs, coal, salt, sulfur, minerals, agricultural oils and industrial gases | Relative feedstock routes reorder regional cost curves |
| Utilities and plant inputs | Steam, power, water, catalysts, solvents, pressure equipment and skilled operators | Energy and outage risk are amplified by continuous-process utilization |
| Downstream customers | Packaging, autos, construction, agriculture, electronics, pharma, textiles and consumer goods | Demand is derivative and specification-specific; inventory cycles magnify swings |
| Logistics and trade | Pipelines, tanks, railcars, ships, hazardous transport and customs | Storage and route limits can create regional price islands |
| Capital/policy/externality | Project finance, corporate balance sheets, carbon cost, product safety and waste/recycling systems | Long asset life and environmental liability raise the full-cycle hurdle |

The practical test is to trace a shock through availability, delivered cost, working capital, output, customer economics, financing and eventual substitution—not merely name the adjacent market.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** One metric tonne of commodity polymer from an integrated plant.

**Core equation:** `Cash contribution/t = net selling price − feedstock conversion requirement − energy/catalyst − packaging/freight − variable labor − allocated fixed cash cost`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Net selling price | Quality- and location-adjusted polymer price | $1,200/t |
| Feedstock | Route-specific monomer/feed requirement | $450/t |
| Energy, catalyst and consumables | Steam, power, process loss and chemicals | $150/t |
| Packaging, freight and variable conversion | Bags/bulk handling, logistics and direct labor | $180/t |
| Fixed cash cost | Maintenance organization, site overhead and environmental systems at normal utilization | $120/t |
| Illustrative contribution | $1,200 − $450 − $150 − $180 − $120 | $300/t before sustaining capital, tax and cycle downtime |

**Decision test:** Compare variable margin for short-run operation and full-cycle return for capacity; normalize feedstock route, co-product credit, utilization and contract lag.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Highest variable-cost plant needed after trade and outages | Feedstock, energy, scale, age and co-products define route-specific cost |
| Marginal customer | Converter able to destock, reformulate, substitute material or curtail production | Order rates and customer inventory lead apparent demand |
| Clearing mechanism | Contract/spot prices linked to feedstocks, regional balances and trade parity | List prices are less informative than net transaction price |
| Cash shutdown point | Net price below avoidable feedstock, energy and variable conversion cost | Continuous operations, restart cost and customer contracts complicate shutdown |
| New-capacity incentive | Cycle-average margin supports construction, working capital, environmental and cost-of-capital burden | Peak spreads frequently attract late capacity |
| Adjustment lag | Weeks for inventory/imports, months for operating rates, years for world-scale plants | Inventory cycles and capacity cycles must be separated |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Capacity | Saleable output | Adjust for utilization, turnaround, yield, grade mix and captive consumption |
| Spread | EBITDA margin | Include conversion ratio, co-products, energy, freight, discounts and fixed cost |
| Volume | Underlying demand | Separate channel inventory, exports, captive transfers and price-led mix |
| Specialty chemical | Structurally high margin | Test formulation/IP, switching cost, service content and customer concentration |
| Feedstock advantage | Durable delivered advantage | Add contract terms, energy, logistics, carbon and product destination |
