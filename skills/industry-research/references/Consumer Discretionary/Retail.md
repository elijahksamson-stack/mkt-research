## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

General merchandise, apparel, home improvement, specialty, department, discount, dollar, club, e-commerce, marketplace and direct-to-consumer retail. Grocery is separate.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Mass, discount, dollar and club | Broad/value assortments and membership | sales, stores, members | Format, membership, consumables mix and price |
| Apparel and specialty | Category-focused discretionary retail | units, comps, inventory | Fashion/basic, brand, season and markdown |
| Home improvement and big ticket | Building/home/furniture/electronics categories | tickets, units, pro/DIY | Rates, housing, project, delivery and finance |
| Department and mall-based | Multi-category concession/wholesale retail | sales, area, traffic | Concession, brand mix, real estate and promotion |
| E-commerce, marketplace and DTC | Owned inventory, third-party marketplace and brand direct | GMV, orders, revenue | Principal/agent, fulfillment, returns and acquisition |

### Specifications that change value

- State category, format, channel, geography, price tier, customer mission and owned/marketplace inventory.
- Comparable sales need store cohort, currency, calendar, openings/closures and e-commerce definition.
- Revenue must distinguish GMV, merchandise sales, commission, membership, advertising and finance.
- Gross margin needs initial markup, vendor funds, freight/duty, markdown, shrink and returns.
- Inventory needs units/cost/retail, age, ownership, in-transit and vendor terms.

### Role map

Vendor/brand supplies; retailer buys/curates; marketplace connects; landlord provides site; payment/lender funds; logistics fulfills; shopper chooses; user consumes; advertiser buys retail media; regulator protects.

### Terms that must be explicit

- GMV versus net revenue
- sell-in versus sell-through
- comparable sales, traffic and ticket
- initial margin versus realized gross margin
- owned, consignment, dropship and marketplace


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope note: "Retail" here means the businesses that buy finished goods and resell them to end consumers — mass/discount (Walmart, Target), warehouse clubs (Costco), grocery (Kroger, Albertsons), e-commerce marketplaces (Amazon), home improvement (Home Depot, Lowe's), and specialty/apparel/off-price. U.S. retail and food-services sales run at roughly a **$760B/month** run-rate as of mid-2026 (advance estimate May 2026: $763.7B) [Fact] (U.S. Census Bureau MARTS, 2026 — https://www.census.gov/retail/marts/www/marts_current.pdf). Retail is a **spread business**: it does not make the goods, it moves them. Its inputs are therefore not raw materials in the mining sense but the cost blocks between "landed merchandise" and "customer's hand." The five that actually determine margin: (1) cost of goods / vendor terms, (2) labor, (3) occupancy/real estate, (4) logistics & freight, and (5) shrink & working capital. Below, each is mapped for suppliers, cost sensitivity, bottlenecks, substitutes, and where pricing power sits.

### 1. Merchandise (Cost of Goods Sold) — the dominant input

COGS is 76–89% of revenue for most formats; it dwarfs every other cost. Walmart's FY2025 gross margin was 24.1% (so COGS ≈ 75.9%) [Fact] (Walmart 10-K FY2025 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/104169/000010416925000021/wmt-20250131.htm). Costco runs COGS of ~89% (merchandise gross margin only ~10.9%) [Fact] (Costco 10-K FY2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/909832/000090983224000049/cost-20240901.htm). **Because COGS is so large, a 1% change in cost of goods swings operating profit more than a 20% change in almost any other line.** [Inference]

- **Suppliers:** Two tiers. (a) Branded CPG/vendors (Procter & Gamble, Coca-Cola, Nestlé, Apple, Nike) — these hold pricing power against smaller retailers but negotiate roughly at parity with Walmart/Amazon/Costco, whose scale lets them dictate terms, slotting fees, and payment days. (b) Contract manufacturers (largely Asia) for **private label**, where the retailer owns the brand and captures the manufacturer's margin.
- **Pricing power:** Sits with whichever side has scale. Walmart (~$681B revenue) and Amazon extract the best vendor terms in the world; a regional grocer does not. This is the core structural reason retail concentrates. [Inference]
- **Substitute / margin lever — private label:** Store brands now hold ~24% U.S. grocery unit share, growing 4.4% vs. 1.1% for national brands (H1 2024) [Fact] (Talk Business/SupplySide, 2025 — https://talkbusiness.net/2025/01/the-supply-side-private-brands-continue-to-grow-retail-market-share-in-2024/). Aldi and Trader Joe's are ~80% and ~70% private label [Fact] (same source). Private label typically carries **several hundred basis points more gross margin** than the comparable national brand [Estimate], which is why it is the single most important margin lever a retailer controls directly.

### 2. Imported goods, tariffs & country-of-origin risk

Nearly **98% of clothing sold in the U.S. is imported** [Fact] (AAFA/NRF via World-Collective, 2025 — https://world-collective.com/blogs/news/tariff-roundup-for-fashion-brands-2025). This makes trade policy a direct input cost.

- **Tariff shock propagation:** As of 2025, layered duties (baseline + Section 301 + reciprocal) reached ~45.5% total on some Chinese apparel [Fact] (Freightos, 2025 — https://www.freightos.com/freight-resources/how-to-calculate-china-tariffs-in-2025/). NRF estimates a 10% apparel tariff raises retail prices 3–5% [Fact/Estimate] (NRF via World-Collective, 2025). Retailers with thin margins (grocery, off-price) cannot absorb this and must pass it through, dampening unit volume.
- **Single-point-of-failure:** Concentration in China/Vietnam. Diversification "hit a wall" because 2025 reciprocal tariffs hit Vietnam (46%) and India (26%) too [Fact] (same). There is no cheap escape hatch — the bottleneck is the entire low-cost Asian manufacturing base.
- **Where power sits:** With the U.S. government (tariff-setting) and with a handful of mega-importers who can pre-buy, re-route, or renegotiate. Small retailers are price-takers on landed cost.

### 3. Labor

Retail trade (NAICS 44–45) employs on the order of **15 million** U.S. workers; retail salespersons alone number ~7.68M (May 2025) [Fact] (BLS OEWS, 2025 — https://www.bls.gov/oes/2024/may/featured_data.htm). Median retail sales wage was **$17.46/hr / $36,320/yr** (May 2025), up from $16.62 (May 2024) [Fact] (BLS, 2025 — https://www.bls.gov/ooh/sales/retail-sales-workers.htm).

- **Cost sensitivity:** Labor (store payroll + DC labor) is typically the **second-largest cost after COGS**, ~10–12% of sales for mass, higher for service-heavy formats. Because operating margins are 3–6% (mass) or 1–3% (grocery), a minimum-wage step-up or a tight labor market can erase a year of margin gains. [Inference]
- **Scarcity:** Low skill barrier means high turnover (often >60%/yr) but chronic understaffing during tight labor markets; that turnover itself is a cost (rehire/train). Specialized labor — DC/warehouse operators, pharmacists, HVAC/logistics engineers, retail-media ad-tech staff — is scarcer and commands premiums.
- **Substitute — automation:** Self-checkout and automated fulfillment are the direct labor substitutes. BLS projects cashier employment to fall ~314,000 (2024–34), the largest projected decline of any occupation, driven by self-checkout [Fact] (BLS, 2026 — https://www.bls.gov/opub/mlr/2026/article/industry-and-occupational-employment-projections-overview.htm). **This is the clearest input-substitution story in retail: capital (kiosks, robots) directly displacing a labor input whose unit cost keeps rising.** [Inference]
- **Pricing power:** Historically with the employer, but eroding — unionization drives (Amazon warehouses, Starbucks), state minimum-wage laws, and gig-labor regulation shift power toward labor.

### 4. Real estate / occupancy

For physical formats, store leases are a large fixed input. National average retail occupancy cost rose to **7.73% of sales in 2024**, up from 5.83% in 2023 — a six-year high — with average rents at $16.59/sq ft [Fact] (Datex via Chain Store Age, 2025 — https://chainstoreage.com/datex-retail-real-estate-occupancy-costs-hit-six-year-high-2024).

- **Cost sensitivity by format:** A "healthy" occupancy ratio is ~2.5% for grocery but 12%+ for apparel [Fact] (Datex/AICRE, 2024). This is why high-rent formats live or die on sales-per-square-foot: rent is fixed, so it leverages violently against a sales decline. [Inference]
- **Bottleneck:** Prime A-mall and dense-urban locations are supply-constrained; that scarcity is the landlord's pricing power. Suburban strip and second-tier mall space is oversupplied (the "retail apocalypse"), so power there sits with the tenant.
- **Substitute:** E-commerce substitutes the store with a distribution center + last-mile network — trading rent for logistics cost (see §5). Owning real estate (Costco owns most of its warehouses) substitutes lease expense for capital and insulates against rent inflation. [Inference]

### 5. Logistics, freight & fulfillment

The physical movement of goods — ocean freight, trucking, DCs, and last mile — is the input that a store-based retailer partly hides in COGS and an e-commerce retailer exposes as a first-order P&L line.

- **Last mile is the crux of e-commerce economics:** It now accounts for **~53% of total shipping cost** (up from 41% in 2018), averaging ~$10.10/order [Fact] (Dropoff/McKinsey/Capgemini, 2026 — https://www.dropoff.com/blog/ultimate-guide-last-mile-ecommerce/). Because 9 in 10 buyers expect free shipping but only ~50% of retailers can offer it, delivery cost is often the difference between a profitable and unprofitable online order. [Fact/Inference]
- **Ocean freight:** Container spot rates are volatile; 2026 estimates cited $400–600/FEU on some lanes but with volatile surcharges [Fact/Estimate] (Freightos/Dedola, 2026). A freight spike (as in 2021–22 when rates 5–10×'d) lands directly in next season's COGS.
- **Suppliers/bottlenecks:** Ocean carriers (Maersk, MSC), parcel (UPS, FedEx, USPS, Amazon Logistics), and 3PLs. Port throughput, chassis availability, and warehouse labor are recurring single points of failure. Amazon's owned logistics network is itself a moat built by internalizing this input.
- **Pricing power:** Fragmented enough that large retailers negotiate hard, but concentrated ocean-carrier alliances can reclaim power during capacity crunches.

### 6. Shrink & working capital (the "negative inputs")

- **Shrink:** U.S. retailers lost ~1.68% of revenue to shrink in the 2023 survey — highest in a decade, ~$112B [Fact] (NRF National Retail Security Survey via Retail Dive, 2024 — https://www.retaildive.com/news/no-nrf-annual-retail-shrink-report-2024/729009/). At a 3% operating margin, **1.68% of sales lost to shrink equals over half of operating profit** [Inference]. Causes: internal theft (~42%), external theft (~37%), process error (~12%) [Estimate] (NRF). This is why loss-prevention, camera/AI, and locked cases are capital inputs.
- **Working capital / trade credit:** Retail's hidden financial input is the **cash-conversion cycle**. Best-in-class operators (Amazon, Costco) sell inventory before paying suppliers — negative working capital that effectively funds growth with vendors' money. The "input" here is supplier trade credit; its price is the terms retailers extract, again a function of scale. [Inference]

### 7. Energy, software/IP & capital

- **Energy:** Stores, DCs, refrigeration (grocery) and delivery fleets consume electricity and diesel. Not usually a top-3 cost but material for cold-chain grocers, where refrigeration can be a large share of store energy. A fuel-price spike also raises freight (§5).
- **Software/IP:** POS, ERP, WMS, e-commerce platform, demand forecasting, and increasingly **retail-media ad-tech**. Suppliers include Oracle, SAP, Salesforce, Shopify, and in-house builds. This is a growing input as retail becomes a technology/data business.
- **Financial capital:** Retail is capital-light for asset-light models (marketplace) and capital-heavy for owned-store/DC networks. Cost of capital matters most for cyclical, leveraged, or store-expansion-driven names.

### What caps capacity / determines margin (summary)

- **Margin is set primarily by COGS/vendor terms and private-label mix**, then by shrink, labor, and occupancy. Because base margins are thin (grocery net 1–3%; Kroger operating ~2.5% in 2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/56873/000110465924099127/tm2423658d1_ex99-1.htm [Fact]), small moves in any input are amplified into large earnings swings — retail is an **operating-leverage-on-thin-margins** business. [Inference]
- **Capacity** for physical retail is capped by real estate footprint and DC/last-mile throughput; for e-commerce, by fulfillment and delivery network capacity. The binding constraint has shifted from "shelf space" toward "fulfillment + delivery density." [Inference]

### 8. Full merchandise, store, fulfillment, and funding ledger

Merchandise must be traced by category rather than treated as one COGS line. Apparel/footwear uses cotton, wool/leather, synthetic fiber, rubber, dyes and labor; furniture uses timber, steel, foam/textiles and freight; electronics use semiconductors, displays, batteries and metals; home improvement uses lumber, cement, steel, copper, fixtures and chemicals; general merchandise uses paper/plastic/metals and packaging. Vendors' energy, wage, currency, financing and compliance affect landed cost.

Retail infrastructure includes stores/leases, warehouses, refrigeration where needed, shelving/fixtures, point-of-sale and payment, e-commerce/cloud, robots/material handling, cybersecurity, power, heating/cooling, water, packaging and last-mile/returns networks. Labor spans buying, planning, store associates, warehouse/driver, digital and loss prevention. Customer attention, traffic, credit and payment acceptance are demand inputs.

Capital includes supplier terms, inventory/revolver and asset-backed finance, leases, corporate debt/equity, franchisee capital, private equity, customer deposits/gift cards and private-label/BNPL/card funding. Merchandise and customs documentation, product safety, labor/sourcing, data/privacy, zoning and alcohol/pharmacy/other licenses gate assortment and operations.

Landed cost, markdowns, shrink, wage, occupancy, fulfillment and payment fees set margin; vendor supply, port/freight, warehouse/store capacity, labor, systems and working capital gate throughput. Private label, local sourcing, marketplace/dropship, rental/resale/repair and digital goods substitute for owned imported inventory, but shift quality, brand, commission and service risk.

## 3. Market Landscape


Retail is the terminal link of the consumer-goods value chain: **manufacturer → brand → distributor/wholesaler → retailer → consumer**. U.S. retail + food services runs at a ~$760B/month rate (May 2026 advance: $763.7B) [Fact] (Census MARTS, 2026 — https://www.census.gov/retail/marts/www/marts_current.pdf), of which e-commerce is ~16.1% of total (2024) [Fact] (Census e-commerce, 2024 — https://www2.census.gov/retail/releases/historical/ecomm/24q4.pdf). This file maps who does what along the chain, the moats of the leaders, geography and trade flows, policy/national-security angles, where profit accrues vs. is competed away, and where value is migrating.

### 1. Participants along the value chain

- **Manufacturers / brand owners** (P&G, Nestlé, Nike, Apple, PepsiCo): make the goods; historically held the pricing power via brand equity. Increasingly disintermediated by private label and by DTC.
- **Wholesalers / distributors** (grocery: US Foods, McLane; hardware co-ops: Ace, True Value): aggregate and break bulk. Being squeezed as large retailers buy direct.
- **Retailers** — the focus — segmented by format:
  - **Mass/discount:** Walmart (~$681B rev FY2025 [Fact]), Target, Dollar General, Dollar Tree.
  - **Warehouse club:** Costco, Sam's Club (Walmart), BJ's.
  - **E-commerce/marketplace:** Amazon (North America segment $387.5B sales, $25.0B operating income 2024 [Fact] — https://s2.q4cdn.com/299287126/files/doc_financials/2025/ar/Amazon-2024-Annual-Report.pdf).
  - **Grocery:** Kroger, Albertsons, Aldi, Publix.
  - **Home improvement:** Home Depot, Lowe's.
  - **Off-price:** TJX, Ross, Burlington.
  - **Specialty/apparel/DTC:** wide, fragmented, low-moat.
- **Enablers:** logistics (UPS, FedEx, ocean carriers, 3PLs), payments (Visa/Mastercard, ~2–3% interchange), commerce software (Shopify, Oracle, SAP), and retail-media ad-tech (The Trade Desk, in-house platforms).
- **Customers:** end consumers (B2C) plus small businesses (Costco/Sam's, Home Depot Pro).
- **Regulators:** FTC (antitrust, e.g., it blocked the Kroger–Albertsons merger in 2024), CFPB (consumer credit), USDA/FDA (food safety), OSHA (labor), state AGs, and Customs/USTR (tariffs).

### 2. Sources of competitive advantage (moats)

- **Scale-driven cost advantage (Walmart, Amazon, Costco):** volume → best vendor terms → lowest prices → more volume. The dominant, self-reinforcing retail moat. Walmart's revenue scale lets it undercut on price and still fund logistics. [Inference]
- **Membership lock-in (Costco):** 92.9% U.S./Canada renewal [Fact] (Costco 10-K FY2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/909832/000090983224000049/cost-20240901.htm); the annuity funds low prices, creating a loyalty flywheel rivals cannot cheaply copy.
- **Logistics network / density (Amazon):** owned fulfillment + last-mile is a capital moat; each incremental FC raises delivery speed and lowers cost, which competitors must replicate at enormous capex.
- **Data + retail-media flywheel (Amazon, Walmart):** first-party purchase data monetized as 70–90%-margin advertising [Fact/Estimate] (Stratably, 2025 — https://stratably.com/the-retail-media-wake-up-call/) funds price investment — a moat unavailable to sub-scale retailers who lack the traffic.
- **Off-price buying relationships (TJX):** decades of vendor trust to absorb closeouts opportunistically; hard to replicate and counter-cyclical.
- **Real-estate/location (grocery, Home Depot):** convenience and local density; weaker than it was as e-commerce erodes location value.
- **Where there is NO moat:** undifferentiated apparel/specialty and pure resellers of national brands — these are the graveyard formats (Sears, JCPenney, Bed Bath & Beyond, Toys R Us).

### 3. Geographic clusters & why they exist

- **Sourcing clusters (Asia):** China, Vietnam, Bangladesh, India dominate low-cost manufacturing of apparel/footwear/electronics — legacy of labor cost, scale, and supplier ecosystems. ~98% of U.S. apparel is imported [Fact] (AAFA/NRF, 2025).
- **Retail HQ clusters (U.S.):** Bentonville AR (Walmart), Seattle (Amazon, Costco), Cincinnati (Kroger), Minneapolis (Target), Atlanta (Home Depot) — each anchoring a supplier/agency ecosystem.
- **Distribution geography:** DC networks cluster near population centers and ports (Inland Empire CA, Dallas, Atlanta, NJ) to minimize last-mile distance — the binding constraint for delivery-speed competition.
- **Format geography:** dollar stores and Aldi/Lidl expand fastest in lower-income and rural/underserved areas; warehouse clubs cluster in suburban high-income density.
- **Why clusters persist:** they are self-reinforcing. A sourcing region accumulates specialized suppliers, freight-forwarders, quality inspectors, and financing — the "agglomeration" that makes it cheaper to source there than to rebuild the ecosystem elsewhere. This is precisely why tariff-driven diversification is slow and costly: moving apparel from China to a greenfield country means recreating decades of supplier depth, not just signing a new factory. [Inference] The same logic anchors retail HQ towns — the vendor, agency, and talent ecosystem around Bentonville or Seattle raises the switching cost of relocating.

### 3b. Customer and channel structure

The customer base bifurcates by income and increasingly by channel. Value-seeking and lower-income households drive the dollar-store and hard-discount surge; higher-income households anchor clubs and premium grocery. Channel-wise, the industry is consolidating into **omni-channel** — the same customer transacts in-store, online, and via app, and the retailers winning are those that treat the store as both a shopfront and a fulfillment node. Pure-play single-channel operators (both offline-only specialty and cash-burning online-only DTC) are the structurally weakest positions, caught without either the store's convenience or the platform's scale. [Inference]

### 4. Trade flows, subsidies, industrial policy & national security

- **Import dependence is the industry's defining external exposure.** Tariffs are effectively an input tax (see INPUTS §2): 2025 layered duties reached ~45.5% on some Chinese apparel [Fact] (Freightos, 2025 — https://www.freightos.com/freight-resources/how-to-calculate-china-tariffs-in-2025/). A 10% apparel tariff → 3–5% price increase [Estimate] (NRF via World-Collective, 2025 — https://world-collective.com/blogs/news/tariff-roundup-for-fashion-brands-2025).
- **De minimis:** The $800 duty-free threshold that enabled Shein/Temu's direct-from-China parcels became a major 2024–25 policy battleground; tightening it reshapes cross-border e-commerce economics. [Fact/Inference]
- **Industrial policy:** Retail is rarely subsidized directly, but it is shaped by minimum-wage law, labor/unionization policy, and antitrust (the FTC's 2024 block of Kroger–Albertsons signals hostility to further grocery consolidation) [Fact].
- **National security:** food and pharmacy supply-chain resilience, data privacy of consumer purchase data, and dependence on adversary-nation manufacturing are the live concerns; supply-chain diversification ("China+1") is partly policy-driven.

### 5. Where profit accrues vs. is competed away

- **Competed away:** the merchandise margin itself. In commoditized categories, price transparency (Amazon, comparison engines) drives markups toward the floor — grocery nets 1–3%, mass 3–6%. Selling national brands is a race to the bottom. [Inference]
- **Accrues to:**
  1. **Scale players' vendor terms & private label** — the margin the manufacturer used to keep. Private label reached ~24% grocery unit share [Fact] (SupplySide, 2025 — https://talkbusiness.net/2025/01/the-supply-side-private-brands-continue-to-grow-retail-market-share-in-2024/).
  2. **Retail media / advertising** — the fastest-growing, highest-margin pool. Amazon $56.2B, Walmart $4.4B in 2024 [Fact] (Amazon AR 2024; Digiday, 2024 — https://digiday.com/marketing/with-ad-revenue-reaching-4-4b-buyers-see-walmarts-retail-media-network-as-the-one-to-watch/); Amazon + Walmart take ~84% of U.S. retail-media budgets [Estimate] (Stratably/CNBC, 2024).
  3. **Membership & financial services** — Costco's $4.83B membership fees ≈ half its operating profit [Fact].
  4. **Logistics-as-a-service** — Amazon monetizing FBA/AWS-style infrastructure.
- **The pattern:** profit has migrated from *marking up goods* to *monetizing the traffic and data the goods generate*. **Merchandise is becoming the loss-leader that acquires the customer whose attention and data are then sold.** [Inference]

### 6. What's gaining vs. losing relevance

**Gaining:**
- **Retail media networks** — structurally the biggest value migration; a software-margin business bolted onto retail traffic. Winners are the few with enough first-party traffic (Amazon, Walmart; Instacart, Kroger, Target as second tier). [Inference]
- **Discount & value formats** — Aldi/Lidl/Trader Joe's and dollar stores gaining share; Aldi visits +7.1%, Trader Joe's +11.9% YoY vs. grocery segment +1.8% (H1 2024) [Fact] (eMarketer/Numerator, 2024 — https://www.emarketer.com/content/aldi-trader-joes-lidl-grocery-expansion-private-label-growth).
- **Private label / owned brands** — Walmart Bettergoods and Target Dealworthy among fastest-growing (+200% volume) [Fact] (Numerator, 2024).
- **Omni-channel / same-day** — BOPIS, ship-from-store, and fast delivery from stores-as-mini-DCs.
- **Off-price** — counter-cyclical share gainer.
- **Cross-border ultra-discount (Shein, Temu)** — gained fast on de minimis, now policy-threatened.

**Losing:**
- **Department stores & undifferentiated mall specialty** — structurally declining; occupancy leverage + share loss.
- **Cashier-heavy labor models** — automation displacing ~314k cashier jobs by 2034 [Fact] (BLS, 2026 — https://www.bls.gov/opub/mlr/2026/article/industry-and-occupational-employment-projections-overview.htm).
- **Sub-scale grocers & pure national-brand resellers** — squeezed between Walmart/Costco on price and Aldi on value.

### 7. Disruption / obsolescence risks and value migration

- **AI-driven commerce & agentic shopping:** if AI agents mediate purchases, the value of owning the storefront and the ad real estate could shift again — potentially threatening even retail media if agents bypass sponsored placements. [Inference — speculative]
- **Amazon's platform gravity:** its combination of marketplace + logistics + ads + AWS lets it subsidize retail with services profit; competitors without a services engine are structurally disadvantaged. [Inference]
- **Tariff/geopolitics:** a durable high-tariff regime permanently raises landed cost and favors domestic-heavy, private-label, and value formats over import-dependent specialty.
- **Distinguishing real progress from hype:** genuine structural advantages are **owned logistics density, first-party data at scale, membership annuities, and private-label capability**. Promotional claims to discount are **"GMV growth" funded by subsidized shipping, unprofitable DTC scaling, and generic "omnichannel" spend without a data/ad monetization engine behind it.** The test: does the model produce contribution-positive orders and a durable high-margin ancillary stream, or just top-line? [Inference]

**Net value-migration thesis:** economic value is migrating (a) *up the concentration curve* to a handful of mega-scale platforms, and (b) *sideways out of merchandise margin* into advertising, membership, data, and logistics services. The retailers set to gain are those that own traffic + data + fulfillment (Amazon, Walmart, Costco); those set to lose are sub-scale, import-dependent, national-brand resellers with fixed-cost store fleets and no ancillary high-margin engine. [Inference]

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs include curated availability, price discovery, convenience, credit, delivery, returns, service and trust—not merely resale of goods. Packaging, unsold/returned goods, food/product waste, delivery congestion and data use are residuals; resale, repair, recycling and liquidation are secondary channels.

Shopper, user and payer may differ through gifts, families, employers and credit. Brands/manufacturers supply; wholesalers and marketplaces intermediate; landlords and payment networks tax the transaction; advertisers may fund retail media. Segment customers by income, mission, channel, urgency and credit rather than treating consumption as one pool.

Stores and last mile are local; sourcing and digital platforms are global. Density improves inventory pooling, advertising, fulfillment and supplier terms, but exposes networks to local saturation and regulation. Mass merchants, category specialists, department/discount/dollar stores, franchises, marketplaces, DTC brands, resale and informal commerce overlap.

Private funding is corporate, franchise and consumer capital; public funding and policy affect demand through transfers/tax and supply through transport, urban development and small-business support. Rules cover tariffs/customs, product and food/drug safety, sourcing/forced labor, wages/scheduling, zoning, pricing and consumer protection, payments/credit, privacy/ads, competition/platforms, producer responsibility and waste.

Retail connects consumer credit/rates, real estate, logistics, advertising/media, manufacturing and commodities. Commodity shocks move through vendor cost and tickets with lags; high rates hit big-ticket and retailer interest; e-commerce increases parcel/warehouse demand; retail media shifts brand advertising toward merchants; shrink and returns affect suppliers, insurers and reverse-logistics markets.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


Retail is the business of **buying finished goods wholesale and reselling them to consumers at a markup**, financed largely by suppliers, and monetized increasingly through data and advertising rather than the merchandise margin alone. This file explains the workflow, the competing operating models and their trade-offs, the unit economics/cost stack, the KPIs practitioners actually track, and — in depth — how to value retailers across life-stages.

### 1. The core workflow (merchandise-to-cash)

1. **Assortment & buying.** Merchants decide what to carry (SKU selection), negotiate cost and terms with vendors, and commit open-to-buy dollars. For apparel/seasonal goods this happens 3–9 months ahead; for grocery/replenishment it is continuous.
2. **Sourcing & inbound logistics.** Goods are manufactured (often overseas), shipped via ocean/air, cleared through customs (tariffs applied here), trucked to distribution centers (DCs).
3. **Distribution & allocation.** DCs receive, store, and allocate inventory to stores or fulfillment centers. Allocation decisions (how much of which SKU to which node) are the operational heart of retail — errors create markdowns or stockouts.
4. **Selling.** Through stores (physical), e-commerce (owned site/app), marketplace (third-party sellers), or hybrid omni-channel (buy-online-pickup-in-store / BOPIS, ship-from-store).
5. **Fulfillment.** Customer either self-serves in-store, or the order is picked, packed, and delivered (last mile — the costliest step, ~53% of shipping cost; see INPUTS §5).
6. **Payment & reverse logistics.** Payment is captured instantly; returns (a major e-commerce cost, 15–30%+ for apparel) flow back through reverse logistics.
7. **Replenishment loop.** Point-of-sale data feeds demand forecasting → new POs → repeat. The tighter this loop, the lower the inventory and markdowns.

### 2. Competing operating models and why players choose them

- **Everyday-low-price (EDLP) mass — Walmart, Costco.** Thin markup, massive volume, negative working capital, and relentless cost-out. Costco's merchandise gross margin is only ~10.9% [Fact] (Costco 10-K FY2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/909832/000090983224000049/cost-20240901.htm) — it barely marks up goods and instead makes its profit on **membership fees** ($4.83B in FY2024, ~52% of operating income) [Fact] (same). **Choose EDLP when you have the scale to win on cost; the model self-reinforces because low prices drive volume, which drives better vendor terms, which lowers prices.** [Inference]
- **High-low promotional — department/grocery.** Rotating discounts and loss-leaders to drive traffic. Higher list margins but eroded by promotions and markdowns. Chosen where brand/assortment differentiation exists and shoppers respond to "deals."
- **Off-price — TJX, Ross, Burlington.** Buys excess/closeout inventory opportunistically at deep discounts, no advance commitment. Trades merchandising control for margin. Wins in downturns and is structurally counter-cyclical.
- **Marketplace/platform — Amazon, eBay.** The retailer never owns the inventory; third parties sell and the platform takes a fee (~15% referral + fulfillment + advertising). **This is asset-light and infinitely scalable in SKUs, but the platform's real profit shifts to services (fees, ads, logistics-as-a-service) rather than the goods.** [Inference]
- **Warehouse club — Costco, Sam's.** Limited SKUs (~4,000 vs. 30,000+ in a supermarket), bulk packs, membership gate. Fewer SKUs = higher inventory turns and buying power per item.
- **Vertical/DTC — Apple stores, brand.com.** The brand is also the retailer, capturing both margins but bearing all inventory/demand risk.

### 3. Asset types and their economics

- **Owned real estate** (Costco, some Walmart): high capex, low ongoing occupancy cost, inflation hedge, balance-sheet value.
- **Leased stores** (most apparel/specialty): low capex, high fixed operating cost; leverages hard against sales declines. Occupancy averaged 7.73% of sales in 2024 [Fact] (Datex, 2025 — https://chainstoreage.com/datex-retail-real-estate-occupancy-costs-hit-six-year-high-2024).
- **Distribution/fulfillment centers & automation:** capital-intensive but the backbone of e-commerce and omni-channel; robotics reduce labor per unit.
- **Inventory:** the largest current asset; its turnover is the master efficiency metric.
- **Intangibles — data, private-label brands, retail-media ad platform:** increasingly the highest-return assets because they carry near-zero marginal cost.

### 4. Unit economics & the cost stack

For a mass retailer, ~$100 of sales roughly decomposes to (illustrative, mass-format) [Inference, anchored to Walmart FY2025]:
- COGS ~$76 → **gross profit ~$24** (Walmart 24.1%) [Fact].
- Store + DC labor ~$10–12.
- Occupancy ~$3–8.
- Other SG&A, marketing, D&A ~$5–7.
- Shrink ~$1.7 [Fact, NRF].
- → **Operating income ~$3–6** (Walmart FY2025 operating margin ~4.3%; $29.3B on $681B) [Fact] (Walmart 10-K FY2025 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/104169/000010416925000021/wmt-20250131.htm).

**Marginal-unit logic:** for a store already open, the marginal sale carries only COGS + variable labor + card fees — so incremental sales are highly profitable (high operating leverage), and incremental *lost* sales are highly damaging. This is why comp-sales growth is the number the market fixates on.

**The margin migration that matters:** high-margin ancillary streams now carry the P&L. Retail-media advertising runs **70–90% gross margin** vs. 1–3% net for grocery and 3–6% for mass [Fact/Estimate] (Alexander Group/Stratably, 2024–25 — https://stratably.com/the-retail-media-wake-up-call/). At Walmart, advertising + membership (both high-margin) drove roughly one-third of Q4 operating income [Fact] (Digiday, 2024 — https://digiday.com/marketing/with-ad-revenue-reaching-4-4b-buyers-see-walmarts-retail-media-network-as-the-one-to-watch/). Amazon's ad business alone was **$56.2B in 2024** [Fact] (Amazon 2024 Annual Report — https://s2.q4cdn.com/299287126/files/doc_financials/2025/ar/Amazon-2024-Annual-Report.pdf). **The mechanical insight: the merchandise is increasingly a customer-acquisition and data-generation vehicle; the profit is made on ads, membership, financial services, and (for Amazon) cloud.** [Inference]

### 5. KPIs practitioners actually track

- **Comparable ("comp"/same-store) sales:** growth from stores open >1 year — strips out new-store noise. The single most-watched retail metric. Walmart U.S. comps +4.8% FY2025 [Fact].
- **Inventory turnover** (COGS ÷ avg inventory): Home Depot ~4.8x in FY2024 [Fact] (AisleStock analysis of HD 10-K — https://aislestock.com/inventory-turnover-home-improvement); grocers 12–15x; Costco higher still. Higher turns = less capital tied up, less markdown risk.
- **Gross margin & merchandise margin.**
- **Sales per square foot** (physical productivity) / sales per labor hour.
- **Shrink %** (see INPUTS) — 1.68% of revenue in the 2023 survey, ~$112B [Fact].
- **Cash conversion cycle / days payable** — negative WC is the gold standard.
- **Membership renewal rate** (clubs): Costco 92.9% U.S./Canada [Fact] (Costco 10-K FY2024).
- **E-commerce KPIs:** conversion rate, average order value (AOV), cost per acquisition (CAC), return rate, contribution margin per order after last-mile.
- **Retail-media KPIs:** ad revenue, ROAS for advertisers, take-rate.

### 6. Development timelines & characteristic failure points

- **New store:** site selection → build/fit-out → ~1–3 years to maturity. **New DC/automated FC:** 1–3 years and hundreds of millions in capex.
- **Failure points:** (1) **inventory mismatch** — overbuy → markdowns crush margin; underbuy → stockouts lose sales; (2) **occupancy leverage** — a leased fleet with declining comps bleeds fixed rent; (3) **shrink spiral**; (4) **e-commerce unprofitability** — last-mile + returns exceeding contribution; (5) **debt in a downturn** — many retail bankruptcies (Toys R Us, Sears) were LBO-leverage failures, not merely demand failures.

### 7. Valuation across life-stages

#### (a) Mature, cash-generative retailers (Walmart, Costco, Home Depot, TJX)
- **Primary methods:** P/E and EV/EBITDA on stable, growing FCF; DCF as a cross-check.
- **Typical multiples:** U.S. retail median EV/EBITDA ~9.3x (2024); grocery ~7.7x; specialty ~9.9x; "general retail" as high as ~16.6x [Fact/Estimate] (Statista/Sofer Advisors, 2024 — https://soferadvisors.com/insights/valuation/retail-company-valuation-multiples-complete-guide-2026/). Quality compounders (Costco) trade at large premiums (often 30–50x P/E) because the market capitalizes the **recurring, high-renewal membership annuity** and negative working capital, not the thin merchandise margin. [Inference]
- **What drives the multiple:** durability of comps, ROIC vs. WACC spread, mix shift toward high-margin ads/membership, and free-cash conversion. Value the ad/membership stream separately (sum-of-the-parts) — it deserves a software-like multiple, not a grocery multiple. [Inference]
- **EV/Sales** is used for thin-margin names (grocery ~0.5x) because EBITDA is small and noisy [Fact] (Sofer/Statista, 2024).

#### (b) Cyclical / asset-heavy retailers (department stores, home improvement, big-box, apparel)
- These earnings swing with the consumer cycle and operating leverage, so **avoid valuing at trough or peak multiples**. Use **normalized/mid-cycle EBIT margins** and mid-cycle comps.
- **Methods:** EV/EBITDA on normalized earnings; **sum-of-the-parts including real estate** — an owned-store retailer can be worth more dead than alive if property value exceeds the going-concern enterprise value (the classic activist thesis, e.g., Sears/Seritage). Track **rent-adjusted leverage** (capitalize leases) because IFRS 16/ASC 842 put leases on the balance sheet; a lease-heavy retailer is effectively far more levered than reported net debt suggests.
- **Watch:** operating leverage cuts both ways — a 5% comp decline on a fixed-cost base can halve EBIT. Value the downside with a stress-case comp scenario. [Inference]

#### (c) Pre-profit / early-stage & disruptor retailers (DTC brands, e-commerce upstarts, marketplace platforms pre-scale)
- No stable earnings, so value on **unit economics and cohort behavior, not P/E**:
  - **Contribution margin per order** after COGS, fulfillment, last-mile, returns, and CAC — the path-to-profit litmus test.
  - **LTV/CAC ratio** (lifetime value ÷ customer acquisition cost); >3x is the rough venture benchmark [Estimate]; payback period in months.
  - **Cohort retention curves** and repeat-purchase rate — do later cohorts monetize better?
  - **GMV and take-rate** for marketplaces (revenue = GMV × take-rate); model the take-rate expanding as ads/logistics services attach.
- **Multiples used:** EV/Revenue or EV/GMV, benchmarked to growth and gross margin (the "Rule of 40" logic imported from software for tech-enabled retail). A profitable-unit-economics DTC scaling revenue commands a premium; a "growth funded by subsidized shipping" model with negative contribution margin should be discounted heavily regardless of top-line. [Inference]
- **Optionality assets:** proprietary logistics network, first-party data/customer base, and a nascent retail-media platform are the intangibles that justify paying ahead of GAAP profit — but only if the core order is contribution-positive. **Distinguish real structural advantage (owned fulfillment, data flywheel) from promotional "GMV growth" that evaporates when subsidies stop.** [Inference]

#### Cross-cutting valuation adjustments
- **Capitalize operating leases** before comparing leverage across store-heavy vs. asset-light peers.
- **Separate the high-margin engine (ads/membership/services) from merchandise** and multiple-weight accordingly — the biggest single valuation error in modern retail is applying one blended multiple to a business that is really two businesses. [Inference]
- **Normalize for tariff/freight shocks** and one-time inventory markdowns when computing "steady-state" margin.

### 8. Complete buy-to-sell-through and cash mechanics

The chain is forecast/assort → source and negotiate → order/finance → manufacture/import → allocate to DC/store → market/price → sell/fulfill → return/repair/liquidate → reorder or markdown. Capacity is constrained by SKU-location inventory and labor/fulfillment at the required time, not total units alone.

Revenue includes merchandise, marketplace commission, membership/subscription, delivery/service, advertising, warranty and financial products. Gross margin must reconcile initial markup, vendor allowances, freight/duty, markdown, shrink and returns. GMV is not revenue for an agent marketplace.

Working capital is strategic: inventory and receivables less supplier payables, gift cards and customer deposits. Negative working capital is strongest during growth and can reverse in contraction. Leases are fixed claims; store remodel/maintenance, distribution and technology capital must be distinguished from new-unit growth.

Track comparable sales by traffic/ticket/units, price versus mix, market share, gross margin bridge, inventory growth and turns, in-stock, markdown and shrink, return rate, labor/productivity, occupancy, digital GMV and fulfillment cost, customer acquisition/retention, supplier terms and free cash flow. Stress consumer/rate decline, fashion miss, tariff/freight shock, vendor failure, channel outage, inventory glut, shrink, wage increase and lease/refinancing pressure.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Retail gross profit = net merchandise/commission/membership/ad revenue − landed COGS − markdown − shrink − returns. Store/order contribution subtracts labor, occupancy, payment and fulfillment; enterprise cash adjusts inventory, payables, leases and capex.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gross margin | ~20–50% by category/format | Merchandise economics |
| Comp growth | traffic × ticket adjusted for calendar | Organic demand |
| Inventory turns | often ~2–8x by category | Working capital/markdown |
| Shrink | roughly ~1–3% of sales, category-specific | Leakage |
| Return rate | low-single digits store; often double digits online/apparel | Fulfillment cost |
| Occupancy | often ~5–15% of sales by format | Fixed cost |
| Labor | often ~8–20% sales by service model | Store/fulfillment productivity |
| Digital fulfillment | cost/order or percent sales | Omnichannel contribution |
| CAC/repeat | cost and cohort purchase frequency | DTC economics |
| Lease-adjusted leverage | debt plus lease claims/cash earnings | Fixed-charge resilience |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile GMV/net revenue, vendor allowances, gift cards/deferred, loyalty, private-label credit, marketplace funds, inventory reserves, markdown, returns, leases, store closure and supplier finance.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Consumer income/credit/rates, category replacement, traffic, ticket and promotion.
- Vendor/commodity, tariffs, freight, FX, wage, rent and payments.
- Inventory/markdown, shrink, returns, fulfillment and channel mix.
- Platform/privacy, product/sourcing, labor, zoning and EPR.

### Valuation discipline

Use category/format cohort and store/order contribution, normalized inventory and leases. Separate membership, retail media, marketplace and finance from merchandise.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Last shelf/order slot from a vendor, store or fulfillment node serving a customer | Inventory position and delivery promise define availability |
| Marginal customer | Household able to trade down, postpone, buy used or switch retailer/channel | Category need and disposable income set elasticity |
| Clearing mechanism | Ticket price less promotion/loyalty plus delivery and financing | Net realized price must include returns and markdown |
| Cash shutdown point | SKU/order/store exits when contribution fails to cover avoidable merchandise, labor, fulfillment and occupancy exit cost | Lease and inventory liquidation affect timing |
| New-capacity incentive | Expected four-wall/fulfillment return covers build, inventory, lease and remodel capital | Cannibalization and online economics belong in the hurdle |
| Adjustment lag | Hours for price, weeks for buying, months for inventory, years for stores/networks | Lead time creates bullwhip and markdown cycles |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Comparable sales | Organic volume growth | Split traffic, units, price, mix, calendar, closures and channel transfer |
| GMV | Retail revenue | Deduct third-party seller value, returns, taxes, incentives and pass-through delivery |
| Gross margin | Order/store contribution | Subtract fulfillment, returns, payment, shrink, occupancy and vendor-funding volatility |
| Inventory turns | Healthy inventory | Analyze age, category, in-transit, markdown liability and vendor terms |
| Digital sales | Profitable omnichannel demand | Allocate picking, delivery, returns, acquisition, store labor and cannibalization |
