## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Owned, leased, managed and franchised lodging across chain scales, resorts, extended stay and related loyalty/distribution. Short-term rentals are a substitute rather than the core.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Luxury and upper-upscale | Full-service rooms, F&B, events and amenities | rooms, occupancy, ADR | Destination, brand, group, service intensity and owner capex |
| Upscale/select service | Limited/full-service business and leisure | rooms, occupancy, ADR | Service package, weekday mix, franchise and labor |
| Economy and midscale | Value-oriented transient lodging | rooms, occupancy, ADR | Price, roadside/local demand, franchise and property quality |
| Extended stay and resort | Longer stays or destination experience | room nights, length, spend | Kitchen/amenity, season, ownership and ancillary |
| Brand, management and ownership | Franchise/loyalty/reservation, operations and property capital | rooms, fees, NOI | Asset-light fee versus property risk and contractual split |

### Specifications that change value

- State property, submarket, chain scale, room count, ownership/operator/brand and renovation status.
- Occupancy, ADR and RevPAR need comparable period, available rooms, currency and same-store scope.
- Net ADR must include OTA/agent commission, loyalty, package and promotion.
- Property profit must state departmental/undistributed expense, management/franchise, tax/insurance and FF&E/capex.
- Pipeline needs signed, financed, under construction and open dates, not room announcements.

### Role map

Owner supplies property/capital; brand/franchisor supplies flag/distribution; operator runs; OTA/GDS/agent acquires; corporate/group planner books; guest stays; lender finances; local authority licenses/taxes.

### Terms that must be explicit

- occupancy, ADR and RevPAR
- GOP, hotel EBITDA and owner cash
- owned, leased, managed and franchised
- net ADR and channel mix
- pipeline versus under construction


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope note: "the lodging industry" is actually three stacked businesses with different input profiles — (1) **brand/franchise companies** (Marriott, Hilton, IHG, Wyndham, Choice) that sell intangibles; (2) **owner/operators and REITs** (Host, Park, Pebblebrook) that own the real estate; (3) **distribution intermediaries** (Booking, Expedia, Airbnb). Their input dependencies barely overlap, and confusing them is the single biggest analytical error. This file maps inputs for all three, flagging which input caps capacity or margin for each.

### 1. Financial capital — the binding constraint on supply

New supply is gated by construction financing, not by demand. Building a hotel is a real-estate development project, and the industry's growth rate is set upstream by the cost and availability of debt.

- **[Fact]** HVS's 2024 survey put average U.S. development cost at **$317,000/key** across all product types; **$195,000/key** average for limited-service (median $166k) and **$527,000/key** for full-service (median $380k) (HVS, 2024 — https://www.hvs.com/article/9999-HVS-US-Hotel-Development-Cost-Survey-2024).
- **[Fact]** Construction inflation compounded hard: the Turner Building Cost Index rose 8.0% in 2022 and 6.0% in 2023, cooling to 4.0% in the TTM ending June 2024 (HVS, 2024 — same URL).
- **[Inference]** With per-key costs elevated and post-2022 interest rates high, the yield on cost for new builds fell below the cap rates buyers would pay, so *new construction stalls even when occupancy is healthy*. This is why brand companies grew 2023–24 heavily through **conversions** (re-flagging existing hotels) rather than ground-up builds — conversions need no construction loan. Hilton and Marriott both flagged conversions as a large share of 2024 openings (Hilton, 2025 — https://stories.hilton.com/releases/hilton-closes-out-2024-with-record-levels-of-growth-opening-more-rooms-than-any-year-in-its-history).

**Where pricing power sits:** with lenders. For the asset-light brand companies, capital is *not* their constraint — owners front the ~$150k–$500k/key. That capital-light structure is precisely why Marriott/Hilton trade at premium multiples (see MECHANICS). Capital is the binding input for owners/REITs and caps industry-wide room supply growth.

### 2. Labor — the largest controllable operating input

For anyone who actually operates a hotel (owners, managers, REITs), labor is the dominant cost and the chief margin lever.

- **[Fact]** Hotel labor is typically **30–35% of revenue** and the single largest controllable expense (CoStar/STR, 2024 — https://www.costar.com/products/str-benchmark/resources/data-insights-blog/hotel-fb-driving-labor-cost-growth-2024). In 2023 labor rose to 32.4% of revenue and 51.7% of total expenses.
- **[Fact]** Labor cost per available room (LPAR) grew ~11% year-to-date through 2024, never below 6% in any month (CoStar/STR, 2024 — same URL). F&B (banquets, restaurants) drove the fastest labor growth.
- **[Fact]** U.S. hotels directly employed ~2.1 million people in 2024 paying ~$123.4B in wages, still ~225,000 below the 2.37M employed in 2019; 67.6% of hoteliers reported staffing shortages in Jan 2024 (AHLA, 2024 — https://www.ahla.com/sites/default/files/SOTI.2024.Final_.Draft_.v4.pdf).

**Skills & scarcity:** most roles (housekeeping, front desk, F&B) are low-barrier but chronically short-staffed post-pandemic; revenue managers, GMs, and engineering staff are scarcer and command premiums. **[Inference]** Labor scarcity is why brands push **labor-saving tech** (mobile check-in, reduced daily housekeeping, kiosk F&B) — these are margin defenses against an input the operator cannot control. Wage inflation flows almost 1:1 into GOP compression because rooms revenue can't always be re-priced fast enough; a 5% wage jump on 33% labor load is ~1.65 points of margin absent offsetting rate.

**Where pricing power sits:** with labor in tight markets and with unions in gateway cities (Las Vegas, NYC, SF), where contracts set a hard cost floor. This is a genuine single-point-of-failure risk for owned urban assets.

### 3. Distribution & customer-acquisition (the "demand input")

Guests are an input that must be *acquired*, and the channel mix is a huge margin determinant. The two competing channels: brand direct (loyalty) vs. OTAs.

- **[Fact]** OTA commissions run **15–30%** of booking value; Booking.com standard 15–17% (preferred programs 20–25%), Expedia brands ~18–22% (Cloudbeds/industry, 2026 — https://www.cloudbeds.com/online-travel-agencies/commissions/).
- **[Fact]** Booking Holdings did **$165.6B** gross bookings in 2024; Expedia **$110.9B** at a 12.3% revenue margin (Booking 10-K, 2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1075531/000107553125000010/bkng-20241231.htm; Expedia 10-K, 2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1324424/000132442425000008/expe-20241231.htm).
- **[Fact]** Marriott's loyalty program (Bonvoy) drove **72% of U.S. and 65% of global room nights** in 2024 (Marriott, 2024 — https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/marriott-international-reports-fourth-quarter-and-full-year-2024-results-302372780.html).

**[Inference]** The loyalty program is the brand companies' most valuable "input factory": every room night booked direct avoids a 15–25% OTA toll. That is the core economic reason brands exist and why a hotel pays 8–12% of revenue to fly a flag (below). Scale in distribution is a two-sided moat — OTAs need supply, brands need to keep guests off OTAs.

**Where pricing power sits:** OTAs hold pricing power over independent/unbranded hotels (which have no direct channel); large brands hold it over OTAs. This is the central power struggle in the value chain (see LANDSCAPE).

### 4. Brand & IP (the input owners buy from brand companies)

For an owner, the *brand flag* is a purchased input with a hard price.

- **[Fact]** Franchise royalties run **4–6% of gross room revenue**, plus **1–4%** marketing/reservation contributions, plus loyalty charges — HVS estimates all-in franchise cost of **8–12% of gross revenue** (industry/HVS, 2024 — https://www.biz2credit.com/franchise-loans/popular-hotel-franchises-to-start; https://insights.ehl.edu/hotel-franchises). Example: Fairfield by Marriott charges 5.5% royalty + 2.5% ad royalty.

**[Inference]** The flag is worth it when brand-driven RevPAR premium and lower OTA leakage exceed 8–12% of revenue. For a limited-service highway hotel it almost always does (no independent demand); for a trophy urban asset with its own reputation it may not, which is why luxury independents and soft brands exist.

### 5. Physical inputs — real estate, FF&E, energy, consumables

- **Land & location** is the irreplaceable physical input; barriers to entry in constrained gateway markets are effectively a permit/zoning moat.
- **FF&E** (furniture, fixtures, equipment) and periodic renovations ("PIPs" — property improvement plans) are recurring capex, typically **4–5% of revenue reserved**. Brand-mandated PIPs are a lever brands use over owners.
- **[Fact]** Energy and utilities are a smaller but volatile line; construction materials and FF&E inflation tracked the Turner index's 8%/6% 2022–23 surge (HVS, 2024 — URL above). **[Inference]** Energy is ~3–6% of operating cost — material but not a swing factor versus labor.
- **Consumables** (linens, toiletries, F&B inputs) pass through food-price inflation directly to the F&B P&L.

**[Inference]** None of the physical consumables individually caps capacity; **land + capital + labor** are the three that do.

### 6. Technology & software

Property-management systems (Oracle OPERA dominant), central reservation systems, revenue-management software (IDeaS, Duetto), and channel managers are critical operating IP. **[Inference]** For brand companies, the reservation/CRS and loyalty tech stack IS the product moat; for owners it is a licensed dependency bundled into the franchise fee.

### 7. Regulation as an input constraint

Zoning/permitting caps supply in desirable markets; labor law (minimum wage, union rules, "fair workweek" scheduling laws) sets the labor cost floor; short-term-rental regulation (NYC's 2023 Local Law 18, EU caps) shapes the Airbnb-vs-hotel competitive balance; ADA, fire/safety, and lodging taxes add fixed compliance cost. **[Fact]** Hotels generated ~$54.4B in state/local tax in 2024 (AHLA, 2024 — URL above), signaling how tax-exposed the sector is.

### 8. Outside-industry dependencies

Lodging is a *derived-demand* business: it consumes **air travel** (leisure + corporate transient), **corporate travel budgets** (business transient + group/convention), and **consumer discretionary income**. **[Inference]** The single most important exogenous input is macro travel demand — GDP, corporate profits, and airfare all feed occupancy. CBRE cut its 2024 RevPAR forecast specifically because leisure demand softened and corporate-profit growth slowed (CBRE, 2024 — https://www.cbre.com/press-releases/cbre-hotels-adjusts-outlook-for-2024-due-to-economic-conditions-predicts-modest-us-revpar-growth). Group/convention demand depends on the meetings industry; airport hotels depend on flight schedules.

### Shock propagation — worked example

A **+100 bps rise in construction-loan rates** → new-build yield-on-cost falls below buyer cap rates → ground-up starts drop → net new supply growth slows 12–24 months out → in a steady-demand market, occupancy and ADR firm for *existing* owners (supply-constrained upside) while brand companies' unit growth (a fee-revenue driver) decelerates and shifts toward conversions. Conversely, a **wage shock** hits owners/REITs immediately in GOP but barely touches brand-company fee revenue (fees are a % of *revenue*, not profit) — illustrating why the asset-light model is insulated from the industry's worst input, labor.

### Which inputs set margins vs. cap capacity

- **Cap capacity (supply):** development capital + land/permits + construction cost. These gate the number of rooms that exist.
- **Set operating margins:** labor (dominant), then distribution/channel mix, then FF&E/renovation capex and energy.
- **Set brand-company margins:** almost nothing on the cost side — their "input" is other people's capital and labor; their margin is set by unit growth and RevPAR, both largely exogenous.

### 11. Full property, operating-consumable, demand, and finance ledger

Development and renovation consume land, permits, concrete, steel, timber, glass, roofing, gypsum, insulation, copper/pipe, HVAC, elevators, furniture, mattresses, textiles, fixtures, kitchen/laundry equipment and IT/networking. Operations consume electricity, gas, water, food/beverage, linens, cleaning and laundry chemicals, toiletries, paper/plastic packaging, replacement FF&E, waste service and security. These link hotels to construction, agriculture, chemicals, utilities and freight.

Labor includes front desk, housekeeping, food service, maintenance, revenue management, sales, security and managers; visas, housing and local transit affect availability. Demand inputs include air/rail/road access, attractions, conventions, corporate travel, events and destination safety. Distribution requires direct booking, loyalty, OTAs, global distribution systems, travel agents and corporate contracts.

Capital comes from owner equity, mortgages/CMBS, construction/mezzanine debt, joint ventures, REIT/private funds, municipal/convention/tourism infrastructure, tax incentives and brand key money. Franchisors supply brand, standards, loyalty, reservation and marketing; operators supply execution. Insurance and liquor/food/occupancy permissions gate operations.

ADR, occupancy, channel commission, wage/productivity, utilities, food and franchise/management fees set property margin; rooms, labor, airlift, permits, utilities and brand standards gate capacity. Short-term rentals, cruise, vacation ownership and alternative lodging substitute by trip type. Renovation/energy efficiency can lower operating inputs but requires room downtime and funding.

## 3. Market Landscape


### The value chain, stage by stage

The industry decomposed itself into specialized layers so that capital and profit could be separated. Reading left to right — where value is created, and where it is captured:

1. **Capital/land owners** — REITs (Host, Park, Pebblebrook, RLJ), private-equity funds (Blackstone, Starwood Capital), sovereign wealth, and individual franchisees. They supply the ~$150k–$500k/key of real estate capital. **Value capture: real-estate operating income, cyclical, capital-intensive — the *thinnest risk-adjusted margin* in the chain.**
2. **Operators/managers** — sometimes the brand itself, sometimes third-party managers (Aimbridge, Highgate, Sonesta). They run the hotel for a base fee (~3% of revenue) + incentive fee. **Value capture: fee income, moderate.**
3. **Brand/franchise companies** — Marriott, Hilton, IHG, Wyndham, Choice, Hyatt, Accor. They own the IP, the reservation system, and the loyalty program, and license the flag. **Value capture: the fattest, most durable, lowest-capital profit in the chain.**
4. **Distribution intermediaries** — OTAs (Booking, Expedia), metasearch (Google, Trip.com, Trivago), GDS (Amadeus, Sabre), and STR platforms (Airbnb, Vrbo). **Value capture: a toll on demand, very high-margin, structurally powerful over independents.**

**[Inference]** The defining structural fact of the last 25 years is that Marriott, Hilton, Hyatt, and Wyndham *sold or spun off their real estate* (Host was spun from Marriott; Park from Hilton) and kept the brand. Value migrated **up the chain from bricks to brand** because franchise/fee income is recurring, high-margin, capital-light, and scales globally. This is the master trend.

### The leading companies and their moats

- **Marriott** — largest branded company, **>9,300 properties / ~1,706,000 rooms**, 2024 gross fees $5,170M, 6.8% net rooms growth (Marriott, 2024 — https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/marriott-international-reports-fourth-quarter-and-full-year-2024-results-302372780.html). Moat: **Bonvoy loyalty scale** (72% of U.S. room nights booked by members) + the industry's widest brand ladder (30 brands from Moxy to Ritz-Carlton).
- **Hilton** — under 1M rooms but the fastest, cleanest asset-light grower: **7.3% NUG in 2024**, ~500k-room pipeline (Hilton, 2025 — https://stories.hilton.com/releases/hilton-closes-out-2024-with-record-levels-of-growth-opening-more-rooms-than-any-year-in-its-history). Moat: brand strength per key + developer preference (highest RevPAR index in many segments).
- **Wyndham** — the volume franchiser: **~8,300+ hotels, 855,000+ rooms**, world's largest by franchised property count, concentrated in economy/midscale (Wyndham/industry, 2024 — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wyndham_Hotels_%26_Resorts). Moat: dominance of the fragmented economy roadside segment where independents have no distribution.
- **Choice** — **~7,600 hotels / 650,000+ rooms**, similar economy/midscale focus (Choice, 2024 — https://media.choicehotels.com/2024-03-11-Choice-Hotels-Provides-Update-on-Exchange-Offer-and-Proposal-to-Acquire-Wyndham-Hotels-Resorts). Choice pursued a hostile bid for Wyndham in 2023–24 (later dropped), signaling scale consolidation pressure.
- **IHG, Hyatt, Accor** — round out the global top tier; Accor leads in Europe (~805k rooms). Hyatt has pivoted hardest to asset-light and luxury/lifestyle (Apple Leisure Group, Standard).
- **Booking Holdings & Expedia** — the demand gatekeepers: **$165.6B and $110.9B gross bookings** in 2024 (Booking/Expedia 10-Ks, 2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1075531/000107553125000010/bkng-20241231.htm ; https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1324424/000132442425000008/expe-20241231.htm). Moat: **two-sided network + performance-marketing dominance**; they own the low-frequency/independent traveler.
- **Airbnb** — **491M nights booked, ~$82B GBV, >$11B revenue** in 2024 (Airbnb 10-K, 2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1559720/000155972025000010/abnb-20241231.htm). Moat: unique non-hotel supply + brand ubiquity; competes with hotels for leisure and long-stay demand.

### Sources of competitive advantage (what actually defends the moat)

- **Loyalty programs + co-branded credit cards**: the single strongest moat. Marriott's Bonvoy and Hilton Honors lock in repeat direct demand and generate high-margin card fees (Marriott partners JPMorgan Chase and American Express). **[Inference]** This is the weapon that keeps the OTA toll at bay — the more direct-booked a brand's demand, the less power OTAs and the higher the value of the flag to owners.
- **Scale in distribution and marketing**: fixed-cost tech and marketing spread over more rooms → lower cost per booking than any independent can achieve.
- **Pipeline / developer preference**: the brand a developer chooses compounds for decades. Hilton and Marriott win here on RevPAR-index track record.
- **For OTAs**: Google/performance-marketing efficiency and merchant-model working-capital float.

### Regional clusters and why they exist

- **U.S.** — the largest, most franchise-penetrated market; the fee model was invented here. Sun Belt and drive-to leisure markets outperformed 2022–24; gateway urban (NYC, SF) lagged on slow business/international recovery.
- **Europe** — more independent and boutique, less franchised; Accor-led; higher barriers to new supply (heritage/zoning) support existing-asset value.
- **Asia-Pacific / China** — the fastest unit-growth region and the core of Marriott/Hilton/IHG pipelines; brands grow via local developer capital.
- **Middle East** — capital-rich (sovereign wealth), luxury/resort and mega-project driven (Saudi Vision 2030, NEOM).
**[Inference]** Clusters form where **demand density + capital + permitting** align: convention cities cluster full-service group hotels; airports cluster select-service; leisure destinations cluster resorts. The brand companies are demand-agnostic and grow wherever developer capital appears.

### Trade flows, subsidies, national security

- **"Trade" here is tourism flows** — inbound international travel is an export (foreign spending in domestic hotels). U.S. inbound recovery lagged post-COVID, capping gateway RevPAR.
- **Industrial policy / subsidies**: tourism-promotion boards, convention-center subsidies, and hotel-development tax abatements (TIF, PILOT) are common local incentives; Gulf-state megaprojects are direct sovereign subsidy.
- **National security / ownership screening**: cross-border hotel M&A can trigger CFIUS-type review when assets sit near sensitive sites; data/loyalty-program privacy and foreign ownership of critical-location real estate are the live concerns. Broadly, lodging is **not** a strategic-materials industry, so security exposure is modest versus, say, semiconductors.

### Where profits accrue vs. where they're competed away

- **Fattest, most durable**: brand/franchise fees (Marriott's $3.1B franchise fees at ~60–70% margin). Competed away slowly, if at all — switching costs and loyalty lock-in are high.
- **Structurally powerful toll**: OTA commissions on independent supply (15–30%). Competed away only where brands claw back direct demand.
- **Thinnest, most cyclical**: owning the real estate. Capital-intensive, operating-leverage-exposed, and the layer everyone else tolls. **[Inference]** This is why the smart-money structure is to *own the brand, franchise the bricks* — and why REIT equity trades at a persistent multiple discount to the fee companies.

### Business models gaining vs. losing relevance

**Gaining:**
- **Asset-light franchising** — the dominant winning model; every major is shedding owned real estate.
- **Extended-stay and midscale conversions** — low-labor, high-margin, financeable in a high-rate world.
- **Soft brands / lifestyle** — let independents keep character while buying into distribution; grows the addressable pipeline.
- **Direct booking + first-party data + loyalty** — the counter-attack on OTAs.
- **Alternative accommodations** — Airbnb's 491M nights show durable share of leisure/long-stay demand; hotels responded with their own home/extended-stay products.

**Losing / at risk:**
- **Unbranded independents** without scale distribution — squeezed between OTA tolls and brand RevPAR premiums.
- **Owning commodity real estate** at cyclical peaks — chronic multiple discount.
- **Pure GDS/legacy distribution** — disintermediated by direct + metasearch.

### Disruption and obsolescence risks

- **OTA/metasearch power** could re-intensify if brands lose the loyalty battle — the perennial threat to the flag's value.
- **AI trip-planning / agentic booking** could re-intermediate discovery, shifting demand-capture value to whoever owns the AI booking layer (Google, OpenAI, or the OTAs themselves). **[Inference]** This is the most credible *new* value-migration vector — if an AI agent books the room, the customer relationship (and the toll) may move again. **[Speculative — watch, don't bank on it.]**
- **Short-term-rental regulation** swings the Airbnb-vs-hotel balance city by city (NYC's Local Law 18 curtailed STR supply, helping hotels).
- **Labor automation** (contactless, robotics) could restructure the operating cost base — real but incremental, not obsolescence.

### Distinguishing real progress from promotion

- **Real**: the asset-light shift (verifiable in fee-revenue growth and balance-sheet deleveraging), loyalty-driven direct-booking share, extended-stay margin superiority, conversion-led growth in a high-rate environment.
- **Promotional**: "revolutionary" AI concierge and robot-staffing announcements have so far moved costs marginally, not structurally; "metaverse hotels" and most crypto-loyalty pilots produced no durable economics. **[Inference]** Judge any claim by whether it changes NUG, RevPAR index, channel mix, or labor-per-occupied-room — if it touches none of those four, it's marketing.

### Where value is likely to migrate next

**[Inference]** (1) Further from bricks to brand and to *data/distribution* — whoever owns the booking relationship (brand loyalty vs. OTA vs. emerging AI agents) captures the toll. (2) Toward **scale**: consolidation (Choice/Wyndham dynamics) favors the largest loyalty networks. (3) Toward **low-labor formats** (extended-stay, midscale) as long as wage inflation persists. The losers are sub-scale independents and peak-cycle commodity real-estate owners; the winners are the largest brand/loyalty platforms and whoever wins the AI-era demand-capture layer.

### 11. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs are room-nights, meeting/event space, food/beverage, amenities, loyalty benefits and destination access. Noise, water/energy use, waste, housing pressure and traffic are externalities. Unsold room-night capacity perishes daily; service quality and reputation persist.

Guest, booker, payer and beneficiary differ for corporate, group, airline crew and leisure travel. OTAs and agents can own discovery; corporate planners and convention organizers aggregate demand; owners, brands and operators split economics. Segment by purpose, source market, channel, weekday/season and price sensitivity.

Supply is hyperlocal while brands/distribution are global. Demand depends on airlift, attractions, office/convention activity, currency and safety; resort and urban/business markets behave differently. Owners, REITs, private funds, brands/franchisors, third-party operators, OTAs and short-term rental hosts capture different layers.

Private real-estate and corporate capital mixes with tourism promotion, convention centers, airports, municipal incentives and sometimes public hotel finance. Rules cover zoning, building/fire/health, labor and lodging tax, liquor/gaming, accessibility, franchise, privacy, short-term rentals and environmental/water constraints. Visa and aviation policy can be demand legislation.

Cross-market links connect hotels with airlines, entertainment, restaurants, real estate, labor and credit. Flight capacity or convention calendars can cap occupancy; exchange rates shift inbound/outbound travel; high rates stop new supply before affecting existing demand; remote work reduces some corporate nights while enabling blended leisure; climate and insurance can strand destination assets.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### The core insight: three separable P&Ls under one roof

A hotel produces a perishable good — a room-night that is worthless the moment it goes unsold. Everything about the industry's economics flows from **perishability + high fixed cost + fixed capacity**. But the *money* is earned by up to three different parties on the same building: the **owner** (real estate P&L), the **operator/manager** (running the hotel for a fee), and the **brand** (licensing IP + distribution for a fee). Modern industry structure has deliberately split these so that the highest-margin, lowest-capital slice — the brand — is owned by different shareholders than the capital-heavy real estate.

### The operating workflow (owner/operator level)

1. **Revenue management** sets a different price for every room every day, forecasting demand and flexing ADR (average daily rate) to maximize RevPAR (revenue per available room). This is the single most important operating skill; software (IDeaS, Duetto) optimizes it.
2. **Distribution**: inventory is pushed to brand.com, the GDS (for corporate/travel-agent bookings), and OTAs. Channel mix determines net rate after commission.
3. **Front-of-house delivery**: check-in, housekeeping, F&B, maintenance — labor-intensive service production.
4. **Cost control**: labor scheduling to occupancy (the key flex), plus fixed costs (property tax, insurance, debt service) that don't flex at all.

### Capacity, and how it's measured

Capacity = **available room-nights** = rooms × days. It is fixed short-term (you cannot add rooms overnight) and expensive to add long-term (12–36 month build). The three universal KPIs:

- **Occupancy** = rooms sold ÷ rooms available. **[Fact]** U.S. average was ~63.6% in 2024, below 2019's 65.8% (AHLA, 2024 — https://www.ahla.com/sites/default/files/SOTI.2024.Final_.Draft_.v4.pdf).
- **ADR** = room revenue ÷ rooms sold (the price).
- **RevPAR** = ADR × occupancy = room revenue ÷ rooms available. This is THE headline metric. **[Fact]** CBRE estimated 2024 U.S. RevPAR at a nominal record ~$100.54, ~114.5% of 2019 (CBRE, 2024 — https://www.cbre.com/press-releases/cbre-hotels-adjusts-outlook-for-2024-due-to-economic-conditions-predicts-modest-us-revpar-growth).

**[Inference]** RevPAR growth driven by **ADR** (price) is far more valuable than growth driven by **occupancy**, because rate flows almost entirely to profit while extra occupancy brings variable cost (cleaning, amenities, more labor). This "flow-through" asymmetry is why revenue managers protect rate in downturns.

### Asset types and their economics

- **Limited-service / select-service** (Hampton, Fairfield, Holiday Inn Express): no restaurant or minimal F&B, ~$195k/key to build (HVS, 2024 — https://www.hvs.com/article/9999-HVS-US-Hotel-Development-Cost-Survey-2024), highest and most stable margins (~45%+ GOP), the workhorse of franchised growth. **Why chosen:** low labor, simple ops, easy to franchise and finance.
- **Full-service / upper-upscale** (Marriott, Hilton flagships): restaurants, meeting space, ~$527k/key, more volatile margins (F&B and group demand swing), ~30% hotel EBITDA margin. **Why chosen:** captures group/convention and corporate demand a limited-service box can't.
- **Luxury / resort** (Ritz-Carlton, St. Regis, Four Seasons): trophy real estate, highest ADR and highest absolute profit per key, but capital-intensive and cyclical.
- **Extended-stay** (Residence Inn, WoodSpring): apartment-like, very low labor per occupied room, structurally the highest-margin niche and fastest-growing segment.

### Unit economics — the cost stack

For an owned hotel, roughly (industry-typical, [Estimate]):
- Rooms department profit margin ~70%+ (rooms are cheap to turn over).
- F&B roughly breaks even to modestly profitable (labor-heavy).
- **Undistributed** costs (sales/marketing, admin, utilities, maintenance) ~25% of revenue.
- **GOP** (gross operating profit) ~35–40% of revenue at a healthy select-service hotel, ~30% full-service. **[Fact]** Host Hotels (upper-upscale/luxury portfolio) posted a 29.2% comparable hotel EBITDA margin in 2024 (Host, 2024 — https://ir.hosthotels.com/news-releases/news-release-details/host-hotels-resorts-inc-reports-results-2024).
- Below GOP: management fee (~3% base + incentive), franchise fee (8–12% all-in if franchised, see INPUTS), FF&E reserve (~4%), property tax, insurance → **net operating income (NOI)**. Then debt service.

**Marginal room-night economics:** the cost to sell one *more* room in an already-open hotel is tiny — housekeeping labor, linens, amenities, utilities: often **$20–40**. So on a $150 ADR, incremental margin approaches 75–85%. **[Inference]** This extreme operating leverage is why hotels are hyper-cyclical: in an upcycle incremental RevPAR is nearly pure profit; in a downcycle fixed costs (debt, property tax, core staff) crush margins fast.

### The brand-company P&L (why it's a different animal)

The brand company doesn't run the marginal-room math above. It collects a **percentage of the owner's revenue** and bears almost no operating cost or capital.

- **[Fact]** Marriott's 2024 gross fee revenue was **$5,170M**: franchise fees $3,113M, base management fees $1,288M, incentive management fees $769M (Marriott, 2024 — https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/marriott-international-reports-fourth-quarter-and-full-year-2024-results-302372780.html). Marriott's system exceeded **9,300 properties / ~1,706,000 rooms** at year-end 2024, growing net rooms **6.8%**.
- **[Fact]** Hilton achieved **7.3% net unit growth** in 2024 with a pipeline of 3,578 hotels / 498,600 rooms (Hilton, 2025 — https://stories.hilton.com/releases/hilton-closes-out-2024-with-record-levels-of-growth-opening-more-rooms-than-any-year-in-its-history).

**[Inference]** For a brand company the "unit economics" are: fee margin ~60–70% (almost no COGS), and the growth algorithm is *net unit growth × RevPAR growth = fee revenue growth*, compounding with global hotel supply regardless of any single asset. This is annuity-like, capital-light, and the reason for the valuation gap below.

### KPIs practitioners actually track

- **RevPAR, ADR, occupancy** (demand/pricing).
- **RevPAR index / fair-share** (STR): your RevPAR ÷ your competitive set's — measures whether the flag is winning share.
- **GOP margin, GOPPAR** (profit per available room), **flow-through** (% of incremental revenue reaching GOP).
- **Net unit growth (NUG)** and **pipeline** (brand-company growth).
- **Cost of customer acquisition / channel mix** (direct vs OTA %).
- For owners: **NOI, cap rate, EBITDA multiple, per-key value**.

### Development / lead timelines

Ground-up: 12 months permitting/design + 18–30 months construction ⇒ **2.5–4 years** concept-to-open. Conversions: months. **[Inference]** This long lag is the source of the industry's supply cycle — capital commits at the peak, rooms deliver into the next trough, which is why gateway oversupply episodes recur.

### Characteristic failure points

- **Over-leverage into a downturn**: fixed debt service + perishable revenue = fastest path to owner default (2008–09, 2020 saw mass forbearance).
- **PIP walls**: brand demands a costly renovation the owner can't finance → flag loss → RevPAR collapse.
- **Channel dependency**: an independent hotel with 60% OTA mix bleeds 15–25% commission.
- **Concentration**: single-market/single-demand assets (Maui resorts after 2023 wildfires materially dented Host's 2024 RevPAR — Host, 2024, URL above).

### VALUATION across life-stages

#### (a) Mature, cash-generative brand companies (Marriott, Hilton)
Value the fee annuity, not real estate. Methods: **EV/EBITDA** and **P/E** on forward earnings, plus **DCF** of the fee stream and a **unit-growth × RevPAR** algorithm.
- **[Fact]** Asset-light operators (Marriott, Hilton) trade at **~18–24x EV/EBITDA**, versus hotel real estate at ~4–14x — a 30–50% premium reflecting royalty-income quality (industry, 2024–26 — https://ctacquisitions.com/hotel-business-valuation/). As of early 2026 Marriott ~20x, Hilton ~27x, Hyatt ~19.5x (valueinvesting.io, 2026 — https://valueinvesting.io/MAR/valuation/ev_ebitda-multiples). **[Flag: these spot multiples are ~2026 and move with the market.]**
- Key value drivers: NUG durability, pipeline conversion, RevPAR, credit-card/loyalty fee growth, capital-return (these firms buy back stock aggressively since they generate FCF and hold little real estate).

#### (b) Cyclical, asset-heavy owners/REITs (Host, Park, Pebblebrook)
Value the real estate and its cash flow *across the cycle*, not at a single point.
- **Cap rate on NOI** (NOI ÷ price): the primary real-estate metric. Upper-upscale/luxury trades ~10–13x stabilized EBITDA / ~6–8% cap rates ([Estimate], CTAcquisitions/industry, 2024–26 — https://ctacquisitions.com/hotel-business-valuation/).
- **EV/EBITDA** (4–14x range depending on quality/market), **price-to-NAV** (REITs frequently trade at discounts to net asset value — a signal of cyclical fear or cheap real estate), **replacement cost** (a floor: if EV/key is far below the ~$317k–$527k/key to build, buying beats building).
- **Per-key value** and **RevPAR multiples** for quick comps.
- **[Inference]** Critically, use **mid-cycle or normalized EBITDA**, not peak or trough — hotels' operating leverage makes trailing EBITDA misleading. A REIT looking "cheap" on peak EBITDA can be expensive on normalized. Discount to NAV + replacement-cost floor together bracket fair value.

#### (c) Pre-revenue / early / development-stage
No operating cash flow yet — value rests on **entitlements, permits, brand affiliation, and probability-adjusted future cash flows**.
- **Development-exit / residual land value**: project stabilized NOI ÷ exit cap rate = stabilized value; subtract total development cost (land + hard + soft + financing) = developer profit. If negative, the project doesn't pencil — exactly the 2023–24 condition when high rates + $317k+/key costs killed starts (HVS, 2024, URL above).
- **Yield-on-cost vs. exit cap** spread is the go/no-go test (need ~150–200 bps of positive spread [Estimate] to justify development risk).
- For a brand/tech startup (booking platform, PMS): value on **gross booking value × take-rate** and revenue multiples, benchmarked to Booking's ~$165.6B GBV and Expedia's 12.3% revenue margin (Booking/Expedia 10-Ks, 2024 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1075531/000107553125000010/bkng-20241231.htm ; https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1324424/000132442425000008/expe-20241231.htm).
- **[Inference]** For early lodging *concepts* (soft brands, STR platforms), the value is optionality on distribution scale and pipeline signings — a signed-but-unopened pipeline (Hilton's 498,600 rooms) is a semi-contractual future fee annuity and is valued as risk-adjusted future NUG.

### 11. Complete reservation-to-stay and property cash mechanics

The chain is create demand and price inventory → distribute/reserve → collect deposit/guarantee → prepare room/property → check in and deliver stay/ancillaries → settle → clean/turn and manage reputation → maintain/renovate. Revenue management optimizes a perishable inventory by date, room type, segment, channel and length of stay.

Property revenue includes room, food/beverage, meetings, resort/parking and other fees. Net ADR must reflect discounts, commissions, loyalty and package allocation. GOP/EBITDA precedes franchise/management fees, insurance/tax, reserve and owner capex. Brand companies earn franchise/base/incentive and loyalty/card fees on system volumes without owning most real estate.

Working capital includes advance deposits/deferred revenue, OTA/card receivables, payroll, inventories and loyalty liabilities. FF&E reserve is not always sufficient economic maintenance capex; renovation cycles require closure/disruption. Debt service and covenants make an asset with positive hotel EBITDA vulnerable during occupancy shocks.

Track occupancy, ADR, RevPAR and index, net revenue after channel, group pace and cancellations, labor hours per occupied room, GOP margin, food/beverage and ancillary spend, guest scores, loyalty/direct mix, pipeline and supply growth, renovation status, debt yield/coverage and fixed charges. Stress recession, airlift/event loss, wage/utility/insurance inflation, OTA dependence, renovation, new supply, epidemic/security event and refinancing.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Room revenue = available rooms × occupancy × ADR. Property cash = rooms + F&B/ancillary − labor, utilities, supplies, distribution and other operating − management/franchise − tax/insurance − FF&E and debt. Brand revenue = system sales × fee rates.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Occupancy | roughly 60–80% stabilized; market/season-specific | Demand and fixed cost |
| ADR | currency/room night by segment | Price |
| RevPAR | occupancy × ADR | Room revenue productivity |
| GOP margin | often ~25–50% by service/market | Operating leverage |
| Labor share | often largest controllable expense | Margin |
| OTA/third-party mix | percent of bookings and commission | Acquisition cost |
| Group pace | bookings for future dates versus prior | Forward demand |
| FF&E reserve/capex | often ~3–5% revenue reserve, but actual cycles vary | Maintenance |
| Debt-service coverage | property cash/debt service | Finance risk |
| Supply pipeline | under-construction rooms/current supply | Future competition |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Separate system sales, fee revenue and owned hotel revenue; gross/net rooms and package allocation; advance deposits/deferred revenue; loyalty; management/franchise fees; leases; FF&E reserve versus actual capex; property debt and JV.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Occupancy/ADR by segment, airlift/events, corporate/group/leisure and currency.
- Wages, utilities, food, insurance/tax, OTA commission and brand fee.
- New supply, renovation, reviews, labor, financing and destination risk.
- Travel/visa, short-term rental, lodging tax, climate and safety.

### Valuation discipline

Value brand/franchise on net room growth, fee yield and owner health; owned hotels on normalized property cash, capex and real estate; management contracts on duration/termination.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Post-9/11 travel shock | Security event sharply reduced air and business travel | Occupancy/ADR and hotel cash fell | Demand shocks hit daily inventory immediately |
| 2008–2009 recession | Corporate/group and finance contracted | RevPAR and transactions fell; supply slowed | Rate/credit and operations interact |
| OTA rise | Online intermediaries gained discovery and booking share | Hotels paid commission and invested in direct loyalty | Distribution owns economics |
| 2020 pandemic | Travel and meetings stopped | Hotels closed/furloughed and debt stressed | High fixed cost plus daily perishability creates tail risk |
| 2022–2025 recovery divergence | Leisure recovered before some business/urban segments | ADR and occupancy paths differed | Segment and market beat industry averages |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Air/TSA, booking pace, search, group/corporate calendar, ADR/occupancy, channel, staffing, reviews, pipeline and credit.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** RevPAR growth only from inflation while occupancy/share falls, signed pipeline without finance, or brand room growth with weak owner returns.
- **Durable lesson:** Hotel expertise separates guest demand, property cash, brand fees and real-estate finance.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Last available comparable room in a location/date or incremental new hotel | Perishable inventory makes short-run supply fixed |
| Marginal customer | Traveler able to change date, location, trip length or lodging type | Purpose and booking window determine elasticity |
| Clearing mechanism | Dynamic room rates by date/segment/channel plus group contracts | ADR averages conceal scarcity and channel cost |
| Cash shutdown point | Hotel may operate while room rate exceeds incremental housekeeping/utility/channel cost | Fixed property and labor obligations drive low-season losses |
| New-capacity incentive | Stabilized NOI value exceeds development/renovation cost at adequate margin | Pipeline should be compared with future demand and financing |
| Adjustment lag | Daily pricing, months for staffing, years for development/renovation | Demand shocks create immediate RevPAR changes |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| RevPAR | Total revenue or owner profitability | Add ancillary revenue, channel cost, labor, fees, fixed charges and capex |
| Occupancy | Demand strength | Pair with ADR, segment, room closures, comps and market supply |
