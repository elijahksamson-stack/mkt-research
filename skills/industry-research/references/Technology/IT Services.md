## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Consulting, systems integration, application development, cloud/cyber/data implementation, managed infrastructure, BPO and technology staffing.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Strategy and consulting | Architecture, operating model and transformation advice | fees, consultants, projects | Deliverable, seniority, outcome and follow-on |
| Systems integration and development | Configure/build/migrate enterprise systems | backlog, labor hours, milestones | T&M/fixed price, platform, scope and acceptance |
| Managed services | Operate applications, infrastructure, cloud and security | contracts, endpoints, workloads, SLA | Transition, automation, ticket/incident and renewal |
| BPO and operations | Finance, HR, contact, claims and transaction processing | FTE, transactions, outcome | Labor location, automation, pass-through and regulation |
| Staffing and project talent | Temporary/contract technical labor | hours, spread, headcount | Bill/pay rate, tenure, classification and client concentration |

### Specifications that change value

- State contract type, funded scope, duration, cancellation, inflation, SLA, penalties and change control.
- Backlog must separate TCV/ACV, pass-through, renewal, unfunded and executable work.
- Labor needs role, level, location, bill/pay, utilization, attrition and subcontractor status.
- Managed service needs baseline, transition, asset inventory, automation, incident and exit terms.
- AI/automation claims need hours removed, quality, price-sharing and revenue cannibalization.

### Role map

Client sponsor funds; procurement contracts; business user defines outcome; service firm designs/delivers; software/cloud/hardware partner supplies; subcontractor staffs; regulator/auditor controls data/security; client accepts.

### Terms that must be explicit

- TCV, ACV, bookings, backlog and revenue
- bill rate, pay rate and gross spread
- utilization and pyramid
- onshore, nearshore, offshore and subcontractor
- SLA, service credit and transition


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope note: "IT Services" here means the professional-services and managed-services layer of the technology sector — consulting, systems integration (SI), application development & maintenance (ADM), IT & business-process outsourcing (ITO/BPO), infrastructure management, and cloud/data/AI implementation. It is a **labor-and-knowledge industry, not a manufacturing one**. That single fact reorders the entire input map: the dominant "raw material" is skilled human time, and almost every margin and capacity question flows back to people, not to steel or silicon.

Sizing anchor: worldwide IT-services spending was projected at roughly **$1.5 trillion in 2024**, becoming the single largest segment of the ~$5.26 trillion global IT market for the first time — [Fact] (Gartner, 2024 — https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-07-16-gartner-forecasts-worldwide-it-spending-to-grow-7-point-5-percent-in-2024).

---

### 1. Labor — the master input (60–70% of cost)

For a pure-play IT-services firm, personnel is by far the largest cost line. TCS, the largest India-based provider, ran a ~24.6% operating margin in FY2024, which implies the overwhelming remainder is people-driven cost (employee benefit expense plus subcontractor cost) — [Fact] (Statista/TCS, FY2024 — https://www.statista.com/statistics/759997/india-tcs-operating-margin/). [Inference] For most listed peers, salaries + subcontractors run ~55–70% of revenue, so a 1-percentage-point move in wage inflation or utilization moves operating margin by roughly 0.5–0.7 points — the industry's single most sensitive lever.

**Scale of the labor pool.** India's technology industry employed **5.43 million people** in FY2024 (the primary global delivery base), adding ~60,000 net — [Fact] (NASSCOM Strategic Review 2024 — https://nasscom.in/knowledge-center/publications/technology-sector-india-strategic-review-2024). Individual firms are enormous: Accenture ~**774,000** employees at Aug-2024 [Fact] (Accenture FY24 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1467373/000146737324000278/acn-20240831.htm); TCS **601,000+** [Fact] (TCS Q4 FY24 — https://www.tcs.com/who-we-are/newsroom/press-release/tcs-financial-results-q4-fy-2024).

**Cost sensitivities that matter:**
- **Wage inflation.** TCS/Infosys/Wipro saw average wage costs rise >5.5% in FY2023-24, driven by lateral hiring in AI/ML/cloud plus low-single-digit increments — [Fact] (Business Standard, 2024 — https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/tcs-infosys-wipro-see-salary-bills-surge-even-as-revenue-growth-slows-124042200166_1.html). Because contracts (especially multi-year fixed-price) lock revenue, wage spikes compress margin unless offset by utilization, pyramid, or automation.
- **Utilization** (billable hours ÷ available hours). Infosys ran **82.2% ex-trainees** in FY2024 and treats ~84–85% as its comfort ceiling — [Fact] (Infosys 20-F FY24 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1067491/000095017024076649/infy-20240331.htm). The gap between actual and ceiling is idle "bench" cost; a few points of utilization is worth more to margin than most price increases.
- **Attrition.** FY2024 attrition fell to ~12.5% (TCS) and ~12.6% (Infosys) from ~20% a year earlier — [Fact] (Business Standard, 2024 — same URL). High attrition raises recruiting/training cost and threatens delivery continuity; the FY24 easing was a tailwind after the 2021-22 "great resignation" spike.
- **The pyramid.** Profitability depends on a wide base of low-cost juniors under a narrow apex of expensive architects. GenAI attacks the base of that pyramid (see MECHANICS), which is why the model is under structural threat, not merely cyclical pressure.

**Scarcity / bottleneck:** "Day-zero-ready" talent in GenAI, MLOps and VLSI is genuinely scarce, triggering poaching and localized wage spikes even amid soft overall demand — [Fact] (Zinnov, 2025 — https://zinnov.com/centers-of-excellence/5-shifts-defining-indias-global-capability-centers-gccs-story-in-2025-blog/).

### 2. Visa / mobility regime — a regulatory input on labor

The offshore-onsite ("global delivery") model of TCS, Infosys, Wipro and HCL was built on **H-1B visas** to place workers at US client sites and coordinate offshore teams — [Fact] (EPI, 2024 — https://www.epi.org/blog/the-h-1b-visa-program-remains-the-outsourcing-visa-more-than-half-of-the-top-30-h-1b-employers-were-outsourcing-firms/). The 2025 introduction of a **$100,000 H-1B fee** raises onsite labor cost sharply and is expected to push work further offshore/nearshore and lift US IT-service prices — [Fact] (Forrester, 2025 — https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-long-term-impact-of-the-100000-h-1b-fee-higher-it-service-prices-and-more-offshore/). Infosys's offshore effort mix already drifted from ~71% (FY15) to ~76% (FY25) [Fact] (same Forrester source). [Inference] Visa policy is thus a *pricing and mix* input as much as a labor one: it is a single point of political failure that can re-rate US-delivered margins overnight.

### 3. Software, IP and platforms — the "materials" that get integrated

IT-services firms sell the *assembly and operation* of other people's software. Their critical upstream dependencies are the platform vendors whose products they implement:
- **Hyperscalers** (AWS ~30%, Azure ~25%, Google Cloud ~13% of a ~$395B cloud-infra market) — [Fact] (Q1 2026 figures — https://businesstats.com/big-three-hold-dominant-lead-in-accelerating-cloud-market/). SIs earn certifications and co-sell; the cloud vendor increasingly acts as "kingmaker," and a systems integrator typically captures **15–25% of deal value** in these trilateral deals — [Fact] (The Love Group, 2024 — https://www.thelovegrp.com/blog-mastering-hyperscaler-partnerships-for-cloud-providers).
- **Enterprise application vendors** — SAP, Salesforce, Oracle, ServiceNow, Workday, Microsoft Dynamics. Large SI practices are essentially resale-and-implementation channels for these ecosystems.
- **Foundation-model providers** — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google — now a fast-emerging upstream input for the GenAI-delivery business.

Pricing power here sits **with the platform vendor**, not the integrator: the vendor controls the roadmap, the certification gate, and the marketplace economics. [Inference] This is a structural vulnerability — SIs are complementors whose margin the platform can squeeze via marketplace fees, first-party professional services, or bundling.

### 4. Physical infrastructure and energy

Relative to manufacturing this is light, but not zero:
- **Delivery centers / real estate** in India, the Philippines, Poland, Latin America — leased office campuses. A hybrid-work shift lowered this cost base post-2020.
- **Data centers** for hosting/managed-infrastructure lines (Kyndryl, DXC, IBM). Here energy and capital intensity rise materially and the economics converge toward asset-heavy hosting.
- **Telecom / connectivity** — reliable low-latency links between offshore centers and client sites are a hard dependency; an undersea-cable or regional outage directly halts delivery.
- **Energy** is a second-order cost for consulting-heavy firms but first-order for data-center-heavy ones and for the AI-inference workloads now being resold.

### 5. Financial capital

Pure services is capital-light and cash-generative — low capex, and receivables (DSO) is the main balance-sheet variable. The real "capital" input for consolidators is **M&A**: Accenture and Capgemini grow partly by serial acquisition of niche capability (cloud, cyber, data, industry expertise). Cheap capital and high equity multiples are therefore an input to the roll-up strategy; a high-rate environment slows it.

### 6. Dependencies on OUTSIDE industries (demand-side inputs)

IT services is **derived demand** — it has no end market of its own; it sells into everyone else's IT budgets. Concentration by vertical:
- **Banking, Financial Services & Insurance (BFSI)** is typically the largest single vertical for most majors — [Inference] often 25–40% of revenue at India-based firms. A financial-services capex freeze (rate shock, recession) is the sector's biggest cyclical risk.
- **Communications, media & tech, healthcare, manufacturing, retail, public sector** round out the mix. NASSCOM flagged Europe, APAC, manufacturing, retail and healthcare as FY24 growth markets — [Fact] (NASSCOM SR 2024 — same URL).

[Inference] Because demand is derived, the true upstream "commodity" whose price gates the industry is the **client's discretionary IT budget**, which is itself a function of interest rates, corporate earnings and macro confidence. This is why the sector trades as a cyclical despite looking like a stable-margin services business.

### 7. Regulation and data

Data-protection and localization regimes (GDPR, India's DPDP Rules 2025, sector rules in healthcare/finance) are compliance inputs that raise delivery cost and constrain where data — and therefore work — can physically be processed — [Fact] (Drishti IAS, 2025 — https://www.drishtiias.com/daily-updates/daily-news-editorials/global-capability-centres-and-indias-growth). Cybersecurity is both a cost and a saleable service; CERT-In handled ~2.94 million incidents in 2025, underscoring the operational-risk input — [Fact] (same source).

---

### What actually determines margin vs. caps capacity

- **Determines margin:** wage inflation, utilization, the onsite/offshore mix, the pyramid shape (junior:senior ratio), pricing model (fixed-price vs. T&M), and increasingly automation/GenAI leverage. These are the five dials management pulls every quarter.
- **Caps capacity:** availability of skilled, hireable talent at target cost — and, at the onsite layer, visa allocation. Unlike a factory there is no physical line rate; "capacity" is billable headcount × utilization. The binding constraint is scarce senior/specialist talent, not junior supply.

### How a shock propagates (worked example)

A sharp US-recession + rate shock → BFSI and tech clients freeze discretionary projects → new bookings and book-to-bill fall → utilization drops as consultants roll off projects onto the bench → because salaries are largely fixed short-term, margin compresses fast → firms cut hiring, shrink the bench, and delay wage hikes to defend margin → with a lag, pricing softens on renewals. Conversely, a **GenAI productivity shock** (30–40% delivery-efficiency gains — [Estimate], Forbes/HFS, 2025 — https://www.forbes.com/sites/peterbendorsamuel/2025/08/13/from-labor-arbitrage-to-ai-the-next-big-disruption-in-tech-services/) is deflationary on the *labor input itself*: it erodes the billable hours that constitute revenue, threatening the top line even as it lowers unit cost — the defining strategic tension of the industry in 2025-26.

### 9. Full labor, infrastructure, partner, and contract input ledger

The primary input is correctly matched labor: architects, developers, data/AI engineers, cybersecurity staff, cloud/platform specialists, ERP consultants, network/field technicians, service-desk staff, project managers, sales and domain experts. Recruiting pipelines, visas, universities, training/certifications, subcontractors and utilization benches determine capacity. Attrition destroys tacit client knowledge.

Physical and digital inputs include offices/delivery centers, laptops and secure endpoints, data centers/cloud, networks, power, telecom, remote-access controls, test environments, monitoring, ticketing, automation and business-continuity sites. Providers depend on software/cloud/hardware vendors, certifications, APIs, licenses and partner economics; a hyperscaler or ERP vendor can be supplier, channel and competitor.

Contracts require customer data/system access, clear scope, change governance, security/privacy permissions, service levels and client staff available for decisions. Managed services need transition documentation and asset inventories; project work needs requirements and acceptance criteria. Insurance, bonding and cyber liability may gate regulated or government work.

Funding is mostly working capital from cash/debt/equity, private equity for consolidation and customer advances; public digital modernization, defense and workforce programs create demand and training support. Wage/subcontractor rates, offshore mix, utilization, pricing and rework set margin; scarce certified talent, visas, client access, partner capacity, security clearance and transition readiness gate output. Automation, software products, low-code, captive centers and freelancers substitute for services but often create new integration/governance demand.

## 3. Market Landscape


The industry is a **fragmented, multi-tier ecosystem** sitting between technology *producers* (hyperscalers, software vendors, chipmakers) and technology *consumers* (every enterprise and government). It captures value by being the labor-and-integration layer that makes producers' products actually work inside consumers' operations. Total addressable spend ~$1.5T in 2024 [Fact] (Gartner, 2024 — https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-07-16-gartner-forecasts-worldwide-it-spending-to-grow-7-point-5-percent-in-2024). No single firm holds more than ~4–5% share — Accenture, the largest, had $64.9B FY24 revenue [Fact] (Accenture FY24 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1467373/000146737324000278/acn-20240831.htm), a low-single-digit slice.

---

### 1. The value chain, stage by stage

**Upstream producers (suppliers to the industry):** hyperscalers (AWS ~30%, Azure ~25%, GCP ~13% of a ~$395B market — [Fact], Q1 2026 — https://businesstats.com/big-three-hold-dominant-lead-in-accelerating-cloud-market/), enterprise-software vendors (SAP, Salesforce, Oracle, ServiceNow, Microsoft, Workday), and foundation-model labs. They set the technology the industry integrates.

**The service tiers:**
1. **Strategy / management consulting** — McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte, Accenture Strategy. Sells "what and why." Highest rate, lowest headcount.
2. **Systems integration & digital transformation** — Accenture, IBM Consulting, Capgemini, Deloitte, Infosys, TCS, Cognizant. Sells "how" — designs and builds. The economic center of gravity.
3. **Application dev & maintenance / IT outsourcing** — India majors dominate; codifiable, scale, offshore. The labor-arbitrage engine.
4. **Managed infrastructure / hosting** — Kyndryl, DXC, Atos. Runs legacy estates; declining.
5. **Business-process outsourcing (BPO)** — Genpact, WNS, Concentrix, TCS/Cognizant BPS. Runs finance, HR, contact-center operations.
6. **Staff augmentation / niche boutiques** — the long tail feeding talent and specialty skills into the tiers above.

**Downstream customers:** enterprises and governments across BFSI (largest), CMT, healthcare, manufacturing, retail, public sector.

**Regulators / gatekeepers:** data-protection authorities (GDPR, DPDP), sector regulators, and — critically — **immigration authorities** (US H-1B), plus security-clearance regimes for government work.

### 2. Leading companies and their moats

- **Accenture** — breadth (strategy→run), industry depth, hyperscaler/vendor alliances, and a serial-M&A machine; $81.2B FY24 bookings, ~774,000 people [Fact] (Accenture FY24 10-K, same URL). Moat = scale + brand trust with the C-suite + one-stop breadth.
- **TCS** — scale, delivery discipline, ~24.6% EBIT margin, huge annuity book; $29.08B FY24 revenue, 601,000+ staff [Fact] (Outsource Accelerator/TCS, FY24 — https://news.outsourceaccelerator.com/tcs-fy2024-revenue/; https://www.tcs.com/who-we-are/newsroom/press-release/tcs-financial-results-q4-fy-2024). Moat = cost + execution reliability at scale.
- **Infosys, HCLTech, Wipro** — variants of the same India-based global-delivery moat.
- **IBM Consulting** ($20.7B 2024) — bundled with IBM software/Red Hat; moat = hybrid-cloud + enterprise incumbency [Fact] (IBM 2024 results — https://newsroom.ibm.com/2025-01-29-IBM-RELEASES-FOURTH-QUARTER-RESULTS).
- **Capgemini** (€22.1B 2024) — European incumbency + engineering (Capgemini Engineering) [Fact] (Capgemini FY24 — https://www.capgemini.com/news/press-releases/full-year-2024-results/).
- **Cognizant** (~$19.7B 2024) — healthcare/BFSI depth, India-US hybrid.
- **Kyndryl** ($16B) / **DXC** ($13.7B) — infrastructure incumbency, but declining; Kyndryl hit by a 2026 accounting probe [Fact] (Fortune, 2026 — https://fortune.com/2026/02/10/ibm-spinoff-kyndryl-stock-dives-50-percent-accounting-probe-cfo-exit-red-flags/).

[Inference] Moats in this industry are **weak-to-moderate**: switching costs (deeply embedded run contracts), scale, certifications, and trust — but almost no IP lock-in and low technical barriers. That is why margins cluster and why AI, which erodes the labor moat, is so threatening.

### 3. Where profit accrues vs. where it is competed away

- **Profit accrues** at the **advisory/transformation top of the stack** (scarce expertise, C-suite trust, outcome pricing) and in **proprietary IP/platforms and annuity run books** with switching costs. Accenture's premium multiple reflects this.
- **Profit is competed away** in **commodity ADM, testing, staff-aug and legacy infrastructure run**, where the offering is near-undifferentiated and the buyer runs reverse auctions. India majors defend these on cost and scale, not price.
- **The hyperscaler squeeze:** in cloud deals the platform vendor is "kingmaker" and the SI captures only **15–25% of deal value** [Fact] (The Love Group, 2024 — https://www.thelovegrp.com/blog-mastering-hyperscaler-partnerships-for-cloud-providers) — value migrating upstream to the platform.

### 4. Regional clusters and why they exist

- **India** — the delivery heartland: ~$254B tech industry, ~$200B exports, 5.43M employees FY24 [Fact] (NASSCOM SR 2024 — https://nasscom.in/knowledge-center/publications/technology-sector-india-strategic-review-2024). Exists because of a large English-speaking STEM graduate pool, decades of process/quality maturity (CMMI), time-zone complementarity with the West, and government-supported IT parks/SEZs.
- **Philippines** — voice BPO and back-office, on cultural/accent affinity with the US.
- **Eastern Europe (Poland, Romania), Ukraine (pre-war)** — nearshore for Western Europe; higher-skill, time-zone-aligned.
- **Latin America (Mexico, Costa Rica, Brazil, Colombia)** — the fastest-rising **nearshore** for North America; rates "holding up strongest" because of time-zone alignment that captures more AI-productivity value [Fact] (Forbes/HFS, 2025 — https://www.forbes.com/sites/peterbendorsamuel/2025/08/13/from-labor-arbitrage-to-ai-the-next-big-disruption-in-tech-services/).
- **US / Western Europe** — the demand centers and the home of high-value advisory; onshore delivery for regulated/security-sensitive work.

### 5. Trade flows and dependencies

The core trade flow is **services exports from India/Philippines/LatAm/EE into US and European IT budgets** — India alone ~$200B of IT/BPM exports FY24 [Fact] (NASSCOM, same URL). This makes the industry acutely exposed to:
- **US immigration policy** — the 2025 $100k H-1B fee directly taxes the onsite leg of the trade [Fact] (Forrester, 2025 — https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-long-term-impact-of-the-100000-h-1b-fee-higher-it-service-prices-and-more-offshore/).
- **Currency** — INR/USD moves flow straight to India-major margins.
- **Data-localization rules** that constrain cross-border data (and therefore work) movement.

### 6. Subsidies, industrial policy, national security

- **India** — SEZ/STPI tax incentives historically underwrote export growth; the government actively courts **Global Capability Centers** as a policy priority.
- **National security** — governments increasingly restrict who can deliver sensitive workloads; US federal and defense work requires cleared, onshore staff, effectively walling India-based commodity delivery out of the highest-trust segment. Data-sovereignty rules (EU, India DPDP) similarly localize work.
- **Cyber** — CERT-In handled ~2.94M incidents in 2025 [Fact] (Drishti IAS, 2025 — https://www.drishtiias.com/daily-updates/daily-news-editorials/global-capability-centres-and-indias-growth); operational resilience is now a policy and procurement criterion.

### 7. The two structural disruptions reshaping the map

#### (a) Global Capability Centers (GCCs / captives) — disintermediation
Enterprises are building their **own** offshore centers instead of buying services. India GCCs generated **$64.6B revenue and 1.9M employees in FY2024**, with ~1,580 centers, projected toward ~$100B by 2030 [Fact] (Zinnov, 2025 — https://zinnov.com/centers-of-excellence/5-shifts-defining-indias-global-capability-centers-gccs-story-in-2025-blog/; Drishti IAS, 2025 — same URL). [Inference] GCCs capture the arbitrage margin the SIs used to earn and pull the highest-value digital/AI work in-house — a direct structural threat to the India majors' growth, though it also creates a market for SIs to *build and operate* GCCs for clients.

#### (b) Generative / agentic AI — deflation of the labor moat
GenAI automates the codifiable, junior-level work at the base of the pyramid. Service providers report **30–40% delivery-efficiency gains**, projected higher over time [Estimate] (Forbes/HFS, 2025 — same URL). HFS Research and Everest Group independently declared the labor-arbitrage model past its "shelf life" in 2026 [Fact] (Forbes/HFS, 2025 — same URL). Market reaction was violent: the Nifty IT index hit multi-month lows and TCS fell ~9% in early June 2026 on AI-disruption fears [Fact] (Forbes/HFS, 2025 — same URL). S&P framed it as "disruption without dislocation — yet" [Fact] (S&P Global Ratings, 2025-26 — https://www.spglobal.com/ratings/en/regulatory/article/ai-impact-on-business-and-technology-services-disruption-without-dislocation-yet-s101669395).

### 8. What's gaining vs. losing relevance

**Gaining:**
- **Outcome-/consumption-based pricing** and **AI-platform/IP-led delivery** (retains productivity value).
- **Nearshore LatAm / EE** (time-zone-aligned, captures AI value).
- **GCC build-operate services** and **AI-transformation consulting**.
- **Cyber, data, and regulated-industry** work where trust and compliance are moats.

**Losing:**
- **Headcount-based T&M** commodity ADM/testing/staff-aug — the historic offshore engine.
- **Legacy infrastructure hosting** (Kyndryl/DXC model) as workloads move to hyperscalers.
- **Pure labor-arbitrage** growth in Pakistan/Philippines commodity segments as AI cuts total demand, not just headcount-per-unit [Inference from Forbes/HFS, 2025 — same URL].

### 9. Who gains, who loses, where value migrates

[Inference, clearly labeled as my analysis]:
- **Likely relative winners:** Accenture and consulting-led firms *if* they convert AI productivity into higher-value outcome work; AI-native services boutiques; nearshore LatAm providers; enterprises building GCCs (they keep the margin); and hyperscalers/platform vendors capturing more of the stack.
- **Likely relative losers:** pure offshore-arbitrage majors on the commodity end (revenue-per-employee deflation risk), legacy infrastructure players (Kyndryl/DXC), and any firm anchored to headcount-based T&M.
- **Value migration:** *upstream* toward platforms/hyperscalers and reusable AI IP, and *inward* toward client GCCs — away from the middle-tier "bodies-for-hire" integration layer that was the industry's historic profit pool.

**Distinguishing real progress from hype:** genuine technical shifts are (i) measurable delivery-efficiency gains now visible in provider margins and pricing-model changes, and (ii) the rise of agentic platforms that break the hours-revenue link. Promotional noise is the blanket claim that AI simply "replaces" offshore labor — early evidence shows AI-heavy customer-service can even cost *more* than offshore humans in some cases [Fact] (CMSWire, 2025-26 — https://www.cmswire.com/contact-center/will-ai-cost-more-than-offshore-human-agents-in-customer-service/), so the transition is uneven, not a clean substitution.

**Staleness flags:** cloud market-share and Accenture-multiple figures are Q1/mid-2026 and move quickly; GCC and AI-productivity numbers are 2025 estimates from Zinnov/HFS/Everest and should be re-verified; the $100k H-1B fee is a 2025 policy whose long-run effect is still unfolding.

### 9. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs include strategy/design, systems integration, application development, cloud migration, cybersecurity, data/AI implementation, infrastructure operations, business-process outsourcing, field support and managed service levels. Documentation, knowledge transfer and resilient operations are products; technical debt, lock-in, failed transformations and data/security incidents are residuals.

The executive sponsor, procurement buyer, IT owner, business user and regulator may all judge success differently. Large enterprises and governments buy multi-vendor programs; SMEs use managed providers; software/hardware firms use channels; private-equity owners may mandate transformations across portfolios. Customer internal capability is both input and substitute.

Delivery is geographically distributed across onshore client teams, nearshore centers and offshore hubs in India, the Philippines, Latin America, Eastern Europe and elsewhere. Wage/talent pools, language, time zone, data residency, political risk and telecom shape location. Global integrators, Indian heritage firms, consultancies, MSPs, specialists, staffing firms, BPOs and customer captives overlap.

Funding is corporate/private capital for providers and customer operating/capital budgets; public procurement, digital-service budgets, defense/security and workforce programs are major demand sources. Policy covers professional and government contracting, labor/immigration, data/privacy, cybersecurity and breach reporting, AI, export/sanctions, accessibility, sector outsourcing and operational resilience.

IT services interacts with software/cloud/hardware, labor, telecom and all customer industries. Cloud migration may reduce legacy work but expands architecture, security and managed-cloud demand; generative AI can compress effort while increasing project volume or price pressure; recessions delay discretionary transformation before essential operations; data-sovereignty rules create local delivery and hosting needs.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


The product is **billable expertise applied to a client's technology problem**, packaged as either a time-bound project or a multi-year managed service. There is no inventory and no unit that rolls off a line. "Production" is the conversion of human hours (leveraged by tools, IP and now GenAI) into a client outcome. Everything about the economics follows from that.

---

### 1. The service workflow, step by step

1. **Demand generation & qualification** — relationship-led (existing accounts) or competitive. Large deals come through **RFP / RFI** processes; strategic ones through advisory-led "land" engagements that seed larger work.
2. **Solutioning & pricing** — a bid team designs the delivery model (team shape, onsite/offshore split, tooling) and prices it. This is where margin is won or lost: the estimate of hours × blended rate, minus assumed productivity.
3. **Contracting** — the pricing model is fixed here (see §3). SOWs, SLAs, and change-control terms determine who bears overrun risk.
4. **Mobilization / transition** — staffing the pyramid, knowledge transfer (KT), environment setup. For outsourcing deals, a multi-month **transition** phase moves an incumbent's operations to the provider — high-risk, often loss-making early.
5. **Delivery** — build/run. Agile squads for development; ITIL-style run books for managed services. Utilization and SLA adherence are tracked daily.
6. **Governance & change** — steering committees, change requests (often the real margin source on fixed-price deals), renewals.
7. **Renewal / expansion / exit** — managed-services contracts (3–7 yr) are annuity revenue; the renewal is where re-pricing and productivity clawbacks happen.

**Lead times:** consulting projects run weeks-to-months; SI programs (e.g., an SAP S/4HANA migration) 12–36 months; large outsourcing deals have 6–12 month sales cycles and 3–7 year terms.

### 2. Competing delivery methods — and why players choose differently

- **Global delivery / labor arbitrage (offshore-heavy).** TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL, Cognizant. A pyramid of low-cost offshore engineers (India, Philippines) does the bulk of work; a thin onsite layer manages the client. Chosen when the work is **codifiable and scale matters** — ADM, testing, infrastructure run, BPO. Wins on price-per-hour; the entire model rested on the wage gap between India and the West.
- **Consulting-led / high-value (onshore-heavy).** Accenture, McKinsey Digital, Deloitte, IBM Consulting. Higher onshore mix, higher rates, sold on outcomes and industry expertise. Chosen when the work is **ambiguous, transformation-scale, and C-suite-sponsored** — strategy, complex change, regulated industries. Wins on trust and breadth, not price.
- **Managed-infrastructure / hosting (asset-heavy).** Kyndryl, DXC. Runs data centers and mainframes for clients. Capital-intensive, lower-margin, structurally declining as workloads move to hyperscalers. Chosen by clients who cannot or will not modernize.
- **GCC / captive.** The client builds its *own* offshore center (see LANDSCAPE) instead of buying the service — chosen when the capability is core/strategic and the client wants control and to keep the arbitrage margin itself.

[Inference] The trade-off is always **price vs. control vs. transformation depth**. Commodity, stable, scale work → offshore arbitrage. Novel, board-level, risky change → consulting-led. The middle is being hollowed out by GenAI and GCCs simultaneously.

### 3. Pricing models = risk allocation (the core economic choice)

- **Time & Materials (T&M):** client pays for hours at agreed rates; provider bears no productivity risk. Simple, low-margin-variance. Losing favor because it explicitly prices *effort*, which AI undermines.
- **Fixed-price:** provider commits to a deliverable for a set fee and **keeps any productivity gains** (or eats overruns). Higher margin *if* the firm executes efficiently — this is where automation/GenAI leverage converts to profit that the client doesn't capture.
- **Outcome- / capability-based:** payment tied to business results or consumption. Growing as AI breaks the effort-value link — "headcount-based models assume value scales with effort, but AI challenges that premise" — [Fact] (Newsweek, 2025-26 — https://www.newsweek.com/ai-forcing-it-services-to-rethink-pricing-11865023).

[Inference] The strategic migration is T&M → fixed-price → outcome-based, because each step lets the provider **retain the value of AI productivity instead of passing it to the client as fewer billed hours.** Firms that stay on T&M in an AI world watch their own revenue deflate.

### 4. Asset types and their economics

- **Human capital** (the pyramid) — the primary asset; off-balance-sheet, rentable, and depreciating (skills go stale).
- **Reusable IP / accelerators / platforms** — codebases, delivery frameworks, industry solutions, and now **agentic-AI platforms**. These break the linear hours-revenue link and are the closest thing to a "product" the industry has; they carry software-like incremental margins.
- **Data-center / hardware** (only for hosting players) — real depreciating capex; drags ROIC.
- **Client relationships / backlog** — the annuity book (managed-services contracts) that smooths the cycle.

### 5. Capacity, KPIs and how they're measured

Capacity = **billable headcount × utilization**. There is no physical ceiling; the constraint is hireable skilled talent (see INPUTS).

KPIs practitioners actually track:
- **Utilization %** — Infosys ~82.2% ex-trainees FY24, target ceiling ~84–85% [Fact] (Infosys 20-F FY24 — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1067491/000095017024076649/infy-20240331.htm).
- **Attrition %** (LTM) — ~12.5–12.6% at TCS/Infosys FY24 [Fact] (Business Standard, 2024 — https://www.business-standard.com/companies/news/tcs-infosys-wipro-see-salary-bills-surge-even-as-revenue-growth-slows-124042200166_1.html).
- **Book-to-bill** (new bookings ÷ revenue) — the leading demand indicator. Accenture booked **$81.2B** new bookings on $64.9B FY24 revenue = ~1.25x book-to-bill [Fact] (Accenture FY24 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1467373/000146737324000278/acn-20240831.htm).
- **Revenue per employee**, **realization** (actual vs. standard rate), **onsite/offshore mix**, **DSO**, **operating/EBIT margin**, **SLA adherence** on run contracts.

### 6. Unit economics — the cost stack

For a blended offshore-heavy provider [Inference, illustrative, anchored on TCS ~24.6% EBIT margin — Statista/TCS FY24 — https://www.statista.com/statistics/759997/india-tcs-operating-margin/]:
- Direct personnel (salaries, benefits) + subcontractor cost: ~55–65% of revenue
- SG&A (sales, admin, facilities): ~12–18%
- → EBIT margin ~20–26% (India majors); ~13–16% GAAP operating margin (Accenture-type) — [Fact] Accenture FY24 GAAP operating margin ~15.5% (Accenture FY24 10-K, same URL).

**Marginal unit ("one more consultant-month"):** cost ≈ that person's fully-loaded salary + a slice of bench/SG&A; revenue ≈ blended bill rate × utilization. Gross margin on incremental billable work is high *if* the person is already hired (utilization play) and low/negative if you must hire and carry bench. This asymmetry is why utilization dominates margin.

**Gross-margin structure:** the wedge is **bill rate − pay rate**, widened by (a) offshore labor arbitrage, (b) pyramid leverage (juniors billed above their cost), and (c) automation/IP reuse. GenAI compresses (a) and (b) by collapsing junior-level effort — the historic profit engine.

### 7. Testing / qualification / approval

Unlike drugs or chips there is no external regulator "approving" the output. Quality gates are contractual and standards-based:
- **Certifications** — hyperscaler and vendor partner tiers (AWS Premier, SAP, ServiceNow), plus process maturity (CMMI), security (ISO 27001, SOC 2), and industry compliance (HIPAA, PCI-DSS). These are the "qualification" that lets a firm bid.
- **Acceptance testing / SLAs** — UAT sign-off on projects; SLA credits on run contracts are the enforcement mechanism.

### 8. Characteristic failure points

- **Fixed-price overruns** — mis-estimated scope eats the whole margin.
- **Bench build-up** in a downturn — fixed salaries, no revenue.
- **Transition failures** on large outsourcing deals — early-life losses that never recover.
- **Attrition spikes** — knowledge loss, re-badging cost, delivery slippage.
- **Concentration** — over-reliance on one vertical (BFSI) or one platform partner.
- **Governance/accounting risk** — e.g., the 2026 Kyndryl accounting probe and CFO exit that halved the stock — [Fact] (Fortune, 2026 — https://fortune.com/2026/02/10/ibm-spinoff-kyndryl-stock-dives-50-percent-accounting-probe-cfo-exit-red-flags/).

### VALUATION across company life-stages

#### (a) Mature, cash-generative services businesses (Accenture, TCS, Infosys, Capgemini)

Valued as **capital-light compounders on earnings and cash flow**:
- **P/E** and **EV/EBIT / EV/EBITDA** are primary. IT-services median ~**10.2x EV/EBITDA, ~1.3x EV/Revenue** [Fact] (Aventis Advisors, 2024 — https://aventis-advisors.com/it-services-valuation-multiples/). Accenture historically commands a premium (~1.7x EV/Revenue, high-teens/low-20s P/E in strong markets; ~14.3x P/E and ~7.7x EV/EBITDA in the 2026 AI-fear drawdown) [Fact] (valueinvesting.io, 2026 — https://valueinvesting.io/ACN/valuation/ev_ebitda-multiples).
- **DCF / FCF yield** works well because capex is low and FCF ≈ net income; DSO and buyback/dividend policy drive per-share value.
- **Key value drivers to model:** organic revenue growth (tracks client IT budgets), margin (utilization × pyramid × automation), and **book-to-bill/backlog** as the forward indicator. A structurally lower growth rate (AI deflation) compresses the multiple — exactly what re-rated Indian IT down in 2026.

#### (b) Cyclical / asset-heavy services (Kyndryl, DXC, infrastructure-run businesses)

Valued **across the cycle on EV/EBITDA and FCF, with heavy attention to declining revenue and contract book**:
- Trade at **low single-digit EV/EBITDA** and often below revenue — the market prices secular decline and capital intensity. DXC ~$13.7B revenue with persistent shrinkage [Fact] (Statista, FY24 — https://www.statista.com/statistics/726399/dxc-technology-revenue-by-year/).
- Model **signings/backlog run-off**, margin from cost-takeout, capex/D&A, and net debt. Value is a mid-cycle EBITDA × a de-rated multiple, sanity-checked against sum-of-parts (some segments growing, legacy declining). Turnaround optionality and buybacks matter more than growth.
- [Inference] These are "melting ice cube plus optionality" cases — you underwrite the pace of decline vs. the cash returned.

#### (c) Pre-revenue / early / capability-led (boutiques, AI-native services startups, roll-up targets)

Little public data; valued on **capability, talent, IP and probability-adjusted future cash flows**:
- **Revenue-multiple / capability premiums.** Niche cloud, cyber, data, and GenAI boutiques are acquired by Accenture/Capgemini at premiums to the sector's ~1.3x EV/Revenue when they add scarce skills or a platform. The asset being bought is **certified talent + client access + reusable IP**, not earnings.
- **Per-head / acqui-hire metrics** — EV per skilled consultant, since talent *is* the asset.
- **Milestone / real-options framing** — for platform-led AI-services startups, value rests on proving an accelerator that breaks the hours-revenue link; underwrite probability-weighted attach to a large services book rather than current EBITDA.
- **Comparable-transaction multiples** from prior SI/consulting M&A are the workhorse; DCF is unreliable pre-scale.

[Inference] Across all three stages the central valuation question in 2025-26 is the same: **does GenAI make this business's revenue deflate (a multiple de-rate) or does it let the firm sell higher-value outcomes and retain the productivity (a re-rate)?** The answer differs by where the firm sits on the T&M→outcome-based and commodity→transformation spectra.

### 9. Complete scope-to-acceptance and cash mechanics

The chain is qualify opportunity → discover requirements and estimate → contract/staff → transition or design → configure/build/test → migrate/deploy → customer acceptance → operate against SLA → improve/renew or exit and transfer knowledge. Project and managed-service economics differ: projects carry scope and acceptance risk; managed services carry transition, automation and service-level risk.

Revenue is time-and-materials, fixed price, milestone/outcome, cost-plus, per ticket/transaction/FTE, subscription or gainshare. Bookings and backlog require tests for termination, funded scope and pass-through content. Fixed-price margin depends on accurate effort, change orders and rework; T&M margin depends on bill rate, utilization and pyramid/offshore mix.

Working capital includes unbilled work, receivables, deferred revenue, subcontractor payables, transition costs and performance penalties. Customer concentration and long payment terms can turn accounting growth into cash use. Training, automation, reusable IP and delivery platforms are productive investment even when expensed; acquisitions add earnouts and integration risk.

Track bookings/book-to-bill, backlog and burn, organic constant-currency revenue, utilization, bill and pay rates, offshore/nearshore and subcontractor mix, attrition, pyramid, project margin, SLA performance, renewals, unbilled receivables, DSO and cash conversion. Stress wage inflation, utilization decline, fixed-price overrun, client insourcing, cloud/vendor change, automation deflation, visa/data-localization rule, cyber incident and discretionary-spend cuts.

Separate discretionary project, recurring managed/BPO and staffing. Normalize utilization/bench, acquisition and restructuring; value retention, cash conversion and automation-sharing.

| Technology partners | Clouds, enterprise software, hardware, cybersecurity and data platforms | Certifications and partner economics create pipeline but may compress independent value |
| Clients | Financials, healthcare, industry, government, telecom and retail | Discretionary projects fall faster than mission-critical managed services |
| Delivery infrastructure | Offices, networks, secure devices, cloud sandboxes and cyber controls | Client security and data-residency requirements limit location flexibility |
| Contracts/capital | Time-and-materials, fixed-price, outcome, managed service, public procurement and working capital | Scope risk and receivable terms determine cash conversion |

The practical test is to trace a shock through availability, delivered cost, working capital, output, customer economics, financing and eventual substitution—not merely name the adjacent market.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** A 100-FTE delivery unit operating for one year.

**Core equation:** `Delivery contribution = billable FTE × billable hours × net rate − employee/subcontract cost − bench/recruiting − travel/tools − allocated sales/overhead` 

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Billable output | 80 billable FTE × 1,700 hours × $90/hour | $12.24m revenue |
| Employee cash cost | 100 FTE × $55,000 average fully loaded cost | $5.50m |
| Subcontract, travel and tools | Specialists, cloud environments, travel and software | $1.20m |
| Bench/recruiting/training | Unbilled capacity and skill renewal | $0.80m |
| Allocated sales/overhead | Account management, facilities, compliance and corporate | $2.00m |
| Illustrative operating contribution | $12.24m − $5.50m − $1.20m − $0.80m − $2.00m | $2.74m, or 22.4%; utilization and rate are key sensitivities |

**Decision test:** Model margin by role/location pyramid and contract, then stress attrition, wage/rate lag, fixed-price rework, bench and subcontract dependence.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Last qualified team/skill/location available for a client and security requirement | Scarce architects/domain experts can bind despite broad junior labor |
| Marginal customer | Client choosing internal staff, another vendor, software automation or project deferral | Transformation ROI and switching risk determine spend |
| Clearing mechanism | Rate cards, project bids, managed-service fees and outcome sharing | Average billing rate mixes role, geography and contract |
| Cash shutdown point | Engagement exits when expected revenue fails to cover delivery, rework and account support | Strategic accounts may contain intentional loss leaders |
| New-capacity incentive | Pipeline and rate support hiring/training ahead of utilization at adequate return | Hiring too early creates bench; too late loses work |
| Adjustment lag | Weeks for contractors, quarters for hiring/training, years for domain capability and trust | Bookings do not equal immediately deliverable revenue |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Headcount | Delivery capacity | Separate billable/bench, role, skill, location, attrition and subcontractors |
| Utilization | Comparable productivity | Define denominator, vacation/training, billable status and realization |
| Bill rate | Net revenue per hour | Adjust discounts, offshore mix, subcontractors, fixed-price overrun and currency |
| Bookings/backlog | Revenue | Risk cancellation, duration, pass-throughs, scope, ramp and renewals |
| Digital/AI revenue | Standardized category | Define included services, double counting, project type and cannibalization |
