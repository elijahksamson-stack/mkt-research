## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Brokerage, property/facilities and project management, valuation, investment management, loan servicing, and merchant development.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Leasing and capital-markets brokerage | Tenant/landlord leasing and property sales | transaction value, square feet, fees | Signed/closed, representation, geography and fee split |
| Property and facilities management | Operations, maintenance and tenant service | square feet, sites, fee base | Pass-through, labor, service level and retention |
| Project/development services | Owner representation, design/construction coordination | project value, fees, backlog | At-risk versus agency, scope and acceptance |
| Investment management and servicing | Funds, asset management and loan servicing | AUM/AUA, loans, fee-earning assets | Owner capital, performance fee and fiduciary role |
| Merchant development | Land entitlement, construction, lease-up and sale/hold | units/square feet, pipeline, yield | Controlled/entitled, cost, financing, prelease and exit |

### Specifications that change value

- State advisory/agency versus principal capital risk.
- Transactions need announced/signed/closed, gross value, fee rate and company share.
- Managed area needs included services, pass-through vendor spend, fee base and retention.
- Development pipeline needs site control, entitlement, finance, NTP, cost-to-complete and prelease/presale.
- AUM and servicing balances require fee-earning, recourse, performance rights and consolidation status.

### Role map

Owner/investor hires; tenant/occupant uses; broker advises; manager operates; developer risks capital; lender finances; contractor builds; municipality entitles; fund LP supplies capital; service platform earns fees.

### Terms that must be explicit

- transaction pipeline versus closed volume
- gross contract value versus net fee revenue
- square feet managed versus fee-bearing scope
- controlled, entitled and under-construction pipeline
- base fee, development fee and promote


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: commercial real-estate services, brokerage, facilities/property management, project management, investment management, and merchant development.

### 1. Human capital and local relationships

Brokers, property/facilities managers, appraisers, project managers, engineers, investment professionals, and local market data are the primary inputs. Producer compensation is often variable, but scarce teams and client relationships drive platform value.

### 2. Transaction and financing activity

Leasing, property sales, debt/equity placement, development starts, and fund raising feed transactional revenue. Credit availability, rates, valuation agreement, and lender capacity are upstream gates. CBRE notes that credit disruptions reduce property sales, leasing, financing, and development activity (**[Fact]** — 2025 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1138118/000113811826000005/cbre-20251231.htm).

### 3. Contracted clients and subcontractors

Facilities/property management requires occupier/owner contracts, client-dedicated labor, maintenance vendors, procurement networks, and operating technology. Gross contract costs can make reported revenue large relative to platform economics.

### 4. Data and technology

Listings, comps, lease data, valuation models, work-order systems, energy data, and portfolio analytics improve advice and execution. **[Inference]** Technology is an enabler; proprietary client workflow and data density create the moat, not software spending alone.

### 5. Development capital and land

Merchant developers require land, entitlement, design, construction, anchor tenants, debt/equity, and an exit buyer. Co-investments and guarantees add balance-sheet risk beyond fee businesses.

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins:** producer compensation, subcontractor pass-through, platform productivity, fee rates, and transaction mix.
- **Cap capacity/growth:** financing, client mandates, qualified labor, entitlements, and capital commitments.
- **Shock path:** rates rise → bid/ask spreads widen → sales/debt placement fall quickly → leasing follows economy → recurring facilities/property management remains steadier.

### 8. Full service-platform and development input ledger

Service businesses require licensed brokers/managers, engineers, project managers, appraisers, facility technicians, local market data, listings, owner and tenant relationships, brand, software, secure records, cybersecurity and access to mortgage/capital markets. Physical operations need offices, vehicles, maintenance tools, call centers, vendor networks and building systems data.

Development adds land/title, entitlements, environmental and geotechnical studies, architecture/engineering, utility and transport connections, construction materials/equipment, contractors and inspectors. The physical substrate and raw materials run through concrete/cement/aggregates, steel, timber, glass, copper, pipe, insulation, petroleum-based roofing/coatings and energy. Power, water, sewer and broadband availability can determine highest and best use.

Capital includes developer equity, land loans, construction debt, mezzanine/preferred equity, joint ventures, presales/preleases, deposits, mortgage and CMBS markets, tax incentives, public infrastructure, subsidies and P3 arrangements. Brokerage/management businesses use working capital and acquisition debt; investment managers need GP commitments and fund capital. Bonding, insurance and hedging are enabling inputs.

Fee rates, transaction volume, labor/productivity and pass-through procurement set service margin; land basis, rents/sale prices, construction, rates and time set development margin. Local relationships, licenses, data, skilled teams, permits, utilities, contractors and capital gate output. Outsourcing, proptech, remote operations, modular construction, adaptive reuse and owner self-management substitute selectively, with trust, code and asset complexity limiting adoption.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Value chain

Services span tenant/landlord leasing, property sales, mortgage origination, valuation, property/facilities management, project management, investment management, loan servicing, and development. Global platforms coexist with local brokers and specialized managers.

### 2. Recurring versus transactional profit

Facilities/property/project management, loan servicing, valuation, and recurring investment-management fees are relatively resilient; sales, leasing, financing, development fees, and carried interest are cyclical. JLL explicitly classifies these streams as resilient and transactional (**[Fact]** — 2025 10-K — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1037976/000103797626000037/jll-20251231.htm).

### 3. Scale and market structure

Global occupiers and institutional owners favor firms able to serve many markets with common systems; local transactions still depend on individual producers and knowledge. CBRE reported $40.6B of 2025 revenue, with facilities management at 50.9% and property management at 6.4% (**[Fact]** — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1138118/000113811826000005/cbre-20251231.htm).

### 4. Gaining relevance

Outsourced facilities/project management, data-center/power infrastructure services, energy retrofits, flexible workplace operations, loan servicing, and integrated portfolio advice. Large occupiers outsource to reduce fixed cost and consolidate vendors.

### 5. Losing relevance

Pure local brokerage without data/platform support, proptech without distribution or workflow control, and speculative development without preleasing or funding. **[Inference]** Transaction recovery creates high incremental margins, but recurring outsourcing determines downside resilience.

### 6. Geography

The largest firms are global, yet earnings follow local capital markets, leasing, labor, and regulation. Cross-border capital and multinational clients reward network breadth; development remains site-specific.

### 7. Value migration

From one-time commissions toward contractual workplace/property/project management and data-enabled services; from asset-light advice into selective co-investment/development only when risk-adjusted spreads justify capital. Platforms increasingly acquire technical infrastructure capabilities adjacent to real estate.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs include transactions, leases, valuations, managed occupancy, facilities uptime, project delivery, loan servicing, investment vehicles and newly developed or repositioned space. Waste, emissions, congestion, displacement, construction disturbance and long-term maintenance are residuals. Service quality is measured by outcome—occupancy, uptime, cost, speed and risk—not headcount alone.

Owners, landlords, tenants, lenders, investors, occupants and governments are distinct customers or decision makers. A corporate occupier may hire facilities management while a landlord controls capital work; a fund investor pays management fees while property tenants generate underlying income; a municipality grants entitlements while residents bear externalities.

Service platforms can be global, but execution, licensing, data and relationships are local. Global corporate occupiers and institutional owners favor integrated providers; smaller properties remain fragmented among local brokers/managers. Developers compete with owner-builders, REITs, homebuilders, public authorities and nonprofits.

Private funding comes from corporate and private-equity capital for platforms and from funds, banks, debt funds, securitization, pensions/sovereigns and owner equity for assets. Public funding uses bonds, tax increment, grants, affordable-housing credits, infrastructure or P3 commitments for policy goals. Rules include brokerage/appraisal and investment-adviser licensing, fiduciary duties, zoning, environmental review, building codes, tenant and fair-housing law, accessibility, tax, mortgage/servicing rules and procurement.

The industry connects rates, banks, REITs, construction, utilities, insurance and employment. Transaction businesses respond quickly to rates and credit; management/facilities fees lag and recur; development reacts slowly, often delivering into a different cycle. Infrastructure and public incentives can create land value, while congestion, climate risk or insurance withdrawal can destroy it.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Revenue models

- **Leasing/sales brokerage:** commission when transaction closes or performance obligation is satisfied.
- **Facilities/property management:** monthly fee, cost-plus, square-foot, rent-collection, or KPI-based contract.
- **Project management:** fixed/reimbursable fee tied to project delivery.
- **Capital markets:** sales, debt/equity advisory, origination, and servicing fees.
- **Investment management:** fee on AUM/invested capital plus performance fees.
- **Development:** fees plus gain/residual value; higher capital and cycle risk.

### 2. Gross versus net revenue

Client-dedicated employees and subcontractors may be reimbursed and presented gross, inflating revenue but not platform margin. JLL reported $17.2B of gross contract costs against $26.1B of 2025 revenue (**[Fact]** — https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1037976/000103797626000037/jll-20251231.htm). Fee revenue/net service revenue is often the better productivity denominator.

### 3. Operating leverage

Producer compensation flexes, but technology, offices, management, and support create fixed cost. Transaction rebounds therefore produce strong incremental margins. Contracted outsourcing grows more steadily with lower reported margin because of pass-through costs.

### 4. KPIs

Fee revenue/net revenue, resilient versus transactional mix, transaction volumes, leasing square feet, sales/financing value, assets under management, facilities square feet, backlog, producer productivity, segment margin, cash conversion, and co-investment/development exposure.

### 5. Development mechanics

Land/entitlement → design/prelease → financing → construction → lease-up → stabilize → sell or hold. Value creation is stabilized value less land, hard/soft costs, financing, leasing cost, and required return. Schedule and exit-cap-rate changes can overwhelm the fee stream.

### 6. Failure modes

Producer-team departures, cyber/data loss, contract underpricing, client concentration, property-cycle transaction collapse, co-investment impairments, development overruns, and acquisition integration. Reported AUM without fee rate, mandate duration, and performance is incomplete.

### 7. Valuation

Use P/E or EV/EBITDA on normalized transaction activity, with sum-of-parts for recurring outsourcing, brokerage, investment management, and development. Apply capital charges to co-investments and guarantees; do not value reimbursed gross revenue like fee revenue.

### 8. Complete assignment-to-cash and project mechanics

Service workflow is win mandate → collect/validate property and market data → market, transact or operate → coordinate vendors/capital work → report and reconcile client funds → renew or close. Development is source site → control and entitle → design/finance → build → lease/sell → stabilize → hold, refinance or exit. The service firm may advise without owning risk; merchant development places capital at risk.

Revenue includes transaction commissions, recurring property/facilities fees, project-management fees, loan servicing, investment-management fees, development fees and performance/promote. Gross versus net presentation matters when vendor spend is passed through. Pipeline and transaction “wins” are not revenue until contingencies and performance obligations clear.

Working capital includes commissions receivable, client funds/payables, reimbursable project costs, accrued bonuses and, for development, land, construction in progress, deposits and interest capitalization. Off-balance-sheet funds/JVs still create commitments, guarantees, reputational and promote exposure. Maintenance software/people investment differs from land and project growth capital.

Track leasing and sales volume, fee per transaction, recurring-fee base, retention, square footage managed, margin and pass-through mix; for investment management, fee-earning AUM, fundraising, deployment and realizations; for development, controlled/entitled sites, cost to complete, prelease/presale, yield on cost, schedule, contingency and expected value. Stress rate/credit closure, transaction freeze, cost inflation, permit delay, tenant default, valuation fall, owner budget cuts and project guarantees.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Service revenue = closed transactions × realized fee share + recurring fee base × rate + project/servicing fees. Development profit = stabilized/sale value − land − hard/soft cost − interest − fees − tax, adjusted for time, guarantees and partner waterfall.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Broker fee yield | basis points or percent of transaction/lease value | Revenue conversion |
| Recurring fee margin | net of pass-through vendors | Platform quality |
| Retention | percent of fee base and clients | Durability |
| Transaction conversion | closed mandates/pipeline | Cycle and execution |
| Development yield on cost | stabilized NOI/total cost | Value creation |
| Prelease/presale | percent before completion | Demand and finance |
| Cost-to-complete | remaining capital plus contingency | Downside |
| Entitlement lead | months to many years | Pipeline probability |
| Fee-earning AUM | average balance and effective bps | Investment management |
| Working capital | unbilled fees, reimbursables and commissions | Cash conversion |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Separate gross pass-through from net fee, announced from closed transactions, reimbursables, commission/bonus accruals, client funds, fund/JV consolidation, promote accrual/realization, land inventory and capitalized interest.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Transaction volume, price, rates, credit, owner/tenant decisions and hiring.
- Labor/productivity, vendor pass-through, contract renewal and technology.
- Land, entitlement, construction, utilities, lease-up, financing and exit cap rate.
- Fundraising, AUM, valuations, servicing runoff and defaults.

### Valuation discipline

Value recurring management/servicing, cyclical transaction, investment management/carry and merchant development separately. Adjust for guarantees, client funds, fund/JV commitments and land risk.

## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2007–2009 transaction freeze | Credit and values fell | Brokerage collapsed before recurring management; development inventories impaired | Service lines have different cycles |
| 2010s outsourced facilities growth | Global occupiers consolidated vendors | Scale platforms gained recurring fees but managed pass-through cost | Square footage is not revenue quality |
| WeWork rise and 2019 failed IPO | Long leases funded short flexible memberships | Governance and maturity mismatch surfaced | Tech branding does not remove real-estate liabilities |
| 2020 occupancy disruption | Offices, retail and hotels faced operational shock | Facilities work continued while leasing/sales slowed | Recurring service can hedge transactions |
| 2022–2025 rate and valuation reset | Financing costs rose and transactions froze | Appraisals, funds and development adjusted with lags | Price discovery requires actual capital |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Financing spreads, transaction pipeline/closures, leasing, owner budgets, managed retention, fundraising, entitlements, prelease and construction cost.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** Managed area growth without net fees, pipeline without close/NTP, or development profit excluding interest/guarantees.
- **Durable lesson:** Separate advice and fee streams from principal property risk, then map where the firm quietly guarantees the latter.

### Indicator stack

- **Leading:** credit; listings; mandates; tours; permits; fundraising; prelease.
- **Coincident:** closed volume; fees; managed area; retention; construction; lease-up.
- **Lagging:** appraisals; promote; impairments; servicing defaults; completions.

### Minimum dashboard

1. **Broker fee yield** — basis points or percent of transaction/lease value; Revenue conversion.
2. **Recurring fee margin** — net of pass-through vendors; Platform quality.
3. **Retention** — percent of fee base and clients; Durability.
4. **Transaction conversion** — closed mandates/pipeline; Cycle and execution.
5. **Development yield on cost** — stabilized NOI/total cost; Value creation.
6. **Prelease/presale** — percent before completion; Demand and finance.
7. **Cost-to-complete** — remaining capital plus contingency; Downside.
8. **Entitlement lead** — months to many years; Pipeline probability.
9. **Fee-earning AUM** — average balance and effective bps; Investment management.
10. **Working capital** — unbilled fees, reimbursables and commissions; Cash conversion.

### Normalization rules

- Use net fees after pass-through.
- Track closed not announced volume.
- Probability-weight development stages.
- Include guarantees and JV commitments.

### Evidence traps

- Using transaction value as revenue.
- Treating square feet managed as homogeneous.
- Accruing promote before realization.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Land and permission | Title, zoning, entitlements, environmental review, utilities and public infrastructure | Residual land value absorbs changes in rent, construction cost, rates and delay |
| Construction supply chain | Architects, engineers, contractors, cement, steel, glass, equipment and labor | Escalation and schedule determine development margin |

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** A 200-unit for-sale residential development.

**Core equation:** `Development profit = completed units × net selling price − land − hard cost − soft/permit cost − finance/carry − selling cost − contingency`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Gross development value | 200 units × $400,000 net selling price | $80m |
| Land | Acquisition, closing and site holding basis | $12m |
| Hard construction | Structure, MEP, finishes, site work and contractor margin | $40m |
| Soft cost and permits | Design, engineering, approvals, insurance and developer overhead | $8m |
| Finance, selling and contingency | $5m carry + $3m sales + $2m contingency | $10m |
| Illustrative profit | $80m − $12m − $40m − $8m − $10m | $10m; 12.5% of revenue and 14.3% on cost before delay or price stress |

**Decision test:** Recalculate residual land value and peak equity under price, absorption, cost, rate and delay scenarios; accounting profit does not reveal cash-at-risk.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Next entitled project or service team that can meet location, use and client requirements | Land, permit, construction and capital define development supply |
| Marginal customer | Occupier/buyer/investor able to rent, buy, outsource, self-manage or defer | Financing and alternative space determine willingness to pay |
| Clearing mechanism | Property sale/rent negotiations, brokerage fees, management bids and development JVs | Each revenue stream has a different price and risk basis |
| Cash shutdown point | Service mandate exits below avoidable labor/platform cost; project may continue if abandonment loses more than completion | Sunk land and guarantees create convex downside |
| New-capacity incentive | Development value exceeds all-in cost/required return; service contract covers team and platform | Cycle-average transaction volume should support permanent overhead |
| Adjustment lag | Days for advisory staffing, quarters for transactions, years for entitlements and development | Fee and principal businesses should not share one cycle assumption |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Transaction volume | Fee revenue | Apply asset mix, geography, fee rate, co-broker share and completion timing |
| AUM | Owned assets or fee-bearing equity | Separate appraisal value, committed/uninvested, leverage and ownership |
| Backlog/pipeline | Contracted revenue or buildable projects | Risk mandate, client approval, land, entitlement, capital and start date |
| Gross development value | Profit or cash proceeds | Subtract unsold units, discounts, tax, all costs, financing and partner shares |
| Fee revenue | Net value added | Identify reimbursable/pass-through procurement, subcontractors, incentives and principal risk |
