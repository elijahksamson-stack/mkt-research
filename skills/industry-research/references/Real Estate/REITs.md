## 1. Taxonomy and Industry Boundaries


Reviewed through 2026-07-14. Define the measured object before comparing firms, assets, or markets.

### Boundary

Listed equity REITs and mortgage REITs, explicitly separated. Equity REITs own/operate property; mortgage REITs own or finance real-estate credit.

### Operating taxonomy

| Segment | What is sold | Primary unit | Do not aggregate without |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Residential and self-storage | Apartments, single-family rental and storage | units, occupancy, rent | Turnover, local supply, affordability and operating intensity |
| Industrial, retail and office | Logistics, shopping and workplace property | square feet, occupancy, rent | Lease length, tenant capex, node and obsolescence |
| Data, tower and digital | Data centers, towers and related infrastructure | MW, cabinets, sites, leases | Power/fiber/spectrum, development and customer concentration |
| Healthcare, lodging and experiential | Medical/senior, hotels, gaming and specialty | beds/rooms/sites, NOI | Operator/reimbursement, daily pricing and regulation |
| Mortgage REITs | Agency/nonagency mortgages, loans and servicing exposure | assets, leverage, spread | Credit, duration, repo, hedge, haircut and margin |

### Specifications that change value

- State property type, submarket, age/quality, lease structure, tenant credit and same-store population.
- Occupancy needs physical, leased, economic and commenced distinctions.
- Rent needs cash/straight-line, gross/net, concession, escalation, reimbursement and mark-to-market.
- NOI must state included property expenses; AFFO must state recurring capex and leasing costs.
- Debt needs secured/unsecured, recourse, fixed/floating, maturity, covenant and JV share.

### Role map

Landlord owns; tenant leases; occupant uses; operator may run business; manager services; lender/JV finances; developer adds supply; municipality permits/taxes; REIT shareholders receive distributions.

### Terms that must be explicit

- NOI, EBITDA, FFO and AFFO
- physical, leased and economic occupancy
- cash rent versus straight-line rent
- cap rate versus discount rate
- equity REIT versus mortgage REIT


## 2. Inputs and Dependencies


Scope: listed equity and mortgage REIT structures, with emphasis on property-owning equity REITs.

### 1. Capital

Debt, common equity, preferred equity, retained cash after dividends, and joint-venture capital fund acquisitions and development. REIT distribution rules limit retained earnings, making external capital access unusually important. At June 2026 listed U.S. REITs had a 35.4% debt ratio and 4.67x coverage on Nareit's market-cap-weighted measures (**[Fact]** — https://www.reit.com/data-research/reit-market-data/report/reit-industry-financial-snapshot).

### 2. Land/buildings and development inputs

Location, zoning, entitlements, construction labor, steel/concrete, MEP equipment, and utility capacity determine new supply. Property types have distinct gates: data centers need power; labs need technical fit-out; industrial needs land/highway access; apartments need permits; towers need carrier demand.

### 3. Tenants and leases

Tenant credit, demand, lease terms, escalators, concessions, renewal options, and tenant improvements are economic inputs. Long leases create visibility but delay mark-to-market; short leases reprice quickly but increase volatility.

### 4. Operations

Property management, maintenance, taxes, insurance, utilities, security, and local labor protect occupancy and net operating income. Climate exposure raises insurance and capex. For triple-net leases tenants bear more costs; hotels and senior housing carry far more operating exposure.

### 5. Regulation and tax

REIT qualification, property tax, rent control, zoning, building/emissions codes, healthcare reimbursement, and sector-specific licensing affect cash flow. Mortgage REITs additionally require secured funding, hedging, collateral, and liquidity.

### 6. Margin inputs vs. capacity gates

- **Set margins/NOI:** rent, occupancy, concessions, taxes, insurance, utilities, and property labor.
- **Cap growth:** cost of capital, zoning, construction capacity, power/water, and acquisition supply.
- **Shock path:** rates rise → public REIT cost of capital/NAV multiple weaken → acquisitions stop first → private property values reprice later → well-capitalized REITs can become buyers after sellers capitulate.

### 8. Full property, operating, and capital input ledger

Equity REIT inputs begin with land/buildings, title, zoning, leases, tenants and local infrastructure. Development and recurring capex consume concrete, aggregates, steel, timber, glass, roofing, insulation, gypsum, copper, pipe, HVAC, elevators, electrical gear, transformers, furniture/fixtures and construction labor; their upstream commodity, energy and freight chains affect replacement cost. Operations consume electricity, gas, water, waste, insurance, taxes, security, cleaning, maintenance, broadband, parking and property-management systems.

Each property type adds critical inputs: data centers need firm power, fiber, cooling and generators; towers need spectrum tenants, ground leases and permits; healthcare needs operator licenses/reimbursement; timber needs biological growth and roads; hotels/senior housing need operating labor; industrial needs truck access; apartments need local wages and household formation. Environmental condition, climate resilience and insurability are property inputs, not external footnotes.

Capital includes common/preferred equity, unsecured and mortgage debt, revolvers, private placements, joint ventures, retained cash and asset-sale proceeds. Tenant credit and lease security are quasi-financial inputs. Mortgage REITs instead require repo/secured funding, collateral, hedging and margin liquidity; they must not be analyzed through physical rent economics.

Rent and occupancy, property operating cost, taxes, insurance and utilities set NOI; rates, credit spreads and cap rates set asset and external-growth returns. Zoning/permits, land, power/water, construction labor, equipment, tenant demand and balance-sheet headroom gate supply. Renovation, conversion, densification, flex space, remote work, e-commerce and shared infrastructure create substitutions whose feasibility depends on layout, code, location and basis.

## 3. Market Landscape


### 1. Property sectors

Apartments, single-family rental, industrial, office, retail, data centers, towers, healthcare, self-storage, lodging, gaming, timber, and specialty REITs have different lease durations, capex, cycles, and customers. Mortgage REITs own financial assets and should not be analyzed like landlords.

### 2. Industry scale and structure

Nareit reported $1.63T of all-REIT equity market cap and $1.55T for equity REITs in June 2026; REITs collectively owned more than $4.5T of commercial real estate assets (**[Fact]** — https://www.reit.com/data-research/reit-market-data/report/reit-industry-financial-snapshot). Public scale gives liquidity, governance, unsecured debt access, and operating data.

### 3. Profit pools

**[Inference]** Rent growth and development spreads accrue where demand exceeds difficult-to-build supply. Operating platforms create advantage in storage, residential, senior housing, and hotels; scale procurement/capital creates advantage broadly. Commodity buildings in oversupplied markets compete away rent.

### 4. Gaining relevance

Data centers/power access, healthcare demographics, logistics, towers/digital infrastructure, and selected residential markets. Emerging property types now represent just over half of the equity REIT index market cap versus traditional office/retail/residential/industrial/diversified (**[Fact]** — Nareit, June 2026 — https://www.reit.com/news/blog/market-commentary/ftse-nareit-all-equity-reits-index-reaches-15-trillion-market).

### 5. Losing relevance

Commodity office without amenity/location advantage, assets facing functional obsolescence, and external growth funded above investment yield. Office property counts held by public REITs fell 28% from 2018 to 2024, while data-center properties rose 15% in 2024 alone (**[Fact]** — https://www.reit.com/news/blog/market-commentary/reits-own-570000-properties-us).

### 6. Geography

Real estate is local: job/population growth, supply, taxes, regulation, climate, and infrastructure shape returns. Portfolios can diversify local risk but cannot erase property-type cycles.

### 7. Value migration

Toward scarce infrastructure-enabled sites, operating platforms, and alternative property types; away from passive commodity ownership. Public REITs can gain share when their cost of capital falls below private buyers or when balance-sheet strength lets them buy distress.

### 8. Complete output, customer, geography, funding, and policy map

Outputs are usable space, location/access, power/connectivity, amenities, operations and lease-duration/credit exposure; mortgage REITs output financing and liquidity. Construction waste, energy/emissions, congestion, land use and eventual demolition are external or residual outputs. A building can be physically occupied yet economically obsolete if layout, energy performance, location or tenant demand no longer fits.

The lessee, daily user, payer and beneficiary may differ: employers lease offices for workers; retailers lease for shoppers; health systems/operators lease for patients; logistics tenants serve merchants; governments subsidize some residential/health demand. Triple-net, gross and management-intensive formats allocate tax, insurance, maintenance and operating risk differently.

Real estate is local by submarket and even node, though capital is global. Demand follows jobs, income, trade, demographics and infrastructure; supply follows zoning, land, construction economics and finance. Public REITs, private funds, pensions, sovereigns, owner-occupiers, developers, nonprofits, governments and households all set transaction and rental clearing prices.

Funding spans public REIT equity/debt, CMBS and mortgages, bank/debt-fund loans, private equity, joint ventures, pension/sovereign capital, municipal infrastructure and housing/health tax or subsidy programs. Policy includes land use, building and energy codes, rent and tenant law, property tax, environmental cleanup, accessibility, foreign-investment review, mortgage/securities rules and the tax/distribution requirements of REIT status.

Cross-markets include rates/credit, construction materials and labor, utilities, insurance, transport, retail, hotels, healthcare and technology. E-commerce shifts value toward logistics and data infrastructure; remote work changes office and residential geography; power scarcity caps data centers; uninsurable climate exposure impairs financing; high rates hit property values before leases reset. Property-type labels are therefore insufficient without lease, tenant, funding and local-supply detail.

## 4. Operating Mechanics


### 1. Property cash flow

Potential rent − vacancy/credit loss + reimbursements/other income = property revenue. Subtract property operating expense to get NOI. Then subtract G&A, interest, recurring capex/tenant costs, and taxes to approach cash available to shareholders.

### 2. Why GAAP EPS is insufficient

Real-estate depreciation reduces GAAP income even when well-maintained property values may not decline similarly. FFO adds back real-estate depreciation and selected sale gains/losses; AFFO further adjusts recurring capex, straight-line rent, and other items—but company definitions vary.

### 3. Lease mechanics

Track occupancy, lease expiry, rent escalators, releasing spreads, free rent, tenant improvements, leasing commissions, and rent collection. Same-store NOI isolates stabilized assets; development yield compares stabilized NOI to cost.

### 4. Balance sheet and NAV

Debt maturity, fixed/floating mix, secured versus unsecured debt, covenants, interest coverage, and liquidity determine resilience. NAV capitalizes forward property NOI at market cap rates, subtracts net debt and other claims, and adds development/other assets.

### 5. KPIs

FFO/AFFO per share, same-store NOI, occupancy, releasing spread, retention, rent collection, development pipeline/yield, acquisition cap rate, recurring capex, leverage, fixed-charge coverage, and NAV premium/discount.

### 6. Failure modes

Funding long-duration assets with short/floating debt, ignoring recurring capex, overbuilding after rent spikes, tenant concentration, poor development leasing, and using low cap rates on weak assets. For mortgage REITs: leverage, repo margin calls, duration/basis mismatch, and book-value volatility.

### 7. Valuation

Use P/FFO or P/AFFO, implied cap rate, dividend yield/coverage, and NAV premium/discount. Compare within property type because lease, capex, and growth differ. A NAV discount can signal opportunity or correctly price obsolete assets and expensive leverage.

### 8. Complete lease-to-cash and asset-lifecycle mechanics

The equity-property chain is acquire/develop → lease and fund tenant work → operate and maintain → renew/re-lease → renovate/redevelop → sell or decommission. Signed, commenced and cash-paying occupancy differ. Lease economics include face rent, free rent, escalators, reimbursements, percentage rent, concessions, tenant improvements, leasing commission, downtime and credit loss.

NOI must be reconciled to recurring cash by subtracting G&A, cash interest, recurring building capex, tenant/leasing spend and preferred/minority claims. Working capital includes rent and other receivables, prepaid or escrowed property costs, payables, tenant deposits and development inventory where applicable. Same-store growth separates organic operation from acquisitions/development; FFO removes property depreciation and gains, while AFFO estimates recurring cash but varies by definition. Straight-line rent is not current cash.

Development value requires stabilized NOI, cost to complete, lease-up time, yield on cost and exit cap rate; acquisitions require going-in yield, financing and future capex. Joint ventures can move debt and capex off the consolidated balance sheet without removing economic exposure. For mortgage REITs, model asset yield, repo/hedge cost, duration, convexity, credit loss, haircut and margin calls instead.

Track occupancy/leased rate, rent spreads, retention, same-store NOI, concessions and tenant capex, lease expiry, tenant concentration, development pipeline and yield, capex, net debt/EBITDA, fixed-rate share, maturity ladder, secured debt, unencumbered assets, interest coverage, NAV and external funding spread. Stress recession, tenant failure, insurance/tax/utility inflation, refinancing, cap-rate widening, construction overrun, oversupply, climate event and functional obsolescence.

## 5. Economics and Valuation


Benchmark bands are diagnostic starting points; refresh for product, asset, geography, contract, and cycle.

### Core unit-economic identity

Equity-property cash = potential rent − vacancy/credit/concessions + other income − property operating expense − recurring capex/leasing cost. Equity value = asset NOI/cap-rate value less debt and claims. Mortgage REIT earnings = asset yield − funding/hedge/credit cost.

### Ten benchmark measures

| Measure | Diagnostic band or unit | Decision use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Occupancy | often ~85–98% by property/cycle | Supply-demand and fixed-cost absorption |
| Same-store NOI growth | price/occupancy/expense on stable pool | Organic operation |
| Rent spread | new/renewal cash rent versus expiring | Embedded growth |
| Lease duration | months/years or WALE | Repricing and credit |
| Cap rate | property NOI/value; asset and market specific | Transaction valuation |
| Net debt/EBITDA | often ~4–8x for equity REITs, property-specific | Leverage |
| Fixed-rate debt share | percent and weighted maturity | Rate resilience |
| Recurring capex/leasing | percent of NOI or per square foot | True cash conversion |
| Development yield spread | yield on cost less stabilized market cap rate | Value creation |
| Payout/coverage | dividend versus AFFO/cash | Funding flexibility |

### Accounting-to-cash bridge

Reconcile GAAP rent/straight-line and depreciation to NOI, FFO and company-defined AFFO; include tenant improvements, leasing commissions, recurring capex, JV debt/claims and noncontrolling interests. Mortgage REITs require fair value, repo, hedges and credit reserves.

### Highest-value sensitivities

- Market rent, occupancy, tenant credit, supply, taxes, insurance, utilities and labor.
- Rates, cap rates, debt maturities, equity cost and external-growth spread.
- Development cost/time, power/water, zoning, construction and lease-up.
- Property-specific obsolescence, climate/insurance and operator/reimbursement.

### Valuation discipline

Use property/submarket NAV and recurring per-share cash together. Apply property-specific cap rates and full debt/JV claims. Do not use equity-REIT FFO logic for mortgage REITs.


## 6. Regulation and Public Funding


Snapshot reviewed through **2026-07-14**. Verify enacted text, implementation dates, litigation, and current guidance.

### Permission chain

Landlord owns; tenant leases; occupant uses; operator may run business; manager services; lender/JV finances; developer adds supply; municipality permits/taxes; REIT shareholders receive distributions.

### Jurisdiction and regime map

| Jurisdiction or layer | Core regimes and authorities | Economic transmission |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States REIT/tax | REIT income/asset/distribution tests, SEC securities, state property/tenant law | Portfolio, payout, tax and governance |
| Local property | Zoning, building/energy/fire, rent/tenant, property tax and environmental rules | Supply, capex, cash rent and asset value |
| Sector-specific | Healthcare reimbursement/licensing, towers/spectrum, gaming, lodging, data-center power/environment | Tenant and operating economics |
| Mortgage/credit | Banking/securities, repo, mortgage, servicing and consumer rules | Leverage, liquidity and credit |

### Public and private funding

Private funding includes public REIT equity/debt, mortgages/CMBS, bank/debt-fund loans, private funds, JVs and retained cash. Public funding includes housing/health tax incentives, municipal infrastructure, subsidies, public tenants and credit agencies.

### Enforcement and liability

Loss of REIT status, securities claims, covenant/default, zoning/building violation, tenant litigation, environmental remediation, foreclosure and sector-license loss are material.

### Update checklist

- Confirm entity, asset, product, customer, location, and authority.
- Separate law, final rule, guidance, permit, litigation, and proposal.
- Date funding, tax, tariff, sanction, quota, and administered-price terms.
- Trace cost, schedule, capacity, price, liability, and funding separately.


## 7. Historical Cases and Failure Patterns


Cases are selected for reusable causal lessons.

### Historical and operating episodes

| Episode | Initial condition or shock | Transmission and outcome | Reusable lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1990s modern REIT expansion | Public capital recapitalized property after credit stress | Transparent platforms and unsecured debt scaled | Capital-market structure changes ownership |
| 2007–2009 property/credit crisis | Values fell while secured debt matured | Dilutive equity and asset sales restored balance sheets | Maturity and liquidity precede NAV recovery |
| 2020 property divergence | Hotels/retail/office fell while data/logistics/residential differed | Property labels produced opposite outcomes | Segment and lease structure matter |
| 2022–2025 rate reset | Debt and cap rates repriced before many leases | Transaction markets froze and refinancing widened | Private marks lag public prices |
| Office functional obsolescence | Hybrid work met older stock and refinancing needs | Vacancy, capex and conversion feasibility diverged | Replacement cost is not value without demand |

### Practitioner extraction

- **Leading signals:** Leasing, tenant sales/credit, market rents, supply pipeline, transactions/cap rates, debt spreads, maturities, power/permits and insurance.
- **Evidence that breaks the easy thesis:** FFO growth from capitalized/leasing omissions, occupancy without cash commencement, or NAV using stale cap rates and hidden JV debt.
- **Durable lesson:** Real estate cash is lease-, building-, submarket-, and capital-structure specific.

### Indicator stack

- **Leading:** leasing tours; concessions; permits; starts; transactions; spreads; maturities.
- **Coincident:** occupancy; cash rent; NOI; collections; capex; debt cost.
- **Lagging:** appraisals; cap rates; defaults; deliveries; impairments.

### Minimum dashboard

1. **Occupancy** — often ~85–98% by property/cycle; Supply-demand and fixed-cost absorption.
2. **Same-store NOI growth** — price/occupancy/expense on stable pool; Organic operation.
3. **Rent spread** — new/renewal cash rent versus expiring; Embedded growth.
4. **Lease duration** — months/years or WALE; Repricing and credit.
5. **Cap rate** — property NOI/value; asset and market specific; Transaction valuation.
6. **Net debt/EBITDA** — often ~4–8x for equity REITs, property-specific; Leverage.
7. **Fixed-rate debt share** — percent and weighted maturity; Rate resilience.
8. **Recurring capex/leasing** — percent of NOI or per square foot; True cash conversion.
9. **Development yield spread** — yield on cost less stabilized market cap rate; Value creation.
10. **Payout/coverage** — dividend versus AFFO/cash; Funding flexibility.

### Normalization rules

- Use same-store pools and cash rent.
- Include recurring capex/leasing.
- Gross up JV debt/claims.
- Separate equity and mortgage REITs.

### Evidence traps

- Using FFO as free cash flow.
- Applying one cap rate to all properties.
- Treating leased as occupied/cash-paying.

## 9. Geographic Operating Models

Geography changes ownership, contracting, cost, funding, regulation and risk; compare like with like.

| Geography / archetype | Operating model | Economic and risk implications |
| --- | --- | --- |
| United States | Listed equity REITs with tax-distribution requirements and specialized property sectors | Unsecured bond access, same-store disclosure and sector specialization support scale |
| Continental Europe/United Kingdom | Listed property companies/REIT regimes with greater mixed-use and development exposure | Valuation, leverage and energy retrofit requirements vary by country |
| Japan/Singapore/Australia | Externally or internally managed listed trusts with sponsor relationships and cross-border portfolios | Management structure, FX and sponsor transactions require normalization |
| Emerging markets | Nascent listed vehicles alongside private/family/state ownership | Liquidity, title, inflation indexation and currency funding dominate |
| Alternative property sectors | Data centers, towers, healthcare, self-storage, logistics and residential platforms | Operating intensity and customer contracts differ fundamentally from traditional landlord models |

## 10. Cross-Industry Dependency Map

| Dependency class | Linked markets and institutions | Transmission into this industry |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Capital markets | Mortgages, unsecured bonds, bank lines, equity, preferreds and joint ventures | Cap rates and refinancing costs reprice asset values before lease cash flows reset |
| Tenants/customers | Households, retailers, offices, logistics, healthcare, digital infrastructure and hospitality users | Tenant revenue and substitution determine rent coverage |
| Physical inputs | Land, construction, utilities, labor, insurance, taxes and maintenance vendors | Replacement cost supports rents but raises capex and operating burden |
| Public systems | Zoning, transit, schools, reimbursement, permits and local tax | Location value and allowed use are partly publicly produced |
| Adjacent markets | Private real estate, developers, owner-occupiers, hotels, infrastructure and housing finance | Asset sales, supply and alternative occupancy set pricing |

The practical test is to trace a shock through availability, delivered cost, working capital, output, customer economics, financing and eventual substitution—not merely name the adjacent market.

## 11. Decision-Grade Unit Economics

> The numerical example below is illustrative, not a current market forecast or universal benchmark. Replace every assumption with asset-, contract-, product- and geography-specific evidence.

**Representative unit:** A 100,000-square-foot stabilized commercial property.

**Core equation:** `Recurring property cash = occupied area × net rent + reimbursements/other − property operating expense − recurring tenant/leasing/building capital`

| Bridge item | Illustrative assumption and calculation | Result / interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Base rent | 100,000 sf × 95% occupancy × $30/sf | $2.85m |
| Reimbursements/other | Tenant recoveries, parking and service income | $0.45m |
| Property operating expense | Tax, insurance, utilities, repairs and onsite labor | $1.00m |
| NOI | $2.85m + $0.45m − $1.00m | $2.30m |
| Recurring capital | Tenant improvements, leasing commissions and recurring building capex | $0.50m |
| Recurring cash/value marker | $1.80m cash after recurring capital; $2.30m ÷ 6.5% cap rate | $1.80m cash and $35.4m unlevered value before debt/corporate cost |

**Decision test:** Value each property on lease-level cash, recurring capital, supply and financing; reported FFO and occupancy cannot replace a rent-roll and capital bridge.

## 12. Industry Balance and Marginal Economics

| Balance concept | Industry-specific definition | What to measure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Marginal supply unit | Next development, conversion or competing building serving a submarket/property use | Replacement cost, land, permits and financing set long-run rent |
| Marginal customer | Tenant choosing space, remote work, ownership, another submarket or reduced footprint | Occupancy cost and business productivity set willingness to pay |
| Clearing mechanism | Negotiated rent, concessions, term, escalation and tenant capital | Face rent is not effective rent |
| Cash shutdown point | Property remains open while rent exceeds avoidable operating/safety cost, but capital can be deferred only temporarily | Debt and covenants influence hold/sale/default |
| New-capacity incentive | Stabilized NOI value exceeds total development cost by adequate risk margin | Yield-on-cost must be compared with market cap rate and lease-up risk |
| Adjustment lag | Months for leasing, years for construction, decades for land-use change | Physical supply reacts slowly while public values move daily |

## 13. Terminology and Comparability Traps

| Reported term | Common but invalid interpretation | Required normalization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| FFO | Recurring distributable cash | Adjust gains, straight-line rent, recurring capex, leasing cost, debt and noncontrolling interests |
| AFFO | Standardized metric | Rebuild from disclosed assumptions; definitions vary materially |
| NOI | Property free cash flow | Subtract recurring tenant, leasing and building capital plus corporate cost |
| Occupancy | Economic utilization | Separate physical/economic, leased/commenced, concessions and tenant credit |
| Cap rate | Expected investment return | State forward/trailing NOI, capex, growth, leverage, transaction cost and terminal value |
